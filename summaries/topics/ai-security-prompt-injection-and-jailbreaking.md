# Topic: AI security, prompt injection, and jailbreaking

## Executive Summary

The records collectively show AI security moving from a narrow concern about “jailbreak prompts” into a broad security discipline covering agentic application design, tool-use authorization, AI-enabled offensive operations, privacy leakage, governance, incident response, and detection engineering. The strongest recurring claim is that prompt injection is not a conventional bug that can simply be patched away; once LLMs consume untrusted content and can act through tools, attackers can steer downstream actions, exfiltrate data, or trigger lateral movement unless the surrounding architecture constrains authority and blast radius [record_id:58] [record_id:71] [record_id:85] [record_id:2358] [record_id:2361] [record_id:2369].

A major trend is the shift from chatbots to agents. Multiple records describe AI systems that read email, calendars, documents, browser pages, code workspaces, enterprise search results, local files, or tool metadata, then perform actions through APIs, shells, browsers, home devices, or business applications. Representative attacks include Gemini agent hijacking through a Google Calendar invite [record_id:53] [record_id:1965], Microsoft Copilot trust manipulation through “data voids” [record_id:2110], malicious MCP servers manipulating Claude’s tool reasoning [record_id:2207], document-embedded injections in AI KYC pipelines [record_id:2328], and promptware that enables persistence, lateral movement, C2, and exfiltration across agentic systems [record_id:2369].

The corpus also emphasizes AI as an accelerator for offense. Records discuss AI-assisted phishing and vishing [record_id:1878] [record_id:2119] [record_id:2192] [record_id:2394], autonomous vulnerability discovery and exploitation [record_id:79] [record_id:1923] [record_id:2329] [record_id:2334] [record_id:2350], AI-assisted malware and C2 [record_id:4] [record_id:62] [record_id:2391] [record_id:2573], and compressed cloud attack timelines observed or hypothesized in the wild [record_id:1972] [record_id:2134] [record_id:2355]. At the same time, many talks propose defensive tools: PyRIT for GenAI red teaming [record_id:135], spotlighting for indirect prompt injection defense [record_id:144], prompt patching and prompt hardening [record_id:2199] [record_id:2506], semantic detection systems such as SYARA [record_id:2342], privacy-preserving AI threat fingerprints [record_id:2327], reference monitors for coding agents [record_id:2345], capability-based authorization [record_id:2370], and production browser-agent prompt-injection detection with BrowseSafe [record_id:2363].

The evidence base is mostly conference abstracts, workshop descriptions, and recorded-session summaries from 2023–2026. It is rich in claims, demos, tool names, and research directions, but uneven in empirical detail. Some records contain quantitative findings—such as spotlighting reducing attack success from over 50% to below 2% [record_id:144], a 7B specialist model for AV/EDR evasion [record_id:62], 200+ zero-days found by autonomous agents [record_id:79], 1.8M prompts distilled into 30 daily alerts [record_id:2377], and BrowseSafe achieving around 0.91 F1 under sub-100ms latency [record_id:2363]. Many other records are high-level, duplicated across venues, or have minimal raw text, so downstream researchers should distinguish between demonstrated research, proposed frameworks, vendor/practitioner talks, and speculative governance or future-risk discussions.

## Major Themes And Trends

### 1. Prompt injection is becoming an application and systems-security problem, not just a model problem

Early records frame prompt injection as a weakness caused by LLMs processing concatenated inputs without reliably distinguishing instruction provenance. The CAMLIS prompt-injection tutorial separates user prompt injection attacks and cross-domain prompt injection attacks while giving attendees hands-on experience building and attacking LLM bots [record_id:171]. The repeated-sequence ChatGPT record reports unusual model behavior involving control characters such as backspace and carriage returns, suggesting a way for user-controlled input to circumvent contextual constraints [record_id:170]. Spotlighting then treats the problem as one of provenance signaling: transforming inputs to help models distinguish untrusted data from instructions, reportedly reducing attack success from above 50% to below 2% in experiments [record_id:144].

By 2025–2026, the dominant view is harsher: prompt injection is persistent and cannot be eliminated solely by guardrails. NCC Group’s Black Hat talk states that “eliminating prompt injection is a lost cause” and argues for architectural controls, threat modeling, and trust-flow modeling [record_id:58]. Stripe’s “Breaking the Lethal Trifecta” similarly says there is no deterministic defense and advocates containment through controlled egress, human confirmation flows, and CI-time tool annotations [record_id:2358]. Google Workspace’s “Perfect Storm” formulation describes the dangerous intersection of sensitive-data access, untrusted content exposure, and external command capability [record_id:2361]. These records converge on a systems-security framing: the model may be manipulable, so the surrounding system must limit what manipulation can achieve.

### 2. Agentic AI expands blast radius through tools, memory, identity, and workflows

A major trend is the move from static chat interfaces to agents that browse, search, write files, call APIs, issue shell commands, manage calendars, and interact with enterprise systems. NVIDIA’s red-team record contrasts early RAG workflows with modern agentic systems that use expanded input modalities, dynamic reasoning, and independent action over sensitive systems [record_id:85]. DEF CON’s multi-agent workflow session highlights tool execution, persistent memory, and inter-agent communication as attack-surface expansions [record_id:1956]. Dropbox’s daily-driver agent talk focuses on capability bounding, prompt-injection detection, memory isolation failures, and OAuth device flow for headless operation [record_id:2359].

Agent attacks in the records often follow a pattern: untrusted content enters a context the agent treats as relevant, and the agent then uses legitimate tools with the victim’s authority. The Gemini Calendar-invite attack embeds an indirect prompt injection in a meeting subject, hijacks Gemini for Workspace agents, and claims exploit effects ranging from toxic content and phishing to calendar deletion, email/calendar exfiltration, geolocation, Zoom streaming, home-appliance control, and a worm targeting Gemini clients [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. Google’s later Workspace defense talk explicitly references a real-world attack where an agent could be hijacked through a calendar invitation and discusses defense-in-depth, chaining policies, and output sanitization [record_id:2361]. AI KYC exploitation shows a similar pattern in document-processing workflows: passports or documents become an “execution environment,” where embedded injects steer extraction agents into cross-record reads and writes without bypassing conventional access controls [record_id:2328].

MCP appears repeatedly as a new integration layer with meaningful security risk. The malicious MCP record shows hidden instructions in metadata responses manipulating Claude Desktop’s reasoning chain and causing local-file reads or arbitrary code execution while hiding actions from the user [record_id:2207]. BSidesLV’s MCP talk calls out tool poisoning, shared-memory exploits, version drift, and line-jumping attacks before explicit tool invocation [record_id:2554]. Prompt||GTFO discussions and demos also treat MCP servers as REST-like layers for local tools, reverse engineering, memory graphs, malware analysis, and coding workflows [record_id:2213] [record_id:2219] [record_id:2228] [record_id:2236].

### 3. Defensive thinking is shifting from “guardrails” to containment, authorization, observability, and reference monitors

Many records criticize soft prompt-only defenses and propose deterministic or architectural controls. Cedar-policy hooks for coding agents intercept shell commands, file reads, and other actions, enforcing policies independent of model compliance [record_id:2345]. Capability-based authorization proposes ephemeral cryptographic warrants that attenuate on delegation, are task-scoped and holder-bound, and prevent compromised agents from escalating or exfiltrating beyond bounds [record_id:2370]. Stripe’s containment architecture focuses on controlled egress and confirmation flows [record_id:2358]. Google Workspace discusses defense-in-depth for Gemini agents using prompt-injection defenses, sandboxing/chaining policies, and sanitization [record_id:2361]. NCC Group emphasizes architectural patterns, input validation strategies, and trust-flow threat modeling [record_id:58].

Several records emphasize observability and detection. Elastic’s endpoint-observability talk asks whether defenders can see what AI tools did on behalf of users, focusing on telemetry for AI-spawned processes and OpenTelemetry-style schemas for GenAI activity [record_id:2341]. Salesforce’s Agentforce defense talk describes processing millions of daily prompts across 12,000 autonomous agents and using behavioral baselines such as Asset Rarity and Query Complexity to reduce activity to fewer than 30 high-fidelity daily alerts [record_id:2377]. Palo Alto’s SYARA proposes YARA-like semantic rules combining strings, embeddings, classifiers, and LLMs for direct/indirect prompt injection, phishing, malicious intent, and disinformation detection, claiming 98% detection under 100ms and $0.001/query [record_id:2342]. Microsoft’s BinaryShield addresses privacy constraints by enabling cross-service prompt-injection threat intelligence without sharing raw prompts [record_id:2327].

Production web-agent defense is also represented. Perplexity’s BrowseSafe record describes training a prompt-injection detector for browser agents, using a benchmark with attacks embedded in realistic high-entropy HTML pages, a fine-tuned Qwen-30B mixture-of-experts model, F1 around 0.91, sub-100ms latency, and observations that linguistic camouflage, multilingual instructions, and visible placement are challenging [record_id:2363]. This is one of the more concrete production-deployment records in the corpus.

### 4. AI red teaming is becoming formalized, automated, and tool-supported

The records include many frameworks and workflows for testing AI systems. PyRIT is described as an open-source, model- and platform-agnostic framework for probing harms, risks, and jailbreaks in multimodal generative AI systems [record_id:135], and a later CAMLIS RED record indicates continued work on accelerating AI red-team operations with PyRIT [record_id:128]. Prompt Hardener automates evaluation, hardening, and adversarial testing of system prompts using techniques such as spotlighting, random sequence enclosure, instruction defense, and role consistency, with payload categories tied to OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications 2025 [record_id:2506]. Intuit’s prompt-patching talk introduces security steerability as a benchmark for system-prompt adherence under conflicting instructions and compares GPT-4.1 variants and Gemini Flash [record_id:2199]. Michael Bargury’s chatbot.ai provides a sandbox for running a jailbreak prompt across multiple models, configurations, system prompts, and temperatures to study transferability [record_id:2212].

Offensive and defensive AI red teaming also appears as workshops and operational exercises. BSidesLV’s LLM Mayhem workshop is a hands-on introduction to red teaming GenAI systems [record_id:2478]. “Don’t be LLaMe” covers LLM internals and red-team attack strategies against applications and agents [record_id:2431]. Block’s Operation Pale Fire describes red-teaming its open-source AI agent goose [record_id:2362]. Health AI red teaming proposes a patient-authored rights framework and asks participants to stress-test AI systems for failure, bias, and exploitability [record_id:2041]. AI governance tabletops simulate real-world AI incidents under NIST AI RMF and EU AI Act lenses [record_id:2387]. These records show red teaming expanding beyond jailbreak prompts into workflows, governance, rights, auditability, and incident response.

### 5. Model-level attacks and defenses remain active, but are only part of the story

Several records focus on LLM internals, model backdoors, jailbreak mechanics, and adversarial ML. The universal-trigger Black Hat record describes a gradient-based prompt-injection technique that discovers context-independent triggers forcing open-source LLMs to emit attacker-chosen text regardless of original task, with potential RCE impact in LLM applications [record_id:18]. HarmNet constructs and refines adaptive multi-turn jailbreak queries [record_id:112]. Textual obfuscation research tests 100+ encoding and encryption methods across nine models and more than 17,000 malicious prompts, highlighting gaps in safety systems [record_id:2375]. A protocol-confusion attack exploits state differences between a chatbot with history and a guardrail seeing only the last message, using ROT13 to smuggle malicious instructions [record_id:2243]. Multi-model prompt-leakage work chains tokenization-boundary manipulation, reflection attacks, and context-window shifting to exfiltrate safety guidelines from major LLMs [record_id:2237].

Defenses include LLM salting, which rotates the internal refusal direction to invalidate previously effective jailbreaks such as GCG without degrading utility [record_id:113], residual-stream activation analysis for attack prompt classification [record_id:148], targeted neuron noising for Trojans [record_id:150], white-box analysis of refusal-signal degradation in backdoored models [record_id:2382], and mechanistic-interpretability-based “Glass-Box Security” using latent-space monitoring for malicious intent and exfiltration [record_id:2346]. ShadowLogic introduces graph-level backdoors in white-box LLMs activated by secret trigger phrases [record_id:114]. Model Leeching shows extraction of task-specific capability from ChatGPT-3.5-Turbo into a smaller model for around $50, and reports attack-transfer benefits [record_id:167]. Mathematical vulnerabilities show semantically equivalent expressions producing divergent or incorrect results across models [record_id:2230]. These records indicate that model internals matter, but most high-impact application risks arise when model behavior is connected to data, tools, and authority.

### 6. AI is changing offensive economics: vulnerability discovery, phishing, malware, C2, and fraud

Autonomous vulnerability discovery is one of the most developed offensive themes. Black Hat’s “AI Agents for Offsec with Zero False Positives” argues that exploit validators let agents make arbitrarily many attempts while requiring humans to review only validated exploits; it reports testing thousands of Docker Hub web apps, identifying over 200 zero-days and obtaining multiple CVEs [record_id:79]. XBOW’s DEF CON record similarly claims over 200 flaws and more than 1000 bug-bounty reports [record_id:2084]. FENRIR reports 100+ vulnerabilities across AI infrastructure since mid-2025, 21 patched CVEs, and a multi-stage pipeline with static pre-triage, LLM validation, and human routing [record_id:2329]. OpenSSL-focused work claims an AI system found 12 vulnerabilities fixed in a January 2026 update, with additional findings across curl, Linux, and wolfSSL [record_id:2350]. Nicholas Carlini’s “Black-hat LLMs” frames this as an economics shift: models can automate attacks previously requiring human adversaries, including finding zero-days in long-tested software [record_id:2334]. DARPA’s AIxCC records show institutional interest in AI systems that automatically find and fix critical-software vulnerabilities [record_id:1923] [record_id:2154] [record_id:2157] [record_id:2164].

AI-assisted malware and C2 are another major theme. Black Hat’s Apple AI talk introduces MLArc, a CoreML-based C2 framework using CoreML, Vision, and AVFoundation for payload embedding, encrypted shellcode, steganographic image/audio payload retrieval, and AI-assisted C2 on macOS [record_id:4]. Specialist-model training for evasive malware uses reinforcement learning fine-tuning on AV/EDR evasion tasks and plans to release a 7B model outperforming generalist models [record_id:62]. BSidesLV’s agentic AI malware talk describes an autonomous PowerShell agent using reasoning models to generate and execute in-memory code with metamorphic obfuscation, while noting current practical advantages may still be limited [record_id:2391]. LLM tuning for stealthy C2 transforms a fine-tuned model into a covert communication bridge for exfiltration, payload deployment, and command execution [record_id:2573]. Sysdig’s “VibeHacking” talk describes two AI-assisted campaigns: an eight-minute AWS escalation and EtherRAT using Ethereum smart contracts for C2, emphasizing speed and scale rather than novel primitives [record_id:2355].

AI-enabled social engineering appears in phishing, vishing, deepfakes, and cognitive security. Records describe AI making phishing more convincing and scalable while also helping defenders with detection and filtering [record_id:1878], fully automated phishing agents that gather OSINT, profile targets, craft personalized spear phish, and purchase/configure domains [record_id:2192], autonomous phishing infrastructure development including domain registration, DNS configuration, landing pages, and credential harvesting [record_id:2394], and AI voice cloning for targeted vishing [record_id:2119]. Aviation voice cloning highlights risks where low-fidelity unauthenticated AM radio intersects with consumer-grade voice cloning [record_id:2047]. Broader cognitive-security records argue that AI lowers the cost of deception, supports human digital twins, and shifts attack surfaces from networks to minds [record_id:93] [record_id:2466].

### 7. AI-assisted software development is both a defensive opportunity and a new attack surface

The corpus repeatedly warns that AI coding tools can generate insecure code, expose credentials, execute unsafe actions, or be manipulated by workspace content. Kudelski’s Black Hat talk argues AI-powered developer tools erode boundaries, include excess functionality, run with elevated permissions, and reintroduce old vulnerabilities [record_id:46]. Veracode’s research finds LLM syntax quality improved while secure-code pass rates remained flat; Java had a 72% failure rate and Python 38%, and models could fix vulnerabilities when asked but did not write secure code by default [record_id:2211]. AI-assisted IDE exploitation catalogs zero-click, one-click, autorun, and time-delayed execution patterns across tools including OpenAI Codex, Amazon Kiro, Google Antigravity, Cursor, and others [record_id:2335]. Vibe-coding records warn about data leakage, model manipulation, social engineering, executives’ unrealistic expectations, and AI-generated code repeatedly failing professional security scrutiny [record_id:2568] [record_id:2201].

At the same time, AI coding and analysis tools are portrayed as useful for defense. Google discusses evaluating emerging cyberattack capabilities and CodeMender for safer software [record_id:2321]. LLM-guided static analysis aims to learn semantic knowledge from code slices and reports over 70 previously unknown bugs in real-world projects including VirtualBox and medical-device systems [record_id:72]. Reverse-engineering workflows connect LLMs to IDA Pro, Binary Ninja, Ghidra, and Cursor/MCP to generate clues, rename functions, extract indicators, and produce detection rules [record_id:47] [record_id:2228] [record_id:2236]. These records imply AI-assisted development must be treated as a privileged automation environment requiring controls, provenance, logging, and secure SDLC integration.

### 8. Privacy, governance, and data leakage are inseparable from AI security

Privacy records emphasize that agentic systems move data through tools, APIs, memories, and modalities, creating new sinks and leak paths. Mireshghallah’s CAMLIS keynote describes persistent memories, automated workflows, deep inference, behavioral manipulation, context stealing, and persuasion attacks, while suggesting data minimization, friction, computational offloading, TEEs, and distributed training [record_id:111]. Apple Intelligence privacy talks scrutinize local versus server-side processing and unexpected data flows or leaks [record_id:5] [record_id:1957]. Enterprise AI search threat modeling warns that tools such as Glean and Guru aggregate sensitive corporate information into a single search interface [record_id:2270]. Browser-extension discovery shows Gemini used to identify 250+ AI-using extensions and analyze privacy policies in an enterprise environment [record_id:2220]. AI notetaker records warn that meeting summaries become official records and introduce steering, consent, discovery, and governance risk [record_id:2179] [record_id:2330].

Governance appears both as formal risk-management work and as a response to fast-moving technical risk. Records discuss AI governance committees [record_id:2337], enterprise governance at Snowflake [record_id:2338], NIST AI RMF and EU AI Act tabletop exercises [record_id:2387], national cybersecurity strategy lessons for AI infrastructure [record_id:2531], and capability-based frameworks to choose relevant controls rather than chase vendor hype [record_id:2347]. Confidential AI proposes TEEs and remote attestation to protect prompts, context, and inference data in public or hybrid clouds [record_id:2343]. Physical AI and military AI records argue that when AI controls robots, medical devices, or military systems, failures can become kinetic hazards requiring safety-first governance and systems-theoretic analysis [record_id:2348] [record_id:2076] [record_id:2458].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records describe a wide range of practical methods rather than a single standard approach.

For prompt-injection defense, spotlighting transforms input to continuously signal provenance and reduce indirect prompt-injection success [record_id:144]. Prompt patching strengthens system prompts against adversarial inputs and measures “security steerability” [record_id:2199]. Prompt Hardener automates system-prompt evaluation using spotlighting, random sequence enclosure, instruction defense, role consistency, and categorized payload testing [record_id:2506]. BrowseSafe trains a production detector for web-browsing agents using realistic HTML-embedded attacks and production feedback [record_id:2363]. SYARA uses layered semantic rules combining string matching, embeddings, classifiers, and LLMs [record_id:2342]. BinaryShield proposes privacy-preserving fingerprints for cross-service GenAI threat intelligence [record_id:2327].

For red teaming and testing, PyRIT provides a composable framework for model- and platform-agnostic GenAI risk identification [record_id:135]. HarmNet constructs adaptive multi-turn jailbreaks [record_id:112]. Text2VLM adapts text-only datasets into multimodal formats for typographic prompt-injection evaluation in VLMs [record_id:115]. Chatbot.ai runs prompts across many models and configurations to test jailbreak transfer [record_id:2212]. BlackIce is present as a containerized AI security testing toolkit, but the raw text is limited to names, so its capabilities require follow-up [record_id:129]. The CAMLIS records also indicate work on attack-surface taxonomies for computer-use agents [record_id:127], adversarial simulation and blue teaming [record_id:130], scam-call agents [record_id:131], package hallucination vulnerabilities [record_id:132], and red-teaming AI red teaming [record_id:133], but raw details are sparse.

For authorization and containment, records propose capability tokens or warrants [record_id:2370], Cedar-policy reference monitors for coding agents [record_id:2345], controlled egress and human confirmation flows [record_id:2358], capability bounding and memory isolation [record_id:2359], sandboxing/chaining policies [record_id:2361], and architectural trust-flow modeling [record_id:58]. These approaches share a core idea: assume the model may be steered and enforce limits outside the model.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

Several records stand out as representative or especially strong evidence for the topic.

The Gemini Calendar-invite attack is one of the clearest examples of agent hijacking through ordinary workflow artifacts. It claims 15 exploitations against Gemini for Workspace assistants and describes concrete effects: spam/phishing, calendar deletion, exfiltration, geolocation, Zoom streaming, home-appliance control, and worm behavior [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The later Google Workspace defense talk appears to address the same class of attack from the vendor-defense side, making the pair especially useful for downstream research into attack/mitigation evolution [record_id:2361].

NCC Group’s “When Guardrails Aren’t Enough” and Stripe’s “Breaking the Lethal Trifecta” are central defensive framing records. Both argue that prompt injection cannot be fully eliminated by guardrails and that agentic AI security requires architecture, containment, trust-flow modeling, egress control, and tool-boundary enforcement [record_id:58] [record_id:2358]. They align with capability-based authorization [record_id:2370] and Cedar reference-monitor approaches [record_id:2345], forming a coherent control philosophy.

The MCP-related records are important because MCP appears as a rapidly adopted tool integration layer. Malicious MCP metadata attacks against Claude Desktop show how tool descriptions or metadata can manipulate reasoning and hide malicious actions [record_id:2207]. BSidesLV’s MCP record generalizes the risk into tool poisoning, shared memory exploits, version drift, and line-jumping [record_id:2554]. MCP also appears in productive reverse-engineering workflows [record_id:2228] [record_id:2236], meaning defenders cannot simply avoid the ecosystem; they need secure integration patterns.

The autonomous vulnerability-discovery records show one of the strongest capability shifts. Black Hat reports 200+ zero-days using exploit validators [record_id:79], FENRIR reports 100+ vulnerabilities and 21 patched CVEs [record_id:2329], and the OpenSSL record claims 12 AI-found zero-days in a heavily audited project [record_id:2350]. AIxCC provides a public competition context for cyber reasoning systems [record_id:1923] [record_id:2154] [record_id:2157] [record_id:2164]. Together, these records suggest AI-assisted vulnerability discovery is becoming operational, but they also raise questions about reproducibility, validation, disclosure quality, and defensive deployment.

The model-level defense records are useful for researchers focused on LLM internals. Spotlighting has concrete reported metrics [record_id:144]. Residual-stream activation analysis, Trojan noising, LLM salting, white-box refusal-signal analysis, and mechanistic interpretability all show attempts to detect or neutralize attacks from inside the model rather than only at the application layer [record_id:148] [record_id:150] [record_id:113] [record_id:2382] [record_id:2346]. However, these often require white-box access or self-hosted infrastructure, limiting applicability to closed frontier APIs.

The AI-enabled social-engineering records are notable because they connect technical automation to human vulnerability. Fully automated phishing agents and phishing-infrastructure automation explicitly claim that AI reduces cost and human labor across the full phishing lifecycle [record_id:2192] [record_id:2394]. Voice-cloning records address vishing and aviation communications [record_id:2119] [record_id:2047]. Cognitive-security talks argue for a broader shift toward manipulation of minds, not just systems [record_id:93] [record_id:2466]. Deepfake detection provides a defensive counterpoint [record_id:1915].

The privacy/governance records are important for enterprise adoption questions. Apple Intelligence privacy talks scrutinize data flows and local/cloud processing [record_id:5] [record_id:1957]. Agentic privacy “data sinks and leaks” provides a conceptual map of persistent memory, automated workflows, surveillance, profiling, and context stealing [record_id:111]. Enterprise governance records at Meta and Snowflake illustrate attempts to balance innovation and risk [record_id:2337] [record_id:2338].

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The most important limitation is that most records are abstracts or short summaries, not full papers. Claims such as “zero false positives,” “200 zero-days,” “12 OpenSSL zero-days,” “73% high-critical risks,” “98% detection,” and “0.91 F1” are valuable leads but require validation through full talks, papers, code, datasets, CVE records, or independent replication [record_id:79] [record_id:53] [record_id:1965] [record_id:2342] [record_id:2363] [record_id:2350].

Several records are duplicated across venues or appear to be the same talk with minor wording differences. AppleStorm appears in Black Hat and DEF CON records [record_id:5] [record_id:1957]. Gemini Calendar-invite promptware appears in Black Hat and DEF CON records [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The abandoned-S3-bucket supply-chain scenario appears twice in DEF CON records [record_id:1972] [record_id:2134]. AIxCC appears through a main talk and short video-team/interview records [record_id:1923] [record_id:2154] [record_id:2157] [record_id:2164]. Downstream agents should avoid overcounting duplicated evidence.

Some records have minimal or empty raw text, making them weak evidence beyond their titles and metadata. This applies to CAMLIS entries on attack surfaces in computer-use agents [record_id:127], PyRIT operations [record_id:128], ScamAgents [record_id:131], package hallucination vulnerabilities [record_id:132], red-teaming AI red teaming [record_id:133], AI security preparedness [record_id:152], and security issues in generative AI [record_id:161]. BlackIce and adversarial simulation records include only names/authors in raw text, so their technical details are not available in the provided material [record_id:129] [record_id:130].

The corpus is stronger on attack descriptions than on standardized defensive evaluation. Many proposed defenses—prompt patching, salting, semantic rules, reference monitors, capability warrants, Confidential AI, mechanistic interpretability, and containment—are plausible but evaluated under different assumptions and threat models [record_id:113] [record_id:2199] [record_id:2342] [record_id:2345] [record_id:2370] [record_id:2343] [record_id:2346] [record_id:2358]. Open questions include which controls are necessary for specific architectures, how they compose, how much usability they cost, and whether they withstand adaptive attackers.

The records also leave unresolved questions about responsibility and governance. Several talks argue vendors are shipping insecure defaults or shifting responsibility to users [record_id:2369], while governance talks emphasize balancing innovation with risk [record_id:2337] [record_id:2338] [record_id:2347]. There is no consensus in the records on liability, minimum security baselines for agentic systems, audit requirements, or how to regulate AI-enabled phishing/malware tools without blocking legitimate security research [record_id:2394] [record_id:2531].

Finally, physical and high-consequence AI risks are present but less technically detailed than software-agent risks. Records discuss medical holographic agents, Health AI rights, aviation voice cloning, military AI hazard analysis, and physical AI governance [record_id:2076] [record_id:2041] [record_id:2047] [record_id:2458] [record_id:2348], but the corpus has fewer concrete exploit chains or mitigations for embodied AI than for browser, workspace, developer-tool, and enterprise agents.
