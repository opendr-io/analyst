# Topic: Network security and NDR

## Executive Summary

The records depict network security and network detection and response (NDR) as a broad, rapidly expanding research area that spans classic packet/flow analysis, DNS intelligence, tunneling abuse, perimeter-device compromise, wireless/RF exploitation, OT/IoT convergence, cloud and SaaS-based command-and-control, and machine-learning-assisted detection. The strongest recurring message is that defenders can no longer treat “network security” as only firewall rules, IDS signatures, and packet captures. Attackers increasingly exploit trusted network infrastructure, commodity protocols, encrypted or whitelisted services, and edge devices that sit at high-trust choke points.

Several 2025 records focus on tunnel and routing abuse. GRE, VxLAN, IPIP, 6in4, and 4in6 appear repeatedly as mechanisms for initial access, spoofing, evasion, and DDoS amplification, with one study claiming roughly 4.3 million vulnerable tunneling hosts and two new amplification techniques [record_id:9]. A related red-team talk describes abusing stateless tunnels such as GRE and VxLAN for intranet access, source-IP-filtering bypasses, and possible BGP/OSPF manipulation after tunnel hijack [record_id:1] [record_id:1907]. “Tunnelpocalypse” frames spoofed DDoS amplification from arbitrary Internet IPs as a major risk [record_id:2021].

Another major trend is the abuse of trusted services and expected traffic patterns for covert operations. Web conferencing media servers and TURN infrastructure are proposed as high-bandwidth C2 relays that blend into normal enterprise collaboration traffic [record_id:33] [record_id:1913]. Google Calendar and other Google services are discussed as C2 and SOCKS tunneling substrates [record_id:2097]. Distributed storage and replication systems such as IPFS, Codex, and Cloudflare R2 are framed as resilient payload, C2, and phishing distribution channels [record_id:1896]. SMTP/IMAP through Outlook is presented as a C2 path that can bypass web-isolation environments focused on HTTP/HTTPS [record_id:2536]. These records collectively suggest that NDR must reason about application behavior, protocol semantics, and service context rather than simply identifying suspicious destinations.

DNS is a particularly strong theme. Records cover DNS exfiltration prevention using eBPF and kernel-level enforcement [record_id:35], DNS-based device fingerprinting and user tracking from 1.5 billion DNS requests [record_id:81], domain intelligence before domains become public IOCs [record_id:1877], passive-DNS tracking of Iranian-backed infrastructure [record_id:2262], DNS artifacts for phishing detection before weaponization [record_id:2278], and DNS exfiltration training data from adversary simulations [record_id:237]. DNS is treated both as a detection goldmine and as a privacy/exfiltration risk.

The records also show continued pressure on perimeter and edge devices. Firewalls are described as targets of a more-than-five-year China-based campaign involving custom exploits and bespoke malware [record_id:56] [record_id:1998]. Cloud VPN/ZTNA/SASE products are said to inherit legacy VPN weaknesses while introducing new cloud attack surfaces [record_id:2062] [record_id:2136]. GlobalProtect, Draytek routers, F5 load balancers, ISP modems, and ISP production networks all appear as examples of high-value network infrastructure with exploitable design or implementation weaknesses [record_id:54] [record_id:2120] [record_id:2522] [record_id:1906] [record_id:2269].

A parallel body of CAMLIS and BSides material emphasizes ML, reinforcement learning, feature engineering, and analyst workflow for NDR. These records discuss campaign detection from Zeek/Bro logs [record_id:193], network-agnostic alert triage [record_id:196], dynamic graph summarization of incident reports [record_id:197], NetFlow-based botnet C2 detection [record_id:179], standardized flow feature extraction with ensemble learning [record_id:2390], autonomous network defense agents [record_id:116] [record_id:185], and predictive or anticipatory defense [record_id:232]. The evidence base here is method-heavy and often includes experimental results, though many claims are abstract-level rather than full-paper-level in the provided text.

Finally, network security is shown extending far beyond enterprise Ethernet and cloud networks. Records cover 5G user-plane trust assumptions [record_id:51], Open RAN vulnerabilities [record_id:57], Stingray/cell-site-simulator detection via cheap hotspots and Qualcomm DIAG telemetry [record_id:1889] [record_id:1922], Wi-Fi Easy Connect/DPP issues [record_id:1926], wardriving/warflying [record_id:1887] [record_id:1893], Meshtastic and LoRa mesh protocols [record_id:1884] [record_id:1895] [record_id:2279], maritime NMEA2000 fingerprinting and VSAT vulnerabilities [record_id:2049] [record_id:2039], satellite DDoS [record_id:2013], space IDS indicators of behavior [record_id:2105], aviation AM-radio trust risks [record_id:2047], and critical public alerting radio protocols [record_id:1894]. The boundary between NDR, OT security, RF security, and critical infrastructure security is therefore porous throughout the corpus.

## Research Landscape

The corpus is dominated by conference-talk abstracts from Black Hat USA 2025, DEF CON 33, BSidesSF 2025, BSidesLV 2025, and CAMLIS across 2017–2026. Black Hat records tend to emphasize polished vulnerability research, protocol exploitation, enterprise perimeter attacks, and large-scale measurement. DEF CON records skew toward hands-on offensive research, RF/wireless exploration, hacker-village talks, and applied tooling. CAMLIS records provide the most sustained line of machine-learning and data-science work for intrusion detection, alert triage, campaign detection, and autonomous defense. BSides records often provide practitioner-focused detection engineering, infrastructure hardening, workshops, and shorter research presentations.

The research area represented here has several overlapping centers of gravity:

- **Protocol and infrastructure exploitation.** This includes tunneling protocols [record_id:1] [record_id:9] [record_id:1907] [record_id:2021], Windows HTTP services [record_id:11], HTTP/2 server push and Signed HTTP Exchange [record_id:21], SSH implementations and exposure [record_id:2100] [record_id:2260], OPC UA [record_id:7] [record_id:2140], TETRA [record_id:60], Wi-Fi DPP [record_id:1926], EV charging HomePlug communications [record_id:1931] [record_id:2145], and Common Industrial Protocol [record_id:2116].

- **Network telemetry and detection.** Records discuss NetFlow, Zeek/Bro logs, incident reports, IDS alerts, DNS logs, passive DNS, HTTP/HTTPS transaction telemetry, TLS/HTTP fingerprints, honeypot data, and traffic/session metadata [record_id:23] [record_id:37] [record_id:81] [record_id:98] [record_id:179] [record_id:193] [record_id:197] [record_id:205] [record_id:208] [record_id:224] [record_id:2365] [record_id:2390].

- **Perimeter, edge, and access-control products.** Firewalls, VPNs, ZTNA/SASE clients, GlobalProtect, F5, Draytek, ISP modems, routers, and egress-control platforms recur as both defense mechanisms and attack surfaces [record_id:54] [record_id:56] [record_id:1998] [record_id:2062] [record_id:2136] [record_id:2120] [record_id:2522] [record_id:2261].

- **Wireless, RF, cellular, and nontraditional networks.** DEF CON RF Village material contributes a large cluster of records on Meshtastic, Flipper Zero, SDR CTFs, wardriving/warflying, 802.11ah repeaters, in-flight Wi-Fi evil twins, public alerting protocols, goTenna, TPMS, and cellular testbeds [record_id:1884] [record_id:1885] [record_id:1887] [record_id:1888] [record_id:1889] [record_id:1890] [record_id:1892] [record_id:1893] [record_id:1894] [record_id:1952] [record_id:2048] [record_id:2149] [record_id:2167] [record_id:2540] [record_id:2578].

- **OT, IoT, maritime, space, and critical infrastructure networks.** Many records are secondary to OT/IoT but still centrally involve network communications, gateways, telemetry, or NDR-like detection. Examples include cellular IoT backend access [record_id:44], O-RAN [record_id:57], TETRA/SCADA packet injection [record_id:60], surveillance cameras as internal-network gateways [record_id:2088] [record_id:2146], smart buses [record_id:2121], OT DMZ breach paths [record_id:2112], OT device discovery behind protocol gateways [record_id:2104] [record_id:2303], naval MOSAICS monitoring [record_id:2075], maritime comms and NMEA2000 [record_id:2045] [record_id:2049], ship-to-shore cranes with surprise cellular modems [record_id:2065], and virtual ship environments for cybersecurity research [record_id:2108].

- **Strategic and geopolitical network resilience.** Some records address network security as national infrastructure or censorship/circumvention: Russia’s TSPU firewall and certificate-authority implications [record_id:1970], Tor operational history [record_id:1975], Taiwan undersea digital blockade concerns [record_id:2011], maritime geopolitical threats [record_id:1946], and global shipping infrastructure exposure [record_id:2022] [record_id:2065].

Overall, the corpus portrays NDR as shifting from “detect packets that match known bad” toward “understand behavior across protocols, infrastructure, identity, topology, and time.” The records repeatedly criticize static blocklists, signature-only systems, or narrow traffic inspection as insufficient against changing infrastructure, encrypted traffic, trusted-service abuse, low-volume attacks, and topology-aware adversaries [record_id:23] [record_id:35] [record_id:37] [record_id:1948] [record_id:205] [record_id:2390].

## Major Themes And Trends

### 1. Tunnels, encapsulation, and routing are becoming first-class attack surfaces

The most explicit network-layer trend is abuse of tunnels and encapsulation protocols. One Black Hat/DEF CON talk describes gaining intranet access without phishing, public-app exploitation, or valid accounts by leveraging stateless tunnels such as GRE and VxLAN [record_id:1] [record_id:1907]. The same material warns about environments lacking source IP filtering, allowing red teams to evade incident-response interception and perform internal password spraying, and about VxLAN behavior in Linux Kernel and RouterOS that could allow tunnel hijacking and internal access even when VxLAN is configured on a private interface through an encrypted tunnel [record_id:1] [record_id:1907]. The talk also notes that if the tunnel carries routing protocols such as BGP or OSPF, manipulation could hijack internal IPs and potentially lead to domain compromise [record_id:1] [record_id:1907].

A second tunnel-focused record reports Internet-wide scanning of IPv4 and a subset of IPv6, finding approximately 4.3 million hosts using unauthenticated tunneling protocols such as IPIP, GRE, 6in4, and 4in6 that can be abused as one-way proxies for spoofing and DoS [record_id:9]. It introduces “Ping-Pong” amplification by looping encapsulated traffic between vulnerable hosts, and “Tunneled Temporal Lensing,” which accumulates packets and releases them as a burst [record_id:9]. “Tunnelpocalypse” presents the same broad risk in a more compressed form, warning that spoofed DDoS amplification may be launched from any IP on the Internet [record_id:2021].

These records suggest several defensive research questions: how to inventory tunnel endpoints, how to validate source filtering, how to detect unexpected encapsulation in enterprise and ISP networks, and how to safely monitor routing protocol behavior inside overlays. The evidence is comparatively strong because it includes measurements, named protocols, specific implementation contexts, and described attack techniques [record_id:9], though detailed technical validation is outside the provided abstracts.

### 2. Trusted, whitelisted, or high-reputation services are increasingly used as covert network infrastructure

A recurring theme is that defenders often allow, whitelist, or exempt from inspection the very services attackers can reuse. Web conferencing is a representative example. “Ghost Calls” describes using real-time collaboration protocols and globally distributed media servers as short-term, high-speed C2 relays, especially because collaboration vendors often recommend whitelisting media-server IPs and exempting them from TLS inspection [record_id:33]. The DEF CON version names TURNt, an open-source tool that routes covert traffic through trusted TURN servers and makes sessions resemble legitimate Zoom meetings [record_id:1913]. This is important because it targets a gap between low-and-slow covert channels and high-bandwidth interactive needs such as SOCKS proxying, pivoting, relaying, and hidden VNC [record_id:33] [record_id:1913].

Trusted cloud and SaaS abuse appears elsewhere. Google Calendar and other Google services are discussed as RAT/C2 and SOCKS tunneling substrates, with a similar Google Calendar technique reportedly observed in an APT41 campaign [record_id:2097]. “Planting C4” generalizes this idea into a modular external-C2 toolkit using trusted sites such as AWS, GitHub, Box, and Notion via WASM-powered plugins [record_id:2117]. Distributed data replication systems including NFTs, IPFS, Codex, and Cloudflare R2 are presented as resilient C2, payload delivery, and phishing-hosting infrastructure resistant to takedown [record_id:1896]. Outlook over SMTP/IMAP is proposed as a way to maintain C2 even when web isolation blocks HTTP/HTTPS from workstations [record_id:2536].

Supply-chain detection via network behavior is the defensive counterpart. BEAM is described as an open-source tool trained on more than 40 billion HTTP/HTTPS transactions, using 65 behavioral signals and application-specific baselines to detect trusted-app compromise with reported accuracy above 95% [record_id:23]. This record is particularly representative of a shift from provenance-only supply-chain controls toward behavioral network monitoring of applications already trusted by the organization [record_id:23].

### 3. DNS remains both a detection backbone and an attack/privacy channel

DNS is one of the best-covered subthemes. On offense and risk, DNS tunneling and DNS C2 are described as hard to prevent because DNS must remain open on firewalls; record 35 argues that timing/volume anomaly detection, signatures, and proxy-based DPI are increasingly ineffective against evasive C2, DGAs, and dynamic infrastructure [record_id:35]. It proposes moving enforcement into the Linux kernel with eBPF, parsing DNS in-kernel, using userspace deep learning trained on obfuscation techniques, dynamically blacklisting domains, and killing malicious implant processes before packets leave the endpoint [record_id:35].

DNS also creates privacy risk. One study analyzes over 1.5 billion DNS requests from 30,000 iOS and Android devices over 30 days, claiming that simple statistics—frequency analysis, correlation metrics, and anomaly detection—can fingerprint devices within minutes and identify them with high accuracy within hours [record_id:81]. It emphasizes that even 20% of DNS traffic can enable precise tracking, and that iOS devices produce substantially more repetitive DNS noise than Android devices [record_id:81].

On detection and threat intelligence, Domain Intelligence Analysis uses DNS artifacts to improve detection before domains become public IOCs [record_id:1877]. Passive DNS is used to connect a typosquatting domain to malicious name servers linked to Iran-nexus activity and malware distribution [record_id:2262]. A BSidesSF record similarly argues that by the time a phishing domain is public IoC, it is already late, and proposes mapping DNS artifacts and adversary infrastructure as they are created [record_id:2278]. Earlier CAMLIS work discusses DNS exfiltration labeled from adversary simulations [record_id:237], and feature engineering work reports improving malicious DNS request classification from roughly 65% to over 90% using security-specific encoders for IPs, URLs, domain names, usernames, and geographic features [record_id:224].

The collective DNS evidence is strong in breadth but uneven in depth: some records provide large-scale measurements or quantitative claims [record_id:81] [record_id:224], while others are short abstracts about workflows or case studies [record_id:1877] [record_id:2262] [record_id:2278].

### 4. Perimeter devices are increasingly treated as contested infrastructure, not passive controls

The records repeatedly show that firewalls, VPNs, routers, modems, load balancers, and ZTNA clients are attractive targets because they sit at trust boundaries and often have privileged network visibility or control. A major firewall campaign record describes more than five years of attacks by a China-based adversary against firewall vendors, including custom exploits and bespoke malware deployed inside firewall operating systems [record_id:56]. The DEF CON version emphasizes that multiple large network-security providers have been targeted with similar tactics and tools, calling for collective industry response [record_id:1998].

Remote access products are similarly scrutinized. GlobalProtect research reports vulnerabilities that bypass VPN or escalate privileges on macOS and Linux endpoints, and frames the highly privileged VPN agent itself as a possible source of vulnerability [record_id:54]. ZTNA/SASE records argue that next-generation cloud VPNs from providers such as ZScaler, Netskope, and Check Point can inherit legacy VPN weaknesses while adding cloud attack surfaces; the described attacks include external reconnaissance, authentication and posture-check bypass, hardware-ID spoofing, insecure IPC exploitation for local privilege escalation, traffic-steering bypass, and malicious ZTNA servers executing code on clients [record_id:2062] [record_id:2136]. BSidesSF also includes a practitioner record on centralizing egress access controls across a hybrid environment at Block, emphasizing consistent outbound policy enforcement across diverse endpoints [record_id:2261].

Other edge-device records include Draytek router unauthenticated RCEs and persistence chains [record_id:2120], F5 service password encryption reversal after device access [record_id:2522], ISP-supplied modem flaws affecting ADSL, fiber, cable, and 5G devices [record_id:1906], and ISP audit findings where simple vulnerabilities and configuration mistakes persist in production networks [record_id:2269]. “Breaking into thousands of cloud-based VPNs with one bug” and related access-product records reinforce that moving from traditional VPN to cloud access does not remove the need for rigorous protocol, client, and control-plane analysis [record_id:2062] [record_id:2136].

### 5. NDR is moving toward behavioral models, graph workflows, and ML-assisted analyst augmentation

The CAMLIS cluster provides a long-running view of ML and data-science methods for network defense. Early records discuss hierarchical analysis over terabytes of enterprise logs, modeling single records, time series, multi-entity patterns, and multiple data sources using boosting and stacking rather than only convolutional deep learning [record_id:257]. Behavioral clustering of enterprise assets is proposed to identify subpopulations and avoid Simpson’s paradox in anomaly detection [record_id:261]. User-based network anomaly detection with self-organizing maps is present but has no raw abstract text, so it should be treated as weak evidence beyond title and metadata [record_id:260].

Several records focus on alert and campaign reconstruction. Automatic campaign detection uses Zeek/Bro logs, incident reports, and other heterogeneous logs by vectorizing log formats and comparing pairs of logs to determine whether they belong to the same attack [record_id:193]. HeAT generates attack-campaign context around a critical IoC by using network-agnostic features and analyst-provided “Alert Episode Heat” [record_id:196]. CLEAR-ROAD mines temporally co-occurring rare critical alerts in near real time without training data, using temporal arrival patterns and constrained databasing to reduce sequences by an average of 90% for individual streams [record_id:208]. Incident-report summarization uses dynamic graphs from Zeek-like connection logs and other relationships, reducing report size up to 61% and improving average precision by 22% in evaluation [record_id:197]. Automated incident prioritization uses multi-modal features, sentence-transformer embeddings, self-attention for explainability, and a neural regressor to score severity across reports from multiple tools [record_id:199].

Other records focus on network threat classification. NetFlow distributional features are used to detect botnet C2 hosts, with validation against denylists and deep packet inspection [record_id:179]. Internet-scale malicious infrastructure prediction uses deep learning and pretraining to expand blocklist/allowlist coverage and improve AUC on two datasets [record_id:205]. A BSidesLV talk proposes standardized metadata and flow-level feature extraction plus dynamic ensemble learning, reporting 97.92% detection accuracy across traffic types [record_id:2390]. Web content filtering uses LLM-generated labels and knowledge distillation to produce a smaller URL categorization model with a 9% accuracy improvement and 175 times fewer parameters than the teacher model, enabling inline scanning at scale [record_id:166].

Autonomous and anticipatory defense is another thread. One record proposes TERLA, topological extensions for reinforcement-learning cyber-defense agents that generalize across network topologies and sizes without retraining [record_id:116]. Another describes deep RL agents organized in a shallow hierarchy, with state diagrams to understand adversarial behavior and feature ablation for explanation [record_id:185]. Exfiltration path analysis uses reinforcement learning with protocol and payload considerations in the Markov decision process [record_id:162]. Anticipatory defense work discusses ASSERT, CASCADES, and CAPTURE for attack modeling, simulation, and forecasting using public-domain signals [record_id:232]. GreyNoise’s Orbie AI agent operates on internet-scale honeypot data to surface emergent threats, identify campaigns, and write detection rules, emphasizing embedded domain knowledge over model choice [record_id:2365].

These records collectively support a trend toward ML as analyst augmentation and scalable triage rather than fully autonomous replacement. However, the evidence is abstract-heavy, and several claims depend on datasets not described in detail in the raw text.

### 6. Bot detection, deception, honeypots, and traffic shaping are converging

A practical defensive trend is controlling adversary interaction rather than simply blocking. Logrip uses data visualization and hierarchical IP hashing to distinguish humans from automated web entities in historical server logs, estimating that 80–95% of traffic in case-study examples came from AI crawlers [record_id:37]. Finch is described as a lightweight reverse proxy that makes allow/block/route decisions based on JA3, JA4, JA4H, and HTTP/2 fingerprints, with an AI agent that monitors events, suppresses boring bots, updates rules, and crafts stub responses for deception [record_id:1948]. The Azazel System implements a “Cyber Scapegoat” gateway on a Raspberry Pi 5, aiming to bind and delay attackers rather than merely observe them like a traditional honeypot [record_id:2397].

Honeypot and data-collection records add context. Adaptive experimental design for intrusion data collection compares observational studies, RCTs, and adaptive honeypot deployment, claiming at least 33% less deployment time, 17% fewer honeypots, and 19% more attack recordings than an analogous control-group honeypot study [record_id:159]. SSH honeypot history discusses Cowrie-based challenges and the evolution of Packet Hacking Village workshops [record_id:1986]. GreyNoise’s Orbie record uses internet-scale honeypot data for emergent-threat detection and rule generation [record_id:2365]. Together, these records treat deception infrastructure as a source of telemetry, adversary delay, training data, and automated detection engineering.

### 7. Wireless, RF, cellular, and protocol-specific networks broaden the meaning of NDR

The RF and wireless records show network security extending into radio protocols and non-IP or partially-IP environments. Meshtastic is examined from LoRa chirps through GNU Radio, Python, Protobuf envelopes, and AES-256-protected payloads [record_id:1884]. A separate record designs Meshtastic-based command-and-control for encrypted shell access and remote Linux host management over low-cost LoRa radios [record_id:1895]. BSidesSF compares APRS and Meshtastic security models, vulnerabilities, encryption, authentication, and attack surface [record_id:2279]. Ham Radio Village also has a brief Meshtastic logistical record with limited technical content [record_id:2149].

Wi-Fi appears in multiple forms: tactical use of a Flipper Zero to locate adversaries using radios, rented cars, local Wi-Fi, and Bluetooth [record_id:1885]; warflying research about how many access points can be detected from aircraft and how better equipment affects collection and visualization [record_id:1887]; a wardriving humor/community talk with limited technical evidence [record_id:1893]; 802.11ah point-to-point repeaters using free corporate Wi-Fi as Internet uplink [record_id:1890]; Evil Twin discovery in commercial airline in-flight Wi-Fi and SOC investigation using on-wing infrastructure and telemetry such as ADS-B/satellite [record_id:1892]; Wi-Fi Easy Connect/DPP security and privacy issues where usability choices expand attack surface compared with WPS [record_id:1926]; and a Wi-Fi assessment workshop covering monitor mode, PCAP collection, Wireshark filters, Aircrack-ng, Wifite, Airgeddon, Reaver, and Wacker [record_id:2578]. The Hak5 pager that “pineapples” is a short gadget-oriented record with minimal detail [record_id:2167], and a BSidesLV gadget-building talk mentions turning a small box into a Wi-Fi spy [record_id:2540].

Cellular and telecom records include 5G user-plane trust-boundary attacks [record_id:51], O-RAN attack surface and 26 memory-corruption vulnerabilities across RIC and RAN implementations resulting in 20 CVEs [record_id:57], open-source cellular testbeds for Rayhunter using OAI, srsRAN_4G, OpenBTS, and Yates GSM [record_id:1889], Rayhunter’s collection and analysis of real-time control-plane traffic via Qualcomm DIAG to detect cell-site simulators [record_id:1922], and a PSTN talk covering interconnection, STIR/SHAKEN bypass, number hijacking, telecom fraud, and privacy [record_id:1933]. These records reinforce that NDR-like monitoring sometimes means collecting control-plane telemetry, RF captures, or telecom signaling rather than conventional enterprise packets.

### 8. OT/IoT convergence turns network paths into safety and mission risks

Many records are not “NDR” in the narrow enterprise sense but are highly relevant because they show how network connectivity creates attack paths into physical systems. OPC UA is presented as an OT/IoT bridge where protocol cryptography may be trusted enough to avoid VPNs; the records describe authentication bypass attacks based on signing oracles, signature-spoofing padding oracles, and timing side channels [record_id:7] [record_id:2140]. Cellular IoT devices can allegedly be modified to control cellular modules, inject communications, and establish MCU-to-module MitM, enabling attacks against backend cloud infrastructure and private networks [record_id:44]. TETRA E2EE analysis reports seven vulnerabilities, including voice injection into E2EE channels, SDS replay, intentionally weakened 56-bit variants, and packet injection against SCADA networks [record_id:60].

EV charging communications are attacked through QCA 7000 HomePlug modems used by CCS and NACS, with persistent DoS, hijacking EV charging communication at a distance, and firmware reverse engineering for code execution [record_id:1931] [record_id:2145]. Smart bus research describes moving from free Wi-Fi into vehicular routers, private bus networks, ADAS/APTS communications, LED displays, passenger/driver data, operational data, and backend APIs [record_id:2121]. Axis surveillance platform research reports pre-auth RCE in Axis.Remoting and passive exfiltration that could map organizations using the equipment [record_id:2088] [record_id:2146]. TPMS signals are described as unencrypted/unauthenticated, enabling low-cost capture, tracking infrastructure, custom messages, and ECU spoofing [record_id:2048].

OT network discovery and segmentation recur. “There and Back Again” discusses discovering devices behind protocol gateways using CIP message forwarding and DNP3 address discovery [record_id:2104], while the BSidesSF version emphasizes IT/OT convergence and discovery behind legacy protocol gateways [record_id:2303]. OT DMZ breach techniques focus on misconfigured remote access, weak segmentation, jump hosts, proxy services, Citrix gateways, and RDP relays [record_id:2112]. MOSAICS Block 1 is presented as a Department of Defense OT-security framework for real-time monitoring, safe active asset discovery, and behavioral threat detection in naval mission systems [record_id:2075]. Common Industrial Protocol exploitation is represented by a short introductory record [record_id:2116].

Maritime and space records broaden this further. VSAT ACU vulnerabilities and firmware rehosting/testbeds target maritime connectivity [record_id:2039]. Maritime comms research proposes an SDN-SDR cross-layer framework using SATCOM for resilient and reconfigurable communications [record_id:2045]. NMEA2000 fingerprinting builds a five-node testbed, collects datasets, and creates deterministic sensor fingerprints to distinguish normal from attacked behavior [record_id:2049]. Virtual ship environments are proposed for cybersecurity education, assessment, traffic generation, test access points, analytics, and directed simulations [record_id:2108]. Space records discuss red teaming satellites and ground stations [record_id:2046] and using SPARTA offensive techniques to generate indicators of behavior for spacecraft IDS [record_id:2105]. Satellite DDoS research models Low Earth Orbit constellation vulnerabilities such as dynamic topology, limited bandwidth, and predictable orbital patterns [record_id:2013].

### 9. Network security is also a geopolitical and resilience problem

Some records frame network security as civil infrastructure, national security, or censorship resilience. Russia’s TSPU is described as an in-path censorship system located close to residential modems and data centers, enabling granular targeted attacks; the same record warns that Russia’s certificate authority and software trust requirements could threaten users interacting with Russian apps and data centers [record_id:1970]. Tor operational “war stories” cover the difficulty of maintaining a global anonymity network, teaching governments and law enforcement about privacy and end-to-end encryption, Russia blocking Azure for a day, and attempted bribery to leave bugs in Tor [record_id:1975].

Taiwan’s “digital blockade” panel discusses sabotage of undersea infrastructure and the geopolitics of cyber-resilience [record_id:2011]. Maritime geopolitical records discuss undersea cables, port cranes, drug smuggling, Navy ships, and surveillance/cyber tradecraft at sea [record_id:1946]. A maritime OSINT record passively maps DNS, email authentication, and foundational Internet services for top global maritime organizations using public data and LLM-assisted analysis [record_id:2022]. Coast Guard crane assessments mention surprise cellular modems and attack paths in ship-to-shore cranes manufactured in China [record_id:2065]. These records are less focused on packet-level methods but important for downstream agents studying network resilience, infrastructure dependency, and national-level threat models.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records describe a wide range of methods and tools, often as conference demonstrations or proposed open-source releases.

For **packet, flow, and log analysis**, the corpus includes Zeek/Bro logs for campaign detection [record_id:193], Zeek-like connection logs and dynamic graph visualization for incident summarization [record_id:197], NetFlow conventional and distributional features for botnet C2 prediction [record_id:179], standardized flow-level features for ensemble NDR models [record_id:2390], Wireshark for Meshtastic decoding and Wi-Fi analysis [record_id:1884] [record_id:2578], and PCAP collection from Wi-Fi assessment rigs [record_id:2578]. Rayhunter adapts cheap cellular hotspots to record and analyze cellular control-plane traffic, effectively bringing “Wireshark-like” visibility to the phone-to-tower link via Qualcomm DIAG [record_id:1922].

For **DNS intelligence and enforcement**, approaches include passive DNS infrastructure mapping [record_id:2262], pre-IOC domain intelligence [record_id:1877] [record_id:2278], DNS statistical fingerprinting for privacy analysis [record_id:81], DNS exfiltration labeled-data generation from adversary simulations [record_id:237], and eBPF in-kernel DNS DPI plus userspace deep learning and process termination [record_id:35]. ## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

Several records stand out because they combine broad impact, concrete methods, and useful detail.

The tunneling research is among the most consequential. “Amplify and Annihilate” provides a measurable Internet-scale claim—approximately 4.3 million vulnerable tunneling hosts—and names specific protocols, attack modes, ONT device crashes, and amplification techniques [record_id:9]. The red-team stateless-tunnel records complement this by focusing on initial access, intranet evasion, VxLAN behavior in Linux/RouterOS, and routing-protocol manipulation [record_id:1] [record_id:1907]. “Tunnelpocalypse” is much less detailed but reinforces the same attack class as a DDoS amplification issue [record_id:2021].

The firewall campaign records are representative of edge-device compromise as strategic threat activity. The Black Hat record describes a persistent China-based adversary developing custom exploits and bespoke malware for enterprise firewalls over more than five years [record_id:56]. The DEF CON record repeats and slightly condenses the claim, emphasizing that multiple vendors face the same adversary and need collective response [record_id:1998]. These records are important because they frame firewalls not merely as controls but as malware-hosting targets inside customer environments.

The “Ghost Calls” records are notable for connecting network policy assumptions to C2 tradecraft. The Black Hat version explains why interactive C2 is hard in monitored networks and why conferencing media servers are attractive relays [record_id:33]. The DEF CON version names TURNt and Zoom-like traffic patterns, making the detection challenge more concrete [record_id:1913]. This is a representative case for downstream research into detecting abuse of whitelisted infrastructure.

BEAM is a strong defensive representative because it proposes detecting supply-chain compromise purely from web traffic using application-specific behavior baselines. Its raw text includes scale—over 40 billion HTTP/HTTPS transactions—features—65 behavioral signals—and claimed detection accuracy above 95%, with up to 99% for predictable applications [record_id:23]. This record bridges NDR and software supply-chain defense.

The DNS privacy record is notable for its measurement scale and simple-method claim. It analyzes 1.5 billion DNS requests from 30,000 mobile devices, claims device fingerprinting within minutes and reliable identification within hours, and argues that simple statistics can be enough without AI [record_id:81]. It is useful for privacy-focused network-security research as well as DNS telemetry policy.

The CAMLIS incident summarization record is unusually detailed in methodology. It explains dynamic graph construction from logs, component/branch/entity features, unweighted and Bayesian hierarchical logistic regression summarization, and evaluation showing 22% average precision improvement and up to 61% report-size reduction [record_id:197]. It is one of the strongest records for analyst-facing NDR workflow design.

The O-RAN record is notable for combining architecture-specific threat modeling, automated testing, and concrete vulnerability counts: 26 previously unknown memory-corruption vulnerabilities, 20 CVEs, and impacts including silent disruption, degradation, crashes, and system-wide failures [record_id:57]. It is strong evidence for the need to test emerging disaggregated network architectures, not just traditional closed telecom systems.

Rayhunter is another highly representative record because it demonstrates low-cost cellular network telemetry for civil-society protection. One record focuses on open-source cellular testbeds for robust testing and detection development [record_id:1889], while another explains collection of real-time control-plane traffic from a cheap hotspot using Qualcomm DIAG and validation in simulated and real-world scenarios [record_id:1922].

The OT/IT convergence records around gateway discovery are important because they address a core NDR blind spot: assets hidden behind protocol gateways. Rob King’s DEF CON talk gives concrete examples—CIP message forwarding and DNP3 address discovery [record_id:2104]—while the BSidesSF version summarizes the same issue for OT device discovery behind legacy protocol gateways [record_id:2303]. These records matter for environments where passive visibility may miss downstream devices.

The maritime and space records are notable as a cluster rather than individually. NMEA2000 fingerprinting provides a concrete testbed and deterministic baseline approach [record_id:2049]. VSAT research includes CVE ranges and testbed validation [record_id:2039]. Maritime comms proposes SDN-SDR SATCOM resilience [record_id:2045]. Virtual ship environments define architecture needs for cybersecurity research [record_id:2108]. Space records connect red teaming to satellite infrastructure [record_id:2046] and SPARTA-derived indicators of behavior for spacecraft IDS [record_id:2105]. Together, they show NDR methods being adapted to mission systems and cyber-physical infrastructure.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The records are primarily conference abstracts, not full papers or datasets. Many claims are plausible and technically detailed, but the provided raw text rarely includes enough methodological detail to independently assess reproducibility. Quantitative claims such as “4.3 million hosts” [record_id:9], “over 95% accuracy” [record_id:23], “1.5 billion DNS requests” [record_id:81], “over 100 critical pre-auth vulnerabilities” [record_id:11], “26 vulnerabilities” and “20 CVEs” [record_id:57], “97.92% detection accuracy” [record_id:2390], and “600+ vulnerabilities” in changelog monitoring [record_id:2278] are important but should be validated against slides, papers, code, disclosures, or vendor advisories before downstream agents rely on them.

There is also uneven coverage across subdomains. DNS, tunnels, C2-over-trusted-services, OT/IoT, RF, and ML-assisted detection are well represented. Traditional NDR topics such as Suricata rule engineering, Zeek package development, packet broker architecture, east-west data-center telemetry, encrypted traffic analysis at enterprise scale, TLS interception governance, and SOC deployment economics are less directly covered. Zeek appears in campaign-detection and incident-graph contexts [record_id:193] [record_id:197], but Suricata is not a major raw-text theme.

Many offensive records include tool releases or proof-of-concept claims without corresponding defensive evaluation. TURNt [record_id:33] [record_id:1913], Google-service SOCKS tunneling [record_id:2097], Meshtastic C2 [record_id:1895], C4 external C2 [record_id:2117], Angry Magpie DLP bypass [record_id:2272], Draytek Arsenal [record_id:2120], SSHamble [record_id:2100], and Wi-Fi cracking tools in training [record_id:2578] all create practical research leads, but the raw text often provides limited detection logic. Some records do include countermeasures or mitigation discussion, but details are not present in the abstracts [record_id:1] [record_id:33] [record_id:35] [record_id:44] [record_id:1926] [record_id:2536].

A major open question is how to combine behavior-based NDR with privacy and governance. DNS telemetry can detect infrastructure and exfiltration [record_id:35] [record_id:1877] [record_id:2262] [record_id:2278], but it can also fingerprint and track users [record_id:81]. Web traffic baselines can detect supply-chain compromise [record_id:23], but may require broad inspection of application usage. TLS/HTTP fingerprinting can block bots and drive deception [record_id:1948], but fingerprint stability and evasion remain open. The records do not resolve how organizations should balance detection fidelity, user privacy, legal requirements, and operational friction.

Another open question is generalization. Many ML records explicitly address it—TERLA for topology generalization [record_id:116], standardized feature extraction and dynamic ensembles [record_id:2390], network-agnostic HeAT features [record_id:196], Internet-scale malicious infrastructure models [record_id:205], and security-specific feature encoders [record_id:224]. Yet the corpus also repeatedly hints that narrow datasets, imbalanced classes, changing infrastructure, and topology differences make generalization difficult [record_id:159] [record_id:237] [record_id:2390] [record_id:2505]. More open datasets, reproducible benchmarks, and cross-environment validation would strengthen this area.

Finally, some records are sparse, ambiguous, duplicate, or logistical. Records with empty or nearly empty raw text cannot support detailed claims beyond title and source context [record_id:177] [record_id:260]. Some DEF CON records duplicate Black Hat content, which strengthens coverage but not independent evidence [record_id:1] [record_id:1907] [record_id:33] [record_id:1913] [record_id:56] [record_id:1998] [record_id:7] [record_id:2140] [record_id:1931] [record_id:2145] [record_id:2088] [record_id:2146] [record_id:2062] [record_id:2136]. A few records are broad panels or community/gadget talks with limited technical detail [record_id:1893] [record_id:1946] [record_id:1975] [record_id:1991] [record_id:2011] [record_id:2149] [record_id:2167] [record_id:2540].
