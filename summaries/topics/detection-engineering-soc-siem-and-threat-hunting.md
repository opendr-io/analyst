# Topic: Detection engineering, SOC, SIEM, and threat hunting

## Executive Summary

The records collectively describe a broad, rapidly evolving detection-engineering and security-operations landscape. The central story is that defenders are trying to move from static signatures, siloed alert queues, and manual analyst workflows toward context-rich, telemetry-driven, and increasingly AI-assisted systems. Across Black Hat, DEF CON, CAMLIS, BSides, Prompt||GTFO, and [un]prompted records, recurring concerns include alert fatigue, fragmented tools, low visibility, poor data quality, lack of labels, difficulty validating detections, adversary adaptation, and the need to translate threat intelligence into operational detections.

Several strong themes emerge. First, SOC modernization is a dominant concern: records emphasize alert prioritization, clustering, explainability, incident timelines, analyst workflows, and organizational structure [record_id:59] [record_id:84] [record_id:105] [record_id:192] [record_id:197] [record_id:199] [record_id:2255] [record_id:2530]. Second, AI and ML are now mainstream in detection research, but the records show a spectrum from cautious, interpretable ML and feature engineering to agentic SOC systems and LLM-assisted hunting [record_id:87] [record_id:89] [record_id:125] [record_id:137] [record_id:139] [record_id:142] [record_id:145] [record_id:153] [record_id:168] [record_id:2190] [record_id:2222] [record_id:2240] [record_id:2292] [record_id:2364] [record_id:2380]. Third, detection engineering is increasingly threat-informed and validation-oriented: adversary emulation, purple teaming, assumed compromise, attack-flow reconstruction, and synthetic data are repeatedly presented as ways to find visibility gaps and improve detection logic [record_id:1908] [record_id:1939] [record_id:2015] [record_id:2105] [record_id:2344] [record_id:2525] [record_id:2562].

The corpus also shows detection expanding beyond traditional endpoint and network logs. Records cover CI/CD and software supply chain monitoring [record_id:49] [record_id:2004] [record_id:2305], cloud and identity telemetry [record_id:156] [record_id:175] [record_id:2312] [record_id:2388], OT/ICS and maritime/space/vehicle environments [record_id:2049] [record_id:2075] [record_id:2079] [record_id:2102] [record_id:2105], AI-system observability and GenAI threat detection [record_id:2341] [record_id:2342] [record_id:2377], and fraud/social-engineering detection [record_id:78] [record_id:2235] [record_id:2244] [record_id:2317]. Evidence strength varies: some records report quantitative results, production deployment, or real-world case studies, while others are short conference teasers, panels, trainings, or records with minimal raw text.

## Research Landscape

The landscape is dominated by conference-session abstracts and workshop descriptions rather than full papers. CAMLIS records, especially from 2017–2025, contribute much of the methodological depth around ML for security operations: graph embeddings, anomaly detection, label generation, model evaluation, analyst workflow, incident summarization, feature engineering, and data pipelines [record_id:153] [record_id:154] [record_id:157] [record_id:159] [record_id:184] [record_id:191] [record_id:192] [record_id:193] [record_id:194] [record_id:197] [record_id:214] [record_id:221] [record_id:223] [record_id:224] [record_id:236] [record_id:237] [record_id:244] [record_id:251] [record_id:257] [record_id:261]. The overall research area is therefore not a single discipline but an intersection of detection engineering, SOC design, telemetry architecture, applied ML, threat intelligence, adversary emulation, and human factors. The strongest records tend to describe either production-scale systems, such as Google’s FACADE insider-threat system scanning billions of events daily [record_id:87], SOC alert clustering over large managed-response datasets [record_id:192], or specific incident case studies such as tj-actions [record_id:49] [record_id:2004] and the Cyberhaven breach [record_id:2311]. Thinner records include panels, high-level training abstracts, and records with empty raw bodies where only titles and metadata indicate relevance [record_id:173] [record_id:174] [record_id:177] [record_id:178] [record_id:188] [record_id:255] [record_id:260] [record_id:263].

## Major Themes And Trends

### 1. SOCs are overloaded, fragmented, and searching for better operating models

A recurring theme is that defenders do not lack alerts; they lack usable context, prioritization, and workflow coherence. The Black Hat talk “Death by Noise” argues that attackers can operate while generating only medium- and low-severity alerts that SOCs often deprioritize, exposing a process-level weakness rather than a purely technical one [record_id:59]. Splunk’s main-stage session frames modern SOC difficulty around fragmented tools, limited visibility, skills shortages, disconnected workflows, and rigid architectures [record_id:105]. BSidesSF’s “One SOC, the Whole SOC…” similarly treats SOC organization itself as a major determinant of effectiveness [record_id:2255].

Several records propose technical interventions for analyst overload. Event clustering for SOC workflow aims to group similar alerts, use prior analyst decisions, and allow group-level disposition to reduce workload [record_id:192]. Incident-report summarization and visualization reduce report size and improve precision while making narrative structure easier to see [record_id:197]. Automated incident prioritization uses multi-modal features and attention to score severity before analyst review [record_id:199]. CLEAR-ROAD searches for temporally co-occurring rare critical alerts in streaming IDS data without training data [record_id:208]. HeAT reconstructs “HeATed Attack Campaigns” around critical IoCs to reveal stages buried in millions of alerts [record_id:196].

More recent records extend this same theme into agentic SOCs and AI-assisted triage. Night Beacon uses a fine-tuned BERT model to assess alerts, provide risk probabilities, learn from analyst feedback, and reprioritize alerts [record_id:2240]. BSidesLV’s “SOC Like a Genius” describes SLM/LLM agents that enrich alerts, reconstruct timelines, and guide response based on business impact [record_id:2530]. Salesforce’s “Beyond the Chatbot” records argue that the next SOC architecture moves from Q&A copilots to supervisor-worker “Agentic SOC” systems where agents plan, reason, and act [record_id:2380] [record_id:2381]. Datadog’s threat-hunting automation work is more cautious: it identifies an “automation boundary,” stressing trust, hallucinations, evaluation, and where AI accelerates rather than replaces hunters [record_id:2364].

### 2. Detection engineering is becoming more data-first and behavior-first

Many records emphasize behavior, context, and telemetry rather than static indicators. BEAM detects supply-chain compromise from web traffic by creating application-specific behavioral baselines across 65 signals and large-scale HTTP/HTTPS telemetry [record_id:23]. Logrip detects automated bot and AI crawler behavior in server logs using visualization, hierarchical IP hashing, and statistical measures of non-human access patterns [record_id:37]. Finch uses TLS and HTTP fingerprints such as JA3, JA4, JA4H, and HTTP/2 to make reverse-proxy decisions before traffic reaches production or honeypots [record_id:1948]. “Trawling for IOCs” presents a data-first approach to building detection rules and threat feeds from large security datasets [record_id:2278].

This trend also appears in supply-chain and CI/CD detection. The tj-actions breach record emphasizes that a single anomalous outbound connection from a pipeline run led to discovery of a compromised GitHub Action and recommends detecting future CI/CD supply-chain attacks through deviations from normal network activity rather than signatures [record_id:49]. The DEF CON tj-actions record frames the same incident as a broader, longer-running campaign with lateral movement across open-source projects and evasion [record_id:2004]. Records on malicious packages and open-source collaboration describe threat hunting for package typosquatting, author impersonation, malicious packages, sentiment- and keyword-based detection of social attack surfaces, and suspicious GitHub activity [record_id:123] [record_id:2305].

The behavior-first trend is particularly strong in endpoint and process telemetry. ProblemChild models parent-child process relationships as a graph to find suspicious attack sequences and living-off-the-land patterns [record_id:221]. Registry frequent itemset mining seeks “immutable signatures” of persistence behavior by recording scripted examples and mining time-bounded registry logs [record_id:183]. Shell command parsing and command-line anomaly/classification records show a long-running effort to treat command syntax and command sessions as specialized security language, from Shell Language Preprocessing to DistilBERT shell-session anomaly detection and benign-anomalous data augmentation [record_id:191] [record_id:169] [record_id:89]. Endpoint-focused records also include AMSI-based PowerShell malware scoring [record_id:216], tree-sitter deobfuscation and custom AMSI provider work [record_id:2430], macOS Endpoint Security framework visibility [record_id:1964], and GenAI endpoint observability for AI-spawned processes [record_id:2341].

### 3. AI/ML is pervasive, but the records repeatedly warn about evaluation, false positives, hallucination, and operational cost

The corpus shows a clear progression from classical ML and feature engineering toward LLMs and agents. Older and mid-period CAMLIS records focus on features, labels, benchmarks, clustering, graph methods, and model uncertainty [record_id:214] [record_id:223] [record_id:224] [record_id:236] [record_id:248] [record_id:251]. Later records add LLMs for TTP extraction, attribution, log analysis, YARA generation, Sigma conversion, phishing-language assessment, and multi-agent triage [record_id:137] [record_id:61] [record_id:2190] [record_id:2204] [record_id:2222] [record_id:2228] [record_id:2233] [record_id:2235].

However, the corpus is not uniformly enthusiastic. Several records explicitly address evaluation and false-positive costs. “Is F1 Score Suboptimal…” argues that cybersecurity false positives and false negatives have unequal costs and proposes a cost-aware metric that produced average cost savings in experiments [record_id:142]. Firenze evaluates ML models using weak signals and domain-expert “markers” because labels are noisy, limited, or biased [record_id:184]. Malware benchmark work argues that small accuracy changes matter at industry scale and proposes harder train/test splits to detect small effect sizes [record_id:158]. The [un]prompted record on measuring agent effectiveness argues that as agents become easy to build, the hard part is measuring whether they work in the wild, requiring multi-dimensional evaluation of reasoning, evidence gathering, and tool-calling [record_id:2322].

Several records contrast ML and LLM approaches with deterministic or interpretable alternatives. The Splunk-to-Sigma Prompt||GTFO record is especially representative: an LLM-based conversion pipeline with validation was replaced by an LLM-authored deterministic parser, making conversions far faster and suggesting LLMs should often build tools rather than be the tool [record_id:2233]. “Root Cause and Attack Flows” explicitly positions clustering, temporal knowledge graphs, and Markov sequencing as explainable alternatives to black-box or LLM-heavy alert correlation [record_id:2525]. DEF CON’s IoT alert-contextualization talk similarly advertises an LLM-free approach based on lightweight correlation using time, topology, and behavior [record_id:1935].

### 4. Threat hunting is increasingly threat-intelligence-driven and adversary-emulation-driven

Threat hunting records range from domain intelligence and passive DNS to attribution, OSINT, adversary group clustering, and structured analytic techniques. Domain Intelligence Analysis aims to use DNS artifacts before domains become public IoCs [record_id:1877]. Passive DNS is used to connect typosquatting domains to broader Iranian-backed infrastructure [record_id:2262]. CTI fragmentation is treated as a systemic problem, with massive IOC volumes and feed overlap/delay complicating defender action [record_id:2439]. MISP integration is presented as a way to augment enterprise tools and improve threat-investigation workflows [record_id:2559].

Attribution and adversary tracking also appear repeatedly. APTinder vectorizes threat-activity “documents” using tools, infrastructure, timing, and targeting to help analysts assess similarity between adversary groups [record_id:243]. LLM-based TTP extraction and threat-actor attribution use vector search and human-in-the-loop analyst input to improve attribution from CTI documentation [record_id:137]. Microsoft’s main-stage record describes a real-time feedback loop between threat intelligence, incident response, and threat hunting against actors such as Star Blizzard and Mint Sandstorm [record_id:95]. Other records focus on tracking specific threat ecosystems: North Korean IT worker operations [record_id:22] [record_id:2124], Dark Caracal cyber mercenaries [record_id:2271], Russian APT adoption of offensive research [record_id:2024], Chinese operational relay networks [record_id:2025], MUT-1244 targeting researchers [record_id:2032], VexTrio and malicious adtech/TDS operations [record_id:52], and cybercrime operations exposed through Telegram C2 interception [record_id:2027].

Adversary emulation and purple teaming are presented as a major mechanism for making detections realistic. Internal adversary simulation generated labeled data for DNS-exfiltration detection and grew into a security data-science practice [record_id:237]. DEF CON panels argue that purple teaming should be continuous, intelligence-driven, and aligned around realistic attacker behavior [record_id:1908] [record_id:1939]. “Letthemin” gives an assumed-compromise purple-team methodology focused on tool visibility across the attack lifecycle [record_id:2015]. BSidesLV’s adversary-emulation workshop includes SIEM, EDR, web proxies, MITRE ATT&CK mapping, and custom emulation plans [record_id:2562]. Space-cyber and OT records show this trend moving into specialized domains through Moonlight Defender exercises and SPARTA-derived Indicators of Behavior [record_id:2071] [record_id:2105].

### 5. Detection is expanding into cloud, identity, SaaS, GenAI, OT, maritime, space, and fraud domains

Identity and SaaS telemetry appear as major detection areas. MFA bypass detection uses DNS-at-authentication-time and targeted filtering to create labeled malicious authentication samples [record_id:156]. Azure sign-in detection uses heterogeneous relational graph embeddings to model users, devices, services, and authentications [record_id:175]. Google Workspace attacks exploit logging gaps; the record emphasizes investigation even without sufficient logs [record_id:2312]. Active Directory records cover defensive logging, WEF aggregation, dMSA privilege escalation detection, NTLM relay, Kerberoasting, delegation weaknesses, and related mitigation [record_id:2388] [record_id:2547]. Credential leak and credential-stuffing-adjacent records discuss automated credential threat monitoring, synthetic passwords designed to pollute logs, SMS 2FA revenue-share fraud, and credential misuse [record_id:2268] [record_id:2556] [record_id:2317] [record_id:253].

Cloud and infrastructure records include multi-cloud red/blue training across AWS, Azure, and GCP [record_id:2489] [record_id:2490] [record_id:2491] [record_id:2492], living-off-the-cloud attacks [record_id:2273], SQL-driven cloud MLOps for detection pipelines [record_id:157], and quantum-platform monitoring for suspicious circuit/API patterns in a post-quantum context [record_id:1996] [record_id:2457]. RAG and MCP are proposed for SOC alert triage, gap analysis, policy/threat-intel/log context, and compliance mapping [record_id:2515]. Local LLM-based RAG is proposed for threat hunting where environmental data is confidential [record_id:2516].

OT, IoT, maritime, automotive, and space records show detection engineering moving into environments with different telemetry and operational constraints. NMEA2000 fingerprinting creates deterministic sensor baselines and tests controlled attacks [record_id:2049]. MOSAICS is framed as real-time monitoring, safe asset discovery, and behavioral threat detection for naval mission systems [record_id:2075]. OT SOC and incident response records discuss specialized SOC capabilities, continuity, and safety [record_id:2079]. Automotive CAN detection uses inter-signal correlations and sequence learning without decoding signal structures [record_id:2102]. OT neural ASR rules use Suspicious2Vec and malware templates to detect suspicious OT behaviors at scale [record_id:2113]. Maritime OSINT and incident-response talks add proactive intelligence and war-room-style response perspectives [record_id:2074] [record_id:2133]. Space-related records cover purple-team exercises and SPARTA-derived behavioral indicators for spacecraft IDS [record_id:2071] [record_id:2105].

Fraud, abuse, and social engineering appear as adjacent detection disciplines. TF-IDF is repurposed for real-time fraud detection by treating transaction metadata, device identifiers, and behavioral signals as a corpus [record_id:78]. AI-enhanced phishing attack and defense records focus on real-time detection, adaptive filtering, and behavioral analysis [record_id:1878]. Prompt-engineered classification of phishing/social-engineering emails uses compact yes/no labels to supplement Snort, YARA, and PCRE [record_id:2244]. Linguistic phishing investigation uses grammar interference and cultural cues for triage [record_id:2235]. Reddit defense at scale emphasizes traffic analysis, abuse defense, resiliency, and tailored in-house detection [record_id:2019].

### 6. Adversaries attack the detection pipeline itself

Several records are explicitly about evasion, deception, or blinding defenders. ETW event injection can be used by blue teams to safely replicate attack telemetry, but also by red teams to mislead analysts or trigger EDR capping so Defender for Endpoint ignores later logs [record_id:17]. “Death by Noise” describes attackers blending into medium/low-severity alert noise and suppressing or downgrading critical detections [record_id:59]. Web conferencing C2 via TURN servers exploits trusted media infrastructure and TLS-inspection exemptions, creating detection challenges because sessions resemble ordinary meetings [record_id:33] [record_id:1913]. Outlook SMTP/IMAP C2 bypasses web isolation that blocks HTTP/HTTPS and requires different detection logic [record_id:2536]. Fine-tuned LLMs as covert C2/exfiltration channels are presented as difficult for traditional tools to detect [record_id:2573].

The records also show defenders responding with deception and delay. Honeypots appear in multiple contexts: adaptive honeypot experimental design [record_id:159], Cowrie-based SSH honeypot workshops [record_id:1986], GreyNoise honeypot-scale AI detection and deception [record_id:2365], and Azazel’s “Cyber Scapegoat” gateway for delaying attackers [record_id:2397]. ICS deception using MITRE Engage and APT44-oriented strategies is also represented [record_id:2095]. These records frame deception not merely as observation but as an operational way to control time, increase attacker cost, and generate detection intelligence.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records describe many concrete methods and tools. In detection-rule engineering, Sigma is a recurring standard: Prompt||GTFO covers Splunk-to-Sigma conversion and the value of deterministic parsing [record_id:2233], BSidesLV covers Sigma hunting workflows and creation challenges [record_id:2448] [record_id:2529], and RAGnarok generates Sigma rules from threat intelligence with local LLMs [record_id:2516]. YARA and related detection-rule generation appear in LLMDYara’s automated YARA generation with DNAHash and LLM-assisted feature evaluation [record_id:61], Binary Ninja MCP’s automatic YARA/Suricata/Sigma output from malware samples [record_id:2228], and traditional YARA comparisons in obfuscated script detection [record_id:2430]. SYARA extends YARA-like concepts to semantic GenAI threats using strings, embeddings, classifiers, and LLMs [record_id:2342].

Graph-based approaches are prominent. Kubernetes sessions are modeled as graphs for GNN-based threat detection [record_id:153]. User-resource audit logs become heterogeneous weighted bipartite graphs for insider-threat link prediction [record_id:154]. Azure sign-ins use heterogeneous relational graph embeddings [record_id:175]. Threat intelligence objects are embedded from threat graphs for personalized retrieval and scoring [record_id:145]. Dynamic graphs represent incident reports and log relationships for summarization and visualization [record_id:197]. Temporal knowledge graphs and Markovian sequencing support root-cause and attack-flow detection [record_id:2525]. Graphistry’s “vibes investigating” uses AI agents and graph-oriented workflows in CTF-style security investigations [record_id:2222].

Anomaly detection appears in many variants: DIP-ECOD for multimodal distributions [record_id:139], DistilBERT shell-session anomaly detection [record_id:169], point-process user authentication modeling [record_id:246], self-organizing maps for user-based network anomaly detection [record_id:260], distributional NetFlow features for botnet C2 detection [record_id:179], context-aware CAN anomaly detection [record_id:2102], insider anomaly detection with contrastive learning [record_id:87], and command-line anomaly detection repurposed to generate diverse benign training data [record_id:89]. Several records emphasize that anomaly detection alone often creates high false positives, so it must be paired with supervised classifiers, context, analyst feedback, or cost-aware evaluation [record_id:89] [record_id:142] [record_id:184] [record_id:244].

LLM and agent workflows are varied. Records describe LLMs generating their own prompts for log analysis and DLP/anomaly detection [record_id:2190], OpenAI structured output for threat-intel entity extraction and Sigma generation [record_id:2204], N8N workflows for threat intelligence collection and Slack digests [record_id:2226], Claude Code multi-agent investigation templates [record_id:2222], local LLM/RAG/multi-agent threat hunting [record_id:2516], RAG + MCP for SOC/compliance automation [record_id:2515], LLM-assisted incident response to Shai-Hulud-style large-scale data leaks [record_id:2332], and AI automation boundaries for threat hunting [record_id:2364]. AI is also used to extract IoCs from infostealer screenshots [record_id:26] [record_id:1945], classify web content via distilled LLMs [record_id:166], generate YARA rules [record_id:61], and benchmark cybersecurity capabilities such as SQL generation, severity prediction, and IOC summarization [record_id:168].

Telemetry and pipeline architecture are another methodological layer. SQL-driven MLOps on Snowflake and ECS/Kubernetes supports cloud-centric detection pipelines across email, firewall, and endpoint data [record_id:157]. AWS infrastructure supports scalable malware labeling, historical label rollback, GDPR constraints, and global reputation exports [record_id:214]. OmnibusCyber proposes a TypeDB-based strongly typed cyber database to unify security objects, support inference, embeddings, and OLAP-style analysis [record_id:189]. Splunk datasets are presented as curated data for researchers using Splunk MLTK [record_id:236]. BETH provides real host telemetry for anomaly detection and out-of-distribution research [record_id:194]. Adaptive experimental design offers a more causal approach to intrusion data collection through controlled honeypot deployment [record_id:159].

Human workflow and organizational methods include incident command systems [record_id:2423], roleplaying incident-response exercises [record_id:2425], structured analytic techniques for CTI bias mitigation [record_id:2561], AppSec partnership models that connect platform engineering, compliance, and threat detection [record_id:2252], third-party supplier incident handling [record_id:2285], and community cyber-defense volunteering [record_id:2001]. These records matter because the corpus repeatedly suggests detection quality is not only a technical problem but also a coordination, training, and decision-making problem.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

Several records stand out as especially representative or evidence-rich.

Google’s FACADE record is one of the strongest examples of production-scale ML detection. It claims seven years of use at Alphabet, billions of events scanned daily, contrastive learning on benign data, clustering for robustness, and extremely low false-positive rates for insider detection [record_id:87]. It is representative of the corpus’s push toward contextual, self-supervised detection, but stronger than many records because it reports operational scale and performance.

The Timesketch AI-agent record is notable because it targets a core IR pain point: reconstructing attack timelines from hundreds of millions of records and many log types. It claims evaluation on 100 real-world compromised systems with high recall and precision for identifying and contextualizing attack-chain logs [record_id:84]. Together with incident summarization [record_id:197], HeAT [record_id:196], CLEAR-ROAD [record_id:208], and root-cause attack-flow correlation [record_id:2525], it anchors a theme of automated context reconstruction.

The tj-actions records are important case studies for supply-chain detection. The Black Hat record is detection-centered: an anomalous outbound connection from CI/CD to gist.githubusercontent.com exposed a compromised action, credential dumping into logs, and chained compromise through reviewdog [record_id:49]. The DEF CON record extends the case into campaign reconstruction and attacker objectives beyond credential leakage [record_id:2004]. Together they show how detection engineering must cover build pipelines, trusted automation, logs, secrets exposure, and normal network baselines.

The “Death by Noise” EDR record is notable because it frames alert fatigue as an attack surface. Rather than bypassing tools by disabling them, attackers downgrade, suppress, or blend malicious behavior into lower-severity noise across CrowdStrike, SentinelOne, and Defender for Endpoint [record_id:59]. This pairs with ETW telemetry manipulation and EDR capping [record_id:17], web-conferencing C2 [record_id:33] [record_id:1913], and synthetic-password log pollution [record_id:2556] to show adversaries attacking both sensors and human triage assumptions.

The Prompt||GTFO Splunk-to-Sigma record is a useful practical counterweight to LLM hype. It documents a journey from LLM conversion with guardrails to deterministic parsing written with LLM assistance, emphasizing speed and reserving LLMs for outliers [record_id:2233]. Similar pragmatism appears in log analysis with prompt caching and batching [record_id:2190], threat-intel tooling with structured outputs and cost management [record_id:2204], and Datadog’s explicit discussion of trust and hallucination in threat hunting automation [record_id:2364].

The Sigma hunting records [record_id:2448] [record_id:2529], RAGnarok [record_id:2516], LLMDYara [record_id:61], Binary Ninja MCP [record_id:2228], and SYARA [record_id:2342] collectively show a trend toward rule generation and portability: defenders want detection logic that can be authored faster, explained, validated, and translated across environments.

Finally, the OT/space/maritime records are notable because they broaden the detection-engineering domain. NMEA2000 fingerprints [record_id:2049], automotive CAN context-aware detection [record_id:2102], naval MOSAICS monitoring [record_id:2075], OT SOC/IR [record_id:2079], SPARTA-derived spacecraft IoBs [record_id:2105], and Moonlight Defender space purple teams [record_id:2071] show that modern detection engineering must account for physical consequences, constrained telemetry, legacy protocols, and mission-specific baselines.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The corpus is rich but uneven. Many records are abstracts or talk teasers, so evidence often consists of claimed results rather than reproducible datasets, code, or full evaluations. Some records report clear metrics—BEAM’s accuracy claims [record_id:23], FACADE’s false-positive rates [record_id:87], DIP-ECOD AUC improvements [record_id:139], cost-aware Cscore savings [record_id:142], BETH dataset size [record_id:194], URL model performance [record_id:223], and incident-summary precision gains [record_id:197]. Others are high-level panels or training descriptions with little technical detail [record_id:1881] [record_id:1908] [record_id:2255] [record_id:2423] [record_id:2425]. Several CAMLIS records have empty raw text, so their relevance is inferred only from title and metadata, not substantive content [record_id:173] [record_id:174] [record_id:177] [record_id:178] [record_id:188] [record_id:255] [record_id:260].

Important open questions include:

- **Evaluation realism:** How should SOC agents, detection rules, and ML classifiers be tested against unseen attacks, distribution shifts, and adversarial adaptation? Records propose harder splits [record_id:158], weak-signal evaluation [record_id:184], cost-aware metrics [record_id:142], and agent-effectiveness metrics [record_id:2322], but there is no unified standard.
- **Human-AI trust:** Many records propose AI agents for triage and hunting, but fewer detail governance, failure modes, escalation criteria, or how analysts should calibrate trust [record_id:2240] [record_id:2292] [record_id:2364] [record_id:2380].
- **Detection portability:** Sigma and YARA-like formats address portability, but translating detections across telemetry schemas, SIEMs, EDRs, cloud logs, and AI/OT environments remains unresolved [record_id:2233] [record_id:2448] [record_id:2529] [record_id:2342].
- **Data quality and labels:** Many records depend on labeled malicious data, benign baselines, or analyst feedback, but label scarcity, drift, delayed intelligence, and privacy constraints remain major problems [record_id:214] [record_id:236] [record_id:184] [record_id:2327].
- **Adversary manipulation of telemetry:** ETW injection, alert-noise abuse, trusted-service C2, log pollution, and LLM-based covert channels suggest that defenders must validate telemetry integrity and model adversarial pressure on the SOC itself [record_id:17] [record_id:59] [record_id:1913] [record_id:2556] [record_id:2573].
- **Specialized-domain detection:** OT, maritime, aviation, space, and automotive records often present early baselines or demos. More open datasets, operational evaluations, and cross-domain telemetry schemas are still needed [record_id:2049] [record_id:2075] [record_id:2102] [record_id:2105].
