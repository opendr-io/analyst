# Topic: Cryptography key management and post-quantum security

## Executive Summary

The records collectively portray cryptography key management and post-quantum security as an applied, operationally difficult, and rapidly evolving research area. The strongest recurring theme is that cryptographic strength depends less on abstract algorithm choice alone and more on deployment realities: key storage, hardware security modules, certificate authority design, protocol misuse, secret extraction paths, and incident response under failure conditions. Several records focus directly on Hardware Security Modules (HSMs), including both their operational burden and possible future-proofing through reconfigurable FPGA-based designs [record_id:2058] [record_id:2304] [record_id:2409]. Another cluster addresses cryptographic misuse and attack education, such as hands-on cryptographic attacks, HMAC implementation flaws, password encryption reversal, and ransomware cryptography recovery [record_id:2476] [record_id:2551] [record_id:2522] [record_id:2442].

Post-quantum concerns appear in multiple forms. One record frames the issue conventionally, emphasizing “Harvest Now, Decrypt Later” risk, quantum-resistant encryption, quantum key distribution, secure multiparty computation, fully homomorphic encryption, and AI-assisted anomaly detection [record_id:2457]. Another presents a speculative or unconventional biological cryptography concept using DNA origami and biomolecular encoding as an alternative response to quantum threats [record_id:2109]. A separate record approaches cryptographic agility from the hardware side, arguing that reconfigurable HSMs could allow deployed systems to adapt as cryptographic algorithms evolve or are deprecated [record_id:2058].

Overall, the evidence base is dominated by conference and workshop abstracts from DEF CON 33, BSidesSF 2025, and BSidesLV 2025. The records are useful for mapping current practitioner concerns and planned talks, but they provide limited technical depth, evaluation data, or independent validation. They strongly indicate community interest in practical key management, HSM affordability and usability, post-quantum migration, cryptographic attack training, and protection of secrets in hardware. They are weaker on measurable performance, standards alignment, production deployment outcomes, and comparative analysis of post-quantum migration strategies.

## Research Landscape

The included records are mostly 2025 security conference talks and workshops. DEF CON 33 contributes two records: one on reconfigurable HSMs for hardware security agility [record_id:2058] and one on DNA-based “BioCypher” cryptography in a post-quantum framing [record_id:2109]. BSidesSF contributes a talk focused on the operational pain of HSM-based key management [record_id:2304]. BSidesLV contributes the majority of records, covering practical CA infrastructure on inexpensive HSM-like devices [record_id:2409], post-quantum cryptography combined with AI-based threat detection [record_id:2457], cryptographic attack education [record_id:2476], HMAC misuse [record_id:2551], F5 password encryption reversal [record_id:2522], ransomware cryptography recovery in an incident-response setting [record_id:2442], and physical destruction of GPUs to protect keys and sensitive assets in trusted execution environments [record_id:2563].

The overall landscape is not primarily theoretical cryptography. Instead, it is applied and operational. Records emphasize how cryptographic systems fail or succeed in real environments: whether keys are protected by HSMs, whether certificate authorities can be safely managed, whether implementers understand cryptographic attacks, whether secret storage formats leak usable keys, and whether hardware containing cryptographic material can be reliably protected or destroyed. Even the post-quantum records tend to be framed around migration, detection, and preparedness rather than purely mathematical construction [record_id:2457] [record_id:2058].

The HSM-related records form the clearest throughline. One record argues that traditional HSMs are rigid and may not adapt well as algorithms evolve or vulnerabilities appear, proposing FPGA-based reconfigurable HSMs as a future-proofing mechanism [record_id:2058]. Another takes a more operationally skeptical tone, calling key management “Cryptography’s dirty secret” and describing HSMs as promising “salvation” but delivering “operational nightmares and hidden costs” [record_id:2304]. A third HSM-related workshop shifts from enterprise hardware to accessible deployment, arguing that “Practical HSMs are cheap” due to PIV/CAC-driven PKCS#11 device availability and describing how to build a CA using a YubiKey for an offline root signing key [record_id:2409]. Together, these records suggest a research/practitioner conversation about making key protection more flexible, more affordable, and more operationally survivable.

The post-quantum subset is broader and more uneven. One record offers a mainstream risk framing around quantum computing making traditional cryptographic systems vulnerable, especially through “Harvest Now, Decrypt Later” attacks [record_id:2457]. It also expands into AI anomaly detection, comparing Isolation Forest and Autoencoders, and includes quantum-resistant encryption, quantum key distribution, secure multiparty computation, and fully homomorphic encryption [record_id:2457]. Another record is much more speculative, proposing DNA Origami Cryptography using viral scaffolds and nanometer-scale keys “over 700 bits long,” plus protein-binding steganography and molecular folds [record_id:2109]. While both invoke quantum threats, their evidentiary posture differs: the former aligns with known security planning topics, while the latter reads as an exploratory or provocative hacker-conference concept.

A separate set of records focuses on cryptographic attacks and implementation flaws. The cryptographic attacks workshop covers real-world vulnerabilities including CVE-2020-0601 and hands-on exercises ranging from decrypting ciphertext to recovering private keys and generating TLS and SSH private key files [record_id:2476]. The HMAC talk focuses on misunderstood or misused HMAC deployments, with code demos, vulnerability breakdowns, Python, and open-source tools [record_id:2551]. The F5 talk describes extracting a unit-specific master key from an F5 device and decrypting configuration passwords stored in the `$M$` format after exploit or legitimate access [record_id:2522]. These records emphasize that mature cryptographic primitives can fail through implementation, operational exposure, and key-management weaknesses.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Cryptographic agility is becoming a hardware and lifecycle problem

A major theme is that cryptographic systems must adapt over time. The reconfigurable HSM record explicitly states that “as cryptographic algorithms evolve and new vulnerabilities emerge,” traditional HSMs face “a critical limitation: their rigidity” [record_id:2058]. The proposed answer is FPGA-based reconfigurable HSMs that can be updated after deployment, allowing organizations to respond to cryptographic breakthroughs or deprecations without replacing hardware [record_id:2058]. This places cryptographic agility not just in software libraries and protocol negotiation, but in the hardware root-of-trust and key custody layer.

This concern connects naturally to post-quantum migration. If algorithms are deprecated or replaced because of quantum threats, systems that cannot update hardware-backed cryptographic functions may become expensive or slow to migrate. The post-quantum record on AI and quantum-era cybersecurity emphasizes that traditional cryptographic systems are becoming vulnerable and that organizations need preparation for a “quantum-secure future” [record_id:2457]. Although it does not explicitly link to HSM hardware agility, it reinforces the same underlying trend: cryptographic assumptions have a lifecycle, and institutions need mechanisms to change them before adversaries can exploit stored encrypted data.

### HSMs are central but operationally painful

The records show HSMs as both necessary and difficult. The BSidesSF talk states bluntly that “your security is only as strong as your key management” and describes HSMs as offering a promise of “salvation” while also bringing “operational nightmares and hidden costs” [record_id:2304]. This highlights an enduring tension: HSMs are a best-practice answer for key custody, but their integration, cost, governance, and maintenance can be difficult enough to undermine their value.

The cheap-HSM CA workshop offers a practical counterpoint. It argues that government adoption of PIV and CAC has lowered the price of PKCS#11 devices and that an expensive enterprise HSM is not necessary for an offline root signing key [record_id:2409]. The workshop proposes generating a CA contained on a YubiKey, issuing intermediates for online signing, and distributing certificates to applications and end-user devices [record_id:2409]. This suggests a trend toward democratizing key management, especially for private PKI and smaller organizations.

The reconfigurable HSM talk adds a third angle: even if HSMs are operationally viable and affordable, fixed-function hardware may be too rigid for future cryptographic transitions [record_id:2058]. Taken together, these records suggest three HSM research questions: how to operate HSMs safely, how to make them affordable and accessible, and how to design them for algorithmic change.

### Post-quantum security is framed as both migration planning and threat detection

The clearest post-quantum planning record argues that quantum computing threatens existing encryption and highlights “Harvest Now, Decrypt Later” attacks, where adversaries exfiltrate encrypted data today to decrypt later using quantum technology [record_id:2457]. This record broadens post-quantum defense beyond simply replacing algorithms. It includes AI-enhanced anomaly detection for early identification of “quantum-powered attacks,” compares Isolation Forest and Autoencoders, and surveys quantum-resistant encryption, quantum key distribution, secure multiparty computation, and fully homomorphic encryption [record_id:2457].

This is notable because the record mixes several domains that are often treated separately: cryptographic migration, detection engineering, privacy-preserving computation, and quantum communications. The synthesis implies that post-quantum preparedness is not only about deploying lattice-based or hash-based algorithms; it also includes identifying data at risk, monitoring unusual access patterns, and planning how sensitive sectors such as finance, healthcare, and government preserve confidentiality over long time horizons [record_id:2457].

However, the evidence is high-level. The record claims AI can enhance anomaly detection and that attendees will receive actionable insights, but the abstract does not provide experimental results, dataset details, accuracy metrics, or concrete post-quantum implementation guidance [record_id:2457]. Downstream research should treat it as evidence of practitioner interest and topic framing, not as proof of effectiveness.

### Unconventional post-quantum ideas appear alongside mainstream migration

The BioCypher/DNA Origami Cryptography record presents a very different post-quantum framing. It claims that quantum computing threatens traditional encryption and proposes “DNA Origami Cryptography” using viral scaffolds to create nanometer-scale encryption keys over 700 bits long [record_id:2109]. It also mentions protein-binding steganography, multi-part message integrity, encrypted communication through “braille-like molecular folds,” and a tool named BioCypher for plasmid design to embed cryptographic messages [record_id:2109].

This record is important less as established cryptographic practice and more as evidence that hacker-conference discussions of post-quantum security include speculative, biological, and artistic explorations. Its claims are broad and colorful, and the abstract does not provide a security proof, threat model, implementation details, or comparison against standardized post-quantum cryptography [record_id:2109]. It should therefore be treated as exploratory source material rather than strong evidence for a practical post-quantum replacement.

### Cryptographic failures often arise from misuse, misunderstood primitives, and exposed key material

Several records emphasize that cryptographic systems can fail even when recognizable primitives are involved. The HMAC talk asks what happens when a widely trusted integrity and authenticity mechanism is “misunderstood, misused, or just plain broken” [record_id:2551]. It promises code demos, vulnerability breakdowns, Python examples, and open-source tools to show how mature systems can fall victim to “quiet flaws” [record_id:2551]. This suggests a practical education focus: developers and defenders need to recognize misuse patterns, not merely select standard primitives.

The cryptographic attacks workshop reinforces this educational theme. It states that cryptography is subtle and mistakes can produce significant vulnerabilities, then promises hands-on implementation of attacks after explanation [record_id:2476]. Its examples include CVE-2020-0601, decrypting ciphertext, recovering private keys from public key attacks, and creating TLS certificate private key and SSH private key files [record_id:2476]. The reference to Cryptopals-style exercises suggests a pedagogical approach that turns abstract attacks into implementable labs.

The F5 password encryption record highlights a concrete key-management failure mode: devices store secrets in configuration files encrypted by a unit-specific master key, and the talk describes extracting that master key with device access and decrypting configuration passwords [record_id:2522]. This is a classic applied cryptography problem: local encryption of secrets often reduces to whether the key is extractable under realistic access conditions. The record also says the technique was derived through documentation reading and manipulation of data storage formats [record_id:2522], suggesting reverse engineering rather than cryptanalysis of a primitive.

### Incident response and data recovery can involve “cracking cryptography,” but details are thin

The ransomware incident-response record describes a nation-state campaign, a ransomware attack across a large organization, “countless terabytes” of data held hostage, and “no possibility of a key” [record_id:2442]. It then claims the responders discovered a method to exploit the ransomware and reclaim the majority of encrypted data, framing the talk as “the art of cracking cryptography” [record_id:2442]. This record is relevant because it connects applied cryptography to real-world recovery under adversarial conditions.

However, the abstract does not specify whether the recovery came from a cryptographic implementation flaw, key reuse, bad randomness, ransomware logic errors, file format properties, partial backups, or operational mistakes by attackers [record_id:2442].
