# Topic: OT and IoT security

## Executive Summary

The records portray OT and IoT security as a broad, rapidly expanding research area centered on cyber-physical consequences: compromised devices do not merely leak data; they can affect vehicles, energy systems, maritime navigation, water treatment, healthcare delivery, elections, building access, communications, and personal safety. The strongest recurring message is that modern OT/IoT risk is no longer confined to isolated industrial networks. Commodity TCP/IP, cloud APIs, cellular modules, embedded Linux, protocol gateways, mobile apps, dealer portals, and consumer IoT ecosystems have become practical paths into systems that control physical processes [record_id:44] [record_id:1978] [record_id:2104] [record_id:2112] [record_id:2121].

The corpus is dominated by 2025 conference abstracts from Black Hat, DEF CON villages, and BSides events, supplemented by CAMLIS research on autonomous cyber defense and detection for cyber-physical systems. It includes deep vulnerability research against industrial protocols, EV charging, cameras, scooters, routers, modems, automotive systems, maritime systems, space systems, radio networks, voting systems, and embedded firmware. It also includes policy, governance, and resilience discussions for water, healthcare, food, power, emergency services, elections, and other lifeline critical infrastructure [record_id:107] [record_id:2413] [record_id:2427] [record_id:2434] [record_id:2534] [record_id:2564].

Across the records, several themes stand out:

* Protocol-level and cryptographic assumptions are repeatedly shown to be fragile, including OPC UA authentication bypasses, TETRA E2EE weaknesses, goTenna key-store poisoning, AirPlay zero-click vulnerabilities, post-quantum signature verification fault attacks, and unauthenticated radio or alerting protocols [record_id:7] [record_id:60] [record_id:65] [record_id:80] [record_id:1894] [record_id:1952] [record_id:2140].
* Embedded supply chains propagate weaknesses at scale through SDK reuse, opaque chip provenance, rebadged OEM products, insecure firmware update mechanisms, hardcoded credentials, and devices resold without firmware sanitization [record_id:1925] [record_id:1978] [record_id:2034] [record_id:2073] [record_id:2131].
* Maritime, space, automotive, EV, and RF systems are heavily represented, suggesting that researchers are increasingly treating transportation and communications infrastructure as OT environments with unique safety, timing, and physical constraints [record_id:16] [record_id:31] [record_id:32] [record_id:1931] [record_id:2033] [record_id:2039] [record_id:2046] [record_id:2083].
* Defensive work emphasizes simulation, testbeds, autonomous decision-making, anomaly detection, OT SOCs, deception, cyber-informed engineering, and threat-informed detection frameworks, but the evidence base is uneven: some records describe concrete experiments and datasets, while many are talk abstracts with limited validation details [record_id:136] [record_id:163] [record_id:186] [record_id:2049] [record_id:2075] [record_id:2079] [record_id:2095] [record_id:2105] [record_id:2113].

Overall, the records collectively describe a field moving from “find bugs in devices” toward “understand systems-of-systems risk.” The most persuasive records are those with named protocols, devices, CVEs, PoCs, testbeds, or empirical experiments. The thinner records are short village previews, high-level panels, or policy-oriented abstracts that identify important concerns but provide little technical evidence.

## Research Landscape

DEF CON 33 dominates the record count and broadens the topic into villages and community talks. The DEF CON material includes IoT Village and ICS Village previews, RF Village talks, Voting Village sessions, maritime village-style sessions, embedded reverse engineering, physical security talks, automotive talks, and critical infrastructure panels [record_id:1855] [record_id:1856] [record_id:1858] [record_id:1865] [record_id:1867] [record_id:1868] [record_id:1869] [record_id:1871] [record_id:1879] [record_id:1882] [record_id:1884] [record_id:1888] [record_id:2147] [record_id:2152] [record_id:2171] [record_id:2172] [record_id:2173]. These talks often emphasize hands-on reverse engineering, RF tooling, accessible hardware hacking, or practical red-team operations.

A smaller but technically important CAMLIS cluster focuses on machine learning and autonomous defense for cyber-physical systems. These records differ from the conference-village abstracts because they describe modeling methods and experimental results: cooperative multi-agent decision-making for OT defense in vehicle platooning, multi-agent reinforcement learning for maritime OT, and sticky HDP-HMM temporal attack detection across avionics and consumer robotics [record_id:136] [record_id:163] [record_id:186].

BSidesLV and BSidesSF records add a resilience and public-interest perspective. They focus on lifeline sectors, community preparedness, water, healthcare, emergency services, food systems, EOL equipment, energy resilience, cyber-informed engineering, critical infrastructure mapping, and threat intelligence for nuclear reactor control systems [record_id:2413] [record_id:2427] [record_id:2434] [record_id:2435] [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437] [record_id:2451] [record_id:2487] [record_id:2504] [record_id:2534] [record_id:2544] [record_id:2564]. BSidesSF contributes OT protocol-gateway discovery and decentralized communications comparisons [record_id:2279] [record_id:2303].

The research area is therefore not a single subfield but a set of overlapping domains: industrial protocols, embedded firmware, RF systems, cyber-physical safety, supply chain security, critical infrastructure governance, and detection/response. The connective tissue is that these systems mediate physical operations, operate under constraints unlike enterprise IT, and often inherit insecure assumptions from legacy engineering, opaque vendor ecosystems, or consumer-grade deployment models.

## Major Themes And Trends

### 1. OT/IT convergence is expanding the attack surface

Many records explicitly or implicitly reject the idea that OT environments remain isolated. OPC UA is described as a bridge “within and between OT networks,” between IT and OT, and to the cloud, which motivates cryptographic authentication bypass research [record_id:7] [record_id:2140]. Rob King’s protocol-gateway work frames OT/IT convergence as a 15–20 year shift toward commodity TCP/IP and protocol translation, creating discovery and protection problems for assets behind gateways [record_id:2104] [record_id:2303]. The OT DMZ talk emphasizes that enterprise footholds, jump hosts, proxy services, Citrix gateways, RDP relays, and misconfigured remote access can become paths into control networks [record_id:2112].

This trend also appears in consumer and infrastructure IoT. Cellular IoT devices are framed as trusted hardware with backend network access that can be modified to control cellular modules, inject communications, and attack cloud or private backend infrastructure [record_id:44]. Solar microgrids are described as depending on unauthenticated cloud APIs, unsigned firmware, hardcoded root credentials, plaintext MODBUS, and rebadged devices sharing cloud infrastructure [record_id:1978]. Smart buses connect passenger Wi-Fi, vehicular routers, private bus networks, ADAS/APTS communications, backend APIs, passenger data, and LED displays into a single attack narrative [record_id:2121]. Automotive dealer platforms show that business APIs can become an unexpected route to vehicle remote control and ownership reassignment [record_id:2036] [record_id:2099].

The evidence is strong that researchers see convergence as a systemic problem, not a one-off architecture mistake. Multiple independent domains—industrial automation, solar, transit, vehicles, cameras, routers, and maritime communications—show the same pattern: convenience integrations create trust bridges that defenders may not inventory or monitor.

### 2. Embedded device weaknesses persist across generations and vendors

A recurring concern is that embedded ecosystems amplify vulnerabilities because many products share chipset SDKs, OEM firmware, cloud services, update mechanisms, and rebadged hardware. The “immortality of SDK bugs” record explicitly argues that once vulnerable chipset SDK code is bundled into many vendors’ product lines, patching becomes a perpetual whack-a-mole problem [record_id:2073]. ISP modems are described as affected by more than 35 severe flaws rooted in outdated IoT SDKs, with manufacturers and ISPs allegedly refusing to address them [record_id:1906]. Webcams that “secretly run Linux” are used to show how silicon vendors and OEMs can introduce vulnerabilities upstream that silently ship in tens of thousands of devices [record_id:1925].

Supply chain opacity is a parallel theme. Hardware bill of materials work argues that chips inside IoT devices carry provenance and integrity risks that conventional BOMs do not expose [record_id:2034]. Solar supply-chain research highlights rebadged devices, cloned OEMs, shared cloud infrastructure, and vendor-enabled kill switches [record_id:1978]. Returned IoT device research asks whether retailers properly reset firmware before resale and presents firmware persistence as a potential route to botnets, implants, surveillance, or data exfiltration [record_id:2131]. Notably, record [record_id:2132] has a title about building cybersecurity, but its raw text repeats the returned-IoT firmware persistence scenario; downstream agents should treat the raw text as evidence and the title as potentially inconsistent.

These records collectively suggest that vulnerability management in IoT is not just a CVE-patching problem. It involves provenance, firmware update trust, return logistics, OEM accountability, SDK inheritance, and the difficulty of identifying all affected downstream devices.

### 3. Cyber-physical impact is the central risk differentiator

The corpus repeatedly emphasizes physical or safety consequences. EV charger work includes destructive testing of seven products, bypassing safety mechanisms, and showing destruction, collateral damage, and latent hazards [record_id:32]. EV charging communications research targets QCA 7000 HomePlug modems used by CCS and NACS and claims persistent denial of service, hijacking of charging communications, and firmware code execution [record_id:1931] [record_id:2145]. Xiaomi scooter research describes BMS remote code execution, battery safety threshold manipulation, ransomware, tracking, denial of service, and battery damage via undervolting [record_id:43]. Smart farming automation research reports vulnerabilities enabling global tractor tracking, manipulation, and safety compromise [record_id:31].

Transportation and mobility are strongly represented. Automotive processor glitching targets ECU-class devices and readout protection [record_id:83]. A head-unit talk describes a crafted PNG backdoor against a novel automotive RTOS [record_id:2037]. TPMS research shows low-cost capture, tracking, spoofing, and custom message generation against tire-pressure systems lacking encryption/authentication [record_id:2048]. Odometer manipulation research reverse engineers a CAN-message rewriting device and discusses detection after removal [record_id:2051]. OBD-II emissions testing work considers convincing scanners that “everything is fine” [record_id:2091]. Autonomous vehicle safety testing appears in an adversarial teleoperation fuzzer that generates malicious trajectories, conflicting signals, and environmental perturbations to test rejection of unsafe commands [record_id:55].

Maritime and aviation records show similar kinetic concerns. Marine autopilot research describes unsigned firmware delivered through trusted update channels, persistent code execution, and CAN injection on NMEA 2000 networks [record_id:2083]. Maritime red-team accounts describe physical access to communication and engine-control rooms and manipulation or disabling of navigation and communications [record_id:2033]. Air traffic control voice cloning discusses fraudulent AM radio communications at runway crossings and low-visibility conditions [record_id:2047]. Elevator security, safes, hotel locks, access control, and RFID cloning extend the topic into cyber-physical access and life-safety systems [record_id:1971] [record_id:1983] [record_id:1999] [record_id:2002] [record_id:2125].

### 4. Cryptography is often present, but trust and implementation fail

Several records are specifically about cryptographic design or implementation failures. OPC UA is notable because the protocol’s built-in authentication and transport security can lead operators to skip VPNs, yet the research claims two protocol flaws turned into practical authentication bypasses across implementations and configurations, involving signing oracles, padding oracles, and timing side-channel amplification [record_id:7] [record_id:2140]. TETRA E2EE research claims seven vulnerabilities, including critical issues, weakened key variants, voice injection, SDS replay, key extraction, covert implants, and packet injection against SCADA networks [record_id:60]. goTenna research argues that one unauthenticated RF packet can overwrite public keys, enabling man-in-the-middle attacks despite AES-256 encryption [record_id:1952].

The same theme appears at lower levels. Post-quantum signatures may resist quantum cryptanalysis but still be bypassed through voltage fault injection against verification logic on real microcontrollers [record_id:65]. ReVault targets a secure embedded chip trusted with passwords, key material, and biometrics, demonstrating compromise, secret extraction, persistence, and even “hack Windows back” behavior [record_id:48] [record_id:1942]. NVIDIA’s billion-core RISC-V architecture work is defensive rather than exploit-focused, but it demonstrates the scale and complexity of building secure boot, attestation, separation kernels, ISA extensions, and future memory-safety controls into proprietary embedded ecosystems [record_id:67] [record_id:1944].

A recurring lesson is that cryptography alone is insufficient. Key lifecycle, firmware validation, update-channel authentication, side-channel resilience, implementation correctness, and surrounding protocol logic matter as much as algorithm selection.

### 5. RF, wireless, and decentralized communications are active research terrain

RF-focused records cover both vulnerability research and tooling. Meshtastic appears in multiple contexts: protocol dissection from LoRa chirps through Wireshark, crafting valid frames, and understanding Protobuf and AES-protected payloads [record_id:1884]; command-and-control over Meshtastic for low-bandwidth encrypted shell access to remote Linux hosts [record_id:1895]; a DEF CON Ham Radio Village preview of Meshtastic offerings [record_id:2149]; and a BSidesSF comparison of APRS and Meshtastic security models, vulnerabilities, and real-world applications [record_id:2279]. SDR appears as a method and training platform in RF CTF infrastructure [record_id:1888].

Other RF records cover practical reconnaissance and spoofing. Flipper Zero is presented as an opportunistic tool for radio, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and local mission constraints [record_id:1885]. Weather Radio and SAME alerting are analyzed as public alerting systems whose open broadcasts and lack of origin verification can allow generated alert signals [record_id:1894]. goTenna, TPMS, in-flight Wi-Fi evil twins, 802.11ah repeaters, stored-value contactless cards, BLE appliances, and Bluetooth-controlled blenders show the diversity of short-range and low-power attack surfaces [record_id:1891] [record_id:1892] [record_id:1890] [record_id:1952] [record_id:2048] [record_id:2553].

### 6. Maritime and space are emerging as distinct OT subdomains

Maritime security is one of the densest clusters. Records discuss ship red teaming, maritime threat dynamics, NMEA2000 fingerprinting, VSAT firmware and ACU vulnerabilities, resilient SDN-SDR maritime communications, marine engines, naval MOSAICS deployment, maritime OSINT, virtual ship environments, maritime incident response, ship-to-shore crane missions, software failures in marine systems, and lessons from the Titan submersible [record_id:1946] [record_id:2033] [record_id:2035] [record_id:2039] [record_id:2045] [record_id:2049] [record_id:2057] [record_id:2065] [record_id:2068] [record_id:2074] [record_id:2075] [record_id:2083] [record_id:2092] [record_id:2108] [record_id:2133]. The records emphasize that maritime systems combine legacy OT, satellite communications, safety-critical navigation, physical access challenges, remote environments, and high economic stakes.

Space security appears in vulnerability research, red teaming, purple teaming, and detection. One Black Hat/DEF CON pair describes vulnerabilities across ground and onboard spacecraft software, including PoC exploits with potential unauthorized control [record_id:16] [record_id:2103]. Space red teaming examines satellites, ground stations, and supporting infrastructure as a new offensive discipline [record_id:2046]. Moonlight Defender describes realistic space-cyber exercises involving satellites, cyber ranges, virtual satellite simulators, ICS/OT, and enterprise environments [record_id:2071]. SPARTA-based detection work aims to turn offensive exploitation of space systems into indicators of behavior for spacecraft IDS [record_id:2105]. Satellite DDoS work discusses LEO constellations, predictable orbital patterns, limited bandwidth, and targeted multi-zone disruptions [record_id:2013].

### 7. Critical infrastructure resilience and governance are prominent, especially for lifeline sectors

The policy and resilience records focus less on exploit primitives and more on societal consequences. CISA’s keynote frames critical infrastructure defense as national security and emphasizes operational collaboration [record_id:107]. BSidesLV’s I Am The Cavalry-style track highlights water, power, healthcare, EMS, food, public safety, and community preparedness. Volt Typhoon and other APTs are cited as pre-positioning or targeting water, power, telecommunications, and lifeline services [record_id:2413] [record_id:2427]. Healthcare records focus on ransomware, legacy medical devices, unsegmented networks, third-party dependencies, emergency care delays, and policy challenges [record_id:2434]. EOL equipment is framed as producing “forever-day” vulnerabilities in sectors including healthcare, manufacturing, energy, and transportation [record_id:2435].

Water-sector cyber-informed engineering workshops emphasize designing out cyber risk before a system is placed into operation [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. Food-sector risk is tied to logistics, processing, refrigeration, and supply-chain coordination with limited cybersecurity maturity [record_id:2451]. Critical infrastructure mapping records argue that disconnects between federal definitions and local realities leave cities under-resourced and unprepared [record_id:2487]. Energy resilience is tied to AI infrastructure and transformer dependencies [record_id:2504]. WarGames 2027 and “Time is Running Out” focus on near-term measures for destructive cyberattacks on lifeline functions and household/community resilience [record_id:2534] [record_id:2564]. A nuclear threat-intel record claims discovery of an attack aimed at backdooring or incapacitating nuclear reactor control systems [record_id:2544].

### 8. Detection and autonomous defense are increasingly tailored to OT constraints

The defensive records emphasize that OT cannot simply inherit enterprise controls. CAMLIS work states that OT defensive actions are less mature due to brittle legacy infrastructure, design-time engineering assumptions, and lack of full-scale modern controls [record_id:163]. Cooperative decision-making and MARL work explore autonomous cyber-defense frameworks for vehicle platooning and maritime integrated platform management systems [record_id:136] [record_id:163]. Temporal multimodal attack detection with sticky HDP-HMM is presented as useful when architecture details are unavailable and system behavior is heterogeneous [record_id:186].

DEF CON defensive records include OT SOC and incident response practices [record_id:2079], deception in ICS using MITRE Engage and an APT44 case study [record_id:2095], context-aware CAN anomaly detection without decoding [record_id:2102], NMEA2000 deterministic fingerprints for maritime sensors [record_id:2049], SPARTA-derived indicators of behavior for spacecraft IDS [record_id:2105], MOSAICS monitoring and behavioral threat detection for naval mission systems [record_id:2075], and AI-generated neural ASR rules from OT/IT suspicious behavior data [record_id:2113]. Log contextualization for IoT environments proposes lightweight correlation logic rather than heavy ML or LLM inference [record_id:1935]. These records collectively show a move toward behavior, topology, timing, and physical context rather than signature-only detection.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The corpus includes a wide range of offensive, defensive, and engineering methods.

Reverse engineering is foundational. Researchers describe firmware extraction, Ghidra disassembly, JTAG/UART exploration, binary analysis, SVD-based register mapping, HAL recovery, RTOS task identification, firmware repackaging, and CAN subroutine analysis [record_id:1871] [record_id:1981] [record_id:2008] [record_id:2078] [record_id:2081] [record_id:2083]. Firmware rehosting is presented as a scalable approach for embedded Linux analysis by modifying kernels and QEMU machines while leaving userspace firmware largely untouched [record_id:1981]. Firmware decryption work examines vendor encryption techniques and how analysts can crack them with or without hardware [record_id:2078]. Bare-metal reverse engineering methods focus on ARM Cortex, Ghidra, BSP/HAL/RTOS layers, SVD loader, custom type recovery, function matching, and BSim [record_id:2081].

Fuzzing and automated testing appear in several domains. Autonomous vehicle teleoperation commands are tested with an adversarial scenario fuzzer that iteratively generates malicious trajectories, conflicting guidance, and environmental perturbations [record_id:55]. 5G baseband research uses dependency-aware, stateful fuzzing on emulated firmware with symbolic analysis to uncover state variables and reach deep NAS execution paths, yielding seven previously unknown vulnerabilities and multiple CVEs [record_id:73]. These methods suggest that embedded fuzzing is moving toward stateful, emulation-based workflows rather than shallow input mutation.

Hardware attack methods include voltage fault injection against PQC signature verification [record_id:65], glitching automotive lockstep processors to bypass readout protection [record_id:83], laser/light-based hardware tooling for fault injection, hardware malware detection, supply-chain chip replacement detection, and logic-state imaging [record_id:2479], and open-source quantum sensor construction for magnetometry and chip-behavior inference [record_id:1973]. RFID badge cloning and physical access-control tooling include Flipper Zero, iCopy-X, gooseneck readers, wall implants, clipboard cloning devices, and HID iClass downgrade/legacy attacks [record_id:1971].

RF methods include SDR, GNU Radio, Python frame crafting, inspectrum, Wireshark decoding, LoRa physical-layer analysis, 802.11ah hardware/firmware work, APRS/Meshtastic comparisons, and RF CTF challenge transmission using challengectl [record_id:1884] [record_id:1888] [record_id:1890] [record_id:2279]. These records show a strong culture of reproducible, low-cost RF workflows.

Protocol and network methods include CIP message forwarding, DNP3 address discovery, MODBUS replay analysis, NMEA2000 fingerprinting, HomePlug modem reverse engineering, OT DMZ pivoting, protocol-gateway device discovery, and Common Industrial Protocol exploitation introductions [record_id:1978] [record_id:2049] [record_id:2104] [record_id:2112] [record_id:2116] [record_id:2145]. Several records emphasize that visibility across gateways and behind NAT-like OT racks remains an unresolved operational challenge [record_id:2104] [record_id:2303].

Defensive and governance approaches include cyber-informed engineering for the water sector [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437], CARVER vulnerability analysis for voting systems [record_id:1858], critical infrastructure mapping for city-level preparedness [record_id:2487], unified incident response for cascading failures across water/power/healthcare/EMS [record_id:2413], OT SOC and incident response models [record_id:2079], MOSAICS for naval OT [record_id:2075], SPARTA-based space indicators of behavior [record_id:2105], deception in depth for ICS [record_id:2095], OSINT for maritime cyber-physical risk [record_id:2074], and practical short-term resilience actions for households and communities [record_id:2564].

AI appears both as an attack surface and an engineering aid. Promptware records describe Gemini agent hijacking via calendar invitations, with claimed physical outcomes through home appliances and inter-device lateral movement [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. Physical AI governance work argues that vision-language-action robotics require safety-first system-level architecture and governance beyond model accuracy [record_id:2348]. HoloConnect AI discusses offline embedded holographic agents for medical contexts, sandboxed AI logic, and voice-spoofing defense [record_id:2076]. AI-assisted embedded development is treated cautiously: one record says AI coding tools failed on STM32 firmware until the human manually set up the project and constrained edits to small comment-delimited insertions [record_id:2206], while another uses AI assistance to make a conventional weather station “smart” through Home Assistant and generated C++ [record_id:2123].

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

Several records stand out because they name specific protocols, products, vulnerabilities, or empirical outcomes.

The OPC UA cryptographic attack records are among the most technically significant because they challenge a core trust assumption in industrial automation: that protocol-native security may remove the need for VPNs across trust zones. The claims include two protocol flaws, practical authentication bypasses across implementations/configurations, signing oracles, padding oracles, and timing side-channel amplification [record_id:7] [record_id:2140].

The TETRA E2EE analysis is notable because TETRA is used by police, military, industrial environments, utilities, pipelines, transportation, water, and SCADA telecontrol. The record claims reverse engineering of a Sepura radio’s proprietary E2EE solution, seven vulnerabilities, critical issues, voice injection, SDS replay, intentionally weakened 56-bit effective keys, key extraction, covert implanting, and SCADA packet injection demonstrations [record_id:60].

EV charging is strongly represented by two complementary angles: safety/destructive EVSE testing [record_id:32] and protocol/modem exploitation of QCA 7000 HomePlug in CCS/NACS deployments [record_id:1931] [record_id:2145]. ISO 15118 is treated as a policy/standards case study where a cybersecurity-oriented standard can mitigate some risks while shifting or creating others [record_id:42].

The Axis camera work is representative of IoT devices as internal-network beachheads. It focuses on Axis.Remoting, pre-auth RCE, passive data exfiltration, and the possibility that surveillance infrastructure used by government agencies, schools, medical facilities, and Fortune 500 firms could become an attacker runway into internal networks [record_id:68] [record_id:2088] [record_id:2146]. Dahua camera RCE work provides a related camera-security thread, especially for infrastructure-heavy environments such as retail, banking, and traffic [record_id:2050].

The Xiaomi e-scooter record is notable for tying reverse engineering, firmware modification, ransomware tooling, battery safety threshold manipulation, privacy, and physical degradation into one compact consumer IoT case study [record_id:43]. The smart farming tractor record similarly shows that agricultural automation inherits OT-like legacy and safety issues while introducing global tracking and remote manipulation risks [record_id:31].

Maritime records are particularly rich. NMEA2000 fingerprinting contributes a dataset-oriented defensive baseline for maritime CAN-like networks [record_id:2049]. VSAT research gives concrete CVE ranges for ACU web vulnerabilities and discusses firmware collection, rehosting, and testbed validation [record_id:2039]. Marine autopilot work provides a clear firmware-trust failure: unsigned updates accepted through chart plotters over NMEA2000 leading to persistent code execution and CAN injection [record_id:2083]. Shipboard red-team operations, ship-to-shore crane missions, and maritime incident response add operational realism [record_id:2033] [record_id:2065] [record_id:2133].

Space security records matter because they treat spacecraft as critical infrastructure with both onboard and ground-segment attack surfaces. The vulnerability briefing claims PoC exploits with potential unauthorized spacecraft control [record_id:16] [record_id:2103]. Moonlight Defender and SPARTA records are important because they move beyond “space hacking is possible” into exercises, cyber ranges, indicators of behavior, and IDS design [record_id:2071] [record_id:2105].

On the defense side, the CAMLIS MARL and Bayesian detection records are among the more research-methodologically explicit. They report comparative training outcomes for MAPPO versus IPPO, robustness under reduced alert success probabilities, and interpretable attack detection across avionics and consumer robot CPS logs [record_id:163] [record_id:186]. Co-Decyber adds cooperative decision-making in a GPS-spoofed vehicle-platooning scenario [record_id:136].

Voting-system records are notable as cyber-physical civic infrastructure. They cover ballot marking device manipulation without malware, physical security weaknesses in locks and seals, CARVER analysis, regulatory failures, legal exemptions for security research, insider threats involving access to voting equipment/software, and historical TTBR/EVEREST lessons [record_id:1855] [record_id:1856] [record_id:1858] [record_id:1859] [record_id:1865] [record_id:1867] [record_id:1868] [record_id:1869]. These records are more governance-heavy than exploit-deep, but they support a theme that security claims based on procedures, obscurity, or assumed physical controls require scrutiny.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The records are mostly abstracts, not full papers or transcripts. Many claims are plausible and specific, but the evidence available here often lacks exploit details, measurement methodology, affected-version scope, patch status, reproduction artifacts, sample sizes, or independent validation. For example, several records claim severe vulnerabilities or widespread impact—millions of NAS devices [record_id:1850], billions of AirPlay-enabled devices [record_id:80], millions of ISP modems [record_id:1906], global vehicular-router exposure [record_id:2121]—but the raw abstracts do not provide enough data to independently verify scale.

The corpus has a strong 2025 U.S. conference bias. DEF CON and Black Hat dominate, which means the material favors novel attacks, demonstrations, villages, and conference narratives. Operational defender case studies are present but fewer, and long-term remediation outcomes are rarely documented. Many governance records identify systemic risks in water, healthcare, food, energy, and emergency services, but they often do not provide technical architectures or measurable before/after improvements [record_id:2427] [record_id:2434] [record_id:2451] [record_id:2534].

There is also a gap between vulnerability discovery and deployable safety engineering. EV, scooter, automotive, maritime, and aviation records show plausible physical consequences, but the abstracts rarely discuss hazard analysis, formal safety cases, fail-safe design, or certification pathways in detail. Cyber-informed engineering workshops address this gap conceptually for water systems [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437], and Physical AI governance calls for safety-first architectures [record_id:2348], but the corpus would benefit from more concrete examples of secure-by-design retrofits in deployed OT.

Patch and disclosure outcomes are uneven. Some records mention CVEs or patches, such as goTenna Pro 2.0.3 and CVE-2024-47130 [record_id:1952], VSAT CVEs [record_id:2039], Draytek CVEs [record_id:2120], and 5G baseband CVEs [record_id:73]. Others discuss vendor inertia, ignored reports, denials, or delayed CNA/CERT processes [record_id:1906] [record_id:2111]. Downstream research should ask which vulnerabilities were patched, which remain exploitable, and how asset owners can detect affected devices.

AI-related OT/IoT records raise important but underdeveloped questions. Promptware records claim physical outcomes via home appliances [record_id:53] [record_id:1965], embedded AI records discuss offline holographic agents in medical settings [record_id:2076], physical AI governance addresses embodied risks [record_id:2348], and AI coding records show both promise and fragility for embedded development [record_id:2123] [record_id:2206].
