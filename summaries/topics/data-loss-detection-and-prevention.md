# Topic: Data loss detection and prevention

## Executive Summary

The records collectively portray data loss detection and prevention as a broad, fast-moving area that now spans traditional DLP, cloud and network egress controls, CI/CD secret exposure, insider-risk analytics, AI-agent containment, ransomware-leak investigation, and privacy footprint reduction. A recurring message is that sensitive data loss is no longer limited to obvious outbound file transfers: it can occur through DNS tunneling, compromised build pipelines, exposed process metadata on shared Linux systems, over-permissive cloud identity paths, malicious or compromised AI/coding agents, public package ecosystems, ransomware dumps, and ordinary developer mistakes.

Several records argue that traditional DLP and signature-based controls are insufficient against modern exfiltration. DNS exfiltration defenses based on passive anomaly detection or static filtering are described as too slow and unable to stop packets before they leave the endpoint [record_id:35]. A BSidesSF talk claims “data splicing” can bypass major enterprise DLP vendors by exploiting architectural flaws in endpoint and proxy-based systems [record_id:2272]. The CI/CD supply-chain record similarly emphasizes that trusted tooling can be weaponized to dump credentials into logs without needing a conventional exfiltration channel [record_id:49].

The strongest technical themes are: moving enforcement closer to where data leaves or is accessed; using machine learning and LLMs for classification, anomaly detection, secret detection, and PII extraction; reducing the number of secrets that can be leaked; and containing high-risk automation systems such as AI agents. The evidence base is mostly conference abstracts and talk descriptions, so it is strong for identifying research directions and practitioner concerns, but thin on empirical validation except where talks provide concrete claims, such as Google’s FACADE scanning billions of events daily over seven years with very low false-positive rates [record_id:87], or a Black Hat briefing describing kernel-level DNS exfiltration prevention with eBPF [record_id:35].

## Research Landscape

The dataset is dominated by 2025–2026 conference talks from Black Hat, DEF CON, BSidesSF, BSidesLV, Prompt||GTFO, CAMLIS, and [un]prompted. These records are not product documentation or academic papers; they are primarily talk abstracts, workshop summaries, or short event descriptions. That means they are good indicators of what practitioners and researchers are presenting publicly, but they rarely include full methodology, experimental setup, benchmark data, or reproducibility details.

The research area is diverse. Some records are clearly and centrally about DLP or exfiltration prevention. Examples include kernel-enforced DNS exfiltration prevention [record_id:35], a claimed bypass technique against enterprise DLP [record_id:2272], secret scanning at scale [record_id:2408], secret leakage in shared Linux environments [record_id:2090], and PII extraction from ransomware leaks [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. Others are adjacent but relevant because they address sensitive data exposure through supply-chain compromise, cloud identity misconfiguration, AI-agent behavior, or privacy footprint management [record_id:49], [record_id:1969], [record_id:2345], [record_id:2358], [record_id:2369], [record_id:2495].

The records also show a notable convergence between DLP and AI. Several talks use ML or LLMs defensively: contrastive learning for insider threat detection [record_id:87], reinforcement learning for exfiltration path analysis [record_id:162], LLM-based log analysis and data classification [record_id:2190], small language models for secret detection in code [record_id:2251], Claude Artifacts for compliance taxonomies and DLP entities [record_id:2239], and OCR-plus-LLM processing of ransomware leaks [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. Other records focus on AI as a new source of data-loss risk, particularly agentic systems that can read files, invoke tools, execute commands, and exfiltrate data under prompt-injection or “promptware” conditions [record_id:2345], [record_id:2358], [record_id:2369].

Overall, the landscape is less about one canonical DLP product category and more about a set of recurring security problems: where sensitive data lives, how it moves, how attackers or insiders gain access to it, how defenders classify it, and how organizations can prevent or investigate its exposure.

## Major Themes And Trends

### 1. Traditional DLP is portrayed as necessary but structurally incomplete

Multiple records criticize conventional DLP approaches. The DNS exfiltration briefing argues that traditional defenses rely on timing and volume-based passive anomaly detection, signature filtering, or DPI through proxies and middleware, and that these approaches suffer from delayed detection, longer dwell time, and data loss before threat removal [record_id:35]. The data-splicing talk is even more direct, claiming a technique “capable of bypassing all major DLP vendors listed by Gartner” and promising to dissect “architectural flaws in endpoint and proxy-based DLP” [record_id:2272].

The emerging trend is toward enforcement that is earlier, more contextual, and harder to bypass. Record 35 proposes moving DNS exfiltration security into the Linux kernel using eBPF and in-kernel DNS parsing so that “no exfiltrated packets ever leave the endpoint” [record_id:35]. AI-agent containment talks similarly frame exfiltration as something that must be prevented through deterministic interceptors, egress control, human confirmation flows, or policy enforcement rather than relying only on model behavior or soft guardrails [record_id:2345], [record_id:2358].

### 2. Secrets exposure is a central form of data loss

Secrets appear across the records as one of the most common and damaging data-loss categories. The tj-actions supply-chain breach record describes a GitHub Action used by more than 23,000 repositories that was weaponized to exfiltrate secrets; the compromised action dumped CI/CD credentials from memory and exposed them in build logs [record_id:49]. The Linux shared-environment talk describes database passwords, API tokens, internal URLs, and other secrets exposed to unprivileged users through ordinary process inspection mechanisms and insecure scripting practices [record_id:2090]. The BSidesLV “secret scanning at scale” talk frames hardcoded API keys, tokens, and credentials as leaked “at an alarming rate” and explores low-cost scanning of VS Code extensions and other public sources [record_id:2408].

A related trend is secret elimination rather than secret detection alone. The BSidesLV talk on “Authenticating With No Secrets” argues for deleting static, manually managed secrets where possible and using AWS IRSA and Azure Workload ID to allow Kubernetes clusters in different clouds to authenticate without manually managed secrets [record_id:2396]. This shifts DLP from “find the leaked token” to “reduce or remove the token inventory that could be leaked.”

### 3. CI/CD, developer ecosystems, and build logs are high-risk data-loss surfaces

The tj-actions breach is the clearest example of CI/CD environments as exfiltration targets. The record describes a popular GitHub Action being compromised through a chained supply-chain attack involving a prior compromise of the reviewdog GitHub Action, after which attackers redirected all tags to a malicious commit [record_id:49]. The action dumped credentials from memory and exposed them in build logs, creating a data-loss event without needing a separate outbound channel beyond the build platform’s normal behavior [record_id:49].

This theme connects with secret detection in code [record_id:2251], public-source secret scanning [record_id:2408], and secret-free authentication architectures [record_id:2396]. Together, the records suggest that developer workflows are both sources of sensitive data leakage and promising places for preventive controls: scanning code and package ecosystems, monitoring build network behavior, reducing static credentials, and rotating or invalidating exposed secrets.

### 4. Insider risk is framed as a large-scale anomaly-detection problem

The FACADE record is the major insider-risk record. It presents Google’s internal AI system for detecting malicious insiders, described as scanning billions of events daily over seven years across logs such as document accesses, SQL queries, and HTTP/RPC requests [record_id:87]. The system uses self-supervised learning, context around each action, a multi-action-type model, contrastive learning based only on benign data, and clustering to improve robustness [record_id:87]. The claimed false-positive rate is below 0.01%, and for single rogue actions such as illegitimate access to a sensitive document, as low as 0.0003% [record_id:87].

This record suggests that insider data risk is not only about detecting bulk downloads. It includes suspicious single actions, such as illegitimate access to sensitive documents, and requires context-aware modeling across heterogeneous enterprise activity [record_id:87]. Compared with other records, this one offers unusually specific operational and performance claims, though the abstract does not provide independent validation.

### 5. Network and cloud exfiltration increasingly bypass simple perimeter assumptions

Several records focus on exfiltration paths that survive common network controls. DNS is highlighted as a foundational protocol that must often remain open on firewalls, making it attractive for C2 channels, DNS tunneling, and exfiltration [record_id:35]. The CAMLIS record explores reinforcement learning to model exfiltration paths with payload and protocol considerations, including adversaries stealing data over long periods using system-native ports or protocols to avoid detection [record_id:162].

Cloud identity and internal access controls create another class of exposure. The Google Cloud IAP talk asks what happens when IAP “quietly swings open,” describing subtle misconfigurations that can lead to data exposure even with no public IPs, strict VPC Service Controls, and hardened perimeters [record_id:2495]. It claims a new IAP vulnerability enabling data exfiltration that bypasses traditional network controls without sending traffic to the public internet [record_id:2495]. This reinforces a broader shift: defenders need to understand identity-mediated access paths and internal trust boundaries, not just public ingress/egress.

### 6. AI and LLMs are both defensive tools and new exfiltration risks

The records present AI in two opposing roles. As a defensive capability, LLMs and ML are used for data classification, DLP entity generation, log analysis, secret detection, PII extraction, and anomaly detection. Prompt||GTFO includes a demonstration of LLMs generating their own prompts to improve log analysis, data classification for DLP/sensitive data detection, and anomaly detection with incomplete API request logs [record_id:2190]. Another Prompt||GTFO record describes a Claude Artifacts tool that generates regulatory compliance taxonomies, named entities for DLP tools, business intents, and data classifications tied to regulation sections [record_id:2239]. BSidesSF includes fine-tuning small language models for secret detection in code with low enough inference time to run on a laptop [record_id:2251]. BSidesLV includes OCR and LLMs to extract meaningful PII from ransomware leak dumps without exposing PII directly [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471].

As a risk, agentic AI introduces new ways for data to leave systems. A talk on Cedar policy language proposes a reference monitor using Rust hooks and Cedar policies to intercept shell commands, file reads, and other actions, with a live demo forbidding exfiltration and destructive behavior in coding agents such as Cursor, Claude Code, and GitHub Copilot CLI [record_id:2345]. Stripe’s “Breaking the Lethal Trifecta” talk argues there is no deterministic defense against prompt injection and advocates containment: controlled egress, human confirmation flows, and CI-time guardrail enforcement using tool annotations [record_id:2358]. Johann Rehberger’s “Your Agent Works for Me Now” describes “promptware” that can enable persistence, lateral movement, promptware-powered C2, and data exfiltration across agentic systems [record_id:2369].

### 7. Post-breach data-loss investigation is becoming a data-processing and ethics problem

The two “Indexing the Chaos” records focus on ransomware leaks, where attackers exfiltrate and publish terabytes of internal documents [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. These records are less about preventing exfiltration and more about investigating exposed data responsibly. The HIBR toolchain/public search engine uses crawling, OCR, and LLMs to find PII in scanned passports, HR forms, insurance records, contracts, PDFs, and image scans [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. Both records emphasize not exposing PII, ethical constraints, legal concerns such as GDPR, and the risk of building a tool that becomes a “privacy nightmare” [record_id:2470].

This theme broadens DLP into breach impact analysis: once data is leaked, organizations and affected individuals need ways to understand what was exposed without amplifying the harm.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

One major approach is endpoint or kernel-level enforcement. The Black Hat DNS exfiltration record describes an eBPF-driven Linux kernel framework that parses DNS in the kernel, applies threat intelligence across the kernel network stack and mandatory access control layer, and uses a userspace deep-learning model trained on DNS payload obfuscation techniques [record_id:35]. It also describes dynamic domain blacklisting, in-kernel network policy creation, event streaming, and real-time termination of malicious C2 implant processes [record_id:35]. This is a prevention-first model rather than a detect-and-respond-after-loss model.

Another approach is behavior and anomaly detection in enterprise logs. FACADE uses self-supervised ML and contrastive learning on benign corporate logs covering document accesses, SQL queries, and HTTP/RPC requests [record_id:87]. The LLM log-analysis record suggests using LLMs to generate prompts for log analysis, split DLP and anomaly detection into separate prompts, use large context windows such as Google Gemini’s, and optimize cost through batching and prompt caching on platforms such as AWS Bedrock [record_id:2190]. The tj-actions record emphasizes detection logic based on deviations from established normal network activity in CI/CD pipeline runs, specifically noting that an anomalous outbound connection to gist.githubusercontent.com triggered discovery of the breach [record_id:49].

Secret detection and secret reduction are addressed through several methods. Small language models are proposed for fine-tuned secret detection in code, with the goal of low inference time and local laptop execution [record_id:2251]. Low-cost distributed scanning using Kubernetes, cloud credits, and automation is proposed for scanning VS Code extensions and public sources for hardcoded keys, tokens, and credentials [record_id:2408]. Secret-free authentication is proposed through AWS IRSA and Azure Workload ID, demonstrated with Kubernetes clusters authenticating across AWS and Azure without manually managed secrets, with Terraform source code offered for reproducibility [record_id:2396].

For cloud and identity-mediated exfiltration, the records emphasize trust-boundary analysis and detection strategies. The IAP talk focuses on over-permissive IAM bindings, misplaced trust in user-supplied headers, overlooked endpoints, and the internal workings of Google Cloud IAP [record_id:2495]. It suggests that environments with no public IPs and hardened perimeters can still leak data if identity-aware access layers are misconfigured or vulnerable [record_id:2495].

For AI agents, the records discuss deterministic control layers and containment architectures. The Cedar policy talk proposes Rust hooks and Cedar policies as a reference monitor for intercepting shell commands, file reads, and other agent actions [record_id:2345]. Stripe’s containment approach includes controlled egress, human confirmation flows, and CI-time enforcement through tool annotations [record_id:2358]. Rehberger’s promptware talk provides the adversarial counterpart, describing delayed tool invocation, intent activation, persistence, lateral movement, C2, and data exfiltration against agentic systems [record_id:2369].

For post-exfiltration investigation, HIBR uses crawlers, OCR, and LLMs to process ransomware leak dumps while attempting to identify PII without displaying it [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. The Shai-Hulud post-mortem similarly describes moving from simple scrapers to multi-agent triage engines that parallelize victimology and automate secret-impact analysis during internet-scale incidents where massive amounts of victim data were leaked onto GitHub [record_id:2332].

Finally, the CAMLIS record introduces reinforcement learning for exfiltration path analysis. It extends prior RL work by incorporating protocol and payload into a Markov decision process to emulate network-based exfiltration more realistically, including payload size over time and protocol choice [record_id:162]. This is more of an adversary-behavior modeling approach than a deployed DLP control.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The kernel-enforced DNS exfiltration briefing is one of the most directly relevant records because it focuses on preventing data from leaving endpoints over DNS, a protocol frequently left open by firewalls [record_id:35]. It is notable for proposing in-kernel DNS parsing, eBPF enforcement, userspace deep learning for obfuscation-aware detection, and process-level implant termination [record_id:35]. Its evidence is a talk abstract with strong technical claims; downstream researchers should look for the full talk, code, benchmarks, or independent evaluation.

The tj-actions supply-chain breach record is important because it provides a concrete incident narrative involving secrets exfiltration in CI/CD [record_id:49]. It gives a timeline, names the compromised GitHub Action, describes the scale of use, explains how credentials were dumped from memory into logs, and discusses detection through anomalous outbound network behavior [record_id:49]. It is one of the clearest records tying data loss to software supply-chain compromise and build-system telemetry.

FACADE is a key record for insider threat detection and enterprise-scale sensitive-data access monitoring [record_id:87]. It is notable because it claims long-term deployment at Alphabet, scanning billions of events daily over seven years, and because it reports specific false-positive rates [record_id:87]. The system’s use of benign-only contrastive learning is relevant for organizations that lack large corpora of confirmed insider incidents [record_id:87].

The data-splicing DLP bypass talk is notable because it directly challenges enterprise DLP architectures and claims bypass coverage against major Gartner-listed vendors [record_id:2272]. It also mentions an open-source red-team toolkit, Angry Magpie, for replicating the attacks [record_id:2272]. Because the abstract is short and claim-heavy, it is high priority for follow-up but requires validation.

The Linux shared-environment talk is representative of a quieter class of data loss: secrets exposed by default system visibility rather than exploit chains [record_id:2090]. It highlights process inspection mechanisms, insecure scripting practices, hosting-panel infrastructure, and passive collection of database passwords, API tokens, and internal URLs by unprivileged users [record_id:2090]. This record is useful for researchers interested in misconfiguration-driven leakage and multi-user host hardening.

The ransomware-leak records are notable because they shift the focus from preventing exfiltration to responsibly analyzing the aftermath [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. HIBR’s use of OCR and LLMs to find PII in scanned and unstructured leak data is representative of a growing need for breach impact analysis at scale, but it also raises ethical and legal concerns [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471].

The AI-agent records are collectively important because they define a new DLP frontier. The Cedar-policy talk provides a deterministic reference-monitor model for coding agents [record_id:2345]. Stripe’s talk offers a containment model built around controlled egress, human confirmation, and CI-time guardrail enforcement [record_id:2358]. Rehberger’s talk describes promptware and exploit chains that enable data exfiltration, persistence, lateral movement, and C2 across agentic systems [record_id:2369]. Together they suggest that future DLP will need to govern autonomous tool use, not only human user activity.

The records on compliance taxonomy generation and LLM-based log classification show more experimental or workflow-oriented uses of AI for DLP [record_id:2190], [record_id:2239]. They are less incident-specific but useful for understanding how practitioners are applying LLMs to classify sensitive data, derive DLP entities, and work with incomplete logs.

The secret-scanning and secret-free authentication talks are practical and prevention-oriented [record_id:2396], [record_id:2408]. They frame secrets both as leaked artifacts to be found at scale and as architectural debt to be eliminated where possible.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The biggest limitation is that most records are abstracts rather than full papers or detailed technical reports. They identify claims, methods, and tools, but usually do not include source code, experimental results, false-positive/false-negative rates, deployment constraints, or independent validation. This is especially important for high-impact claims such as bypassing all major DLP vendors [record_id:2272], stopping all exfiltrated DNS packets before they leave the endpoint [record_id:35], or enabling data exfiltration through a new IAP vulnerability [record_id:2495].

The records also do not provide a unified evaluation framework for DLP. There is no shared benchmark across DNS exfiltration detection, CI/CD secret leakage, insider access anomalies, ransomware PII extraction, LLM-based classification, or AI-agent containment. This makes it hard to compare approaches. For example, FACADE reports very low false-positive rates in Google’s environment [record_id:87], while LLM-based log classification and small-LM secret detection records are more exploratory and do not provide comparable metrics [record_id:2190], [record_id:2251].

Another gap is operational integration. The records discuss impressive techniques, but they rarely explain how alerts are triaged, how policy exceptions are managed, how user privacy is protected during monitoring, or how organizations avoid overwhelming security teams. This is especially relevant for insider detection [record_id:87], ransomware-leak PII indexing [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471], and LLM-driven log or data classification [record_id:2190], [record_id:2239].

There are also unresolved questions about the safety of AI-assisted DLP. LLMs are proposed for sensitive-data classification, prompt generation, PII extraction, and secret-impact analysis [record_id:2190], [record_id:2239], [record_id:2332], [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. But the records do not deeply address risks such as model hallucination, prompt injection against analysis workflows, accidental exposure of sensitive logs to third-party model providers, or auditability of AI-generated classifications. At the same time, agentic AI is presented as a data-exfiltration risk requiring containment, suggesting a tension between using AI for DLP and protecting DLP workflows from AI-related failure modes [record_id:2345], [record_id:2358], [record_id:2369].

Finally, privacy and legality are only partially explored. The personal privacy workshop focuses on OSINT footprint reduction and obfuscation [record_id:1969], while the HIBR records explicitly discuss GDPR, legal ambiguity, and avoiding exposure of PII [record_id:2470], [record_id:2471]. However, the broader dataset does not fully address legal constraints on monitoring employees, scanning public repositories, crawling ransomware leak sites, or using cloud-hosted LLMs for sensitive data classification.
