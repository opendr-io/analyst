# Topic: Threat modeling

## Executive Summary

Across these 34 records, “threat modeling” appears less as a single canonical method and more as a broad family of structured ways to reason about what can go wrong before, during, or around deployment. The collection is dominated by 2025–2026 conference abstracts from Black Hat, DEF CON, BSidesSF, BSidesLV, CAMLIS, and [un]prompted. The strongest recurring trend is the expansion of threat modeling into new architectures: agentic AI, AI-assisted development, enterprise AI search, AI-enabled desktop applications, cloud-managed machine identities, Open RAN, EV charging, OT/ICS, maritime systems, voting systems, and critical infrastructure.

Several records are explicitly about threat modeling as a practice or method: a constitutional-law keynote that argues the legal system “is terrible at threat modeling” [record_id:103], a CARVER-based analysis of voting systems [record_id:1858], breach path analysis with an open-source graph ontology [record_id:2260], AI search threat modeling [record_id:2270], STPA-Sec hazard analysis for military AI systems [record_id:2458], TRIZ as a systematic contradiction-resolution method for cybersecurity analysis [record_id:2486], and a cloud identity-focused threat model of CSP-managed non-human identities [record_id:2552]. Other records treat threat modeling as part of a broader engineering or security workflow, such as AI agent security [record_id:58], [record_id:1956], [record_id:2299], security agents that automate threat modeling [record_id:2324], AI coding tools that pull threat models before generation [record_id:2373], or Cyber-Informed Engineering that aims to “design out cyber-risk before a system is placed into operation” [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437].

The records collectively emphasize that security design analysis must consider architecture, incentives, trust boundaries, human operators, legal and policy structures, physical-world consequences, and changing adversary capabilities. Evidence is strongest for trend identification and talk-level positioning, but thinner for detailed methodology: most records are abstracts rather than full papers, and several describe forthcoming talks or workshops without giving full technical details. There are few direct references to classic frameworks such as STRIDE, attack trees, misuse cases, or abuse cases. Instead, the corpus highlights adjacent or alternative approaches: CARVER, STPA-Sec, Cyber-Informed Engineering, breach path analysis, adversary emulation, attack-surface taxonomies, graph-based modeling, structured AI guardrails, policy-as-code, validation loops, and systems-theoretic hazard analysis.

## Research Landscape

The dataset is conference-heavy and concentrated in recent material. Black Hat US 2025 contributes records on EV charging standards [record_id:42], O-RAN architecture-specific testing and attack-vector taxonomy [record_id:57], architectural controls for agentic AI security [record_id:58], and a keynote connecting threat modeling to constitutional law [record_id:103]. DEF CON 33 contributes a large set of security-village and panel-style records, including election security [record_id:1857], [record_id:1858], [record_id:1864], adversarial mindset and threat-informed defense [record_id:1939], agentic AI workflows [record_id:1956], physical security and elevators [record_id:1999], corporate platform capability abuse [record_id:2000], ICS/OT adversary shifts [record_id:2020], [record_id:2029], maritime cyber-physical consequences [record_id:2035], practical privacy advice [record_id:2106], and continuous attack/crisis simulation [record_id:2129].

BSidesSF 2025 records show applied practitioner interest in making threat modeling accessible and operational. They include breach path analysis “for anyone” [record_id:2260], web application security skills applied to AI [record_id:2263], enterprise AI search threat modeling [record_id:2270], secure design trade-offs in application security [record_id:2280], and threat modeling for autonomous AI agents [record_id:2299]. BSidesLV 2025 extends the theme into education, critical infrastructure, AI-enabled systems, and identity: community threat modeling for marginalized communities [record_id:2416], AI-aware desktop application threat modeling [record_id:2428], Cyber-Informed Engineering for the water sector [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437], STPA-Sec for military AI autonomy [record_id:2458], TRIZ for cybersecurity innovation and forecasting [record_id:2486], and CSP-managed non-human identity threat modeling [record_id:2552].

The CAMLIS records are more research-oriented and machine-learning-related. One develops a threat model for deep reinforcement learning trading policies and proposes active and passive attacks [record_id:203]. Another uses NLP topic modeling to construct and visualize insider threat models from news articles and witness reports [record_id:201]. One CAMLIS entry has a relevant title about “Attack Surfaces in Computer Use Agents: A Practical Taxonomy,” but the raw record text is empty, so it cannot support substantive claims beyond its existence as a record with no raw abstract [record_id:127].

The [un]prompted 2026 records focus on production AI workflows and developer tooling. Stripe’s talk describes AI agents used for threat modeling and security request routing, with modular orchestrator/child architectures, structured inputs/outputs, validation, and evaluation loops [record_id:2324]. Block’s talk describes red-teaming its open-source AI agent “goose” to proactively identify abuse paths [record_id:2362]. Another talk demonstrates an MCP server that injects security context into AI coding workflows by pulling threat models, security requirements, and OWASP guidance before code generation, then verifying output afterward [record_id:2373].

Overall, the landscape is not centered on classroom-style introductions to threat modeling. It is more about applying structured anticipatory analysis to emerging systems, especially where architectural changes create new trust relationships and attack surfaces.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Threat modeling is shifting from checklist activity to architecture-level reasoning

A major theme is that modern security failures arise from structural capabilities and architectural trust flows, not only from individual bugs. The Black Hat agentic AI record says prompt injection cannot simply be eliminated and argues that developers need “architectural patterns,” “input validation strategies,” and ways to “model trust flows in agentic environments” [record_id:58]. The same architecture-first concern appears in O-RAN, where disaggregated, cloud-native, microservice-based systems introduce exposed interfaces and inter-component dependencies; the talk proposes mapping attack surfaces and introducing a taxonomy of attack vectors to guide threat modeling and defense [record_id:57].

The “Dark Capabilities” record similarly frames threat modeling as an exercise in asking what large technology platforms are structurally able to do, regardless of stated intent: “it’s never about what a system claims to do. It’s about what it can do” [record_id:2000]. That framing recurs in cloud identity, where CSP-managed non-human identities are examined by asking “What can go wrong?” and using exploits unique to each provider as a framework for identifying design shortcomings [record_id:2552].

### AI and agentic systems are the dominant emerging application area

Many records apply threat modeling to AI, especially LLM-based applications and autonomous agents. The records emphasize that AI changes security boundaries by adding tool execution, persistent memory, inter-agent communication, embedded local models, enterprise data aggregation, and AI-assisted code generation.

Agentic AI appears in several variants. One record argues for architectural controls and trust-flow modeling to mitigate natural language injection risks even when AI systems consume untrusted data [record_id:58]. Another extends copilot security methods to multi-agent ecosystems, covering threat modeling for multi-agent pipelines, supply-chain defenses, sensitive workflows, prompt injection at scale, policy-as-code guardrails, fine-grained access controls, and red-team strategies [record_id:1956]. A BSidesSF talk focuses on “threat modeling of real-world autonomous AI systems,” model poisoning, prompt injection, and mitigation strategies [record_id:2299]. Block’s [un]prompted talk frames red-teaming an AI agent as a proactive way to identify how attackers will abuse it [record_id:2362].

AI threat modeling is also moving into enterprise search and developer tooling. Enterprise AI search tools aggregate corporate data into a single interface, which the record frames as “Google, but for juicy, sensitive corporate information,” and proposes threat modeling and controls for deployments [record_id:2270]. AI-assisted coding workflows are described as bypassing traditional AppSec controls; the proposed response is to inject threat models, security requirements, and OWASP guidance into the coding loop before generation and verify the output afterward [record_id:2373]. Stripe’s record pushes this further by using AI agents to streamline threat modeling itself, with modular architectures, structured I/O, validation, and evaluation loops to manage quality and trust [record_id:2324].

The AI theme is not limited to LLMs. CAMLIS includes a threat model for deep reinforcement learning trading policies, with active test-time manipulation and passive adversarial policy imitation attacks against DQN trading agents [record_id:203]. BSidesLV includes STPA-Sec for AI-enabled military systems, emphasizing that AI can “fail dangerously without ever ‘breaking’” and that systemic risks can arise from misaligned recommendations, inadequate feedback loops, and interface ambiguity [record_id:2458]. Desktop applications embedding local LLMs are also presented as a renewed threat modeling concern, where prompt injection and adversarial inputs combine with memory corruption, unsafe parsing, protocol bugs, vulnerable plugins, tampered models, and insecure automation [record_id:2428].

### Cyber-physical and critical infrastructure records emphasize consequences, safety, and domain norms

Several records apply threat modeling to systems where cyber actions have physical or civic consequences. EV charging standards are presented as both risk-reducing and risk-creating: ISO 15118 mitigates some cybersecurity issues while reshaping the threat landscape and introducing new attack vectors in the EV charging ecosystem [record_id:42]. Open RAN similarly shows how openness and interoperability can expand the attack surface in next-generation cellular infrastructure [record_id:57].

OT/ICS records emphasize shifting adversaries and preparedness. The repeated DEF CON abstract on ICS/OT says defenders previously focused on state adversaries, then ransomware and hacktivists after Colonial Pipeline, and now face increasing alignment among state adversaries, ransomware operators, and hacktivists [record_id:2020], [record_id:2029]. The water-sector Cyber-Informed Engineering workshop frames threat modeling-like activity as engineering practice: applying CIE principles through a hypothetical project to design out cyber risk before operation [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437].

Physical-world context also matters. The elevator record is about incorporating elevators into security design and understanding how badly configured elevator systems can be abused to access restricted areas through special operating modes, controller manipulation, or hoistway entry [record_id:1999]. The maritime record argues that understanding the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea can affect whether cyber manipulation of rudder angle creates “big effect or no effect,” showing that threat modeling cyber-physical systems requires domain-specific operational norms and legal liability context [record_id:2035].

### Election and civic systems require models beyond cryptographic guarantees

The election-related records argue that security properties must be modeled in context, not assumed from a single control. The online voting record challenges the idea that end-to-end verifiability is a “magic bullet,” arguing that E2E-V can be irrelevant to attacks outside the cryptographic loop and especially to client-side malware that can alter or sabotage voting applications and devices [record_id:1857]. The CARVER panel applies a World War II-era vulnerability analysis framework—Criticality, Accessibility, Recuperability, Vulnerability, Effect, Recognizability—to voting systems [record_id:1858]. Ron Rivest’s record highlights “software independence” and “evidence-based elections” as concepts that can dramatically strengthen election security [record_id:1864].

Together, these records show threat modeling applied to election systems as a layered analysis of clients, servers, cryptography, recovery, evidence, and institutional trust. They also illustrate a recurring pattern in the broader dataset: a control that is valuable in one part of the system may not address threats elsewhere.

### Human, social, legal, and community contexts are part of threat modeling

Not all records are technical architecture talks. Jennifer Granick’s keynote says the legal system “is terrible at threat modeling,” trusting the wrong insiders, overreacting to outsider threats, and failing to adapt when circumstances shift [record_id:103]. This broadens the topic into governance and civil liberties, suggesting that institutions also perform implicit threat models that can be wrong or harmful.

The community-defense record focuses on activists, healthcare access, authoritarian governments, marginalized communities, and harm reduction. It explicitly says threat models differ for different identities and proposes storytelling frameworks to explain privacy and security concepts to non-technical audiences [record_id:2416]. The personal digital security record similarly critiques viral advice that does not address the risks people are most likely to face and proposes analyzing high-profile concerns for effectiveness and practicality before and after an issue arises [record_id:2106].

Insider threat modeling also incorporates social and psychological factors. The CAMLIS insider record describes organizational, technical, psychological, and social approaches, and proposes a custom insider threat model using NLP topic modeling over news articles and witness reports to help investigators ask about motivation, assets targeted, controls, and bypasses [record_id:201].

### Threat modeling is increasingly tied to validation, simulation, red teaming, and continuous feedback

Many records connect threat modeling to active testing rather than static documentation. O-RAN threat modeling feeds systematic testing, dynamic tracing, static analysis, and constraint-driven test generation that uncovered vulnerabilities and CVEs [record_id:57]. AI agent security records combine threat modeling with red-team strategies and mitigation patterns [record_id:1956], [record_id:2299], while Block’s “Operation Pale Fire” explicitly describes red-teaming an AI agent to proactively identify abuse [record_id:2362].

The TotalTest record critiques point-in-time penetration tests as inadequate and proposes an intelligence-led, integrated attack and crisis simulation program that turns breach simulations into a continuous, metrics-driven resilience program [record_id:2129]. Stripe’s security-agent record likewise emphasizes measuring and improving agent quality over time through offline and online evaluation loops, including different handling for subjective threat modeling outputs versus higher-signal routing feedback [record_id:2324]. This trend suggests downstream researchers should treat threat modeling in these records as part of a lifecycle: anticipate, test, simulate, measure, update.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records mention several concrete or semi-concrete approaches.

CARVER is the clearest named vulnerability analysis framework. The voting-system panel describes CARVER as enumerating components of complex systems and assessing Criticality, Accessibility, Recuperability, Vulnerability, Effect, and Recognizability, then applying that security lens to voting systems [record_id:1858]. This is a structured alternative to the classic STRIDE/attack-tree vocabulary and is particularly suited to prioritizing targets or weaknesses in complex infrastructure.

STPA-Sec is another named methodology. The BSidesLV military AI talk introduces it as a systems-theoretic approach for identifying and mitigating hidden hazards in AI-enabled environments, especially generative and predictive models. The method is positioned around systemic hazards such as misaligned recommendations, inadequate feedback loops, and interface ambiguity [record_id:2458]. This record is important because it treats AI security as a safety-and-control problem, not just an adversarial-input problem.

Cyber-Informed Engineering appears in two duplicate workshop records for the water sector. The workshop uses a hypothetical engineering project, workbook-based exercises, real-world case-study inspiration, and CIE principles to teach a methodology for designing out cyber risk before a system is placed into operation [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437]. This is one of the most explicitly pre-implementation records in the corpus.

Breach path analysis is presented as a practical, accessible method for mapping how attackers might move laterally through an organization’s assets. The BSidesSF record promises a conceptual engineering framework, no- or low-cost tools, examples, and an open-source security graph ontology [record_id:2260]. This suggests a graph-oriented modeling style that connects assets, relationships, and lateral movement paths.

Attack-surface taxonomy and architecture-specific testing are central to the O-RAN record. The authors propose mapping new attack surfaces introduced by microservice-based, cloud-native O-RAN architecture, creating a taxonomy of attack vectors, and using dynamic tracing plus static analysis to uncover inter-component dependencies and generate constraint-driven test inputs [record_id:57]. This is among the strongest records connecting threat modeling to vulnerability discovery.

Adversarial mindset, adversary emulation, red teaming, and threat-informed defense appear as operational methods. The DEF CON panel argues defenders must think like attackers, use offensive techniques to test systems and inform defensive strategies, and embed adversarial thinking into security programs to uncover blind spots and build resilience [record_id:1939]. TotalTest extends this into continuous attack and crisis simulations with metrics [record_id:2129].

AI-focused approaches include architectural controls, trust-flow modeling, structured guardrails, validation, access controls, policy-as-code, and evaluation loops. The Black Hat AI record claims practical architectural patterns and input validation strategies can mitigate natural language injection attacks and emphasizes modeling trust flows in agentic environments [record_id:58]. The DEF CON multi-agent record adds policy-as-code guardrails, fine-grained access controls, red-team strategies, supply-chain defenses, and protection for tool execution, persistent memory, and inter-agent communication [record_id:1956]. Stripe’s record describes modular orchestrator/child architectures, targeted tools, structured inputs/outputs, validation to reduce variance, and offline/online evaluation loops for AI agents used in security workflows [record_id:2324].

Threat modeling is also embedded into developer workflows. The “vibe coding” record proposes an MCP server that pulls threat models, security requirements, and OWASP guidance before AI-assisted code generation, then verifies generated code against vulnerabilities and security standards [record_id:2373]. The BSidesSF web-app-for-AI record argues that existing web application security knowledge can be applied to LLM-based applications, with some unique AI-specific additions [record_id:2263].

TRIZ is introduced as an engineering method for resolving contradictions and forecasting innovation patterns in cybersecurity. The record describes a contradiction matrix as a “decision tree for conflicts” such as “secure but open” or “privacy vs functionality,” and suggests applications to software supply chains, dark web economies, critical infrastructure, anti-phishing, and internal data leakage prevention [record_id:2486]. It is more innovation and analysis method than conventional threat model, but it fits the corpus’s interest in structured anticipatory reasoning.

NLP-based insider threat modeling is another distinctive approach. The CAMLIS insider record proposes using topic modeling over news articles to identify themes such as methodologies, motivation, insider role, and organizational weaknesses, then applying the resulting custom model to witness reports and visualizing incidents as graphs with temporal, causal, and narrative clues [record_id:201].

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The Black Hat keynote “Threat Modeling and Constitutional Law” is notable for extending threat modeling into law and governance. Its core claim is that constitutional law doctrines have made longstanding threat modeling mistakes, including trusting the wrong insiders, overreacting to outsider threats, and adapting poorly to changed circumstances [record_id:103]. This is not a technical method record, but it is conceptually important because it treats threat modeling as a general institutional reasoning failure.

The O-RAN Black Hat record is one of the most technically detailed and evidence-rich abstracts. It describes a shift from closed RANs to disaggregated, software-driven O-RAN architectures, identifies exposed interfaces, proposes a taxonomy of attack vectors for threat modeling, and reports systematic testing that found 26 previously unknown memory-corruption vulnerabilities and 20 CVEs [record_id:57]. It is representative of architecture-specific threat modeling tied to empirical vulnerability discovery.

The agentic AI Black Hat record is representative of a major trend in the dataset. It argues that prompt injection cannot be eliminated solely with guardrails and that security must be addressed through architecture, input validation, and trust-flow modeling [record_id:58]. This theme is reinforced by the DEF CON multi-agent workflow record [record_id:1956], the BSidesSF autonomous AI agent record [record_id:2299], Block’s AI agent red-team record [record_id:2362], and Stripe’s AI security-agent workflow record [record_id:2324].

The voting records form a coherent mini-cluster. The E2E verifiability talk challenges cryptographic “magic bullet” claims for online voting by pointing to attacks outside the cryptographic loop and client-side malware [record_id:1857]. The CARVER talk applies a structured vulnerability prioritization framework to voting systems [record_id:1858]. The software independence and evidence-based elections talk supplies a complementary resilience concept [record_id:1864]. Together these records are strong evidence for threat modeling that evaluates whether a security property covers the full system and adversary model.

The BSidesLV STPA-Sec record is notable because it explicitly frames AI security around hazards and control structures rather than only vulnerabilities. Its key contribution is the claim that AI systems can fail dangerously without “breaking,” and that systems-theoretic analysis can identify risks in recommendations, feedback loops, and interfaces before harm occurs [record_id:2458]. This makes it a strong representative record for safety-oriented threat modeling.

The cloud non-human identity record is a focused example of threat modeling a modern infrastructure design pattern. It examines CSP-managed NHIs across the big three clouds, asks “What can go wrong?”, compares exploitation techniques and impact, and uses provider-specific exploits to reveal design shortcomings and mitigations [record_id:2552]. This is one of the clearest records about concrete cloud architecture risk.

The Cyber-Informed Engineering workshop records are important for pre-implementation design. They explicitly teach a methodology for designing out cyber risk before operation using a hypothetical engineering project and hands-on exercises [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437]. These records help anchor the topic description’s emphasis on identifying threats before implementation.

The breach path analysis record is notable for accessibility and tooling. It frames breach path analysis as a “treasure map” of lateral movement through company assets, with a conceptual engineering framework, low-cost tools, and an open-source security graph ontology [record_id:2260]. It is representative of graph-based threat modeling and attack path analysis.

The CAMLIS records add research depth outside the conference-practitioner mainstream. One develops a threat model and attacks for DRL trading policies [record_id:203]. Another models insider threats using NLP and graph visualization from narrative reports [record_id:201]. These records show threat modeling applied to ML behavior and human/organizational incidents, respectively.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The largest limitation is that most records are talk abstracts, not full proceedings. They state intended content, claims, or methods, but usually do not provide enough detail to evaluate reproducibility, assumptions, metrics, or implementation quality. For example, the agentic AI records mention architectural patterns, guardrails, policy-as-code, evaluation loops, and red-team strategies, but the abstracts do not provide complete pattern catalogs or experimental data [record_id:58], [record_id:1956], [record_id:2299], [record_id:2324], [record_id:2362].

Classic threat modeling frameworks are underrepresented. The corpus includes CARVER [record_id:1858], STPA-Sec [record_id:2458], TRIZ [record_id:2486], Cyber-Informed Engineering [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437], and breach path analysis [record_id:2260], but there is little or no raw-text evidence about STRIDE, attack trees, abuse cases, misuse cases, PASTA, LINDDUN, DREAD, or formal architecture risk analysis. Downstream agents should not assume those frameworks are discussed unless they consult materials beyond these records.

There is also limited disagreement in the records. Most abstracts advocate a method or warn about new risks; few compare methods or present opposing views. The voting E2E-V record does dispute promoter claims that end-to-end verifiability is a “magic bullet” for internet voting [record_id:1857], and the AI guardrails record argues that treating guardrails as first-order controls can create privilege escalation risks [record_id:58]. But there is little direct debate about which threat modeling method works best in which context.

Another gap is evidence of adoption and outcomes. Some records claim real-world experience or findings: O-RAN testing found vulnerabilities and CVEs [record_id:57], TotalTest draws on hundreds of red-team engagements [record_id:2129], Stripe discusses production use of AI agents for threat modeling and routing [record_id:2324], and Block red-teamed its own AI agent [record_id:2362]. However, most records do not quantify how threat modeling changed design decisions, reduced vulnerabilities, improved resilience, or affected business outcomes.

AI threat modeling is heavily represented, but important questions remain unresolved: how to make AI-generated threat models reliable, how to evaluate subjective threat model quality, how to handle dynamic agent behavior, how to model memory and tool access over time, and how to prevent security automation from creating false confidence. Stripe explicitly notes the challenge of handling subjective outputs in threat modeling versus higher-signal routing feedback [record_id:2324], making evaluation a key open question.

Cyber-physical and civic systems records raise domain-specific modeling questions but do not fully answer them. EV charging standards can shift and create risk [record_id:42], maritime rules shape cyber-physical impact [record_id:2035], elevators affect physical access control [record_id:1999], and water-sector CIE aims to design out risk [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437]. But the abstracts do not provide detailed assets, adversary capabilities, control hierarchies, or residual risk assessments.

Finally, one record has no raw abstract text. The CAMLIS “Attack Surfaces in Computer Use Agents: A Practical Taxonomy” entry appears relevant by title and metadata, but because its raw record text is empty, it should not be used as evidence for any substantive claim about methods or findings [record_id:127].

## Coverage And Evidence Notes

This section accounts for all records and highlights how strongly each contributes to the topic.

The strongest primary threat-modeling records include the constitutional-law keynote, which applies threat modeling to legal doctrine and institutional trust failures [record_id:103]; the CARVER voting-system panel, which applies a named structured framework to election infrastructure [record_id:1858]; the tech-company capability-abuse talk, which models what platforms can structurally do rather than what they claim to do [record_id:2000]; the BSidesSF breach path analysis talk, which proposes a practical graph-oriented framework and open-source ontology [record_id:2260]; the enterprise AI search threat modeling talk [record_id:2270]; the STPA-Sec military AI hazard-analysis talk [record_id:2458]; the TRIZ cybersecurity analysis talk [record_id:2486]; and the CSP-managed non-human identity threat model [record_id:2552].

Several records are secondary but highly relevant because they apply threat modeling to emerging architectures. ISO 15118 and EV charging are discussed as standards that mitigate, shift, and create risks, showing how policy and technical standards can reshape a threat landscape [record_id:42]. O-RAN is treated as an architecture whose openness expands attack surface and requires a taxonomy of attack vectors plus architecture-specific testing [record_id:57]. Agentic AI security is covered through architectural controls and trust-flow modeling [record_id:58], multi-agent pipeline threat modeling and policy-as-code defenses [record_id:1956], autonomous AI agent threat modeling with poisoning and prompt-injection demos [record_id:2299], red-teaming of Block’s open-source AI agent [record_id:2362], and Stripe’s production AI agents for threat modeling and request routing [record_id:2324]. AI-assisted development is covered through an MCP server that injects threat models and security requirements into the coding loop [record_id:2373]. AI-enabled desktop applications are covered through AI-aware threat modeling that combines new AI risks with legacy memory, parsing, protocol, and plugin risks [record_id:2428]. A CAMLIS record on computer-use agent attack surfaces has empty raw text and therefore provides no substantive evidence beyond the record’s presence [record_id:127].

The election-security cluster is relevant but not uniformly framed as threat modeling. E2E verifiability is criticized as insufficient for online voting because client-side malware and systems outside the cryptographic loop remain threats [record_id:1857]. CARVER is applied directly to voting systems [record_id:1858]. Software independence and evidence-based elections are presented as core concepts for strengthening election security [record_id:1864].

Several OT, IoT, and cyber-physical records contribute domain-specific design-analysis perspectives. Elevator security is framed around correct incorporation into physical security design and abuse of misconfigured systems [record_id:1999]. ICS/OT adversary evolution is described in duplicate or near-duplicate records warning that defenders are not prepared for alignment among state adversaries, ransomware operators, and hacktivists [record_id:2020], [record_id:2029]. Maritime cyber-physical modeling is represented by the COLREGs/rudder-manipulation talk, which argues that professional norms and legal liability affect the consequences of cyber manipulation [record_id:2035]. The water-sector CIE workshop appears twice and teaches a hands-on methodology for designing out cyber risk before operation [record_id:2436], [record_id:2437].

Human-centered and organizational modeling records broaden the topic. The insider threat NLP record combines organizational, technical, psychological, and social approaches into a custom model and graph visualization of incidents [record_id:201]. The adversarial-mindset panel argues for red teaming, adversary emulation, offensive techniques, and threat-informed defense to uncover blind spots and improve resilience [record_id:1939]. The practical privacy record critiques viral advice that does not match likely risks and proposes analyzing concerns for effectiveness and practicality [record_id:2106]. The community-defense record teaches threat modeling as harm reduction for marginalized communities and emphasizes that threat models differ by identity [record_id:2416].

Other records are adjacent but still relevant to structured security design. The DRL trading-policy record explicitly develops a threat model and attacks for deep reinforcement learning trading agents [record_id:203]. The TotalTest simulation record argues that point-in-time penetration tests are inadequate and proposes an intelligence-led, metrics-driven attack and crisis simulation program [record_id:2129]. The BSidesSF web-app-security-for-AI talk says practitioners can apply web application security skills to LLM-based apps while accounting for AI-unique issues [record_id:2263]. The Application Security Weekly live recording focuses on secure design, security trade-offs, and developer needs [record_id:2280].

Taken together, the records support a broad view of threat modeling as anticipatory, structured reasoning about adversaries, misuse, hazards, trust flows, capabilities, and design trade-offs. The most evidence-rich areas are AI/agentic systems, cyber-physical infrastructure, election security, and architecture-specific attack-surface analysis. The thinnest areas are traditional named threat modeling taxonomies, rigorous method comparison, and measured outcomes from threat modeling practice.
