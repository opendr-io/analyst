# Topic: Identity, OAuth, and access delegation

## Executive Summary

The records portray identity and access systems as a rapidly expanding attack surface whose failures increasingly arise at the boundaries between “standard” authentication protocols, proprietary platform abstractions, cloud-managed credentials, non-human identities, and agentic automation. The strongest cluster of evidence concerns OAuth/OIDC, Microsoft Entra ID, Azure integrations, Active Directory, Kerberos, and token/session abuse. Multiple records argue that authentication and authorization remain frequently confused in practice, especially when OAuth is used as an authentication layer, when consent and token validation are delegated to platform services, or when cloud providers introduce hidden token stores and connection abstractions [record_id:3] [record_id:15] [record_id:20] [record_id:2383].

A second major cluster focuses on enterprise identity infrastructure: Active Directory, Entra ID hybrid trust, Group Policy Objects, Windows LAPS, delegated Managed Service Accounts, Primary Refresh Tokens, WSFC cluster credentials, and Jamf service accounts. These records emphasize that enterprise identity systems are not just login infrastructure but lateral-movement and persistence infrastructure. Attackers can abuse design features, excessive permissions, undocumented flows, and service-account privileges to move from endpoints or on-premises AD into cloud tenants, or from one managed system into broad administrative control [record_id:10] [record_id:76] [record_id:77] [record_id:1929] [record_id:1966] [record_id:1987] [record_id:2082] [record_id:2547].

A third trend is scrutiny of passwordless authentication, passkeys, biometrics, MFA, and account recovery. The records do not reject these mechanisms wholesale, but they repeatedly show that “phishing-resistant” or “passwordless” does not mean abuse-proof. Passkeys and WebAuthn/FIDO2 are attacked through browser extension clickjacking, WebAuthn proxying, synced-passkey phishing, CTAP/API confusion, and flawed attestation implementations [record_id:74] [record_id:1851] [record_id:1992] [record_id:1997] [record_id:2080]. Windows Hello for Business and biometric storage are examined as complex local authentication systems with implementation weaknesses [record_id:45]. Account recovery and MFA are treated as both usability necessities and security weak points, with records on account recovery testing, RBA effectiveness, MFA bypass phishing, IP geolocation, device enrollment, and SMS-based 2FA fraud [record_id:70] [record_id:126] [record_id:156] [record_id:187] [record_id:188] [record_id:2317].

A fourth trend is the rise of non-human identity (NHI), service-to-service authentication, secrets governance, and workload identity. Records argue that long-lived credentials and unmanaged machine identities are an increasingly poor fit for cloud and automation-heavy environments. Proposed directions include deleting static secrets, workload identity federation, CSP-managed NHI threat modeling, OWASP NHI risk governance, capability-based authorization for AI agents, and cryptographic delegated warrants [record_id:2396] [record_id:2468] [record_id:2484] [record_id:2552] [record_id:2572] [record_id:2370].

Finally, identity abuse is increasingly intertwined with AI. Agentic systems are described as consumers and re-delegators of OAuth tokens; promptware can exploit the permissions of Google Workspace/Gemini agents; AI agents can find and validate authorization flaws; AI-driven KYC workflows can become data-access execution environments; and AI can automate social engineering and phishing at scale [record_id:3] [record_id:53] [record_id:2192] [record_id:2326] [record_id:2328] [record_id:2359] [record_id:2370].

## Research Landscape

The corpus is dominated by conference talk abstracts and workshop descriptions from Black Hat, DEF CON, BSides, CAMLIS, Prompt||GTFO, and [un]prompted. Black Hat and DEF CON records tend to emphasize offensive research, live exploitation, reverse engineering, and newly disclosed vulnerabilities. CAMLIS records are more measurement- and ML-oriented, focusing on authentication telemetry, risk-based authentication, graph modeling, and fraud or anomaly detection. BSides records include a mix of technical talks, defensive guidance, password-focused sessions, and cloud/IAM governance. [un]prompted records skew toward AI-agent security and authorization for agentic systems.

The most evidence-rich records are detailed abstracts describing specific attack surfaces, exploit chains, or empirical methods. Examples include connection-based OAuth in AI/integration platforms [record_id:3], Azure API Connections and token stores [record_id:15], Entra OAuth authorization mistakes [record_id:20], hybrid AD-to-Entra lateral movement [record_id:76], Windows Hello for Business internals [record_id:45], MFA bypass phishing detection [record_id:156], malicious Azure sign-in graph modeling [record_id:175], dMSA/BadSuccessor privilege escalation [record_id:1966] [record_id:2547], FIDO2 CTAP attacks [record_id:1992], passkey/WebAuthn proxying [record_id:1997], and secret-free workload identity [record_id:2396].

The landscape is broader than OAuth alone. It covers classic identity stores and protocols such as Active Directory, Kerberos, NTLM, LDAP, GPOs, LAPS, PRT cookies, and browser cookies; cloud identity systems such as Entra ID, Azure-AD, Google Cloud IAP, Google Workspace, AWS IRSA, Azure Workload ID, IAM roles, and ZTNA/SASE products; application-layer authorization such as GraphQL/REST access controls and service mesh authorization; and physical identity systems such as RFID badges and physical access control controllers [record_id:1971] [record_id:2018] [record_id:2125] [record_id:2495].

There is also a strong “identity is data and behavior” line of work. Several CAMLIS talks model authentication or account behavior statistically rather than focusing on protocol exploitation: point processes for temporal authentication behavior [record_id:246], graph-based insider-threat exposure and over-privileged access [record_id:154], heterogeneous graph embeddings for Azure sign-ins [record_id:175], TF-IDF for fraud-linkage across transaction/device/behavioral signals [record_id:78], and fake-account burst detection using demographic-distribution anomalies [record_id:247].

## Major Themes And Trends

### OAuth, OIDC, consent, and delegated access are being re-abstracted in risky ways

Several records argue that OAuth/OIDC failures are not only caused by developers misunderstanding specifications, but also by platforms adding new abstraction layers that obscure where authority actually resides. The clearest example is connection-based OAuth for agentic AI and integration platforms. One record describes orchestration engines such as Microsoft Power Automate and Copilot Studio obtaining third-party provider tokens on behalf of users, with token retrieval and lifecycle management outsourced to cloud “Credential Manager” or “Token Store” services. It argues that proprietary “OAuth connections” reintroduce vulnerabilities that standards had mitigated, including session fixation, open redirect, confused deputy, XSS, and cross-window communication attacks, enabling takeover of authenticated agents or integrations without explicit user consent [record_id:3].

Azure integration services appear repeatedly. Azure API Connections are described as a hidden infrastructure underpinning Logic Apps, Power Apps, and Power Automate. The record claims these connections can be exploited for near-unrestricted API access with minimal privileges and even cross-tenant impact, traversing ARM, APIM, custom connectors, consent servers, and token stores to leak Key Vault secrets and access connected services such as Jira, Salesforce, databases, and mail [record_id:15]. Together, these records suggest a systemic trend: low-code, integration, and AI-agent platforms simplify delegated access for developers but may centralize tokens and consent decisions in ways that create high-impact confused-deputy and token-reuse risks [record_id:3] [record_id:15].

OAuth/OIDC implementation mistakes remain a recurring topic outside Microsoft-specific platforms. A BSidesLV session focuses on missing `state` parameters, improperly validated discovery documents, token validation failures, and JWT replay with OWASP ZAP, framing OAuth/OIDC misconfiguration as a path to authentication bypass, impersonation, and unauthorized access [record_id:2383]. Another Black Hat record explicitly frames Entra OAuth abuse as a confusion between authentication and authorization, noting that OAuth is an “Open Authorization” standard often used for authentication and that code examples omit critical checks. The presenter claims a common misconfiguration allowed access to over 20 internal Microsoft applications [record_id:20].

Cloud access gateways and application-layer authorization also appear in this theme. Google Cloud Identity-Aware Proxy is treated as a gatekeeper that can fail through subtle misconfigurations, overly permissive IAM bindings, misplaced trust in user-supplied headers, overlooked endpoints, and a new vulnerability enabling exfiltration even without public IPs [record_id:2495]. A service mesh talk describes Yelp shifting authentication and authorization from infrastructure to the application layer after prior challenges, implying a pragmatic architectural response to limitations in infrastructure-centric access control [record_id:2281].

### Enterprise identity infrastructure is an attack platform, not just an authentication backend

Active Directory and Entra ID dominate the enterprise identity portion of the corpus. Multiple talks describe ways identity infrastructure itself becomes command-and-control, lateral movement, privilege escalation, or persistence infrastructure. A DEF CON talk explicitly describes Active Directory as analogous to C2 infrastructure because it centrally coordinates network assets; it focuses on Group Policy Objects as a powerful but under-studied primitive for enumeration, persistence, and privilege escalation in segmented environments, accompanied by tools for enumeration and exploitation [record_id:1929].

Hybrid AD/Entra trust is a major concern. One Black Hat record asks whether there is a security boundary between AD and Entra ID and says the answer has changed due to Microsoft hardening. It presents undocumented authentication flows and new lateral movement techniques starting from on-prem AD that can bypass authentication and MFA, exfiltrate data stealthily, and compromise a tenant with few useful logs, emphasizing that several techniques are design features rather than classic vulnerabilities [record_id:76]. Mac PRT cookie theft extends this cloud/endpoint identity line by studying Microsoft SSO in Intune Company Portal for macOS and claiming attackers can bypass process validation, obtain authentication tokens, register new devices using tokens with MFA claims, and persist beyond original token expiry [record_id:1987].

Several records focus on specific AD account or credential-management features. Delegated Managed Service Accounts in Windows Server 2025 are described as enabling BadSuccessor, an attack where a common permission can trick a Domain Controller into issuing Kerberos tickets for arbitrary principals, including Domain Admins and Domain Controllers, and can be extended to obtain NTLM hashes of every user without touching the domain controller [record_id:1966] [record_id:2547]. Windows LAPS is examined from the client side: the talk proposes abusing LAPS internals with Frida and Detours hooks to capture, manipulate, and rotate local admin passwords on demand, using LAPS for persistence on compromised on-prem and Entra-joined devices [record_id:2082]. A BSidesLV training covers foundational AD attacks such as NTLM Relay, Kerberoasting, Machine Account Quota abuse, and Unconstrained Delegation, with defenses including delegation restrictions, MachineAccountQuota reduction, LDAP signing, LDAP diagnostics, and Windows Event Forwarding [record_id:2388].

Other enterprise infrastructure records broaden this pattern. Windows Server Failover Clusters are described as underexamined high-availability systems where a single node compromise can lead to cluster takeover, lateral movement, and domain compromise via cluster credential extraction, Kerberos manipulation, and excessive cluster-object permissions [record_id:10]. Jamf APIs are framed as a path for lateral movement and code execution on managed macOS systems after compromise of Jamf administrators or service accounts [record_id:77]. RPC endpoint races and RPC abuses can manipulate high-integrity processes to authenticate a machine account to attacker-chosen servers [record_id:2016], while Windows domain controllers are described as vulnerable to DoS and DDoS-style abuse that can break authentication and paralyze operations [record_id:1940].

### Passwordless, passkeys, biometrics, and MFA are improving security but shifting failure modes

The corpus treats passwordless authentication as important but not inherently sufficient. Windows Hello for Business is analyzed as Microsoft’s passwordless flagship, used not only for login but also Personal Data Encryption, Administrator Protection, Passkeys, and Recall. The record promises a reverse-engineered view of WHfB internals, including biometric data storage, TPM role, Enhanced Sign-in Security, and what is transmitted to identity providers. Its central claim is that a local administrator can exchange biometric features in facial recognition databases to authenticate as any enrolled user, including a domain administrator, enabling lateral movement [record_id:45].

Passkeys and FIDO2 receive repeated scrutiny. Android key attestation is tied to passwordless flows such as WebAuthN in banking apps, but the record describes PKI vulnerabilities in an implementation and a systemic issue in Google’s open-source parsing library for Android key attestation X.509 chains, enabling bot-protection bypass and motivating tooling for testing attestation implementations [record_id:74]. A FIDO2/CTAP talk introduces eleven CTRAPS attacks in client impersonation and API confusion classes, claiming exploitation can wipe credentials, perform unauthorized factory resets, and track users across USB HID and NFC transports, including demos against popular authenticators and relying parties [record_id:1992]. A passkey/WebAuthn talk claims attackers can proxy WebAuthn API calls to forge registration and authentication responses when sites rely on passkeys without enforcing attestation or metadata checks [record_id:1997]. Another record states that synced passkeys can be phished despite being marketed as phishing-resistant, offering responsible-use guidance [record_id:2080]. Browser extension clickjacking can also affect passkey authentication in addition to leaking credentials, TOTP, credit cards, and personal data from password managers [record_id:1851].

MFA and account recovery are treated as operationally messy. One study evaluates risk-based authentication in production 2FA systems against opportunistic, targeted, and advanced attacks, arguing that heuristic and anomaly methods can improve security while preserving user experience [record_id:126]. Another CAMLIS record studies reverse-proxy MFA bypass phishing at scale, noting that tools such as evilginx can proxy credentials, passcodes, MFA flows, and cookies so that the provider sees a valid login from a different IP. The work uses point-in-time DNS, URL and behavioral filtering, and classification models to detect malicious authentications better than unsupervised methods [record_id:156]. Account recovery is framed as a hidden risk in a passwordless future: the researchers tested recovery mechanisms across heavily visited websites, recovered accounts thousands of times, and produced a test suite/auditing framework and best-practice recommendations [record_id:70]. SMS-based 2FA is examined from a fraud angle through International Revenue Share Fraud, where attackers inflate SMS traffic to siphon revenue from authentication flows [record_id:2317].

### Passwords are not gone; password policy, cracking, browser storage, and hidden accounts remain live issues

Despite the passwordless emphasis, many records show passwords remain central. One BSidesLV talk describes cracking 936 million passwords, with hardware, tools, wordlists, rules, CPU/GPU tradeoffs, statistics, and defenses, claiming 92% of passwords were found [record_id:2417]. Another challenges password expiry policies using real-world data, arguing that forced rotations cause users to mutate old passwords into predictable credentials, decreasing entropy and failing to reduce compromise risk; alternatives include time-to-crack scoring, event-driven rotations, and credential-risk thresholds [record_id:2500]. Password-audit/cracking is also framed through compliance and attack scenarios involving fictional organizations and a criminal group [record_id:2501].

Hidden identities are another password concern. The SaaS economy produces non-corporate and non-SSO work-related accounts outside corporate IdPs and password governance; the record identifies password strength, reuse, and sharing as risks and proposes methods to secure these hidden identities [record_id:2418]. Browser extensions are another recurring password-theft vector. One BSidesLV talk analyzes how malicious browser extensions steal user identity information, passwords, and authentication details through specific permissions and methods [record_id:2438], while the DEF CON clickjacking record reports vulnerabilities across widely used password managers leading to leakage of login credentials and TOTP [record_id:1851]. Information stealer malware is also described as capturing password vaults, browser extensions, MFA bypass material, and screenshots [record_id:2253].

Attackers are evolving around password defenses. A talk on synthetic passwords says botnets increasingly use fabricated, non-reused, high-entropy or AI-shaped passwords to evade credential-stuffing and password-reuse detection, pollute logs, obscure real attack traffic, and overwhelm detection systems [record_id:2556]. Credential monitoring is framed defensively by a BSidesSF talk arguing attackers increasingly “log in through the front door” using dark-web credentials and proposing Automated Credential Threat Monitoring [record_id:2268]. A “Phish-Back” talk describes defensive phishing pages intended to recover stolen credentials before they reach marketplaces, while acknowledging challenges from fingerprinting technologies and internet noise [record_id:2502].

### Session tokens, cookies, and credential material remain high-value targets

Several records focus directly on session and token theft. Chrome’s AppBound encryption is analyzed as a two-tier cookie-protection system using DPAPI permission levels and ChaCha20Poly1305, but the record says attackers can still extract decrypted cookies and compares cookie formats and encryption across Gecko, macOS Chromium, and WebKit. It also previews Device Bound Session Cookies as a TPM-backed improvement [record_id:2543]. Another session-focused talk reviews the 30-year arms race around cookie theft and abuse, emphasizing why old defenses remain valuable and discussing Google’s proposed Device Bound Session Credentials [record_id:2570]. Mac PRT cookie theft is particularly important because PRTs link endpoint compromise to Entra ID persistence [record_id:1987].

The software supply chain appears as a credential-exposure and trust-abuse vector. The tj-actions incident is described as leaking GitHub credentials through the supply chain, but the record claims leakage was not the primary goal; instead, attackers compromised multiple open-source projects for lateral movement and low-profile targeting of Coinbase [record_id:2004]. Enterprise vaults, normally intended to protect secrets and credentials, are also presented as exploitable: one Black Hat record claims remote code execution chains against HashiCorp Vault and CyberArk Conjur, including pre-auth RCE in Conjur and RCE against default configurations [record_id:88].

### Non-human identity and service-to-service authentication are becoming first-class identity problems

The corpus repeatedly argues that machine identities are outgrowing traditional credential practices. A concise BSidesLV record asks what would happen if application and workload identity were rethought rather than relying on long-lived credentials for humans and machines alike [record_id:2468]. Another claims NHIs such as service accounts, bots, and automation outnumber humans by at least 45 to 1, are top attacker targets, and require governance beyond secret storage, especially as shadow IT and AI automation accelerate the problem; it anchors this in the OWASP Top 10 Non-Human Identity Risks in 2025 [record_id:2572].

Secret-free or reduced-secret architecture is a practical theme. A BSidesLV talk advocates deleting manually managed secrets where possible and demonstrates two Kubernetes clusters, one in AWS and one in Azure, authenticating to each other without manually managed secrets using AWS IRSA and Azure Workload ID, with Terraform source code [record_id:2396]. CSP-managed NHI threat modeling examines risks across the big three clouds by asking what can go wrong and using provider-specific exploit frameworks to highlight design shortcomings and mitigations [record_id:2552]. Another multi-cloud talk covers machine identity attack paths, misconfigurations, IAM permission visualization, and open-source tools such as SecBridge, Cartography, and AWSPX [record_id:2484].

Agentic AI introduces a related but distinct form of NHI or delegated authority. One [un]prompted talk argues identity-based authorization fails for AI agents and proposes ephemeral cryptographic warrants that attenuate on delegation, are task-scoped and holder-bound, and can be verified offline by tools; the model is inspired by Macaroons/UCAN and aims to limit blast radius even if an agent is compromised [record_id:2370]. Another practical agent-building talk discusses capability bounding, prompt-injection detection, multi-agent memory isolation failures, and OAuth device flow for headless operation [record_id:2359].

### Identity abuse increasingly blends with AI agents, prompt injection, and automated social engineering

AI appears both as an attacker force multiplier and as a new access-delegation substrate. The connection-based OAuth record is explicitly about agentic AI and integration platforms, arguing that re-delegated OAuth tokens let attackers take over well-authenticated AI agents or integrations [record_id:3]. A Gemini for Workspace record describes targeted promptware attacks delivered through Google Calendar invites, email, or shared Google Docs. The attacker’s prompt hijacks application context and exploits agent permissions to delete calendar events, exfiltrate emails/calendar events, geolocate victims, control home appliances, stream video via Zoom, spam/phish, and launch worms across Gemini clients [record_id:53].

[un]prompted records extend this into access-control testing and KYC. AI agents can be built to find and validate authentication and authorization flaws by using validators that confirm successful login or access to protected resources, enabling exploitation of “Auth-by-One” errors in production systems [record_id:2326]. AI-driven KYC pipelines are framed as execution environments, where document-embedded injections can steer extraction agents into cross-record reads/writes and data exfiltration without bypassing access controls directly [record_id:2328]. Another record proposes capability-based authorization specifically to survive prompt injection in multi-agent setups [record_id:2370].

AI-driven social engineering and phishing are also present. A Prompt||GTFO record describes an AI-powered phishing agent that automates OSINT, vulnerability profiling, personalized spear-phishing email generation, and phishing-domain purchase/configuration, eliminating much of the cost difference between spray-and-pray and spear phishing [record_id:2192]. A DEF CON record discusses AI voice cloning used to impersonate specific people in vishing research [record_id:2119], and another discusses AI’s dual role in making phishing more convincing while also aiding real-time detection, adaptive filtering, and behavioral analysis [record_id:1878]. A BSidesSF talk describes LLM-based tools for automating IAM, threat detection, and vulnerability-management workflows, showing AI as a defender productivity tool as well [record_id:2275].

### Access control failures span APIs, applications, cloud gateways, vehicles, and data systems

Several records are not primarily about identity providers but about authorization failures at resource boundaries. A GraphQL/REST case study of the Feeld dating app describes lack of access controls exposing personal data, sensitive photos, videos, and private messages [record_id:2018]. A dealer-platform case study says API flaws enabled creation of a national admin account, ownership reassignment, remote vehicle controls, customer PII lookup, and user impersonation that negated 2FA [record_id:2036]. The [un]prompted Auth-by-One work frames modern application authentication and authorization as bespoke and difficult to test, then proposes AI-agent-assisted exploitation and validation [record_id:2326].

Cloud and SaaS access paths are also highlighted. Hidden access paths to “crown jewels” include common access patterns, hidden paths, and engineer-created backdoors used to get work done [record_id:2249]. Google Workspace is described as an enterprise productivity platform where attackers exploit logging gaps to escalate privileges, exfiltrate data, and evade detection, with investigation techniques even without sufficient logs [record_id:2312]. AdapterSwap is a more defensive ML/data-governance record, proposing fine-grained control over data access and deletion in LLM training and inference by dynamically composing low-rank adapters [record_id:151].

### Physical and IoT access control remains connected to identity risk

A smaller but notable subset concerns physical or IoT identity. RFID badge cloning is covered through remote cloning devices, Flipper Zero, iCopy-X, gooseneck readers, wall implants, clipboard cloning devices, and HID iClass SE/SEOS downgrade/legacy attacks [record_id:1971]. A physical access control best-practices session argues many vendors install minimum viable systems that are unreliable or trivial to bypass, and covers wiring, supervision, encryption, tamper resistance, clone-resistant badges, badge-reader configuration, controller security, badge management, and maintenance [record_id:2125]. EEG-based authentication is proposed for IoT environments where static biometrics and conventional digital security are inadequate, using brain electrical activity as a lightweight, hard-to-replicate security layer without storing sensitive data [record_id:1932].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records discuss both offensive and defensive methods.

On the offensive side, OAuth and token testing methods include exploiting session fixation, open redirect, confused deputy, XSS, and cross-window communication in proprietary OAuth connection flows [record_id:3]; exploring ARM, APIM, custom connectors, consent servers, and token stores to reach cross-tenant Azure API Connection abuse [record_id:15]; using OWASP ZAP to intercept and replay JWTs in OAuth/OIDC token replay demonstrations [record_id:2383]; and abusing legitimate Microsoft login functionality to phish credentials directly through `login.microsoftonline.com` rather than separate attacker infrastructure [record_id:1960].

Enterprise identity offensive methods include extracting and leveraging WSFC cluster credentials, manipulating Kerberos, and abusing cluster object permissions [record_id:10]; using undocumented AD-to-Entra authentication flows for MFA bypass and data exfiltration [record_id:76]; exploiting GPOs for enumeration, persistence, privilege escalation, and AD-as-C2 behavior, with release of enumeration/exploitation tools [record_id:1929]; abusing dMSA attributes to obtain arbitrary Kerberos tickets or NTLM hashes [record_id:1966] [record_id:2547]; extracting PRT cookies from macOS and registering new devices for Entra persistence [record_id:1987]; and hooking Windows LAPS client logic with Frida or Microsoft Detours to capture/manipulate local admin passwords [record_id:2082].

Passwordless and session attack methods include browser-extension clickjacking against password managers and passkeys [record_id:1851], WebAuthn API proxying to forge passkey registration/authentication [record_id:1997], CTAP client impersonation and API confusion using Android apps, Electron apps, Proxmark3, USB HID, and NFC [record_id:1992], Android key attestation parsing/PKI testing tools [record_id:74], and browser-cookie extraction against AppBound encryption plus analysis of DBSC/Device Bound Session Credentials [record_id:2543] [record_id:2570].

Cloud and NHI defensive approaches include replacing static secrets with workload identity federation using AWS IRSA and Azure Workload ID [record_id:2396], mapping cloud IAM and attack paths with SecBridge, Cartography, and AWSPX [record_id:2484], threat modeling CSP-managed NHIs across major cloud providers [record_id:2552], and implementing organization-wide NHI governance aligned with OWASP Top 10 NHI Risks [record_id:2572]. Capability-based agent authorization uses cryptographic warrants inspired by Macaroons/UCAN, with attenuation on delegation and offline verification by tools [record_id:2370].

Detection and analytics methods include RBA heuristics and anomaly detection evaluated in production 2FA systems [record_id:126]; point-in-time DNS enrichment, URL/behavioral filtering, and classification of MFA bypass phishing authentications [record_id:156]; heterogeneous relational graph convolutional networks for Azure-AD sign-in detection [record_id:175]; graph-based user-entity behavior analytics and link prediction for insider risk and over-privileged access [record_id:154]; point-process models for temporal authentication behavior [record_id:246]; TF-IDF similarity over transaction metadata, device identifiers, and behavioral signals for fraud-linkage [record_id:78]; fake-account burst detection through demographic-distribution anomalies [record_id:247]; spill-tree-like clustering and neighbors-of-neighbors methods for credential misuse/domain squatting [record_id:253]; and Automated Credential Threat Monitoring for leaked credentials [record_id:2268].

Access-control assurance methods include AI-agent validators for login and protected-resource access to find exploitable auth flaws [record_id:2326], test suites for account recovery evaluation [record_id:70], practical AD attack/defense labs with Azure-hosted VMs and logging configuration [record_id:2388], and physical access control hardening practices covering wiring, supervision, encryption, tamper resistance, and badge-reader configuration [record_id:2125].

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The strongest single record for emerging OAuth delegation risk is the Black Hat talk on connection-based OAuth in agentic AI and integration platforms. It is notable because it connects traditional web vulnerabilities to a newer architectural trend: cloud token services and proprietary OAuth “connections” used by AI agents and low-code tools [record_id:3]. It pairs naturally with the Azure API Connections record, which gives a platform-specific path through token stores and consent infrastructure toward cross-tenant Key Vault leakage and broad API abuse [record_id:15].

For Entra/OAuth authorization failure, the internal Microsoft applications record is representative because it explicitly argues that the authentication/authorization boundary remains misunderstood and that example code and shared responsibility contribute to critical misconfiguration [record_id:20]. For general OAuth/OIDC practitioner education, the BSidesLV token replay talk is a concise representative record because it lists specific misconfigurations and demonstrates replay with OWASP ZAP [record_id:2383].

For enterprise identity compromise, the AD-to-Entra lateral movement talk is one of the most important because it frames hybrid trust as an uncertain and evolving boundary and emphasizes undocumented flows, MFA bypass, tenant compromise, and limited logging [record_id:76]. The GPO-as-C2 record is important because it reframes ordinary AD management machinery as an attacker primitive [record_id:1929]. The dMSA/BadSuccessor records are notable because they concern a new Windows Server 2025 identity feature and claim impact even in domains not using dMSAs [record_id:1966] [record_id:2547]. The Mac PRT record is important because it extends well-known Windows token theft concepts to macOS and Intune-managed environments [record_id:1987].

For passwordless and passkeys, the FIDO2 CTRAPS record is especially strong because it names attack classes, transport layers, tool implementations, and concrete impacts such as credential wiping, factory resets, and tracking [record_id:1992]. The passkey WebAuthn proxying record is notable because it targets relying-party assumptions around attestation and metadata checks [record_id:1997]. The synced-passkey phishing record is short but important because it challenges the “unphishable” marketing narrative [record_id:2080]. The Windows Hello for Business reverse-engineering record is notable for its depth into biometric storage and ESS/TPM claims [record_id:45].

For detection, the MFA bypass phishing record provides one of the clearest empirical workflows: reverse-proxy kits, point-in-time DNS enrichment, targeted filtering, labeled malicious authentications, and comparison of classification methods [record_id:156]. The Azure sign-in graph-embedding record is also representative of ML-based identity detection and provides more methodological detail than many talk abstracts [record_id:175]. The point-process authentication record supplies a rigorous statistical framing for temporal dependency in login behavior [record_id:246].

For NHI and secretless identity, the workload identity talk is the most practical architectural record because it demonstrates AWS-to-Azure Kubernetes authentication without manually managed secrets using IRSA and Azure Workload ID [record_id:2396]. The OWASP NHI governance record is important for downstream agents tracking industry framing and policy priorities [record_id:2572]. The capability-based agent authorization record is notable because it gives a concrete alternative to coarse IAM roles and prompt-injection filters for delegated AI-agent authority [record_id:2370].

For access-control failures outside traditional IdPs, the GraphQL/REST Feeld case study [record_id:2018], dealer-platform vehicle-control case study [record_id:2036], Google Cloud IAP exfiltration record [record_id:2495], and AI “Auth-by-One” agent exploitation record [record_id:2326] collectively show that authorization failures often live in application-specific business logic and integration edges rather than in the identity provider itself.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The records are mostly abstracts, so many claims are not independently verifiable from the provided text. Several records promise live demos, tools, or disclosures, but the corpus does not include full papers, exploit details, patches, vendor responses, CVEs, or post-disclosure timelines. This is especially relevant for high-impact claims about Microsoft internal app access [record_id:20], Azure cross-tenant Key Vault leakage [record_id:15], dMSA Kerberos ticket abuse [record_id:1966] [record_id:2547], HashiCorp Vault and CyberArk Conjur RCE [record_id:88], Google Cloud IAP exfiltration [record_id:2495], and cloud VPN/ZTNA authentication bypass [record_id:2136].

There is limited comparative evidence about prevalence. Many records show that a weakness exists or that a technique worked in specific environments, but fewer quantify how common the misconfiguration is across enterprises. Exceptions include empirical or production-scale records such as RBA evaluation [record_id:126], MFA phishing detection [record_id:156], Azure sign-in graph modeling [record_id:175], password cracking at scale [record_id:2417], password expiry metrics [record_id:2500], and fake-account burst detection [record_id:247].

The corpus also leaves open how defenders should prioritize overlapping identity risks.
