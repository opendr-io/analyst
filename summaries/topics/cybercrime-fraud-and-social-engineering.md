# Topic: Cybercrime fraud and social engineering

## Executive Summary

Across the 16 records, the topic is framed as a broad 2025 security research landscape around cybercrime ecosystems, scam operations, social engineering, criminal monetization, and defensive disruption. The records are dominated by DEF CON 33 and BSidesLV 2025 talks. They collectively portray fraud and social engineering as no longer isolated “phishing email” or “romance scam” problems, but as mature ecosystems involving darknet markets, cryptocurrency scams, pig-butchering compounds, ransomware leak economies, botnet proxy services, remote-work infiltration, credential theft workflows, AI-enabled phishing infrastructure, and psychology-driven victim manipulation.

Several recurring trends stand out. First, cybercrime is described as industrialized and transnational: darknet markets evolve, collapse, and re-form around scams and exit strategies [record_id:1916]; pig-butchering scams are presented as a $75 billion global criminal industry tied to forced labor in scam compounds [record_id:1962]; and botnet infrastructure can support criminal proxy markets across 180 countries [record_id:2550]. Second, records repeatedly emphasize the human element: scammers exploit trust, optimism bias, cultural norms, staff cooperation, remote-work assumptions, and outdated security awareness models [record_id:2472] [record_id:2555] [record_id:2582]. Third, AI and automation are treated as accelerants, especially in phishing infrastructure generation and agent-mediated scam surfaces [record_id:2394] [record_id:2466], as well as in synthetic credential attacks [record_id:2556]. Fourth, multiple talks focus on “active defense” or disruption: baiting scammers [record_id:2150], tracking crypto scam funds [record_id:1920], using open-source tools against pig-butchering operations [record_id:1962], recovering stolen credentials through “Phish-Back” defenses [record_id:2502], and coordinating botnet takedowns [record_id:2550].

The evidence is strongest as a map of what researchers, practitioners, and conference speakers considered salient in 2025. It is weaker as independently verified empirical evidence, because the records are mostly abstracts rather than full papers or datasets. Some include quantitative claims—such as “more than $75 billion stolen globally” in pig-butchering scams [record_id:1962], a million-user phishing simulation dataset [record_id:2582], and 180-country proxy infrastructure [record_id:2550]—but the provided text does not include methodological detail sufficient to validate those numbers. The records are therefore best used as leads for downstream research: they identify important cases, proposed defensive approaches, and emerging questions, but they do not by themselves settle factual disputes.

## Research Landscape

The corpus consists mainly of conference talk descriptions from DEF CON 33 and BSidesLV 2025. DEF CON records in this set focus heavily on scams, darknet markets, cryptocurrency fraud, pig-butchering, and scam baiting [record_id:1916] [record_id:1920] [record_id:1962] [record_id:2150]. BSidesLV records expand the scope into AI-enabled phishing infrastructure, agentic-web scam surfaces, ransomware leak analysis, physical intrusion through social engineering, remote-work fraud, credential-defense strategies, ransomware as societal disruption, botnet takedowns, synthetic password attacks, underground culture, and security awareness reform [record_id:2394] [record_id:2466] [record_id:2471] [record_id:2472] [record_id:2493] [record_id:2502] [record_id:2517] [record_id:2550] [record_id:2555] [record_id:2556] [record_id:2557] [record_id:2582].

The sources are not uniform. Some are explicitly about cybercrime ecosystems, such as darknet markets [record_id:1916], crypto scams [record_id:1920], pig-butchering operations [record_id:1962], ransomware [record_id:2517], botnet proxy services [record_id:2550], and underground culture [record_id:2557]. Others are adjacent but relevant because they show how fraud intersects with identity theft, physical security, data leaks, remote hiring, or AI-mediated interaction [record_id:2471] [record_id:2472] [record_id:2493] [record_id:2502] [record_id:2556]. A few are brief or logistical; for example, the Scam Village record simply says, “Join Scam Village to safely bait scammers,” but it still reflects a community practice around scam engagement and anti-scam education [record_id:2150].

The overall research area looks interdisciplinary. It combines threat intelligence, fraud analysis, psychology, identity security, infrastructure takedowns, incident response, privacy, policy, and user education. Several records explicitly reject a purely technical model of cybercrime. The “remote grift” talk argues that hybrid crimes blend old-fashioned con artistry with technical tools, and that victims can be manipulated into committing crimes themselves [record_id:2555]. The “PEBKAC Rebooted” talk argues that breaches do not happen because users are “stupid,” but because attackers exploit predictable cognitive biases and misplaced confidence in technical controls [record_id:2582]. The physical intrusion talk similarly points to social engineering and “inadvertent staff cooperation” as persistent weaknesses even where technical controls or stricter security practices exist [record_id:2472].

At the same time, several records emphasize infrastructure and monetization. Darknet market operations are discussed as multi-level ecosystems involving carding forums, large platforms, scams, exit strategies, market collapse, forged documents, and operational screenshots or chat logs [record_id:1916]. Crypto scam analysis follows a fraud workflow and tracks victim funds after transfer [record_id:1920]. Pig-butchering is presented as a large-scale transnational business model backed by criminal syndicates and scam compounds [record_id:1962]. Botnet infrastructure is described as the basis for the NSOCKS criminal proxy network and linked to multiple criminal groups and APTs [record_id:2550]. Ransomware is presented not only as extortion but as a disruptive ecosystem affecting critical social services and functions [record_id:2517].

## Major Themes And Trends

### Industrialization of scams and fraud operations

A central theme is the movement from individual scams to organized, scalable operations. The darknet-market record explicitly describes “over 10 years” of experience across “every level of darknet markets,” from carding forums to “multi-million dollar platforms,” and frames scams as evolving from simple carding to sophisticated exit strategies [record_id:1916]. This record also emphasizes marketplace fragility, claiming that markets “always collapse” and that psychological tactics, such as a forged document, can destroy a marketplace overnight [record_id:1916]. The talk’s value for downstream research is that it points to internal market dynamics—trust, sabotage, exits, panic, and collapse—as core parts of criminal economics.

Pig-butchering is presented as another industrialized model. The raw text claims more than $75 billion has been stolen globally and says thousands of trafficked people are forced to run scams from compounds across Asia [record_id:1962]. It describes these operations as “military-grade psychological ops” backed by transnational crime syndicates, suggesting a blend of organized crime, human trafficking, and large-scale social engineering [record_id:1962]. This record is highly relevant to victim targeting and monetization because it frames heartbreak, trust-building, and financial manipulation as a profitable business model rather than a series of isolated romance scams [record_id:1962].

The crypto scam record also treats fraud as a workflow. It describes a fraud story with a “unique financial angle,” then a breakdown of the scam workflow, tracking of funds as they move from the victim’s possession, and advice for prevention [record_id:1920]. This suggests that modern scam analysis increasingly combines narrative reconstruction, actor history, workflow modeling, and financial tracing.

### Human psychology as a primary attack surface

Multiple records argue that social engineering succeeds because it exploits normal human behavior rather than ignorance alone. The “remote grift” talk describes a hybrid crime where a grifter manipulates a trusting victim, causing the victim to unknowingly commit a crime; “the only fingerprints at the scene belong to the mark” [record_id:2555]. That framing is important because it shows a victimization pathway in which the target becomes both harmed and legally exposed, complicating incident response and law-enforcement narratives [record_id:2555].

The same talk criticizes current security awareness training as “checkbox security” that overemphasizes “don’t click” guidance and yesterday’s high-tech crimes, while failing to address gut instinct and human psychology [record_id:2555]. This connects directly to the “PEBKAC Rebooted” record, which argues that attackers hijack predictable cognitive “bugs in wetware,” including optimism bias, Dunning-Kruger effects, and “technology bias,” described as overconfidence that EDR, AI mail filters, or zero-trust controls will catch everything [record_id:2582]. That record claims users who score high on technology bias click links 140% more often, and that click-through rates double if phishing simulations pause for three months [record_id:2582]. It proposes continuous “people-patching,” instant feedback loops, and neuroscience-based interventions that allegedly reduce real-phish clicks eightfold and triple report rates [record_id:2582].

Physical security records also reinforce the human theme. The Japan/U.S. physical intrusion talk cites the DarkVishnya case, where banks lost tens of millions due to physical intrusion and malicious devices, and says Japan’s relatively lenient security can leave organizations vulnerable to intrusions through social engineering and inadvertent staff cooperation [record_id:2472]. It also states that the U.S. has stricter measures due to higher risk awareness but remains susceptible to human-factor vulnerabilities [record_id:2472]. This suggests that cultural context shapes how social engineering manifests, but does not eliminate it.

### AI as an accelerant for phishing and scam ecosystems

Several records identify AI as a force multiplier for fraud. The phishing infrastructure talk investigates how attackers can use LLMs and AI agents to autonomously create phishing infrastructure, including domain registration, DNS configuration, hosting spoofed websites, and harvesting credentials [record_id:2394]. It explicitly distinguishes this from earlier work on persuasive phishing emails, shifting focus to back-end automation of the phishing lifecycle [record_id:2394]. It also proposes evaluating frontier and open-source models, including DeepSeek, Claude Sonnet, and GPT-4o, with and without human intervention, using metrics such as task completion rate, cost, time, and human intervention required [record_id:2394]. This record is a strong indicator that researchers are concerned not merely with AI-generated lures, but with end-to-end phishing scalability.

The “Agentic Web” talk broadens the threat model. It envisions a digital landscape where AI agents perform tasks such as buying goods and services, job searches, home searches, and relationship searches on behalf of humans [record_id:2466]. The key fraud implication is “bot-vs-bot warfare,” where scammers unleash agents to exploit agents acting for unsuspecting humans [record_id:2466]. This reframes social engineering: attackers may increasingly target delegated machine decision-makers and the human trust placed in those agents, rather than targeting only human inboxes.

AI also appears in credential attack evolution. The synthetic passwords record says attackers are mass-using fabricated, non-reused credentials generated algorithmically, “either with scripts or using AI,” to evade traditional credential-stuffing and password-reuse detection [record_id:2556]. These inputs are described as high-entropy or randomly generated, intended to pollute logs, obscure real attack traffic, and overwhelm detection systems [record_id:2556]. This shows a defensive blind spot: controls tuned for reused leaked credentials may miss attacks that use fabricated inputs as cover or noise.

### Active defense, disruption, and community mobilization

Another major trend is a move beyond passive awareness toward active disruption. The Scam Village record is minimal, but its invitation to “safely bait scammers” reflects a community-oriented anti-scam practice [record_id:2150]. The crypto scam record describes researchers observing scams, analyzing workflows, tracking funds, and developing defense methods [record_id:1920]. The pig-butchering record explicitly calls for open-source tools, hacker ingenuity, public education, community mobilization, and disruption of criminal networks through Operation Shamrock [record_id:1962].

The “Phish-Back” record proposes a defensive strategy to recover stolen credentials that otherwise would not end up on marketplaces [record_id:2502]. It frames this as “playing the same game as our opponents” after a crisis caused by spear phishing, while acknowledging challenges in exposing defensive phishing pages to the internet, managing fingerprinting technologies, and filtering internet noise [record_id:2502]. This is notable because it occupies a gray area between defensive deception, credential recovery, and potentially controversial mimicry of attacker infrastructure.

The botnet takedown record is one of the clearest examples of operational disruption. Black Lotus Labs reportedly took down the “ngioweb” botnet in November 2024, which formed the basis of the NSOCKS criminal proxy network [record_id:2550]. The record says the network had proxies in 180 countries, was popular with criminal groups, and had ties to APTs [record_id:2550]. The team spent a year tracking nodes and C2s, then built cooperation with ISPs and ASNs to simultaneously deny traffic to known control layers [record_id:2550]. The threat actors allegedly responded through social media, cease-and-desist letters, and attempted DDoS activity against the company [record_id:2550]. This record highlights both the importance and difficulty of sustained cybercrime infrastructure disruption.

### Cybercrime as societal and geopolitical disruption

Several records broaden fraud and cybercrime beyond financial loss. The ransomware record argues that ransomware is among the most prevalent and expensive cyber incidents and may be more pervasive and disruptive than outright disruptive cyberattacks [record_id:2517]. It focuses on impacts to critical social services and functions, and asks how unchecked ransomware operations may cause unacceptable disruption to vital services [record_id:2517]. It also argues that defensive countermeasures and preventative investment against ransomware can curtail more advanced actor operations, and that pervasive cyber threats may justify enhanced countermeasures to deny, deter, or degrade adversary capabilities [record_id:2517].

The remote-work infiltration record connects fraud to geopolitical funding. It describes a story involving a senior software engineer, a farmer, and a supply chain funding North Korea’s weapons programs through organizations “just like yours” [record_id:2493]. It links pandemic-era remote work and over-employment schemes to conditions enabling workforce infiltration and enrichment of the Kim regime [record_id:2493]. This record suggests that social engineering and employment fraud can become national-security concerns when remote hiring, identity verification, and organizational trust models are exploited.

The underground-culture record similarly argues that the boundaries between cybercrime, fraud, sextortion, and violent extremism have blurred [record_id:2557]. It claims the hacker underground changed over the past decade as infosec professionalization and bug bounties drew talent away, the underground lost its monopoly on knowledge, remaining participants increasingly turned to cybercrime, and Bitcoin price growth helped transform the scene into “The Com” [record_id:2557]. This is significant because it frames cybercrime prevention partly as youth diversion, culture change, and intervention before the next generation enters fraud communities [record_id:2557].

### Data leaks and secondary victimization

The ransomware leak PII record examines a downstream consequence of extortion: leaked data becomes an exposed resource for discovery, indexing, and potential abuse [record_id:2471]. The HIBR tool is described as crawling ransomware gang leak sites, downloading leaked material, and using OCR plus LLMs to identify PII in scanned IDs, contracts, HR PDFs, and other documents [record_id:2471]. The abstract stresses that the talk does not show PII, but knows where it is, and frames the work as involving uncomfortable data, legal ambiguity, and risk of liability [record_id:2471]. This contributes to the topic by showing how ransomware leaks can feed fraud ecosystems: passports, HR records, contracts, and identity documents can become raw material for identity theft, scams, and targeted social engineering.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

Several records describe analytical or defensive methods, though most are abstracts rather than implementation details.

One method is insider-style reconstruction of criminal markets. The darknet-market talk promises screenshots, chat logs, and operational details to explain market evolution, sabotage, exit strategies, and collapse dynamics [record_id:1916]. The method implied is historical and experiential: using internal artifacts and firsthand narratives to reconstruct how darknet markets operate and why they fail.

Another method is scam workflow analysis combined with financial tracing. The crypto scam talk describes introducing a scam scenario, reviewing actor history, observing the scam to develop defense methods, breaking down the workflow, and tracking funds after they leave the victim [record_id:1920]. This suggests an investigative model useful for researchers: document the lure, trace the interaction path, map monetization, follow funds, then derive victim-protection guidance.

For pig-butchering, the described approach includes exposing the scope of the scam, analyzing technical infrastructure, and using Operation Shamrock with open-source tools and community mobilization to educate targets and disrupt networks [record_id:1962]. The record does not name specific tools, but it emphasizes open-source tooling, hacker ingenuity, and community action [record_id:1962].

For phishing infrastructure automation, the BSidesLV AI-agent project proposes a structured evaluation of models tasked with domain registration, DNS configuration, landing-page deployment, and credential harvesting [record_id:2394]. The methodology includes comparing frontier and open-source models, testing with and without human intervention, and measuring task completion, cost, time, and required human input [record_id:2394]. This is one of the more concrete research designs in the corpus.

The Agentic Web talk describes conceptual threat modeling around AI agents, economic adoption pressures, real-world exploitation examples, and near-future scenarios [record_id:2466]. Its method appears to be scenario analysis: exploring how agent-mediated activity creates new scam surfaces and how attacker agents may manipulate victim agents.

The ransomware-leak record describes a technical pipeline: HIBR crawls ransomware leak sites, downloads material, and uses OCR plus LLMs to sift through scanned IDs, contracts, HR PDFs, and other documents for PII [record_id:2471]. This is relevant as a privacy-risk and fraud-prevention method, though the abstract notes legal and ethical ambiguity [record_id:2471].

The physical intrusion talk uses comparative case studies from Japan and the United States, based on penetration-testing experience, to identify detection gaps and social-engineering vulnerabilities [record_id:2472]. The method is practical red-team analysis with cultural comparison.

The “Phish-Back” talk describes a defensive phishing-page strategy for recovering stolen credentials that do not reach marketplaces [record_id:2502]. It highlights engineering challenges: managing dozens of fingerprinting technologies, filtering internet noise, and exposing defensive pages safely [record_id:2502]. This suggests a deception-based identity-defense workflow, but the abstract leaves open questions about ethics, legality, and operational safeguards.

The ransomware-as-disruption talk appears to use impact analysis: reviewing ransomware effects on critical social services and then linking ransomware defense to broader countermeasures against advanced actors [record_id:2517]. It is less a tool talk than a policy and ecosystem analysis [record_id:2517].

The botnet takedown talk describes a coalition-based interdiction method. Researchers tracked nodes and C2s for a year, built trust with ISPs and ASNs, and coordinated simultaneous denial of traffic to known control layers [record_id:2550]. It also studies adversary response, including coercive communications and DDoS attempts [record_id:2550]. This is a strong example of operational collaboration as a cybercrime countermeasure.

The synthetic-password record describes an attacker method rather than a defense: algorithmically generated or AI-shaped credentials are used at scale by botnets to evade credential-stuffing and password-reuse detection, pollute logs, and overwhelm detection systems [record_id:2556]. For defenders, the implication is the need for detection logic that distinguishes noise, camouflage, and real account attacks without relying solely on leaked-password reuse.

For human-centered defense, the remote-grift talk calls for security training that addresses psychology, instinct, and the human element rather than only technical “don’t click” messages [record_id:2555]. The PEBKAC record goes further, proposing continuous “people-patching,” instant feedback loops, and neuroscience-based interventions based on a dataset of one million phishing-simulation users and 170,000 perceptual survey responses [record_id:2582].

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The strongest representatives of criminal ecosystem analysis are the darknet-market, pig-butchering, botnet, ransomware, and underground-culture records. The darknet-market talk matters because it promises an insider view of carding forums, multi-million-dollar markets, exit scams, sabotage, and collapse dynamics [record_id:1916]. The pig-butchering talk matters because it connects scam workflows to transnational organized crime, forced labor, technical infrastructure, and community disruption [record_id:1962]. The botnet takedown talk matters because it gives a concrete case of infrastructure interdiction against the ngioweb botnet and NSOCKS proxy network, including international ISP/ASN coordination and adversary retaliation [record_id:2550]. The ransomware talk matters because it frames extortion as a societal-disruption problem affecting critical services rather than only a financial crime [record_id:2517]. The underground-culture talk matters because it links cybercrime to cultural shifts, Bitcoin, sextortion, and violent extremism, raising prevention questions beyond technical defense [record_id:2557].

The most representative records for scam and victim workflow analysis are the crypto scam, remote grift, and PEBKAC records. The crypto scam record provides a classic investigative structure: define the scam, observe the actors, analyze the workflow, trace funds, and provide victim-protection advice [record_id:1920]. The remote-grift record contributes an important legal and social twist: manipulated victims may be left appearing to be the perpetrators, producing “grave injustice” when incident response and training fail to account for deception dynamics [record_id:2555]. The PEBKAC record provides the most detailed quantitative claims about user behavior, including technology bias, click-rate increases after training pauses, and claimed improvements from continuous people-patching [record_id:2582].

The most important AI-related records are the AI phishing infrastructure study and the Agentic Web talk. The former is concrete and operational, describing domain registration, DNS setup, spoofed website deployment, and credential harvesting as tasks for LLM agents, measured by completion rate, cost, time, and human intervention [record_id:2394]. The latter is more strategic, arguing that scammers will target and exploit agents acting for humans in everyday tasks such as shopping, job seeking, housing, and relationships [record_id:2466]. Together they suggest that AI-enabled fraud research is expanding from message generation to lifecycle automation and agent-to-agent manipulation.

The most notable identity and credential records are “Phish-Back” and synthetic passwords. “Phish-Back” is notable because it proposes defensive deception to recover stolen credentials before they reach marketplaces, while acknowledging operational challenges around fingerprinting and noise [record_id:2502]. Synthetic passwords are notable because they describe a shift in attacker behavior from known leaked credentials or plausible guesses toward fabricated high-entropy inputs that defeat assumptions in credential-stuffing detection [record_id:2556].

The physical intrusion and ransomware-leak records broaden the fraud landscape. The physical intrusion record shows that social engineering is not limited to online scams; cultural expectations, low perceived crime rates, and staff cooperation can enable physical compromise [record_id:2472]. The ransomware-leak PII record shows how criminal leak sites create downstream identity and fraud risks, and how OCR plus LLMs can be used to locate sensitive documents in chaotic leak archives [record_id:2471].

The Scam Village “Video Team” record is brief but still representative of community practice: it explicitly invites participants to “safely bait scammers,” indicating a conference/community interest in scam-baiting as education or disruption [record_id:2150].

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The main limitation is evidentiary depth. Most records are talk abstracts, not full transcripts, papers, source code, datasets, or post-event reports. Claims about losses, scale, success rates, user behavior, and takedown outcomes are therefore leads rather than validated findings. For example, the pig-butchering abstract claims more than $75 billion stolen globally and thousands of trafficked people in scam compounds, but the provided text does not identify the data source or estimation method [record_id:1962]. The PEBKAC record cites a million-user phishing simulation dataset and 170,000 survey responses, but the abstract does not describe sampling, measurement, or independent validation [record_id:2582]. The botnet record claims proxies in 180 countries and ties to APTs, but the abstract does not provide indicators, attribution criteria, or measurement methodology [record_id:2550].

There is also limited comparative evidence across scam types. The records cover darknet markets, crypto scams, pig-butchering, phishing, ransomware, employment infiltration, botnets, physical intrusion, and underground culture, but they do not provide a unified taxonomy or comparable metrics for victimization, profitability, operational structure, or defensive success. Downstream researchers would need to build that comparative layer.

The AI-related records raise major open questions. The phishing infrastructure study proposes evaluating LLM agents’ ability to register domains, configure DNS, host spoofed sites, and harvest credentials [record_id:2394], but the abstract does not provide results. The Agentic Web talk warns of bot-vs-bot scam warfare and cognitive security risks [record_id:2466], but it is unclear from the record which examples are already observed and which are speculative. Future research should distinguish current attacker adoption from plausible near-future risk.

The legality and ethics of active defense remain underdeveloped in the records. Scam baiting [record_id:2150], “scamming scammers” [record_id:1962], “Phish-Back” credential recovery [record_id:2502], ransomware leak crawling [record_id:2471], and botnet takedown coordination [record_id:2550] all raise questions about authorization, evidence handling, safety, privacy, collateral damage, and jurisdiction. The abstracts acknowledge some ambiguity—especially the HIBR record’s “blurry legal zones” [record_id:2471] and the Phish-Back record’s challenges exposing defensive phishing pages [record_id:2502]—but do not resolve it.

The human-centered records suggest that conventional awareness training is inadequate, but the corpus does not provide enough detail to determine which interventions generalize. The remote-grift talk calls for training that “engage[s] both the brain and the gut” [record_id:2555], and the PEBKAC record claims continuous people-patching and feedback loops can reduce clicks and increase reporting [record_id:2582]. Open questions include how these programs perform across cultures, job roles, age groups, neurodiversity, remote workers, and high-stress operational environments.

The cybercrime-culture record raises prevention questions that are not answered elsewhere. If cybercrime, fraud, sextortion, and violent extremism increasingly overlap, as asserted in the underground-culture talk [record_id:2557], then purely technical interventions may be insufficient. Future research would need to examine recruitment pathways, community moderation, youth intervention, cryptocurrency incentives, law-enforcement strategy, and alternatives such as bug bounty or legitimate security career pipelines.
