# Topic: Security education community and conference operations

## Executive Summary

The records collectively depict security conferences as education ecosystems, not just research venues. The dominant material is conference programming from DEF CON 33, BSidesLV 2025, BSidesSF 2025, Prompt||GTFO, and [un]prompted 2026. These records emphasize opening and closing remarks, community interviews, contests and CTFs, workshops, social events, villages, speaker development, volunteer and organizer culture, and training formats designed to make security knowledge more accessible.

A major trend is the movement away from passive, lecture-only security education toward participatory formats: CTFs, tabletop roleplaying, hands-on workshops, fishbowl discussions, stakeholder breakouts, puzzle panels, pub quizzes, escape-room-like exercises, and “hotwash” review sessions [record_id:2385] [record_id:2425] [record_id:2432] [record_id:2508] [record_id:2509] [record_id:2577] [record_id:2579]. Several records explicitly criticize boring or ineffective training and propose human-centered, game-based, community-based, or emotionally engaging alternatives [record_id:2425] [record_id:2432] [record_id:2555].

Another major theme is community infrastructure: conferences support identity-based and affinity groups, volunteers, speakers, mentors, organizers, people in recovery, youth hackers, veterans, and local educators [record_id:2148] [record_id:2155] [record_id:2166] [record_id:2447] [record_id:2507] [record_id:2519] [record_id:2520] [record_id:2521] [record_id:2541]. DEF CON’s records particularly highlight community media and behind-the-scenes interviews, while BSidesLV records show a dense schedule of formal and informal events that sustain the conference as a social and professional network [record_id:2141] [record_id:2142] [record_id:2158] [record_id:2159] [record_id:2160] [record_id:2169] [record_id:2170].

Evidence strength is strongest for identifying what events existed, what topics organizers intended to cover, and what educational formats were promoted. It is weaker for assessing outcomes: the records rarely provide post-event evaluations, attendance data, pedagogical measurements, or participant feedback. The topic is therefore best understood as a map of security community practices and conference programming priorities rather than a rigorous evaluation of training effectiveness.

## Research Landscape

The corpus is largely composed of short conference abstracts, schedule entries, video descriptions, and community media blurbs. It includes several distinct kinds of records.

First, there are formal conference framing records: DEF CON 33 opening remarks by Jeff Moss / The Dark Tangent [record_id:2044], BSidesSF Day Two opening remarks [record_id:2245], BSidesLV opening and closing ceremony entries [record_id:2497] [record_id:2498] [record_id:2415], and multiple [un]prompted 2026 opening and closing words by Gadi Evron [record_id:2319] [record_id:2336] [record_id:2351] [record_id:2367]. These records establish that conference operations themselves are a documented part of the security community record.

Second, the dataset contains a large group of DEF CON 33 “Video Team” or interview-style records. These focus on the people, villages, communities, art, badges, traditions, and side events that make up the conference experience: HackerPager [record_id:2141], The Dark Tangent interview [record_id:2142], VetCon [record_id:2148], Crypto Privacy Village [record_id:2151], QueerCon [record_id:2155], Arts and Entertainment goon culture [record_id:2158], DDoS Community [record_id:2159], Illumicon [record_id:2160], speaking at DEF CON [record_id:2161], Torvik from Tulip Tree Tech [record_id:2163], DEF CON Run lore [record_id:2165], DEF CON NextGen youth hacking [record_id:2166], KevOps pool party culture [record_id:2168], badge and visual style design [record_id:2169], and Retro Tech / Badge Life [record_id:2170]. These records show a conference culture where media production, interviews, lore, and community storytelling are integral.

Third, BSidesLV 2025 contributes many schedule entries for community events, career development, training workshops, meet-ups, and social programming. Some are minimal logistical entries, such as “Board Game Night” [record_id:2404], “Lunch, Tuesday” [record_id:2483], “Happy Hour” events [record_id:2454] [record_id:2455], and pool or karaoke events [record_id:2399] [record_id:2400]. Others contain richer abstracts about educational practice, such as community security education for marginalized groups [record_id:2416], secure coding education for high school and university students [record_id:2452], roleplaying-based incident response training [record_id:2425], and cybersecurity policy workshops using stakeholder exercises [record_id:2579].

Fourth, the corpus includes conference culture and history records. Phrack’s 40-year history connects hacker zines, underground publishing, information security, and digital dissent [record_id:2143]. BSidesLV’s sticker-preservation talk treats laptop stickers as artifacts of hacker identity and community memory [record_id:2477]. The BSidesSF 15-year behind-the-scenes panel foregrounds organizational history and survival [record_id:2307]. These records broaden the topic from event logistics to cultural continuity.

Finally, a smaller set addresses creative or alternative conference formats. Prompt||GTFO includes an AI-generated cybersecurity music performance using Suno [record_id:2202]. DEF CON includes Hacker Jeopardy [record_id:1910], an improv slide game [record_id:1927], and a puzzle panel from a team involved in contests, CTFs, TFH, Crash&Compile, and Dungeons@Defcon [record_id:1911]. [un]prompted includes a poem to open the event [record_id:2320], flash talks [record_id:2366], micro talks about tools created at the conference [record_id:2352], and an opening critique that “research conferences aren’t effective” because attendees need more intentional ways to find who they can help and who can help them [record_id:2319].

## Major Themes And Trends

### Conferences as learning communities rather than only lecture tracks

The records consistently portray security conferences as multi-layered learning environments. Formal talks and workshops are present, but they sit alongside villages, CTFs, mentor meet-ups, community receptions, informal games, social gatherings, and affinity spaces.

BSidesLV is especially explicit about this: it includes training tracks, career workshops, social events, CTF scheduling, meet-ups, receptions, and support gatherings in the same program. The result is a model of security education where learning occurs through structured instruction, competition, professional networking, and community participation [record_id:2385] [record_id:2405] [record_id:2416] [record_id:2425] [record_id:2447] [record_id:2507]. DEF CON’s video-team material shows a similar emphasis on villages, culture, traditions, and behind-the-scenes contributors [record_id:2151] [record_id:2158] [record_id:2169] [record_id:2170].

This broad definition of education includes beginner onboarding. A DEF CON BiC Village talk promises a “pain free intro” to Linux by comparing it to Windows and explaining key concepts for newcomers [record_id:1883]. Secure coding education is framed as something that can reach high school, computer science, and software engineering students through free workshops, GitHub materials, and YouTube walkthroughs [record_id:2452]. DEF CON NextGen is described as the “Youth Hacking component of DEF CON” [record_id:2166]. These records indicate a sustained concern with bringing new learners into security.

### Participatory, game-based, and experiential training

A dominant educational trend is the use of play, competition, roleplay, and scenario-based practice. The corpus contains many examples: Hacker Jeopardy [record_id:1910], puzzle contests and CTF culture [record_id:1911], Gen Sec CTF [record_id:2175], DEF CON contest updates [record_id:2177], BSidesLV’s PvJ CTF schedule and hotwash sessions [record_id:2508] [record_id:2509] [record_id:2510] [record_id:2511] [record_id:2512] [record_id:2513], and a Wi-Fi workshop that ends with a CTF so participants can apply what they learned [record_id:2578].

Roleplaying appears repeatedly as a training method. One BSidesLV workshop proposes transforming incident response exercises with dice rolls, character abilities, dynamic scenarios, MITRE ATT&CK-based challenges, and an “Incident Master” facilitation role [record_id:2425]. A related talk argues that Dungeons & Dragons-style collaborative storytelling can turn difficult training scenarios into engaging adventures and help learners move beyond isolated theoretical learning [record_id:2432]. Another event, “WhoAmI.exe - Can You Find The Threat?”, describes a role-action tabletop experience inspired by murder mystery dinners and escape rooms [record_id:2577]. Career training also borrows from tabletop RPG mechanics: two “Career Campaigns” workshops invite participants to transform their resumes into “character sheets” and reframe prior experience for cybersecurity hiring [record_id:2411] [record_id:2412].

The recurrence of these formats suggests a broader shift: security educators are trying to solve engagement problems by making learning social, embodied, narrative, and hands-on.

### Critique of traditional training and conference effectiveness

Several records explicitly or implicitly critique conventional approaches. The [un]prompted opening words frame the problem starkly: “Research conferences aren’t effective,” especially for introverts and for attendees who do not know whom they should speak with, who can help them, or whom they can help [record_id:2319]. This record points toward intentional conference design as a response to the limits of random encounters.

Training criticism appears in multiple BSidesLV abstracts. The incident-response roleplaying workshop opens by asking whether attendees are tired of “boring tabletop exercises that put your team to sleep” [record_id:2425]. The Dungeons & Dragons training talk similarly asks whether security training puts teams to sleep and argues that game-based learning can make professional development work better, not merely make it fun [record_id:2432]. A fraud/social-engineering talk criticizes security awareness training as checkbox-driven, overly focused on “don’t click,” and insufficiently attentive to human psychology and gut instinct [record_id:2555]. Together, these records show a recurring concern that security education must become more human-centered, engaging, and context-aware.

### Community care, inclusion, and affinity spaces

The records also show security conferences acting as social support infrastructure. QueerCon appears both as a DEF CON interview subject and as a BSidesLV mixer [record_id:2155] [record_id:2514]. Recovery Hackers provides supportive gatherings for people “taking Summer Camp one day at a time,” explicitly distinguishing itself from a formal 12-step meeting while offering a recurring space across Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday [record_id:2519] [record_id:2520] [record_id:2521]. VetCon is represented through a DEF CON community interview [record_id:2148]. The Proving Ground Mentors meet-up invites current and former mentors to gather with “fellow BSides heroes” [record_id:2507]. A speaker reception is designed for presenters to meet the Program Committee and one another [record_id:2541], and a volunteer reception recognizes conference labor [record_id:2398].

Community defense and public-good education are also significant. One BSidesLV talk describes teaching digital security and privacy to vulnerable communities, including activists, people accessing healthcare information, and marginalized groups, using threat modeling and storytelling frameworks for non-technical audiences [record_id:2416]. Another emphasizes practical local community engagement with low-income students and parents as a way both to build one’s own skills and cultivate security and privacy awareness [record_id:2405]. A cyber civil defense talk outlines pathways into volunteering, including state civilian cyber corps, university cyber clinics, nonprofit volunteer groups, and local security policy work [record_id:2422]. These records treat security education as mutual aid, civic defense, and community empowerment.

### Conference operations as a craft

A substantial portion of the corpus is about how conferences are made, sustained, and improved. BSidesSF’s 15-year panel promises stories from experienced organizing veterans about how the conference has “grown (and survived)” [record_id:2307]. Its wrap-up session offers behind-the-scenes numbers, CTF winners, and thanks to contributors [record_id:2318]. DEF CON includes interviews with the founder [record_id:2142], the opening remarks from the founder [record_id:2044], contest updates [record_id:2177], and behind-the-scenes interviews about community infrastructure and visual design [record_id:2169].

BSidesLV includes a “Global BSides Organizers Un-Conference Meet-up,” with light structure for regional organizers to make connections and learn from one another [record_id:2447]. That record is especially representative of conference operations as a knowledge-sharing practice among organizers, not simply a logistical necessity. Speaker development is also part of operations: one talk teaches how to write a CFP, explaining what review boards want and using BSidesLV, DEF CON, and Black Hat examples [record_id:2539]. Another hands-on workshop trains participants to become more confident public speakers [record_id:2441]. These records show a pipeline from attendee to speaker to organizer.

### Hacker culture, memory, media, and creative expression

Several records emphasize cultural preservation and expression. Phrack’s 40-year anniversary panel is a major example, linking hacker underground publishing, zines, digital dissent, and information security over four decades [record_id:2143]. Sticker preservation treats laptop stickers as markers of identity, belonging, and hacker history, with the practical problem of preserving them across laptop upgrades [record_id:2477]. DEF CON badge life and retro tech appear as cultural practices in their own right [record_id:2170], while the DEF CON badge and visual style interview describes the craft of creating the convention’s visual environment [record_id:2169].

Creative and entertainment programming includes Hacker Jeopardy [record_id:1910], “Whose Slide Is It Anyway?” improv [record_id:1927], karaoke [record_id:2399], pub quiz [record_id:2401], board game night [record_id:2404], pool parties [record_id:2168] [record_id:2400] [record_id:2542], and AI-generated cybersecurity-themed music at Prompt||GTFO using Suno [record_id:2202]. These are not peripheral to the topic: they demonstrate how security communities build shared identity, humor, memory, and informal learning.

### Career development, workforce, and professional identity inside community events

Many records are classified secondarily into professional development, but they matter here because conferences are presented as venues for career transition, hiring support, mentoring, and worker identity. BSidesLV’s Hire Ground programming includes mixers [record_id:2459] [record_id:2460], resume reviews across multiple days [record_id:2461] [record_id:2462] [record_id:2463] [record_id:2464] [record_id:2465], and a Hire Ground panel [record_id:2560]. Career workshops use RPG metaphors to help people reframe experience for cybersecurity hiring [record_id:2411] [record_id:2412]. A keynote-style talk argues that cybersecurity success depends not just on being “the best you,” but on team, users, community, and self-care [record_id:2444]. Another talk addresses generational knowledge sharing and collaboration in cybersecurity [record_id:2558]. A labor-focused talk argues that the InfoSec community should build worker power through unions and the labor movement [record_id:2566].

These records indicate that conferences function as labor-market and professional-identity institutions, not only technical education venues.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The educational methods discussed across the records are diverse.

Beginner-friendly analogy and conceptual demystification appear in the Linux introduction, which promises comparisons to Windows, explanations of key concepts, and practical starting tips [record_id:1883]. Secure coding education is approached through free workshops, a public GitHub repository, and a YouTube channel with walkthroughs [record_id:2452]. Public-facing security education uses threat modeling adapted to different identities and storytelling frameworks for non-technical audiences [record_id:2416].

Hands-on technical workshops rely on prepared environments and toolchains. The cryptographic attacks workshop provides a VM and a Python tool for implementing attacks, with overlap with Cryptopals-style challenges, and exercises ranging from decrypting ciphertext to recovering private keys and creating TLS and SSH private key files [record_id:2476]. The Wi-Fi workshop uses Kali Linux, 802.11 cards in monitor mode, PCAP collection, Wireshark filters, and tools such as `iw`, `iwconfig`, `hostapd`, `wpa_cli`, `wpa_supplicant`, Aircrack-ng, Wifite, Airgeddon, Reaver, and Wacker, culminating in a CTF and take-home lab setup [record_id:2578].

Scenario-based and game-based approaches are especially prominent. Incident response roleplaying uses dice rolls, character abilities, dynamic scenarios, MITRE ATT&CK, small-group scenario design, peer testing, and facilitation by an “Incident Master” [record_id:2425]. Dungeons & Dragons-style training uses structured play and collaborative storytelling to reduce professional barriers and improve engagement [record_id:2432]. A role-action tabletop exercise draws on murder mystery and escape-room formats to teach threat identification [record_id:2577]. Career workshops use the “character sheet” metaphor for resumes and “reskilling” or “dual-classing” to help participants navigate hiring [record_id:2411] [record_id:2412].

Conference-improvement tooling appears in the [un]prompted “Vibe Coded” micro talks, where participants “vibecoded to make the conference better” and created tools at the conference [record_id:2352]. Creative community media uses AI music generation: Rotem Bar uses Suno to compose cybersecurity-themed tracks from lyrics he writes, including a custom song celebrating event presenters and community [record_id:2202]. DEF CON’s HackerPager interview suggests hardware or communications-oriented community tooling, though the record only states that Silk interviewed the team behind HackerPager and does not describe the system’s technical details [record_id:2141].

Conference facilitation methods include openings, closings, flash talks, receptions, meet-ups, hotwashes, and organizer unconferences. [un]prompted uses opening words, closing words, an opening poem, flash talks, and micro talks as part of its event structure [record_id:2319] [record_id:2320] [record_id:2336] [record_id:2351] [record_id:2366] [record_id:2367]. BSidesLV’s CTF hotwashes suggest structured post-activity reflection [record_id:2508] [record_id:2509]. The Global BSides Organizers Un-Conference Meet-up adds “a little light structure” to support topic gauging and non-blocking conversations among regional organizers [record_id:2447]. The cybersecurity policy workshop uses fishbowl discussions and stakeholder breakout sessions to help participants analyze policy problems and develop recommendations [record_id:2579].

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

Several records are especially representative of the topic.

The [un]prompted opening critique, “Research conferences aren’t effective,” is notable because it directly theorizes conference design. It identifies a social coordination problem: attendees may not know whom to speak with, who can help them, or whom they can help, and random encounters only reach a few people. The phrase “We have a plan” suggests intentional event architecture as a remedy, though the record does not provide the plan’s details [record_id:2319].

The BSidesSF “15 Years of BSidesSF: Behind the Scenes AMA” is important as an explicit reflection on conference sustainability. It frames conference organizing as a long-term practice involving growth, survival, and institutional memory, voiced by experienced organizing veterans [record_id:2307]. The BSidesSF wrap-up record also matters because it mentions behind-the-scenes numbers, CTF winners, and thanking contributors, capturing how conferences close the loop with participants and volunteers [record_id:2318].

The Phrack anniversary panel is one of the strongest culture/history records. It spans hacking, zines, digital dissent, information security, and the role of Phrack over 40 years, including a reboot and anniversary production with authors and contributors [record_id:2143]. It anchors the corpus in a longer tradition of community publishing and hacker knowledge exchange.

The roleplaying incident response workshop and Dungeons & Dragons training talk are central evidence for the gamification trend. The workshop is highly concrete, describing dice, character abilities, MITRE ATT&CK-based challenges, scenario creation, group testing, facilitation skills, and take-away resources [record_id:2425]. The related talk supplies the pedagogical argument: game-based learning creates collaborative environments where participants engage experientially rather than memorizing concepts in isolation [record_id:2432].

The community defense talk is a key record for public-interest security education. It connects digital security and privacy training to activists, healthcare access, marginalized communities, authoritarian threats, harm reduction, identity-specific threat models, and storytelling for non-technical audiences [record_id:2416]. It is complemented by the local community engagement talk involving K-12 students, families, privacy, security, and open source [record_id:2405], and by the cyber civil defense volunteering talk [record_id:2422].

The CFP-writing talk is one of the clearest records about growing the speaker pipeline. It explains CFPs, review boards, and what reviewers want, while explicitly framing speaking as a way to “give back” to the community [record_id:2539]. The public speaking workshop similarly turns speaker development into hands-on practice where everyone delivers a short talk [record_id:2441].

DEF CON’s video-team records are notable less for individual technical depth than for their breadth of community documentation. They cover founder interviews [record_id:2142], community interviews [record_id:2148] [record_id:2155] [record_id:2159], village offerings [record_id:2151] [record_id:2176], youth hacking [record_id:2166], conference art and badges [record_id:2169] [record_id:2170], contests [record_id:2175] [record_id:2177], traditions such as the DEF CON Run [record_id:2165], and entertainment/social culture [record_id:2168]. Together, they demonstrate how conference media functions as cultural memory.

BSidesLV’s social and support schedule is also representative. Karaoke, pub quiz, board game night, pool parties, happy hours, receptions, Queercon Mixer, Recovery Hackers, and staff events show how conference operations include deliberate social design and attendee care [record_id:2399] [record_id:2401] [record_id:2404] [record_id:2400] [record_id:2454] [record_id:2455] [record_id:2538] [record_id:2541] [record_id:2514] [record_id:2519] [record_id:2520] [record_id:2521] [record_id:2542].

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The records are strong evidence for what conferences advertised, scheduled, or described. They are weak evidence for what actually happened in the rooms, how participants responded, and whether the educational goals were achieved. Many records are one-line schedule entries, especially social events and ceremonies [record_id:2398] [record_id:2400] [record_id:2415] [record_id:2483] [record_id:2497]. Others are video descriptions without transcripts, so they establish the topic of an interview or talk but not its detailed content [record_id:2141] [record_id:2163] [record_id:2372].

The corpus leaves several open questions:

- How effective are game-based and roleplaying security trainings compared with conventional tabletop exercises or lectures? The records argue for engagement and practical value, but do not provide measured outcomes [record_id:2425] [record_id:2432] [record_id:2577].
- What specific plan did [un]prompted use to make conference interactions more effective, and how was success assessed? The opening record identifies the problem but not the implementation details [record_id:2319].
- How inclusive and accessible are the community spaces in practice? QueerCon, Recovery Hackers, VetCon, youth programming, mentors, and speaker receptions are documented, but the records do not include participant experiences or accessibility analysis [record_id:2155] [record_id:2148] [record_id:2166] [record_id:2507] [record_id:2519] [record_id:2541].
- How are conference labor, volunteer sustainability, and organizer burnout handled? BSidesSF and BSidesLV point to organizing history, volunteer receptions, and organizer meet-ups, but do not provide operational playbooks or staffing metrics [record_id:2307] [record_id:2398] [record_id:2447].
- How should security awareness training be redesigned around human psychology? The remote-grift talk strongly critiques checkbox training and calls for humanized training, but the record only summarizes the proposed direction [record_id:2555].
- How do creative media, AI-generated music, badges, stickers, and hacker zines affect community cohesion and learning? The corpus shows these practices matter culturally, but does not evaluate their educational impact [record_id:2202] [record_id:2169] [record_id:2477] [record_id:2143].

There is also some source noise. One BSidesSF wrap-up record includes conference description text followed by sponsor or advertisement-like material about “Discover Claude,” “Join Anthropic,” and identity security imagery. This should be treated as raw source contamination or schedule-page residue rather than as part of the session’s core meaning [record_id:2318].
