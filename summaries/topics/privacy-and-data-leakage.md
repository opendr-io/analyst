# Topic: Privacy and data leakage

## Executive Summary

The 66 records collectively portray privacy and data leakage as a broad, fast-moving security research area spanning AI systems, mobile and web platforms, encrypted messaging, network metadata, surveillance infrastructure, SaaS and API failures, ransomware leak processing, and privacy-preserving computation. A major throughline is that systems marketed as private often still create exploitable data flows, metadata trails, side channels, or administrative access paths. This appears in scrutiny of Apple Intelligence data movement [record_id:5] [record_id:1957], Signal and Signal-like messaging systems [record_id:29] [record_id:1958] [record_id:2003], Google Privacy Sandbox APIs [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030], decentralized Nostr clients [record_id:39], and browser extensions/password managers [record_id:1851].

AI is a dominant privacy concern. Records emphasize new “data sinks” and leak paths in agentic AI, persistent memory, automated workflows, RAG, embeddings, enterprise AI search, KYC agents, health AI, AI browser extensions, and prompt leakage [record_id:111] [record_id:1943] [record_id:2220] [record_id:2270] [record_id:2328] [record_id:2041] [record_id:2237]. The records also include countervailing privacy-preserving approaches: secure multiparty computation, confidential computing, Trusted Execution Environments, oblivious computation, privacy-preserving AI threat fingerprints, and data deletion/access-control architectures for LLMs [record_id:149] [record_id:151] [record_id:2054] [record_id:2294] [record_id:2302] [record_id:2327] [record_id:2343].

Another recurring theme is that privacy leakage does not require direct content access. DNS traffic can fingerprint users and devices [record_id:81], delivery receipts can expose online status and device activity [record_id:2003], TPMS radio signals can enable vehicle tracking [record_id:2048], keyboard acoustics and emanations can leak plaintext before encryption [record_id:1938], and advertising ecosystems can replace third-party cookies with server-side data harvesting [record_id:2093]. Several records frame privacy as a civil liberties and community defense problem, especially for journalists, activists, marginalized communities, students, patients, border travelers, and people under authoritarian surveillance [record_id:102] [record_id:103] [record_id:1975] [record_id:2392] [record_id:2416] [record_id:2421].

Evidence strength varies. Some records describe concrete empirical studies, CVEs, proof-of-concept attacks, large-scale measurements, or named tools, such as the 1.5 billion DNS request study [record_id:81], Apple Intelligence traffic/OS inspection [record_id:5] [record_id:1957], Nostr exploit work [record_id:39], Rayhunter cellular simulator detection [record_id:1889] [record_id:1922], HIBR ransomware PII extraction [record_id:2470] [record_id:2471], Curl MPC evaluation [record_id:149], and browser extension clickjacking against password managers [record_id:1851]. Other records are policy panels, workshops, biographies, or high-level calls to action; they are important for context but provide thinner technical evidence [record_id:2122] [record_id:2127] [record_id:2139] [record_id:2469] [record_id:2535].

## Research Landscape

The records come mainly from security conference abstracts and talk descriptions across Black Hat, DEF CON, CAMLIS, BSidesSF, BSidesLV, Prompt||GTFO, and [un]prompted. They are not a uniform literature corpus: some are academic-style abstracts with evaluations, some are conference talk blurbs, some are keynotes or workshops, and some are short panel descriptions or speaker bios. The most technically specific records tend to describe attacks on deployed systems, privacy-preserving system designs, or measurement studies. The less technical records still reveal what communities are treating as privacy-relevant: student data pipelines, patient rights, digital rights policy, community privacy education, border device searches, and movement-building around data sovereignty.

Black Hat and DEF CON records dominate the practical offensive privacy angle. Black Hat includes Apple Intelligence privacy analysis [record_id:5], Signal privacy guarantees and linked-device risk [record_id:29], political campaign personal information sharing via fake identities [record_id:34], Nostr encrypted DM leakage and impersonation [record_id:39], DNS-based user tracking [record_id:81], Citizen Lab counter-intelligence and spyware investigations [record_id:102], and constitutional threat modeling [record_id:103]. DEF CON extends this with talks on Apple Intelligence [record_id:1957], Apple ecosystem zero-days and data exposure [record_id:1994], Signalgate/TeleMessage plaintext message exposure [record_id:1958], browser extension clickjacking leaking password-manager data [record_id:1851], Privacy Sandbox deanonymization [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030], GraphQL/REST access-control exposure in a dating app [record_id:2018], and AI model/embedding “shadow data” extraction [record_id:1943].

CAMLIS contributes a strong machine-learning privacy thread. It includes agentic AI privacy framing [record_id:111], private LLM inference with MPC via Curl [record_id:149], AdapterSwap for continual training with deletion and access-control guarantees [record_id:151], XAI-enabled membership inference/model extraction [record_id:204], perceptual hashing client-side scanning risks in E2EE contexts [record_id:212], and older black-box model inversion/model family inference concerns [record_id:234]. This portion shows that privacy in ML is not limited to memorization; it includes inference from explanations, embeddings, access patterns, model APIs, training-data removal, and client-query exposure.

BSides and community-focused sources add applied privacy, governance, and harm-reduction themes. BSidesSF includes enterprise AI search threat modeling [record_id:2270], confidential computing [record_id:2294], PETs such as MPC and homomorphic encryption [record_id:2302], and SaaS data platform exposure risks [record_id:2473]. BSidesLV includes EFF policy discussion [record_id:2392], community privacy education [record_id:2416], border-search legal/security issues [record_id:2421], third-party risk and privacy regulation cost trends [record_id:2469], ransomware leak PII extraction [record_id:2470] [record_id:2471], invitation-system privacy breaches [record_id:2406], real-world augmented perception/surveillance tooling [record_id:2518], and lessons from sex work communities for technologists facing marginalization and abuse [record_id:2535].

Prompt||GTFO and [un]prompted records emphasize AI operations and enterprise AI security. They cover using Gemini to inventory AI browser extensions and analyze privacy policies [record_id:2220], multi-model orchestration to exfiltrate LLM safety rules/guidelines [record_id:2237], privacy-preserving AI fingerprints for cross-service threat intelligence [record_id:2327], AI-driven KYC pipelines that can be steered into cross-record reads/writes [record_id:2328], confidential AI using TEEs and remote attestation [record_id:2343], and capability-based AI security governance for a healthcare chatbot handling PHI [record_id:2347].

## Major Themes And Trends

### 1. AI systems create new privacy surfaces beyond classic model memorization

A central trend is that generative AI and agentic AI are treated as privacy infrastructure, not merely as models. The CAMLIS keynote on agentic AI argues that GenAI has moved from chat interfaces into autonomous agents that move data through tools, APIs, modalities, memories, workflows, and future ambient/robotic contexts, producing new “data sinks” and leak paths [record_id:111]. This framing recurs across records about Apple Intelligence data flows [record_id:5] [record_id:1957], enterprise AI search aggregating “juicy, sensitive corporate information” [record_id:2270], AI browser extensions and their privacy policies/data-processing locations [record_id:2220], AI-driven KYC document extraction agents that can become execution environments for data theft [record_id:2328], and health AI deployments in diagnostics, mental health chatbots, and EHR surveillance without clear patient consent [record_id:2041].

The AI privacy problem is also described as hidden or latent data exposure. “Shadow data” in fine-tuned models, RAG workflows, embeddings, and vector databases can allegedly expose PII and social security numbers through model inversion and embedding inversion [record_id:1943]. XAI methods can leak information useful for membership inference and model extraction [record_id:204]. Black-box predictive APIs can enable model inversion even when raw user data is not directly exposed [record_id:234]. Prompt leakage appears as another AI-specific disclosure channel, with multi-model orchestration used to exfiltrate safety filtering rules and guidelines from major LLMs [record_id:2237].

The records suggest a shift from “does the model memorize?” toward “where does sensitive context travel, persist, get summarized, get embedded, get indexed, get retrieved, get acted on, or get inspected by third parties?” This is visible in persistent memories and automated workflows [record_id:111], Apple Intelligence local-versus-server processing [record_id:5] [record_id:1957], enterprise AI search indexing corporate stores [record_id:2270], and KYC agents writing or reading across records [record_id:2328].

### 2. Privacy-preserving computation is presented as both necessary and still hard to deploy

Several records discuss technologies intended to preserve privacy while enabling computation. Curl uses secure multiparty computation to protect client queries and proprietary models, improving non-linear function evaluation with wavelet-encoded lookup tables and reporting large round/communication reductions versus CrypTen [record_id:149]. BSidesSF’s PETs introduction highlights MPC and homomorphic encryption across the data lifecycle [record_id:2302]. Confidential computing is presented as isolating cloud workloads and cryptographically proving what code ran [record_id:2294], while confidential AI extends this to prompts, context, sensitive data, and models during active processing using TEEs and remote attestation [record_id:2343]. Oblivious computation is framed as hiding data access patterns that encrypted databases otherwise leak, with applications in contact discovery, blockchains, LLMs, Signal, and Ethereum [record_id:2054].

The records also show privacy-preserving design at the application/ML lifecycle level. AdapterSwap organizes knowledge into dynamically composed low-rank adapters to support continual learning, user-based access controls, and document removal guarantees while reducing forgetting [record_id:151]. BinaryShield is described as a privacy-preserving fingerprinting system for sharing AI prompt-injection threat intelligence without exposing raw user prompts across organizational boundaries [record_id:2327].

A recurring tension is practicality. Curl explicitly targets MPC usability and communication overhead [record_id:149]. Confidential computing talks emphasize real-world deployment [record_id:2294] [record_id:2343]. Agentic AI privacy proposals include data minimization, intentional friction, computational offloading, distributed training, and TEEs, suggesting that no single PET is sufficient for emerging AI workflows [record_id:111].

### 3. Metadata and side channels remain powerful privacy leakage mechanisms

Many records stress that private content can be protected while behavior still leaks. DNS queries can fingerprint devices and track individuals across networks using frequency analysis, correlation metrics, and anomaly detection; the study claims analysis of more than 1.5 billion DNS requests from 30,000 iOS and Android devices, with accurate tracking possible from partial traffic [record_id:81]. E2EE messengers can leak online status, screen activity, and device usage via delivery receipts, and WhatsApp’s prekey handling can be attacked to degrade perfect forward secrecy [record_id:2003]. Encrypted databases can leak through access patterns unless oblivious computation is used [record_id:2054]. Keyboard attacks capture plaintext before encryption through keyloggers, RF/Van Eck-style emanations, wireless keyboard signals, and acoustic typing analysis [record_id:1938].

Physical and radio-layer metadata appear repeatedly. TPMS sensors broadcast tire data without encryption or authentication, making low-cost signal capture and vehicle tracking feasible [record_id:2048]. Rayhunter detects possible Stingray/cell-site simulator activity by collecting cellular control-plane traffic from a low-cost hotspot [record_id:1922], and a related RF Village talk describes open-source cellular test beds for testing Rayhunter and developing detections [record_id:1889]. Russia’s TSPU censorship architecture is described as in-path and closer to end hosts, enabling more granular targeted attacks and interacting dangerously with a domestic certificate authority requirement [record_id:1970].

### 4. “Privacy-preserving” or “secure” products can fail through implementation details, defaults, and ecosystem complexity

A strong theme is skepticism toward privacy claims. Apple Intelligence is marketed around local models and Private Cloud Compute, but both Black Hat and DEF CON versions of AppleStorm describe traffic analysis and OS inspection to identify what information is accessed, how it moves, and where it is transmitted, with some flows raising privacy concerns [record_id:5] [record_id:1957]. Another Apple ecosystem talk claims zero-days and subtle issues in Siri, Spotlight, Safari, Apple Intelligence, and support systems that could disclose sensitive data, bypass Face ID protections, leak internal prompts/Private Cloud Compute data to ChatGPT, and retrieve customer data via an IDOR [record_id:1994].

Encrypted messaging records similarly challenge assumptions. Signal’s design is presented as strong but complex, with focus on Double Ratchet, Sealed Sender, linked-device synchronization, and a critical vulnerability found and fixed [record_id:29]. The Signalgate talk describes a Signal knock-off, TM SGNL, whose source allegedly contradicted claims about end-to-end encryption, followed by hacks that exposed plaintext messages and large memory dumps [record_id:1958]. E2EE client-side scanning based on perceptual hashing is criticized as both evadable and privacy-invasive, with attacks reportedly succeeding against more than 99.9% of images while preserving content and thresholds creating massive decryption/flagging concerns [record_id:212].

Decentralized and web privacy systems are also fragile. Nostr clients allegedly allow forged “encrypted” DMs, profile impersonation, confidential DM leakage, and tampering due to cryptographic design flaws, missing signature checks, and TOCTOU caching issues [record_id:39]. Google’s Privacy Sandbox is designed to replace third-party cookies, yet client-side deanonymization attacks against Attribution Reporting, Shared Storage, debugging reports, destination hijacking, and storage limit oracles may reconstruct browsing history or leak cross-site stored data [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030]. Marketing firms are portrayed as adapting to cookie deprecation by building more invasive server-side data harvesting across streaming services, devices, ISPs/location points, and sales points while framing it as consumer protection/privacy [record_id:2093].

### 5. Data access control failures and over-collection create direct exposure

Some records focus less on side channels and more on straightforward data exposure from application/API design. A GraphQL/REST case study on the Feeld dating app describes missing access controls exposing personal data, sensitive photos, videos, and private messages [record_id:2018]. Invitation-system business logic flaws in Facebook and Snapchat are said to enable unauthorized access, persistent connections, account blocking, and privacy breaches [record_id:2406]. Modern SaaS data platforms are described as processing enormous user and enterprise datasets, with architectural and logic pitfalls that can expose critical customer data [record_id:2473]. Bug bounty anecdotes include researchers being unintentionally added to internal distribution lists and receiving sensitive data they did not request [record_id:2042]. Browser extension clickjacking against password managers allegedly required only one click to leak credit cards, personal data, credentials, TOTP, and potentially passkey authentication flows [record_id:1851].

These records suggest that privacy risk often follows from ordinary application security defects: missing authorization, IDOR, business logic flaws, unsafe browser extension interaction models, insecure default flows, and improper handling of internal distribution or support data.

### 6. Surveillance, civil liberties, and community defense are inseparable from technical privacy

Several records frame privacy as a human-rights and power issue. Citizen Lab’s keynote covers investigations into state espionage, mercenary spyware targeting journalists/activists/human rights defenders, surveillance linked to Jamal Khashoggi’s circle, campaigns in multiple countries, and future challenges from AI-enabled subversion, Dark PR, and advertising intelligence [record_id:102]. Tor’s “war stories” talk concerns operating a global anonymity network for political dissidents, national security analysts, and others while educating law enforcement and governments about privacy and E2EE [record_id:1975]. EFF panels at DEF CON and BSidesLV center digital rights, privacy, surveillance, freedom of expression, hardware hacking, and legal/policy issues [record_id:2139] [record_id:2392]. Constitutional threat modeling is described as a legal-system failure: trusting wrong insiders, overreacting to outsiders, and struggling to adapt to political shifts [record_id:103].

Community and individual defense records include privacy workshops on OSINT footprinting and obfuscation [record_id:1969], practical analysis of viral digital privacy/security advice [record_id:2106], public-good digital security education for activists, healthcare seekers, and marginalized communities using threat modeling and storytelling [record_id:2416], and legal/security issues for border searches of phones and laptops under the Fourth Amendment border search exception [record_id:2421]. ReclaimTech and Veilid records explicitly seek alternatives to major platforms and data extraction, emphasizing autonomy, sovereignty, peer-to-peer support, and user ownership of data [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086] [record_id:2043].

### 7. Ransomware leaks and dark-web data create ethical dilemmas for privacy tooling

Records about ransomware and dark-web interception show a recurring dilemma: analyzing leaked data can help victims but can also amplify privacy harms. HIBR crawls ransomware leak sites and uses OCR plus LLMs to extract meaningful PII from scanned IDs, HR forms, insurance records, contracts, PDFs, and image scans while attempting not to expose PII and addressing GDPR/legal/ethical constraints [record_id:2470]. A related Skytalks version describes the same system as “half tool, half moral panic generator,” emphasizing uncomfortable data and legal gray zones [record_id:2471]. The murder-for-hire dark-web talk recounts exploiting a site to intercept orders containing names, addresses, pattern-of-life information, photos, and bitcoin payments, leading to target warnings and law-enforcement cooperation [record_id:2028]. These records show that data leakage research can itself involve handling extremely sensitive victim information.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records collectively describe a wide range of methods.

For empirical privacy analysis, AppleStorm uses traffic analysis and OS inspection to trace Apple Intelligence data flows, local/server processing, encrypted traffic, and possible leaks [record_id:5] [record_id:1957]. DNS tracking work uses frequency analysis, correlation metrics, and anomaly detection over large-scale mobile DNS datasets [record_id:81]. The political personal-information study uses active OSINT with realistic fake identities, one-time transactions, passive collection of email/voicemail/SMS responses, sentiment analysis, geographic comparison, and planned generative-AI interaction engines with machine-generated personalities [record_id:34]. OSINT is also used defensively in privacy workshops to identify and reduce an individual’s footprint [record_id:1969].

For protocol and application attacks, Nostr research examines cryptographic design, event verification, signature checks, link previews, and TOCTOU caching flaws to forge DMs, impersonate users, tamper with data, and leak confidential messages [record_id:39]. Messaging research analyzes Double Ratchet, Sealed Sender, linked devices, prekey depletion, delivery receipts, and PFS degradation [record_id:29] [record_id:2003]. Client-side scanning research proposes black-box and white-box attacks against perceptual hashing algorithms [record_id:212]. Privacy Sandbox research combines debugging-report abuse, Referrer-Policy bypass, destination hijacking, storage limit oracles, and insecure Shared Storage worklets [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030].

For AI privacy attacks, records mention model inversion, embedding inversion, vector database attacks, extraction of PII from RAG/LLM ecosystems, and failures of conventional PII scanners [record_id:1943]. XAI attacks use explanations to facilitate membership inference, model extraction, evasion, poisoning, and backdoor attacks [record_id:204]. Prompt leakage work chains tokenization boundary manipulation, reflection attacks, and context-window shifting across multiple LLMs [record_id:2237]. KYC-agent exploitation embeds injections in documents to steer extraction agents into cross-record reads/writes and exfiltration [record_id:2328]. Enterprise AI search threat modeling focuses on controlling aggregation of company-wide sensitive data [record_id:2270].

For privacy-preserving computation and architecture, Curl implements MPC non-linear functions as wavelet-compressed lookup tables, preserving a tensor/programming model and reducing communication/latency [record_id:149]. AdapterSwap uses dynamically composed low-rank adapters for continual learning, fine-grained data access, and deletion [record_id:151]. Confidential computing isolates cloud execution and proves code identity [record_id:2294], while confidential AI uses TEEs across CPUs/GPUs/accelerators and remote attestation to protect prompts, context, inference data, and models [record_id:2343]. Oblivious computation hides data access patterns in encrypted database-like workloads [record_id:2054]. PETs talks cover MPC and homomorphic encryption in real-world data lifecycle applications [record_id:2302]. BinaryShield provides privacy-preserving prompt-injection fingerprints for cross-service threat intelligence without sharing raw prompts [record_id:2327].

For surveillance detection and circumvention, Rayhunter uses an Orbic RC400L hotspot and Qualcomm DIAG protocol access to collect cellular control-plane traffic and detect potential Stingray/cell-site simulators [record_id:1922]. Open-source cellular base stations such as OpenAirInterface 5G, srsRAN_4G, OpenBTS, and YateBTS/Yates GSM are used to build test beds for Rayhunter [record_id:1889]. TSPU research studies in-path censorship and circumvention possibilities [record_id:1970]. Tor operations provide long-running anonymity-network practice and policy engagement [record_id:1975]. Off-grid datarunning, sneakernet, and PirateBox are gestured at as resilient communications in oppressive regimes, though the raw record is mostly biographical rather than methodological [record_id:2127].

For data-leak processing, HIBR crawls ransomware leak sites, downloads files, uses OCR and LLMs to identify PII in PDFs/images, and designs around not exposing sensitive identifiers [record_id:2470] [record_id:2471]. The murder-for-hire investigation used exploitation to intercept orders, triage victims, warn targets, and coordinate with law enforcement [record_id:2028]. Bug bounty work emphasizes opportunistic discovery of sensitive-data leaks, blind XSS, and accidental distribution-list exposure [record_id:2042].

For endpoint, browser, IoT, and physical privacy leakage, browser extension clickjacking uses fake intrusive web elements to induce one-click leakage from password managers and other extensions [record_id:1851]. Keyboard leakage methods include keyloggers, electromagnetic emanation attacks, RF attacks, and acoustic typing eavesdropping [record_id:1938]. TPMS tracking uses low-cost RF capture and spoofed TPMS messages [record_id:2048]. IoT vape-detector research uses hardware teardown, firmware dumping, filesystem release, firmware decryption scripts, and custom firmware packing to analyze surveillance devices in schools [record_id:1984]. EEG-based IoT authentication is proposed as a dynamic biometric that avoids storing sensitive data and fits constrained devices [record_id:1932]. The augmented-reality ESP-style system applies computer vision, object detection, and hardware setups to real-world spatial awareness, raising privacy/surveillance implications even though the framing is playful [record_id:2518].

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The most representative AI privacy record is the agentic AI keynote, because it provides the broad conceptual map: sensitive user/professional data, persistent memories, automated workflows, deep inference, surveillance/profiling, consent gaps, context stealing, persuasion attacks, ambient AI, robotics, distributed training, TEEs, and data minimization [record_id:111]. It helps connect many otherwise separate records: Apple Intelligence data flows [record_id:5] [record_id:1957], enterprise AI search [record_id:2270], health AI rights [record_id:2041], KYC extraction-agent exploits [record_id:2328], and confidential AI [record_id:2343].

AppleStorm is notable because it targets a major consumer AI ecosystem and specifically tests privacy assurances through traffic analysis and OS inspection [record_id:5] [record_id:1957]. The companion Apple ecosystem talk broadens the concern from Apple Intelligence to Siri, Spotlight, Safari, support systems, Face ID bypasses, prompt leaks, Private Cloud Compute interactions, and customer-data retrieval vulnerabilities [record_id:1994]. Together these records are strong evidence of community interest in scrutinizing platform privacy claims at the data-flow and implementation levels.

The DNS tracking record is one of the strongest empirical measurement abstracts: it reports 1.5 billion DNS requests from 30,000 devices over 30 days and claims accurate device tracking using simple statistics and partial traffic [record_id:81]. Its importance is that it demonstrates privacy leakage from ordinary infrastructure metadata, not from application compromise.

The Nostr record is notable as a decentralized-system warning. It claims the first comprehensive security study of Nostr and popular clients, with practical attacks against encrypted DMs, profile authenticity, link previews, signature verification, and caching behavior [record_id:39]. It is representative of a broader theme: decentralized or censorship-resistant systems do not automatically achieve confidentiality or authentication.

The Signal-related records form a cluster. Black Hat’s Signal talk explains architecture, Rust’s role in reducing memory corruption, Double Ratchet, Sealed Sender, linked-device synchronization, and a critical vulnerability with a fix [record_id:29]. The Signalgate talk is operational and political, focusing on accidental group disclosure, a Signal knock-off, broken E2EE claims, hacked archive servers, plaintext messages, and memory dumps [record_id:1958]. The E2EE messenger side-channel talk adds delivery-receipt tracking and PFS/prekey depletion [record_id:2003]. The client-side scanning record adds a policy/technical critique of perceptual hashing proposals for E2EE environments [record_id:212]. Collectively, these records show that messaging privacy depends on protocol design, implementation, device linking, clones/archives, metadata, and policy interventions.

The Privacy Sandbox records are representative of privacy-preserving web API skepticism. Both versions describe deanonymization via Attribution Reporting, debugging reports, Referrer-Policy bypass, destination hijacking, storage-limit oracles, and Shared Storage leakage [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030]. They matter because they target a major replacement for third-party cookies rather than legacy tracking alone.

The HIBR ransomware-leak talks are notable because they combine privacy research, breach awareness, OCR/LLM tooling, and ethics. They describe extracting PII from ransomware dumps while attempting not to reveal PII, and explicitly discuss legal/privacy risks [record_id:2470] [record_id:2471]. This is a strong example of dual-use privacy tooling: the same capability that helps victims understand exposure could become a privacy nightmare if mishandled.

Curl and confidential AI are important defensive anchors. Curl gives technical depth and quantitative claims for MPC-based private LLM inference [record_id:149]. Confidential computing and confidential AI records describe deployable TEE/attestation approaches for protecting cloud and AI workloads during processing [record_id:2294] [record_id:2343]. Oblivious computation adds the access-pattern dimension often missed by conventional encryption [record_id:2054]. Together with PETs [record_id:2302], AdapterSwap [record_id:151], and BinaryShield [record_id:2327], these records define the constructive side of the topic.

Citizen Lab, Tor, EFF, community-defense, and border-search records are notable because they define privacy as lived risk under surveillance and legal coercion rather than only as technical confidentiality. Citizen Lab’s keynote covers mercenary spyware and civil-society counter-intelligence [record_id:102]. Tor provides anonymity-network operational and policy experience [record_id:1975]. EFF panels address digital rights and surveillance policy [record_id:2139] [record_id:2392]. Community defense and border-search talks translate privacy into practical harm reduction and legal realities [record_id:2416] [record_id:2421].

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

A major limitation is that most records are abstracts, not full papers, source code, datasets, or independent evaluations. Claims about exploitability, scale, and mitigation effectiveness are often not verifiable from the record text alone. For example, the Apple Intelligence talks describe data leaks and unexpected behavior but do not provide the detailed traces in the raw record [record_id:5] [record_id:1957]. The Nostr, Privacy Sandbox, browser extension, Feeld, and Apple ecosystem records claim practical vulnerabilities, but downstream researchers would need full talks, advisories, CVEs, patches, or PoCs to assess reproducibility and severity [record_id:39] [record_id:1851] [record_id:2018] [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030] [record_id:1994].

AI privacy records raise broad concerns but leave open how to measure privacy leakage consistently across agentic systems. The agentic AI keynote calls out memories, automated workflows, deep inference, behavioral manipulation, context stealing, and ambient AI, but the record does not specify standardized tests or metrics [record_id:111]. Shadow-data extraction mentions model inversion and embedding inversion but does not define success rates, attacker assumptions, or affected architectures in the abstract [record_id:1943]. KYC-agent exploitation appears important but needs details on real-world deployment assumptions, access-control boundaries, and whether regulatory workflows reliably complete the exploit chain [record_id:2328]. Enterprise AI search threat modeling identifies a real aggregation risk but does not enumerate concrete controls in the abstract [record_id:2270].

Privacy-preserving technologies are promising but the records leave deployment questions unresolved. MPC and Curl report performance improvements, yet it remains unclear when such systems are cost-effective for production LLM workloads [record_id:149]. Confidential computing and confidential AI depend on hardware trust, remote attestation, cloud integration, and operational usability; the abstracts do not deeply address side channels, enclave supply-chain trust, rollback, observability, or incident response [record_id:2294] [record_id:2343]. Homomorphic encryption and MPC are introduced as PETs, but the record is introductory and does not compare tradeoffs [record_id:2302]. Oblivious computation is positioned as access-pattern protection, but practical performance and developer adoption remain open [record_id:2054].

Several records point to legal and ethical uncertainty. HIBR’s processing of ransomware dumps must avoid re-exposing victims while still providing useful breach awareness [record_id:2470] [record_id:2471]. Murder-for-hire interception involved extreme sensitive data and direct victim warning, raising questions about lawful access, evidence handling, and researcher duty of care [record_id:2028]. Border device searches depend on constitutional doctrine and agency practice rather than purely technical controls [record_id:2421]. Student, health, political, and marketing data records highlight consent and governance gaps but do not provide comprehensive regulatory remedies [record_id:2005] [record_id:2041] [record_id:34] [record_id:2093].

There are also coverage gaps. The corpus is conference-heavy and U.S.-centric, with many 2025/2026 talks. It includes little direct discussion of differential privacy, federated learning beyond brief distributed-training references, privacy budgeting, formal anonymization/deanonymization theory, data broker ecosystem measurement, mobile app SDK leakage, cloud logging retention, or enterprise DLP efficacy. Some records have weak raw evidence for the topic because the provided text is mostly biographical or broad; these should be treated cautiously [record_id:2122] [record_id:2127] [record_id:2535].

## Coverage And Evidence Notes

This section accounts for the full record set while preserving the distinction between strong technical evidence, representative abstracts, and thinner contextual material.

## Coverage Addendum

Record [record_id:247] adds a platform-integrity and demographic-inference dimension that was not covered in the existing report. The talk focuses on detecting “mass generation of fake accounts” by looking for bursts of accounts whose profile details appear plausible but statistically diverge from a platform’s expected user population [record_id:247]. Its privacy relevance is twofold. First, the fake-account ecosystem itself may involve privacy abuse: the record notes that malicious users often make accounts appear legitimate with “plausible names” and “photos scraped from other websites,” directly tying account fraud to reuse or leakage of personal imagery and identity signals from elsewhere on the web [record_id:247]. Second, the proposed defensive method depends on inferring demographic attributes from names, especially gender, and comparing those inferred distributions against baseline distributions for legitimate users [record_id:247].

This record therefore complicates the privacy-preserving-systems narrative by showing how anti-abuse detection can itself become privacy-sensitive. The method—anomaly detection over user demographic distributions—may be useful for identifying coordinated fake-account creation, but it relies on demographic modeling of users and thus sits in what the speaker calls a “minefield of technical and, most importantly, ethical challenges” [record_id:247]. The record explicitly flags the difficulty of detecting gender across cultural contexts and the danger of using gender-related features to identify suspected bad actors [record_id:247]. This is a particularly important caveat because false positives could disproportionately affect already underrepresented groups; the abstract gives the example that false positives among women in contexts where women are underrepresented could further discourage participation, undermining diversity, inclusion, and belonging goals [record_id:247].

As evidence, [record_id:247] is moderately specific but incomplete from the abstract alone. It names a concrete detection approach—monitoring sharp departures from known demographic distributions—and says the talk presents empirical examples from work detecting malicious users [record_id:247]. However, the supplied text does not provide the datasets, statistical thresholds, measured precision/recall, demographic-inference error rates, or governance safeguards needed to independently assess whether the approach is effective or acceptable at scale. Downstream researchers should treat it as a useful representative record for the intersection of privacy, identity abuse, platform trust, and fairness-sensitive detection rather than as a fully specified evaluation.
