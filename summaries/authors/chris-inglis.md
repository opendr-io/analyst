# Topic: Author: Chris Inglis

## Executive Summary

The three records attributed to Chris Inglis portray him as a senior public-sector and national-security voice focused on cyber resilience, geopolitical risk, AI-era governance, and the changing distribution of responsibility between governments, private actors, and technology providers. The records are not a large corpus, but they form a coherent arc: from broad strategic reflection on distributed digital infrastructure and fragile supply chains, to practical use of generative AI for geopolitical cyber policy drafting, to a detailed AI-security framing around frontier-model infrastructure, nation-state targeting, criminal misuse, and defensive hardening.

Across the records, Inglis’s recurring contribution is not a narrow technical exploit technique but a systems-level security perspective. He emphasizes that modern cyber risk is shaped by interdependence: global digital infrastructure, supply chains, AI platforms, national competition, private-sector defense obligations, and human decision-making all interact. The 2025 Black Hat keynote frames this broadly as a shift from older stabilizing forces toward faster-moving instability, where resilience and innovation must be integrated [record_id:100]. The 2026 Prompt||GTFO record shows Inglis applying ChatGPT and DeepSeek to accelerate drafting of a cyber crisis management policy paper for a hypothetical or proposed U.S.-Russia-China summit on cyber and nuclear escalation [record_id:2198]. The 2026 Black Hat briefing with Fred Heiding translates similar concerns into AI security, distinguishing beneficial selective model access for defenders, malicious targeting of AI infrastructure by nation-state actors, and criminal misuse of U.S. AI models against American infrastructure and citizens [record_id:2636].

The evidence base is thin but directionally consistent. Two records are Black Hat abstracts and one is a short talk description from Prompt||GTFO. These sources give strong evidence for topics, framing, and stated claims, but limited evidence for the actual arguments, demonstrations, empirical methods, or policy recommendations delivered in full.

## Research Landscape

The records consist of three attributed talks or presentations, spanning 2025–2026. Two come from Black Hat U.S. and one from Prompt||GTFO. The formats differ: one is a keynote, one appears to be a conference/video talk summary, and one is a co-authored Black Hat briefing. The dominant subject areas are governance, risk, compliance, AI security, geopolitical cyber policy, and infrastructure resilience.

The 2025 Black Hat keynote, “From Slide Rules to GenAi - Musings of a Graybeard Public Servant on What's Changing, What's Not, and What Should,” situates cyber risk in a long historical and institutional frame. Its abstract emphasizes “global reliance on distributed digital infrastructure,” “dangerous vulnerabilities,” “fragile supply chains,” fragmented regulation, nationalism, climate disruption, technological dislocation, and the shifting of defense responsibilities toward private actors [record_id:100]. This is the broadest and most strategic record in the set.

The 2026 Prompt||GTFO record, “Geo-Political Analysis with ChatGPT and DeepSeek,” is narrower and more applied. It describes Inglis, identified in the raw text as a “former NSA official,” using ChatGPT and DeepSeek to rapidly draft a comprehensive cyber crisis management policy paper for a three-nation U.S.-Russia-China summit on cyber and nuclear escalation [record_id:2198]. This record places generative AI in a policy-production workflow rather than treating it solely as an attack surface.

The 2026 Black Hat briefing, “The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly of AI Security,” co-authored with Fred Heiding, focuses directly on AI security. It divides the topic into three parts: defensive pre-release model access for selected defense companies, nation-state targeting of AI infrastructure, and cybercriminal misuse of American AI models [record_id:2636]. It is the most explicitly technical-security-oriented of the three records, mentioning model-weight exfiltration, infrastructure compromise, insider threats, supply-chain attacks, and an infrastructure framework built around “chips, wires, and humans” [record_id:2636].

Taken together, the research landscape is dominated by high-level security strategy and policy, with AI treated as both a productivity tool and a contested infrastructure layer. The records do not present Inglis as focused on low-level vulnerability research, malware reverse engineering, or exploit development. Instead, they show a policy-and-strategy perspective concerned with systemic risk, institutional coordination, and the security implications of frontier technologies.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Systems-level resilience in an unstable digital environment

The clearest recurring theme is resilience under conditions of accelerating instability. The 2025 keynote argues that global dependence on distributed digital infrastructure has created both “unprecedented opportunities” and “dangerous vulnerabilities” [record_id:100]. It frames the current landscape as one in which traditional stabilizing forces are losing “beneficial inertia,” while transformative technologies, nationalism, and fragmented regulation reshape the threat environment [record_id:100].

This systems view reappears in the AI-security briefing. There, AI is not just software but infrastructure with multiple attack surfaces: chips, wires, and humans [record_id:2636]. The briefing’s structure suggests that securing AI requires understanding hardware supply chains, communications and compute infrastructure, organizational insiders, model assets, and adversarial geopolitical behavior together rather than in isolation [record_id:2636].

### Private-sector responsibility and public-private coordination

The 2025 keynote explicitly says that traditional risk management is being overwhelmed as defense responsibilities shift toward private actors [record_id:100]. This is a significant governance theme: the abstract implies that the entities operating or depending on distributed infrastructure cannot simply rely on state protection or traditional regulatory models. They must integrate resilience into their own operations and strategy.

The 2026 AI-security briefing continues this concern in a more specific form. Its “good” category describes AI companies granting selected defense companies pre-release access to new AI models so vulnerabilities can be identified and patched before attackers exploit them [record_id:2636]. This suggests a public-private or private-private defensive model in which model developers, defense firms, and possibly government stakeholders coordinate earlier in the deployment lifecycle.

### AI as both strategic tool and strategic risk

The records treat AI in two different but complementary ways. In the Prompt||GTFO record, AI is a tool for policy analysis and drafting. Inglis reportedly used ChatGPT and DeepSeek to produce a six-page cyber crisis management policy document in an afternoon, a task the raw text says “would have taken months manually” [record_id:2198]. The same record notes that the AI suggested elements he had overlooked in his original framework [record_id:2198]. This presents AI as an accelerant for expert policy work, not merely a replacement for it.

In the Black Hat AI-security briefing, AI is a target and an enabler of malicious activity. Nation-state actors are described as increasingly targeting AI infrastructure via cyber espionage, insider threats, and supply-chain attacks to steal model weights, trade secrets, and nationally sensitive data [record_id:2636]. Cybercriminals are described as using American AI models to infiltrate, manipulate, and defraud American infrastructure and citizens [record_id:2636]. This creates a dual-use theme: AI can augment defenders and policymakers, but it also creates new high-value assets, new attack surfaces, and new misuse channels.

### Geopolitics, national security, and cyber escalation

Geopolitical risk is central across the corpus. The keynote identifies nationalism and fragmented regulation as reshaping the digital security landscape [record_id:100]. The Prompt||GTFO record explicitly concerns a cyber crisis management policy paper for a three-nation U.S.-Russia-China summit on cyber and nuclear escalation [record_id:2198]. The AI-security briefing highlights nation-state targeting of AI infrastructure and refers to China’s alleged illicit use of U.S. AI models to hack American infrastructure [record_id:2636].

The records collectively suggest that Inglis approaches cybersecurity as inseparable from strategic competition and crisis stability. The cyber-nuclear escalation topic in [record_id:2198] is especially notable because it links cyber operations to the most serious forms of geopolitical crisis management. The AI-infrastructure record extends this strategic lens to frontier model systems and nationally sensitive data [record_id:2636].

### Supply-chain fragility and infrastructure interdependence

Supply-chain risk appears in both the broad 2025 keynote and the 2026 AI-security briefing. The keynote states that fragile supply chains heighten systemic risks and that threats from cyberattacks, climate disruptions, and technological dislocations propagate faster and hit harder [record_id:100]. The AI-security briefing identifies supply-chain attacks as one avenue through which nation-state actors target AI infrastructure [record_id:2636].

This pattern suggests a recurring Inglis theme: cyber risk is not confined to direct network compromise. It propagates through dependencies—vendors, infrastructure providers, regulatory environments, hardware, data flows, and human organizations. The “chips, wires, and humans” framework in [record_id:2636] can be read as a concrete AI-era expression of the broader systems-thinking argument in [record_id:100].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records mention several approaches, ranging from governance frameworks to AI-assisted drafting and infrastructure defense.

One approach is systems-level resilience planning. The 2025 keynote calls for “integrating resilience with innovation,” “fostering unified coalitions,” and adopting “systems-level thinking” that aligns technical, strategic, and human factors [record_id:100]. This is not described as a formal methodology with steps, but it is a clear strategic approach: treat resilience as a design and leadership requirement rather than as a reactive function.

A second approach is AI-assisted policy generation. In the Prompt||GTFO record, Inglis uses ChatGPT and DeepSeek to draft a cyber crisis management policy paper for a U.S.-Russia-China summit on cyber and nuclear escalation [record_id:2198]. The described workflow is rapid ideation and drafting: the AI generates a six-page document in an afternoon and contributes overlooked elements to the framework [record_id:2198]. The record does not specify prompt design, evaluation methods, fact-checking procedures, or controls against hallucination, but it is important evidence that Inglis explored generative AI as a serious aid for geopolitical cyber policy work.

A third approach is selective pre-release access to AI models for defensive testing. The AI-security briefing describes AI companies granting selected defense companies early access to new models so they can identify and patch vulnerabilities before attackers exploit them [record_id:2636]. This resembles coordinated vulnerability discovery or red-team-style pre-deployment assessment, applied to frontier AI models and their surrounding infrastructure.

A fourth approach is adversary-informed AI infrastructure defense. The 2026 briefing identifies nation-state methods—cyber espionage, insider threats, and supply-chain attacks—aimed at stealing model weights, trade secrets, and sensitive national data [record_id:2636]. It also states that the presentation demonstrates how defenders can detect and mitigate model-weight exfiltration, infrastructure compromise, and insider threats [record_id:2636]. The raw record does not give implementation details, but it identifies key defensive priorities: protect model weights, harden infrastructure, monitor for compromise, and manage insider risk.

Finally, the AI briefing introduces a practical framework for securing AI infrastructure across “three attack surfaces—chips, wires, and humans” [record_id:2636]. This is the most concrete named framework in the corpus. It implies a layered model: hardware and compute substrates, network/connectivity or communications pathways, and organizational/human factors such as insiders and operational behavior.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The most representative strategic record is the 2025 Black Hat keynote, “From Slide Rules to GenAi” [record_id:100]. It matters because it establishes the broad worldview that helps interpret the other records: rapid technological transformation, fragile infrastructure, fragmented governance, private-sector defense burdens, and the need for resilience-oriented leadership. Its language is sweeping, but it clearly anchors Inglis’s contribution in governance, risk, and systems thinking rather than narrow technical demonstration [record_id:100].

The most notable record for generative AI as a practical policy tool is the Prompt||GTFO talk, “Geo-Political Analysis with ChatGPT and DeepSeek” [record_id:2198]. Its importance lies in the claim that Inglis used two contemporary AI systems to draft a substantial cyber crisis management policy paper in an afternoon, including suggestions that improved or expanded his original framework [record_id:2198]. For downstream researchers, this record is useful evidence that Inglis’s engagement with AI is not only cautionary; he also presents AI as an amplifier of expert analysis and policy production.

The most detailed security record is the 2026 Black Hat briefing, “The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly of AI Security,” co-authored with Fred Heiding [record_id:2636]. It is significant because it moves from broad cyber governance into AI-specific security architecture and threat modeling. It covers pre-release model access, nation-state theft of model weights and trade secrets, cybercriminal misuse of American AI models, Project Glasswing, China’s alleged illicit use of U.S. models, and a Harvard dataset of more than 300 severe cyber incidents against U.S. companies [record_id:2636]. It also introduces the “chips, wires, and humans” framework and promises recommendations for organizations and governments to harden AI infrastructure against large-scale attacks on frontier models [record_id:2636].

Together, these records show an evolution from broad resilience principles to applied generative AI use and then to structured AI infrastructure defense. However, because the records are abstracts or short descriptions, the evidence is strongest for identifying topics and themes, and weaker for evaluating the quality, novelty, or empirical support of the underlying arguments.
