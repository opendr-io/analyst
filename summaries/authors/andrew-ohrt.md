## Major Themes And Trends

### Water infrastructure as life-critical infrastructure

The clearest recurring theme is that water infrastructure is treated as a life-safety and public welfare issue, not merely an IT asset class. The phrase “Water is life” opens the “Defending Our Water - Defending Our Lives” abstract and frames water-system cyber risk as directly tied to human survival and community resilience [record_id:2427]. This framing is reinforced by the title itself, which links defending water to defending lives [record_id:2427].

The records therefore align with a broader critical-infrastructure security perspective: cyber incidents in water systems are not abstract data breaches but potential disruptions to essential services. The language of “our most essential infrastructure” and “resilience” makes the core concern continuity of safe water operations under cyber and geopolitical pressure [record_id:2427].

### Escalating threat environment for U.S. water systems

Record 2427 presents a sharply escalating threat narrative. It states that by 2025, the threat landscape facing U.S. water infrastructure had become “more severe and immediate,” following high-profile 2024 intrusions such as Volt Typhoon and Iran-linked Cyber Avengers [record_id:2427]. It further claims that 2025 had already seen “a surge in attempted and successful breaches targeting municipal and rural water systems” [record_id:2427].

This is the only record in the set that explicitly mentions adversaries or threat actors. The inclusion of Volt Typhoon and Cyber Avengers situates water-sector cyber defense within geopolitical conflict and state-linked or state-adjacent cyber activity [record_id:2427]. The abstract does not provide incident details, technical indicators, or citations, so the evidence is high-level. Still, it clearly identifies adversary pressure and real-world breach activity as motivating concerns for the session.

### Governance, trust, and coordination problems

Another major theme is that technical cyber vulnerability is only part of the problem. The “Defending Our Water” abstract says escalating threats are “compounded by deteriorating trust and coordination between public and private sector stakeholders” [record_id:2427]. It also names “regulatory fragility” as part of a “perfect storm” affecting water infrastructure [record_id:2427].

This suggests that Ohrt’s associated work in these records is not limited to engineering controls. It includes institutional and governance dimensions: how public agencies, private operators, regulators, and other stakeholders coordinate to secure distributed water systems. The water sector is especially fragmented, with many municipal and rural systems, and the abstract’s emphasis on municipal and rural targets supports that governance and capability distribution are central concerns [record_id:2427].

### Cyber-Informed Engineering as a preventive design discipline

The two workshop records shift from threat framing to methodology. “Engineering Cyber Resilience for the Water Sector” is built around Cyber-Informed Engineering, abbreviated CIE, and explicitly aims to teach a “methodology of designing out cyber-risk before a system is placed into operation” [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. This is a preventive, design-stage view of security: rather than bolting on controls after deployment, engineers should understand cyber risks early enough to shape system architecture, operations, and safety assumptions.

The workshop description also highlights a perceived education gap: “What Engineers Need to Know About Cyber and Why (and are not getting this in school)” [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. This positions CIE as both a technical and educational intervention. The intended audience is broad—“anyone who is interested” in the methodology—but the framing centers engineers and engineering education [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].
