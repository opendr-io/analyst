# Topic: Author: Olivier Bilodeau

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Olivier Bilodeau center on the modern infostealer ecosystem: what stealer logs contain, how attackers use them, how defenders can mine them for intelligence, and how browser/platform defenses may or may not change the threat model. Both records portray infostealers as a high-volume, high-impact cybercrime problem involving tens of millions of stolen logs traded underground, with value extending far beyond passwords into session tokens, browser autofill data, screenshots, identity artifacts, and deeply personal victim context [record_id:26] [record_id:2818].

The 2025 Black Hat USA briefing, coauthored by Estelle Ruellan and Olivier Bilodeau, proposes using infection-time screenshots inside stealer logs together with large language models to identify infection vectors, extract indicators of compromise, and track campaigns at scale [record_id:26]. The 2026 BSidesLV Skytalk by Olivier Bilodeau takes a more ground-level and privacy-focused view of what stealer logs reveal, emphasizing their intimacy, operational value for incident responders and red teams, and the likely evolution of stealers toward more persistent, live, and invasive collection [record_id:2818].

Collectively, the records suggest Bilodeau’s contribution in this small corpus is not general malware reverse engineering in the narrow binary-analysis sense, but a broader intelligence-driven approach to infostealers: treating stolen logs as artifacts that expose attacker tradecraft, victim risk, social-engineering lures, post-compromise impact, and future browser-security pressure points.

## Research Landscape

The record set is small: two conference-talk abstracts, one from Black Hat USA 2025 and one from BSidesLV 2026. Both are attributed to Olivier Bilodeau, though the Black Hat record is coauthored with Estelle Ruellan [record_id:26], while the BSidesLV talk lists Bilodeau alone [record_id:2818]. The sources are event abstracts rather than full papers, slide decks, transcripts, or code repositories, so the evidence is strongest for identifying themes, claimed methods, intended talk scope, and proposed research questions, but weaker for validating empirical results, technical reproducibility, or implementation details.

The dominant research area is infostealer malware and the ecosystem around stolen logs. The Black Hat record frames information stealers as “one of the most prolific and damaging threats” and states that more than 30 million stealer logs were traded on underground markets in 2024 [record_id:26]. The BSidesLV record raises the estimated scale further, referring to “50 million” logs “floating around the underground” and describing what they look like “at ground level” [record_id:2818]. These figures are not independently substantiated within the records, but they establish the talks’ shared premise: infostealer logs are abundant enough to be studied at scale and consequential enough to merit dedicated defensive workflows.

The records also differ in orientation. The Black Hat briefing is framed as a technical and operational method: use screenshots from infection moments, apply LLMs, identify infection vectors, extract IoCs, and mitigate campaigns quickly [record_id:26]. The BSidesLV talk is framed as an exposé and strategic forecast: show what real logs reveal, explain why browser autofills and session artifacts matter, discuss implications for incident response and red teaming, and assess defenses such as Chrome’s Application-Bound Encryption and Device Bound Session Credentials [record_id:2818].

Together, these records depict a research landscape where stealer logs are not merely evidence of compromise but a primary intelligence source. They can reveal how victims were lured, what malware-delivery infrastructure was used, what secrets were exposed, what attackers obtain on day one, and how future stealers may become more continuous and invasive [record_id:26] [record_id:2818].

## Major Themes And Trends

### Infostealer logs as high-value intelligence artifacts

The central theme across both records is that stealer logs contain far more than credentials. The Black Hat record highlights browser-stored credentials, session tokens, and “other system secrets,” but focuses especially on screenshots captured at the moment of infection [record_id:26]. These screenshots are framed as underused “crime scene snapshots” that can expose infection vectors, lure pages, URLs, and social-engineering context [record_id:26].

The BSidesLV record extends this idea by emphasizing the personal and operational richness of logs: browser autofills, bank accounts, translated error messages, reused passwords, and intimate personal details [record_id:2818]. It describes stealer logs as “the most intimate piece of intelligence in our industry” and argues that their contents are sold cheaply despite their sensitivity [record_id:2818]. The record’s tone is more provocative, but the underlying research point is consistent with the Black Hat abstract: stealer logs should be treated as rich datasets containing identity, behavioral, technical, and social context, not just credential dumps.

### Moving from passive observation to scalable extraction

A second major theme is the transition from reading logs manually to extracting actionable intelligence at scale. The Black Hat briefing explicitly proposes using LLMs on infostealer infection screenshots to identify infection vectors and extract IoCs, claiming that this approach found “several hundred potential IoCs” in the form of URLs leading to malware-laden payload downloads [record_id:26]. It further claims that applying the method to fresh stealer logs could allow near-real-time detection and mitigation of infection vectors [record_id:26].

The BSidesLV record does not foreground automation in the same way, but it reinforces the scale problem by referencing thousands of logs read by practitioners and 50 million logs in circulation [record_id:2818]. That framing supports the need for scalable triage, summarization, detection engineering, and prioritization, even if the talk abstract does not specify a machine-learning pipeline.

### Infection vectors, lures, and attacker social engineering

The Black Hat record provides the clearest evidence for Bilodeau-associated work on infection vectors and social-engineering tradecraft. It says the proposed analysis uncovered distribution strategies, lure themes, and social-engineering techniques used in successful infection campaigns [record_id:26]. It names three campaign types: cracked versions of popular software, ads pointing to popular software, and free AI image generators [record_id:26]. These examples imply a focus on user-driven execution, search/ad-driven delivery, fake or repackaged software, and exploitation of current consumer interest in AI tools.

The BSidesLV record is less specific about initial infection lures, but it is concerned with what happens after a stealer succeeds: what the attacker receives on day one, how an executive compromise affects incident response, and how red teams should emulate a modern adversary using realistic stealer-log outputs [record_id:2818]. The two records therefore cover adjacent points in the attack chain: infection-vector discovery and campaign tracking in the Black Hat talk, and post-compromise artifact interpretation and attacker enablement in the BSidesLV talk.

### Privacy, victim intimacy, and the human impact of stealer logs

The BSidesLV abstract strongly emphasizes the privacy dimension of stealer logs. It describes logs as exposing not only professional credentials but family details, bank accounts, private relationships, and repeated passwords [record_id:2818]. This moves the topic beyond enterprise compromise into personal harm, surveillance-like exposure, and data-broker-style commodification of victims’ lives.

The Black Hat record also implies privacy and sensitivity through references to browser credentials, session tokens, secrets, and screenshots [record_id:26], but its framing is primarily defensive and intelligence-oriented. The BSidesLV record makes the ethical and human stakes more explicit. For downstream researchers, this suggests a recurring Bilodeau theme: infostealer research must account for both operational security value and the extreme sensitivity of the underlying data.

### Browser security and the future of credential/session theft

The BSidesLV talk introduces a forward-looking theme around browser defenses and attacker adaptation. It names Chrome’s Application-Bound Encryption and Device Bound Session Credentials, characterizing the former as “already broken” but still acceptable in some sense, and the latter as “the interesting one” or “actually-interesting” defense [record_id:2818]. The duplicated wording in the raw abstract reinforces that browser-bound protections are a major planned discussion point.

The record also forecasts “the next generation of stealers” moving toward webcam capture at infection, real persistence in the browser, and a shift from logs as static snapshots to “a live wire” [record_id:2818]. This is important because it suggests an evolution from one-time exfiltration of local secrets toward ongoing browser-resident compromise, persistent session abuse, or live data access. The Black Hat talk’s focus on infection-time screenshots already shows stealers collecting contextual visual data [record_id:26]; the BSidesLV forecast extends that trajectory toward more invasive and continuous collection [record_id:2818].

### Practical audiences: defenders, incident responders, threat hunters, and red teams

Both records are practitioner-oriented. The Black Hat talk promises actionable outcomes: extracting IoCs, tracking campaigns, reducing further infections, and applying LLMs to illicit artifacts for intelligence [record_id:26]. This aligns with SOC, threat hunting, detection engineering, malware-analysis, and threat-intelligence workflows.

The BSidesLV talk explicitly names two beneficiary audiences: incident responders handling cases where a log affects an executive, and red teamers who need to emulate modern adversaries by understanding what stealer logs give attackers on day one [record_id:2818]. This expands the relevance from defensive detection to adversary emulation and crisis response. It also implies that realistic red-team exercises should model the availability of browser autofill data, session tokens, and other log contents, rather than only usernames and passwords.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The most concrete method described is the Black Hat briefing’s use of large language models on infection-time screenshots embedded in stealer logs [record_id:26]. The proposed workflow appears to involve collecting or accessing fresh stealer logs, isolating screenshots taken at or near the moment of infection, using LLMs to interpret screenshot content, identifying the infection vector, and extracting indicators such as URLs that lead to malicious payload downloads [record_id:26]. The abstract claims this method discovered several hundred potential IoCs and could enable near-instantaneous mitigation when applied to fresh logs [record_id:26].

The same record indicates a campaign-analysis approach: use extracted evidence to identify distribution strategies, lure themes, and social-engineering techniques, then break down distinct campaigns involving cracked popular software, ads for popular software, and free AI image generators [record_id:26]. This approach blends malware-intelligence collection, visual/contextual analysis, LLM-assisted extraction, and campaign clustering.

The BSidesLV record discusses methods less as a formal pipeline and more as practitioner interpretation of stealer logs. It suggests that reading many logs reveals patterns about victims, browser autofill exposure, credential reuse, and attacker capabilities [record_id:2818]. It also implies a workflow for incident response: when a log implicates a high-value individual such as an executive, responders need to understand the full scope of what was exposed, not just reset a password [record_id:2818]. For red teams, the approach is adversary emulation informed by actual stealer-log contents: understanding what an attacker gets immediately from a modern infection and using that to make simulations more credible [record_id:2818].

On the defense side, the BSidesLV record names Chrome’s Application-Bound Encryption and Device Bound Session Credentials as browser-security mechanisms relevant to the future of stealer defenses [record_id:2818]. The abstract’s wording indicates a comparative assessment: Application-Bound Encryption is described as already bypassed or broken, while Device Bound Session Credentials are portrayed as more interesting [record_id:2818]. The record does not provide implementation details, bypass methods, or evaluation data, so downstream researchers should treat this as a lead rather than a validated technical conclusion.

The future-looking methods and attacker approaches described in the BSidesLV abstract include webcam capture at infection, persistence inside the browser, and logs becoming live rather than static [record_id:2818]. These are not presented with supporting technical specifics, but they identify directions for further research: browser persistence mechanisms, live session relay or continuous exfiltration, capture of visual context, and defenses that bind credentials or sessions to device state.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The most methodologically specific record is the Black Hat USA 2025 briefing, “Hackers Dropping Mid-Heist Selfies: LLM Identifies Information Stealer Infection Vector and Extracts IoCs,” coauthored by Estelle Ruellan and Olivier Bilodeau [record_id:26]. It matters because it turns an overlooked component of stealer logs—infection screenshots—into a proposed intelligence source. Its key claims are that screenshots can reveal infection vectors, that LLMs can extract IoCs from them at scale, that the approach found several hundred potential malicious download URLs, and that the method can be applied to fresh logs to reduce additional infections [record_id:26]. It also provides concrete campaign examples around cracked software, advertising for popular software, and free AI image generators [record_id:26]. For researchers studying AI-assisted threat intelligence, this is the stronger of the two records.

The BSidesLV 2026 talk, “S.L. Confidential: The Dirty Secrets of InfoStealers,” attributed to Olivier Bilodeau, is notable for its emphasis on the real-world contents, sensitivity, and future evolution of stealer logs [record_id:2818]. It matters because it frames stealer logs as deeply intimate and operationally decisive artifacts. The record claims that practitioners reading thousands of logs see browser autofills, banking data, reused passwords, personal details, and even logs involving operators themselves [record_id:2818]. It identifies incident responders and red teamers as key audiences, and it expands the discussion into browser defenses and next-generation stealer behavior [record_id:2818]. For researchers studying privacy harms, adversary emulation, or browser-bound credential defenses, this record is the broader strategic and conceptual anchor.
