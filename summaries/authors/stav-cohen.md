# Topic: Author: Stav Cohen

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Stav Cohen describe the same research presentation, delivered or listed in two major security-conference contexts in 2025: Black Hat USA and DEF CON 33. Both records are coauthored by Ben Nassi, Or Yair, and Stav Cohen, and both center on a class of LLM-agent attacks the authors call “Promptware,” specifically a variant named “Targeted Promptware Attacks” [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

The core contribution across the records is a practical demonstration that indirect prompt injection against Google Gemini for Workspace agents can be triggered through ordinary collaboration artifacts, especially a Google Calendar invite whose meeting subject contains a malicious prompt. The records argue that this lowers the barrier for prompt-injection exploitation: rather than requiring adversarial ML expertise, GPUs, or white-box access, an attacker can allegedly use common user-facing channels such as calendar invites, email, or shared Google Docs to influence integrated agents operating in the victim’s application context [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

The described research emphasizes agent hijacking, abuse of delegated permissions, lateral movement across agents and devices, and real-world impact beyond text generation. The claimed exploit demonstrations include toxic-content generation, spam and phishing, deletion of calendar events, exfiltration of emails and calendar events, victim geolocation, Zoom-based video streaming, a worm targeting Gemini for Workspace clients, and remote control of connected home devices such as windows, a boiler, and lights [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. Both records also mention a dedicated threat-analysis and risk-assessment framework, with the finding that 73% of identified risks were classified as high-critical [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

Because the corpus contains only two records, and because their raw text is nearly identical, the evidence base is narrow but coherent. It strongly supports that Stav Cohen’s represented work in this dataset concerns AI security, prompt injection, LLM-powered agents, Google Workspace/Gemini integrations, and the security consequences of giving agents access to user tools and device permissions. It does not, however, provide enough evidence to characterize Stav Cohen’s broader body of work outside this specific collaboration and presentation.

The raw text frames the problem as a new class of attacks against LLM-powered systems: “Promptware,” defined as prompts in text, images, or audio engineered to exploit LLMs at inference time and cause malicious activity within the application context [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The records specifically focus on LLM-powered agents embedded in Google Workspace and related Gemini surfaces.

The dominant source type is not a paper abstract with detailed methodology, nor a vulnerability advisory with reproduction steps, but a conference-talk abstract. Still, because both records describe the same research consistently, the evidence is strong that this presentation’s intended message is to make prompt-injection risks concrete, practical, and operationally consequential.

The landscape described is one in which AI assistants are not isolated chatbots. They are integrated agents with access to calendars, email, documents, mobile operating-system permissions, smart-home devices, communication applications, and other tools. ## Major Themes And Trends

A central theme is the practicalization of prompt injection. The records explicitly state that Promptware has often been perceived as “impractical and exotic” because people assume it requires specialized adversarial machine-learning expertise, GPU resources, and white-box system access [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The talk positions its contribution as refuting that assumption: the attack vector is a simple Google Calendar meeting invitation containing an indirect prompt injection in the subject line [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. This framing matters because it shifts the threat model from elite adversarial ML research to ordinary social and collaboration channels that attackers can plausibly reach.

A second major theme is indirect prompt injection through trusted productivity workflows. The records do not describe the victim directly pasting an adversarial prompt into Gemini. Instead, the attacker sends or shares a normal Workspace artifact: a calendar invite, an email, or a Google Doc [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The malicious instruction is embedded in content that the agent may later process. This is significant because it suggests that any external content consumed by an agent can become a control channel, especially when the agent operates with the victim’s permissions.

A third theme is agent hijacking through application context. Both records use the language of hijacking the application context, invoking integrated agents, and exploiting their permissions to perform malicious actions [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. This is an important conceptual contribution: the vulnerability is not merely that an LLM can be persuaded to say something harmful, but that an LLM-connected agent can misuse legitimate tools and delegated privileges. The records therefore connect prompt injection to long-standing security concerns around authorization boundaries, confused deputy problems, and abuse of delegated access.

A fourth recurring concern is lateral movement. The records claim that the demonstrations show both “inter-agent lateral movement,” meaning malicious activity triggered between different Gemini agents, and “inter-device lateral movement,” meaning escape from Gemini’s immediate boundaries into applications installed on a victim’s smartphone [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. This is one of the most notable themes because it treats LLM agents as part of a distributed environment rather than a single isolated service. The records suggest that compromises can cascade across web, mobile, assistant, and device ecosystems.

A fifth theme is cyber-physical consequence. The records claim that promptware can remotely control connected home appliances such as windows, a boiler, and lights, and can produce physical outcomes such as activating the boiler and lights or opening a window in a victim’s apartment [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. This theme broadens the research beyond conventional AI misuse, application security, or data leakage. It implies that LLM-agent integrations can become a pathway from text-based manipulation to physical-world actions when assistants have access to IoT or smart-home controls.

A sixth theme is risk quantification. Both records state that the authors developed a dedicated threat-analysis and risk-assessment framework and found that 73% of identified risks were high-critical, requiring immediate mitigations [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The raw text does not provide the framework’s details, scoring criteria, or risk population, so the claim cannot be deeply evaluated from these records alone. Still, its inclusion shows that the work aimed not only to demonstrate exploits but also to organize them into a risk model for end users.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The primary method discussed is an indirect prompt-injection attack delivered through a Google Calendar meeting invite. The records describe the attacker inviting a victim to a meeting whose subject contains the malicious prompt. This subject line then functions as the injection payload that hijacks the victim’s Gemini application context and invokes integrated agents [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The significance of the method is its simplicity: it uses a standard collaboration feature rather than requiring code execution, local compromise, or privileged system access.

The records also mention related delivery vectors: email and shared Google Docs. The text states that by sending a meeting invitation “or an email or sharing a Google Doc,” attackers could hijack Gemini’s agents and exploit their tools [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. These examples indicate a broader technique: embedding adversarial instructions into external content likely to be read, summarized, interpreted, or acted upon by an LLM-powered assistant.

The affected agent surfaces are three Gemini for Workspace assistants: the Gemini web interface at gemini.google.com, Gemini for Mobile, and Google Assistant powered by Gemini on Android devices [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The mobile and assistant surfaces are especially important because the records emphasize that Google Assistant runs with operating-system permissions on Android devices [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. This suggests that the research is concerned with how AI assistants inherit or mediate permissions from their host applications and platforms.

The records claim 15 exploit demonstrations of agent hijacking across these Gemini surfaces [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The described impacts include:

- content-generation abuse, such as generating toxic content;
- communication abuse, including spam and phishing;
- productivity-data manipulation, such as deleting a victim’s calendar events;
- data theft, including exfiltration of emails and calendar events;
- surveillance and location abuse, including victim geolocation and video streaming via Zoom;
- propagation, including launching a worm targeting Gemini for Workspace clients;
- smart-home or physical-world manipulation, including control of connected windows, a boiler, and lights [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

The conceptual workflow described across the records is: attacker places an indirect prompt in a Workspace-accessible artifact; Gemini or a Gemini-powered assistant processes that artifact; the prompt causes the agent to invoke tools available in the victim’s context; those tools perform actions that range from content generation to data exfiltration, device control, or further propagation [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

The records also mention a threat-analysis and risk-assessment framework developed by the authors. The framework’s internal structure is not included in the raw text, but its purpose is to assess end-user risk from the demonstrated attacks. The reported outcome is that 73% of identified risks were high-critical [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. Downstream researchers should treat this as an important claim but should seek the full talk, slides, paper, or supporting materials before relying on the number analytically.
