# Topic: Author: Ali Ranjbar

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Ali Ranjbar are both Black Hat USA 2025 briefing abstracts focused on vulnerability discovery in modern cellular systems. Together, they portray a research agenda centered on exposing deep, implementation-level weaknesses in next-generation mobile infrastructure through automated or semi-automated security testing. One record examines Open RAN / O-RAN architectures, especially RAN Intelligent Controllers, RAN nodes, xApps, public-facing interfaces, and the new attack surfaces created by disaggregation and cloud-native microservices [record_id:57]. The other examines 5G baseband firmware, especially Samsung and Pixel modem implementations and Non-Access Stratum messaging, using dependency-aware fuzzing on emulated firmware to reach complex state-dependent paths [record_id:73].

Across both records, the recurring contribution is not merely identifying vulnerabilities, but building testing methods adapted to the architectural complexity of modern 5G systems. The O-RAN work reports 26 previously unknown memory-corruption vulnerabilities and 20 new CVEs across O-RAN RIC and RAN implementations [record_id:57]. The baseband work reports 7 previously unknown vulnerabilities, with 5 CVEs assigned so far and several rated high or critical by vendors [record_id:73]. Both records emphasize that modern cellular systems have become harder to test because they are distributed, stateful, proprietary, cloud-native, or deeply dependent on inter-component interactions. Ranjbar’s attributed work, as represented here, sits at the intersection of mobile security, exploit development, vulnerability discovery, fuzzing, reverse engineering, and threat modeling.

The evidence base is narrow but strong for this specific characterization: only two records are included, but both are substantive research-talk abstracts with detailed problem statements, methods, vulnerability counts, exploitation scenarios, and claimed operational impacts. The main limitation is that the records are abstracts, not full papers or slide decks, so they do not provide implementation details, reproducibility artifacts, patch analysis, exploit code, disclosure timelines, or independent validation.

## Research Landscape

The records are both Black Hat briefing descriptions from 2025 and both concern cellular security. They do not cover a broad range of Ranjbar’s possible work; instead, they cluster tightly around next-generation mobile-network vulnerability research. The first record addresses O-RAN, the open and disaggregated evolution of the 5G Radio Access Network. It frames O-RAN as a shift away from “tightly integrated, vendor-specific systems” toward “disaggregated, software-driven architectures” with standardized interfaces and modular RAN Intelligent Controllers [record_id:57]. The second record addresses 5G baseband modem firmware, framing basebands as “the unseen engines of cellular communication” whose proprietary, closed-source, memory-unsafe implementations create a large attack surface with low visibility [record_id:73].

The research landscape represented by these records is therefore split across two sides of the 5G ecosystem:

- **Network-side infrastructure and O-RAN systems**, including RICs, RANs, xApps, under-protected RAN nodes, and public-facing or exposed interfaces [record_id:57].
- **Device-side or modem-side firmware**, including Samsung and Pixel 5G basebands, NAS messaging, emulated firmware execution, CPU architecture changes, C-to-C++ shifts, and redesigned inter-task communication models [record_id:73].

Both records describe work that responds to a similar underlying trend: 5G systems are more complex and more software-driven than prior generations, which creates new routes to vulnerabilities but also requires more sophisticated testing techniques. In O-RAN, openness, interoperability, microservices, cloud-native deployment, and disaggregation expand the attack surface [record_id:57]. In basebands, proprietary code, closed development, C/C++, state dependencies, and evolving firmware architectures make conventional fuzzing and manual harnessing insufficient [record_id:73].

The talks also share a security-research style: they begin from architectural change, define why existing testing approaches are inadequate, propose a targeted testing framework, and report previously unknown vulnerabilities and CVEs. This makes the included records more than high-level awareness talks; they are framed as original vulnerability-discovery contributions with concrete outputs.

## Major Themes And Trends

A central theme is that **5G security risk is increasingly architecture-specific**. The O-RAN record explicitly argues for “architecture-specific security testing” because O-RAN introduces new interfaces, modular controllers, microservice-based deployment, cloud-native architecture, and interactions among RICs, RANs, and third-party xApps [record_id:57]. The baseband record makes a parallel argument in the firmware domain: modern 5G basebands differ from older GSM and LTE basebands because of complex state dependencies, architecture shifts, C++ usage, and redesigned inter-task communication, so prior fuzzing approaches based on extensive manual annotation and harnessing do not scale [record_id:73].

Another strong theme is **automation as a response to complexity**. In the O-RAN record, the authors describe “the first automated security testing framework designed for O-RAN,” combining dynamic tracing and static analysis to uncover inter-component dependencies and generate constraint-driven test inputs [record_id:57]. In the baseband record, the authors describe a stateful fuzzing framework running directly on emulated baseband firmware, using iterative symbolic analysis to uncover state variables and preconditions so fuzzing can reach deep state-dependent paths [record_id:73]. In both cases, the research is not just about finding bugs manually; it is about creating methods that systematically expose bugs that would otherwise remain hidden behind complex interactions and internal state.

A third theme is **memory corruption and memory-unsafe implementation risk**. The O-RAN record reports 26 previously unknown memory-corruption vulnerabilities in widely used O-RAN RIC and RAN implementations, resulting in 20 CVEs [record_id:57]. The baseband record explicitly identifies reliance on memory-unsafe C/C++ as part of the baseband attack surface and reports 7 previously unknown vulnerabilities in real-world devices, with 5 CVEs assigned so far [record_id:73]. The records do not provide full bug-class breakdowns, but both indicate that implementation-level memory safety remains a major problem in critical cellular components.

A fourth theme is **remote or externally triggered exploitation**. The O-RAN record says the vulnerabilities can be remotely exploitable via public-facing interfaces by malicious user equipment or rogue RAN nodes, with possible impacts including silent service disruptions, performance degradation, component crashes, and system-wide failures [record_id:57]. The baseband record mentions real-world exploits such as SMS-triggered and malicious-network-triggered crashes [record_id:73]. This shared focus matters because it places the vulnerabilities in realistic adversarial settings: attackers may interact with cellular infrastructure or devices through protocol-facing surfaces rather than needing local access.

A fifth theme is **the expanding security consequences of openness and interoperability**. The O-RAN record explicitly presents a tension: openness promotes innovation and interoperability, but it “significantly expands the attack surface” [record_id:57]. This is not contradicted by the baseband record, which concerns closed and proprietary systems rather than open architectures, but the two together show that both openness and closedness create different security problems. O-RAN’s openness creates more standardized and exposed interfaces that must be defended [record_id:57]. Baseband closedness creates limited visibility, difficult reverse engineering, and dependence on emulation and symbolic analysis to test proprietary firmware [record_id:73]. The records therefore suggest that neither closed proprietary telecom stacks nor open disaggregated architectures are inherently safe; each demands specialized testing.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The O-RAN record describes a workflow built around mapping attack surfaces, creating a taxonomy of attack vectors, and building automated testing for O-RAN components. The talk proposes to contrast O-RAN’s microservice-based, cloud-native architecture with traditional closed RANs, then map new attack surfaces and protection challenges introduced by the shift [record_id:57]. It also introduces a taxonomy of attack vectors targeting the O-RAN stack to guide threat modeling and defense [record_id:57]. The technical testing approach combines **dynamic tracing** and **static analysis** to uncover inter-component dependencies, then uses those dependencies to generate **constraint-driven test inputs** capable of reaching deep internal logic within RICs, RANs, and third-party xApps [record_id:57]. This suggests an emphasis on dependency discovery across distributed telecom components and on input generation that is aware of those dependencies.

The baseband record describes a different but related approach: reverse engineering and emulation of Samsung and Pixel 5G basebands, focused on Non-Access Stratum messaging [record_id:73]. The talk highlights challenges introduced by the evolution from 4G to 5G, including CPU architecture changes, movement from C to C++, and a redesigned inter-task communication model [record_id:73]. To handle this, the authors present a **stateful fuzzing framework** that runs directly on emulated baseband firmware [record_id:73]. The core method is an **iterative symbolic analysis technique** that progressively discovers state variables and their preconditions, allowing fuzzing to target deep state-dependent execution paths while reducing the path explosion problem [record_id:73].

Taken together, the methods have several common properties:

1. **Dependency awareness**: The O-RAN method uncovers inter-component dependencies across RICs, RANs, and xApps [record_id:57]. The baseband method uncovers state variables and preconditions inside firmware execution [record_id:73].
2. **Hybrid analysis**: O-RAN combines dynamic tracing and static analysis [record_id:57]. Baseband work combines emulation, fuzzing, reverse engineering, and symbolic analysis [record_id:73].
3. **Deep-path reachability**: Both records stress reaching “deep internal logic” or “deep, state-dependent paths” rather than shallow parser paths [record_id:57] [record_id:73].
4. **Real-world target validation**: O-RAN testing is applied to widely used RIC and RAN implementations [record_id:57]. Baseband testing is applied to real-world devices including Google Pixel and Samsung Galaxy models [record_id:73].
5. **Operational impact demonstration**: Both talks claim demonstrations of practical impacts: service disruption and system-wide failures for O-RAN [record_id:57], and SMS or malicious-network-triggered crashes for basebands [record_id:73].

These methods indicate a research approach oriented toward automating the hardest parts of telecom vulnerability discovery: reconstructing hidden dependencies, satisfying complex state preconditions, and exercising realistic protocol-facing attack surfaces.

The abstract frames modern 5G basebands as harder to fuzz than GSM or LTE basebands because manual annotation and harnessing do not scale to complex state dependencies and evolving firmware architectures [record_id:73]. The evidence is strongest where both records independently converge: modern 5G systems are complex, stateful, and difficult to test with older methods; automated analysis can uncover previously unknown vulnerabilities; and the resulting bugs may be remotely triggered through realistic cellular interfaces [record_id:57] [record_id:73].
