# Topic: Author: Mike Holcomb

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Mike Holcomb in this topic are closely overlapping DEF CON 33 entries from 2025 about the rapidly changing security landscape for operational technology and industrial control systems, framed under the title variants “The Worst ICS OT Love Story Ever Told” and “The Worst ICS/OT Love Story Every Told” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. The raw texts are effectively the same abstract: both argue that OT/ICS security is changing quickly, that defenders are not prepared, and that the threat model has shifted from a pre-2021 emphasis on state adversaries to a post-Colonial Pipeline focus on ransomware operators and hacktivists, and then further toward a 2025 environment where state adversaries, ransomware operators, and hacktivists are increasingly aligned [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

The central contribution of these records is a concise threat-landscape argument for OT/ICS: advanced capabilities and tools are becoming available to attackers with “intermediate to no skill,” creating a preparedness gap for critical infrastructure defenders [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. The records do not provide detailed technical methods, case studies, architectures, or defensive playbooks in the supplied text, but they clearly establish the talk’s intended focus: reassessing OT/ICS threat models and discussing how defenders can prepare for what is coming [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

## Major Themes And Trends

### Rapid Change In OT/ICS Security

The clearest theme is acceleration. Both records open with the assertion that “the world of securing OT/ICS is changing FAST” and immediately add that defenders are “not prepared” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. This establishes urgency as the talk’s organizing frame. The abstract suggests that existing OT/ICS security assumptions are lagging behind adversary evolution.

The emphasis on speed matters because OT/ICS environments are often associated with long asset lifecycles, operational constraints, and slower modernization cycles. Although the raw text does not elaborate on those operational details, its warning that the threat landscape is changing quickly implies a mismatch between adversary adaptation and defender readiness [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

### A Three-Phase Threat-Model Shift

Both records describe a chronological shift in how OT/ICS defenders conceptualize adversaries:

1. Before the Colonial Pipeline incident in 2021, the focus was on “protecting against state adversaries.”
2. After Colonial Pipeline, attention shifted toward “ransomware operators and hacktivists.”
3. By 2025, the records claim there is “more alignment between state adversaries, ransomware operators and hacktivists” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

This is the most concrete analytical structure in the records. It frames the Colonial Pipeline incident as a major inflection point in OT/ICS defensive thinking. The records do not provide a detailed account of the incident or its technical consequences, but they clearly position it as a catalyst that broadened defender concern beyond nation-state activity to include financially motivated and politically motivated disruptive actors [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

The 2025 claim of alignment between state adversaries, ransomware operators, and hacktivists is especially significant. It suggests the talk is not merely about additional adversary categories, but about convergence among them. That convergence could imply shared tooling, overlapping motivations, collaboration, imitation, opportunistic reuse of capabilities, or blurred attribution boundaries. The records themselves do not define the nature of the alignment, so that remains an open question, but the theme is central to both abstracts [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

### Preparedness Gap

Both records ask whether defenders are ready for the coming environment and answer directly: “No. But we can be” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. This establishes the talk as both diagnostic and prescriptive. The diagnostic element is the claim that OT/ICS defenders are currently unprepared. The prescriptive element is the promise to “talk about how” preparation can improve [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

However, the raw record text does not include the promised recommendations. It signals that the talk contains or intended to contain a preparation strategy, but it does not reveal what that strategy is. As a result, the records are strong evidence of Holcomb’s framing and concerns, but weak evidence for the specific defensive measures he recommended.

### Democratization Of Advanced Capabilities

Another recurring concern is that advanced capabilities and tools are entering the hands of attackers with “intermediate to no skill” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. This implies a democratization or commoditization trend in offensive capability. The records do not name particular tools, malware families, exploit frameworks, AI systems, open-source packages, or intrusion techniques, but they clearly argue that capability barriers are lowering.

For OT/ICS defenders, this theme is important because the historical model of highly specialized industrial attacks being reserved for sophisticated state actors becomes less reliable. If less skilled attackers can access advanced capabilities, then threat frequency, diversity, and unpredictability may increase. Both records present this as part of the reason the OT/ICS community is not prepared for what is coming [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

### Convergence Of Strategic, Financial, And Ideological Threats

The raw texts distinguish state adversaries, ransomware operators, and hacktivists, then emphasize their increasing alignment in 2025 [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. This theme points to a convergence of strategic, financial, and ideological motivations. Even without further detail, the implication is that OT/ICS defenders can no longer treat these categories as separate planning silos.

The records do not specify whether the alignment is operational, tactical, ideological, or ecosystem-level. Still, the repeated language indicates that Holcomb’s talk likely sought to update OT/ICS threat modeling to account for hybridized adversary behavior [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The supplied text does not identify specific technical tools, software, architectures, defensive products, incident response workflows, or exploitation methods. Instead, it discusses approaches at the level of threat modeling and preparedness.

The primary method implied across both records is adversary-centered threat-model revision. The records organize the OT/ICS security landscape around changing adversary categories over time: state adversaries before 2021, ransomware operators and hacktivists after the Colonial Pipeline incident, and an increasingly aligned ecosystem of all three by 2025 [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. This suggests a methodology of periodically reassessing who the relevant attackers are, what capabilities they possess, and how their relationships or incentives are changing.

A second approach is preparedness planning against capability diffusion. The records warn that “advanced capabilities and tools” may be available to everyday attackers with “intermediate to no skill” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. While the records do not specify the tools, they imply that OT/ICS security programs should not assume that advanced attacks require advanced operators. A downstream research agent could use this as a lead to investigate Holcomb’s broader views on accessible OT attack tooling, adversary emulation, ransomware impacts on industrial environments, and defensive readiness models.

A third approach is scenario-based future readiness. Both records ask: “Are we prepared today for what’s coming?” and answer that defenders are not, but can be [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. This implies a forward-looking security planning approach: evaluate not just current threats, but near-future adversary capability and alignment trends. The record text, however, does not include the concrete preparation framework.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The main limitation is corpus size. There are only two records, and they appear to be duplicate or near-duplicate listings for the same talk [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. This means the records support conclusions about one DEF CON 33 presentation abstract, not about Mike Holcomb’s complete research agenda.

A second limitation is lack of technical specificity. The records mention “advanced capabilities and tools” but do not name them [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. They do not describe malware, vulnerabilities, attack chains, OT protocols, industrial vendors, detection strategies, segmentation models, tabletop exercises, incident response practices, or safety-engineering concerns. Downstream agents seeking technical detail would need the talk video, transcript, slides, or related publications.

A third limitation is ambiguity around “alignment.” Both records say that in 2025 there is “more alignment between state adversaries, ransomware operators and hacktivists” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. The records do not explain whether this means direct collaboration, shared infrastructure, shared tooling, ideological overlap, copycat behavior, state tolerance of criminal groups, or simple convergence in target selection. This is an important open research question because different forms of alignment imply different defensive and intelligence requirements.

A fourth limitation is the absence of the promised preparation guidance. Both records ask whether defenders are prepared and state, “No. But we can be. And we’ll talk about how” [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029]. The raw text does not include the “how.” It is therefore not possible from these records alone to summarize Holcomb’s recommended controls, frameworks, prioritization methods, or maturity model.

- How did he define OT/ICS preparedness?
