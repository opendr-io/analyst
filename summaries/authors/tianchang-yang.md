# Topic: Author: Tianchang Yang

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Tianchang Yang are both Black Hat USA 2025 mobile-security briefings focused on vulnerability discovery in modern cellular infrastructure and devices. Together, they depict a research agenda centered on the security consequences of 5G evolution: disaggregation and cloud-native architecture in Open RAN on the network side, and increasingly complex proprietary 5G baseband firmware on the device side. Both talks emphasize that modern cellular systems expose deep, stateful, architecture-specific attack surfaces that are difficult to test with generic methods and require specialized automated analysis.

The first record, “Open RAN, Open Risk,” examines the security implications of Open RAN’s move from closed vendor-integrated systems toward modular, software-driven, microservice-based architectures. It reports a systematic testing effort that found 26 previously unknown memory-corruption vulnerabilities across O-RAN RIC and RAN implementations, resulting in 20 CVEs, and frames these findings around new attack surfaces reachable by malicious user equipment or rogue RAN nodes [record_id:57]. The second record, “Uncovering ‘NASty’ 5G Baseband Vulnerabilities through Dependency-Aware Fuzzing,” focuses on Samsung and Pixel 5G baseband firmware, especially Non-Access Stratum messaging, and presents a stateful fuzzing framework using emulation and iterative symbolic analysis to uncover deep execution paths. It reports 7 previously unknown vulnerabilities, 5 assigned CVEs, and real-world exploit demonstrations such as SMS- and malicious-network-triggered crashes [record_id:73].

Across both records, Yang’s attributed work appears to contribute to automated security testing for complex 5G systems, combining reverse engineering, static/dynamic analysis, symbolic reasoning, fuzzing, and threat modeling. The evidence base is strong for identifying the topics and claimed technical contributions of the talks, but limited to conference abstracts: the records do not include full papers, implementation details, exploit traces, CVE identifiers, vendor advisories, source code, or independent validation.

## Research Landscape

The included records are both Black Hat USA 2025 Briefings tagged under mobile security, with one additionally tied to network security and threat modeling, and the other to hardware/embedded and OT/IoT security. Both are multi-author presentations in which Tianchang Yang is listed as an author rather than necessarily the sole speaker. The records therefore support a profile of Yang’s work as part of collaborative research teams investigating high-impact vulnerability discovery in cellular systems [record_id:57] [record_id:73].

The research landscape represented here is narrow but coherent. It spans two major layers of the 5G ecosystem:

1. **Network-side 5G infrastructure**, especially Open RAN and O-RAN RIC/RAN implementations. This record concerns architectural openness, standardized interfaces, RAN disaggregation, cloud-native microservices, RAN Intelligent Controllers, xApps, public-facing interfaces, and attacks from malicious user equipment or rogue RAN nodes [record_id:57].

2. **Device-side cellular baseband firmware**, especially Samsung and Google Pixel 5G basebands and NAS messaging. This record concerns reverse engineering, emulation, proprietary firmware, C/C++ memory safety risks, state dependencies, inter-task communication, and stateful fuzzing of baseband firmware [record_id:73].

The dominant source type is the conference talk abstract. As a result, the records provide high-level claims about research motivation, methods, vulnerability counts, affected classes of systems, and demonstration goals, but they do not provide the lower-level technical artifacts that would be needed for reproduction. The abstracts are nevertheless rich enough to identify recurring concerns: modern 5G systems are becoming more complex, more modular, more software-defined, and more stateful; this complexity creates new attack surfaces; and effective vulnerability discovery requires automation tailored to the architecture under test.

## Major Themes And Trends

A central theme across the records is that 5G security cannot be evaluated adequately using legacy assumptions or generic testing alone. Both talks argue that the architecture of the target system fundamentally shapes the attack surface and the testing strategy. In the Open RAN case, the transition from “tightly integrated, vendor-specific systems” to “disaggregated, software-driven architectures” introduces new standardized interfaces, modular RICs, third-party xApps, and microservice-based deployments [record_id:57]. In the baseband case, the evolution from 4G to 5G introduces more complex state dependencies, changes in CPU architecture, a move from C to C++, and a redesigned inter-task communication model [record_id:73]. These shifts are presented not merely as engineering changes, but as security-relevant transformations.

Another major theme is **deep-state vulnerability discovery**. Both records emphasize that the important bugs are not necessarily reachable through shallow input mutation. The Open RAN talk describes an automated framework that combines dynamic tracing and static analysis to discover inter-component dependencies and generate constraint-driven test inputs capable of reaching “deep internal logic” in RICs, RANs, and third-party xApps [record_id:57]. The baseband talk similarly presents a stateful fuzzing framework using iterative symbolic analysis to identify state variables and preconditions needed to reach different execution paths, explicitly addressing the path-explosion problem [record_id:73]. Across both talks, the work is framed around finding vulnerabilities hidden behind inter-component, inter-task, or protocol-state dependencies.

A third recurring concern is **memory corruption in critical cellular components**. The Open RAN record reports 26 previously unknown memory-corruption vulnerabilities across O-RAN RIC and RAN implementations, with 20 new CVEs [record_id:57]. The baseband record attributes the baseband attack surface partly to “memory-unsafe C/C++,” reports 7 previously unknown vulnerabilities, and states that 5 CVEs had been assigned, with several rated high or critical by vendors [record_id:73]. The abstracts do not give the exact vulnerability classes, but both connect memory-unsafe implementation practices and complex cellular software to exploitable reliability and security failures.

Both records also frame the consequences of the discovered vulnerabilities in operational terms. The Open RAN talk claims impacts including silent service disruptions, performance degradation, component crashes, and system-wide failures [record_id:57]. The baseband talk says the researchers demonstrate real-world exploits including SMS-triggered and malicious-network-triggered crashes [record_id:73]. This suggests a shared emphasis on vulnerabilities that cross the line from theoretical parser bugs to practical disruptions in deployed or deployable 5G environments.

A final trend is the emphasis on **automation as a way to scale beyond manual reverse engineering and harnessing**. The baseband abstract explicitly contrasts the proposed framework with prior GSM and LTE baseband fuzzing approaches that required extensive manual annotation and harnessing, arguing that these methods fall short for modern 5G systems [record_id:73]. The Open RAN abstract likewise presents what it calls the “first automated security testing framework designed for O-RAN,” using analysis-driven test generation to explore complex O-RAN components [record_id:57]. In both cases, Yang’s attributed work appears to align with a broader trend in security research: using program analysis and fuzzing automation to make vulnerability discovery feasible in increasingly complex, proprietary, or distributed systems.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records describe several technical methods, though at abstract level rather than implementation level.

For Open RAN security testing, the record describes a process beginning with attack-surface mapping and threat modeling. The talk proposes mapping new attack surfaces and protection challenges introduced by O-RAN’s microservice-based, cloud-native architecture, especially in contrast with traditional closed RANs [record_id:57]. It also introduces a taxonomy of attack vectors targeting the O-RAN stack, intended to guide threat modeling and defense strategies [record_id:57]. This indicates that the research is not limited to fuzzing or bug hunting; it also attempts to organize the O-RAN risk landscape into a structured model.

The Open RAN record then describes an automated security testing framework for O-RAN. The framework combines dynamic tracing and static analysis to uncover inter-component dependencies, then uses those dependencies to generate constraint-driven test inputs that can reach deep logic within RICs, RANs, and third-party xApps [record_id:57]. This approach appears tailored to distributed O-RAN architectures, where behavior is shaped by interactions among components rather than by single isolated binaries. The record emphasizes remote exploitability through public-facing interfaces by malicious user equipment or rogue RAN nodes [record_id:57], suggesting that the testing approach models or exercises externally reachable protocol paths.

For 5G baseband vulnerability discovery, the second record discusses reverse engineering and emulation of Samsung and Pixel 5G basebands with a focus on Non-Access Stratum messaging [record_id:73]. It describes a stateful fuzzing framework that runs directly on emulated baseband firmware [record_id:73]. Running fuzzing on emulated firmware is significant because basebands are proprietary and difficult to instrument, and because deep firmware behavior may depend on internal state and task interactions.

The baseband approach centers on “iterative symbolic analysis.” According to the record, this technique progressively uncovers state variables and their preconditions in order to reach different execution paths, enabling fuzzing of deep, state-dependent behavior while mitigating path explosion [record_id:73]. The abstract positions this as a response to the limitations of earlier fuzzing techniques that required extensive manual annotation and harnessing and were less scalable for modern 5G basebands [record_id:73].

Across both records, the common methodological pattern is dependency-aware or state-aware testing. In Open RAN, dependencies are inter-component dependencies among RICs, RANs, and xApps [record_id:57]. In basebands, dependencies are internal state variables, preconditions, and inter-task communication states within firmware [record_id:73]. Both approaches try to transform hidden architectural or state constraints into inputs that can drive execution deeper into the system.

Tianchang Yang’s presence in both records links the Open RAN and baseband projects into a broader attributed research profile focused on automated vulnerability discovery in advanced cellular ecosystems.
