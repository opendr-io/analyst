# Topic: Author: Klaus Agnoletti

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Klaus Agnoletti, both co-authored with Glen Sorensen and presented at BSidesLV 2025, center on a distinctive contribution to cybersecurity education: using tabletop role-playing game mechanics to improve security training, professional development, and incident response exercises. The records describe both a hands-on workshop and a shorter conference talk. Together, they argue that conventional cybersecurity training and tabletop exercises are often disengaging, overly theoretical, or insufficiently collaborative, and they propose game-based learning—especially techniques inspired by Dungeons & Dragons and tabletop RPGs—as a practical alternative [record_id:2425] [record_id:2432].

Across the records, Agnoletti’s attributed work emphasizes human-centered cybersecurity learning. The core claim is that structured play, collaborative storytelling, role assignment, scenario-based decision-making, and game mechanics such as dice rolls and character abilities can make complex security concepts more accessible and memorable. The workshop record is more implementation-focused, describing a four-hour session in which participants design and test incident response scenarios using elements such as MITRE ATT&CK, character roles, unexpected events, and an “Incident Master” facilitation role [record_id:2425]. The talk record is broader and more persuasive, positioning Dungeons & Dragons-style roleplaying as a “security tool” for professional development, engagement, and skill-building across use cases such as incident response and zero trust architecture [record_id:2432].

The evidence base is narrow but coherent. Both records come from the same event, year, authorship pairing, and thematic area. They do not provide full papers, empirical results, detailed curricula, or post-event evaluations. However, they strongly establish Agnoletti’s recurring theme at BSidesLV 2025: applying game-based and roleplaying methods to cybersecurity education, security culture, and incident response readiness.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Roleplaying Games As Cybersecurity Training Infrastructure

The dominant theme across both records is the use of tabletop role-playing games as a model for cybersecurity learning. The records repeatedly contrast engaging, game-based training with boring or ineffective conventional training. In the workshop description, the premise is that traditional tabletop exercises can “put your team to sleep,” while an “innovative roleplaying framework inspired by tabletop RPGs” can transform incident response training [record_id:2425]. The talk similarly opens from the idea of security training that “puts your team to sleep” and asks whether a powerful cybersecurity training tool has been “sitting in your game room all along” [record_id:2432].

This framing treats tabletop RPG mechanics not as a novelty add-on but as a serious instructional design strategy. The records describe structured play, collaborative storytelling, character roles, abilities, dynamic scenarios, and dice rolls as mechanisms for participation and learning [record_id:2425] [record_id:2432]. The underlying trend is toward experiential and participatory security education rather than lecture-based or document-driven instruction.

### Human-Centered Incident Response

The workshop record explicitly emphasizes “the human elements of incident response,” stating that the approach is designed to make incident response training accessible to both technical and non-technical audiences [record_id:2425]. This is an important recurring concern: cybersecurity incidents are not merely technical events; they involve communication, prioritization, uncertainty, organizational roles, and decision-making under pressure.

By introducing character roles with unique abilities and unexpected scenario events, the workshop appears designed to simulate how teams coordinate during incidents, not just how individuals recall procedures [record_id:2425]. The talk record reinforces this by arguing that people learn through collaboration and that game-based learning creates environments where participants engage with material and break down professional barriers [record_id:2432].

Taken together, the records present incident response training as a socio-technical practice. The technical scenario matters, but so do motivation, group dynamics, facilitation, and psychological engagement.

### From Passive Tabletop Exercises To Dynamic Scenarios

A second major theme is dissatisfaction with traditional tabletop exercises. The records do not provide a formal critique, but both imply that existing approaches can be boring, passive, theoretical, or isolating [record_id:2425] [record_id:2432]. The proposed alternative is dynamic scenario design.

In the workshop, participants are expected to design “realistic incident response challenges using the MITRE ATT&CK framework” and craft “unexpected events” that keep participants engaged [record_id:2425]. This points toward a shift from scripted, predictable tabletop exercises to adaptive, game-like exercises where uncertainty and participant choice matter. The use of dice rolls further suggests controlled randomness, allowing scenarios to evolve in ways that may better approximate the unpredictability of real incidents [record_id:2425].

The shorter talk broadens this concept beyond incident response by saying tabletop role-playing mechanics can turn challenging training scenarios, “from incident response to zero trust architecture,” into adventures [record_id:2432]. This suggests a trend toward using roleplaying not only for crisis simulation but also for abstract security architecture and policy concepts.

### Collaborative Learning And Professional Development

The talk record places strong emphasis on game-based learning as professional development. It argues that traditional training often pushes “isolated, theoretical learning,” while humans learn best when working together [record_id:2432]. Structured play and collaborative storytelling are presented as ways for participants to “live” concepts rather than memorize them [record_id:2432].

This theme is less prominent but still present in the workshop, where participants are described as working in small groups, developing their own scenarios, testing each other’s scenarios, and providing feedback for refinement [record_id:2425]. The design includes peer interaction at multiple stages: demonstration, group scenario creation, testing, and feedback. This indicates that the educational model is not just “use games in training,” but “use collaborative design and play to create shared understanding.”

### Accessibility Across Technical And Non-Technical Audiences

The workshop states that its approach makes incident response training accessible to both technical and non-technical audiences [record_id:2425]. This is significant because incident response often involves stakeholders outside security operations, such as legal, communications, executives, business owners, and compliance personnel. While the record does not enumerate those roles, its accessibility framing indicates that roleplaying may be intended to bridge disciplinary gaps.

The talk similarly discusses breaking down “professional barriers” and transforming resistant learners into engaged participants [record_id:2432]. The repeated concern is not only teaching cybersecurity concepts, but overcoming resistance, disengagement, and separation between professional groups.

### Security Culture Through Play

Both records imply that security training is part of building a broader security culture. The workshop closes by positioning the session as useful for people “responsible for team training or building security culture” [record_id:2425]. The talk frames roleplaying games as part of professional development and as a means of creating genuine understanding through experience [record_id:2432].

The records therefore suggest a view of security culture as something developed through shared practice, story, and interaction. Security culture is not treated as awareness messaging alone; it is tied to rehearsing decisions, exploring scenarios, and building team confidence.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records describe several practical methods and approaches, though they do so at conference-abstract level rather than in implementation detail.

### Tabletop RPG-Inspired Training Framework

The central method is a roleplaying framework inspired by tabletop RPGs. The workshop explicitly mentions dice rolls, character abilities, and dynamic scenarios [record_id:2425]. These mechanics appear to be repurposed for cybersecurity training so that participants take on roles, encounter uncertain conditions, and respond to evolving incident situations.

The talk identifies Dungeons & Dragons and tabletop role-playing mechanics as the model, arguing that roleplaying games can turn difficult training scenarios into engaging “adventures” [record_id:2432]. The evidence indicates a deliberate transfer of game mechanics into professional security learning.

### Character Roles And Unique Abilities

The workshop promises that participants will learn to create “character roles with unique abilities” [record_id:2425]. This likely maps cybersecurity or organizational functions into playable roles, though the abstract does not provide examples. The method may help participants understand the responsibilities, constraints, and strengths of different incident response stakeholders.

Because only the abstract is available, downstream researchers should avoid assuming the exact role taxonomy. What can be stated confidently is that the proposed exercise design includes differentiated participant roles and role-specific capabilities [record_id:2425].

### MITRE ATT&CK-Based Scenario Design

The workshop explicitly references designing “realistic incident response challenges using the MITRE ATT&CK framework” [record_id:2425]. This is the clearest named cybersecurity framework in the records. Its inclusion suggests that the game-based training is intended to remain grounded in recognizable adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures rather than becoming purely fictional play.

The record does not identify specific ATT&CK techniques, adversary groups, platforms, or detection methods. The evidence supports only the broader point that MITRE ATT&CK is used as a scenario design aid in the workshop [record_id:2425].

### Unexpected Events And Dynamic Scenario Progression

The workshop discusses crafting unexpected events to keep participants engaged [record_id:2425]. Combined with dice rolls and dynamic scenarios, this suggests a facilitated simulation in which the scenario can change during play. This method may help participants practice adaptability and decision-making under uncertainty.

The talk also emphasizes structured play and collaborative storytelling, which implies that scenarios unfold through participant interaction rather than fixed lecture content [record_id:2432]. However, the records do not define the mechanics of event timing, escalation, scoring, failure conditions, or debriefing.

### “Incident Master” Facilitation

A notable phrase in the workshop is “Incident Master,” described as a facilitation role participants will learn [record_id:2425]. This clearly echoes the “Dungeon Master” role in Dungeons & Dragons, adapted to incident response exercises. The “Incident Master” appears to guide the scenario, manage events, and facilitate participant engagement.

This is one of the more unique contributions in the record set: it names a facilitator archetype for cybersecurity roleplaying exercises. The abstract indicates that attendees will leave with facilitation skills as an “Incident Master” [record_id:2425], but does not provide a full facilitator guide or competency model.

### Small-Group Exercise Design And Peer Testing

The workshop includes a practical workflow: participants experience a demonstration, develop their own scenarios in small groups, test each other’s scenarios, and provide feedback for refinement [record_id:2425]. This is more than a lecture on game-based learning; it is a design studio format.

This method is important because it suggests that Agnoletti and Sorensen are not only advocating for roleplaying training but teaching others to build such exercises themselves. The stated output is a “ready-to-implement scenario” and community resources for continued development [record_id:2425].

### Game-Based Learning For Professional Development

The talk record uses broader learning-science language, asserting that game-based learning transforms how professionals master complex skills and that humans learn best when working together [record_id:2432]. It frames tabletop role-playing as a way to help participants move beyond memorization into lived experience.

The record claims there is “compelling evidence” behind why roleplaying games work, but the abstract does not cite specific studies, data, or references [record_id:2432]. Downstream researchers should treat this as a claim made in the talk description, not as demonstrated evidence within the record itself.

### Application Beyond Incident Response

Incident response is the strongest and most concrete use case, especially in the workshop [record_id:2425]. However, the talk states that tabletop role-playing mechanics can be applied to scenarios “from incident response to zero trust architecture” [record_id:2432]. This expands the potential scope from operational response exercises to broader architectural or strategic security training.

The record does not explain how zero trust architecture would be converted into a roleplaying scenario, so this remains an intriguing but underdeveloped application in the available material [record_id:2432].
