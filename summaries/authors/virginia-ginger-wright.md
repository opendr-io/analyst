## Executive Summary

The three records attributed to Virginia “Ginger” Wright present a tightly focused body of BSidesLV 2025 material centered on cyber resilience for the water sector. Collectively, they frame U.S. water infrastructure as an urgent cyber-physical security concern, with risk driven by geopolitical threat activity, intrusions affecting water systems, weak coordination among stakeholders, regulatory fragility, and insufficient engineering education around cybersecurity [record_id:2427] [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].

Taken together, the records suggest that Wright’s attributed BSidesLV 2025 work is less about narrow technical exploit research and more about critical infrastructure resilience, cyber-informed design, water-sector risk governance, and the operational consequences of adversarial pressure on municipal and rural water systems.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Water infrastructure as life-critical infrastructure

The most prominent theme is that water-sector cybersecurity is not merely an IT concern but a matter of public safety and essential services. The phrase “Water is life” sets the stakes explicitly [record_id:2427]. The same record describes U.S. water infrastructure as “our most essential infrastructure” and warns that cyber vulnerability, regulatory fragility, and geopolitical tension leave it exposed “at a time when resilience is most critical” [record_id:2427].

This theme places water security in a cyber-physical context. The records do not focus on data confidentiality, enterprise IT compromise, or generic network defense. Instead, they treat water systems as municipal and rural infrastructure whose cyber compromise could affect basic societal function [record_id:2427]. The training records reinforce this by targeting engineers and design processes rather than only security operations teams [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].

### Escalating adversary pressure and geopolitical risk

The threat-landscape record presents a sharp escalation narrative. It states that after “high-profile cyber intrusions of 2024,” including “Volt Typhoon and Iran-linked Cyber Avengers,” 2025 had “already seen a surge in attempted and successful breaches targeting municipal and rural water systems” [record_id:2427]. This record directly links water-sector cyber risk to geopolitical tension and named adversary activity [record_id:2427].

The record does not provide incident details, technical indicators, or case-study evidence for the named intrusions, so downstream researchers should treat this as an event abstract’s framing rather than a substantiated incident analysis. Still, it is important because it situates Wright’s attributed work in a current-threat context: adversaries are not theoretical, and water infrastructure is presented as an active target [record_id:2427].

### Governance, coordination, and trust as resilience problems

Another major theme is that water-sector resilience is not only a technical problem. “Defending Our Water - Defending Our Lives” states that escalating threats are “compounded by deteriorating trust and coordination between public and private sector stakeholders” [record_id:2427]. It also names “regulatory fragility” as part of the “perfect storm” that exposes essential infrastructure [record_id:2427].

This framing suggests that Wright’s attributed work treats water cybersecurity as a systems problem involving institutions, governance, and stakeholder relationships. The record does not specify which regulatory mechanisms are fragile, what trust relationships have deteriorated, or what reforms are proposed. But it makes clear that the risk model includes social and institutional failure modes alongside cyber vulnerability [record_id:2427].

### Cyber-Informed Engineering as a preventive design discipline

The two “Engineering Cyber Resilience for the Water Sector” records introduce the most concrete methodological theme: Cyber-Informed Engineering. The workshop is explicitly about “What Engineers Need to Know About Cyber and Why,” including the claim that engineers “are not getting this in school” [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. It uses a hypothetical engineering project to discuss and apply CIE principles throughout the workshop [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].

The core idea is preventive: participants learn “a methodology of designing out cyber-risk before a system is placed into operation” [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. This is a significant recurring contribution because it shifts the security posture from after-the-fact protection or incident response to early lifecycle engineering choices. In the context of water-sector infrastructure, this implies that resilience should be built into system architecture, operational assumptions, and engineering requirements before deployment.

### Education and hands-on learning for engineers

The training records emphasize pedagogy. The workshop is described as “hands-on,” with exercises that develop understanding of CIE principles [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. Participants receive a workbook intended to structure their learning journey, capture insights and lessons, and provide a takeaway that can support further conversations after the event [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].

The repeated phrase that engineers are “not getting this in school” highlights an educational gap [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. The workshop appears designed to fill that gap by giving non-specialist or cross-disciplinary participants a structured method for thinking about cyber risk in engineering projects. This supports a broader trend in operational technology security: cybersecurity expertise must be integrated into engineering practice, not isolated in security teams.

### Case-based learning using fictionalized scenarios grounded in real-world examples

Both workshop records state that the scenario is fictional but “draws from a selection of real-world case studies” and is crafted to support application of CIE principles [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. This suggests a teaching approach that avoids reliance on a single real incident while still grounding the exercise in practical patterns. The record does not disclose the real-world cases used, but the method implies an abstraction of lessons from multiple incidents or engineering failures into a safe, structured learning scenario.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records discuss a limited but coherent set of approaches.

The most explicit method is Cyber-Informed Engineering. The workshop uses CIE principles as the central framework for building cyber resilience into water-sector engineering projects [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. The described approach involves applying these principles during a hypothetical project scenario rather than treating them as abstract lecture material [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].

A second approach is hands-on exercise-based training. The workshop is described as “filled with exercises” and designed to develop participant understanding through practice [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. The learning model includes a workbook that helps participants structure the workshop experience, capture lessons learned, and retain a concrete artifact for follow-up conversations [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].

A third approach is case-study abstraction. The workshop scenario is fictional, but it draws from real-world case studies [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. This is a useful teaching mechanism for sensitive critical infrastructure domains because it can illustrate realistic risk patterns without disclosing operationally sensitive details or becoming constrained by the facts of a single incident.

A fourth approach is cross-stakeholder resilience framing. The threat-landscape record identifies water-sector cyber risk as a convergence of cyber vulnerability, regulatory fragility, geopolitical tension, and deteriorating public-private trust and coordination [record_id:2427]. While this record does not describe a specific tool or framework, it implies that effective defense requires governance and coordination as well as technical controls.

Finally, the records collectively point to a lifecycle approach to cyber risk. The training workshop’s goal is to design out cyber risk “before a system is placed into operation” [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437]. This contrasts with reactive security approaches and aligns with resilience engineering, secure-by-design principles, and operational technology risk reduction.
