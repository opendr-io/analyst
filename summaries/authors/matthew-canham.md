# Topic: Author: Matthew Canham

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Matthew Canham focus on AI-enabled psychological manipulation, human-centered attack surfaces, and the emergence of “cognitive security” as a necessary extension of cybersecurity. Both are 2025 conference talks, and both treat large language models and AI agents not merely as technical systems, but as systems that alter the economics, scale, and targeting of deception.

Across the records, Canham’s work is framed around a shift from traditional infrastructure-focused cybersecurity toward protection of humans, cognition, social norms, perception, and decision-making. One record, co-authored with Ben D. Sawyer, presents a Black Hat 2025 talk on “human digital twins,” social engineering, and persistent cognitive cyberwarfare [record_id:93]. The other, a BSidesLV 2025 talk by Canham, examines the “Agentic Web,” where AI agents increasingly act on behalf of humans and become both targets and tools in bot-vs-bot fraud and exploitation [record_id:2466].

The strongest shared theme is that AI lowers the cost and raises the scale of manipulation. The records argue that LLMs and agents can exploit human cognitive vulnerabilities, social norms, perceptual limits, and online dependencies, creating new attack surfaces that are partly technical but deeply human [record_id:93], [record_id:2466]. The evidence base is limited to two talk abstracts, so the records provide strong indications of Canham’s research agenda and public messaging, but little detail about empirical results, specific experiments, mitigations, or implementation methods.

## Research Landscape

The available corpus is small but coherent: two 2025 security conference presentations attributed to Matthew Canham. Both are forward-looking talks about AI security, but they approach AI security through human factors, social engineering, and cognitive manipulation rather than conventional model integrity, malware, or network defense.

The first record is a Black Hat USA 2025 briefing titled “Evil Digital Twin, Too: The First 30 Months of Psychological Manipulation of Humans by AI,” co-authored by Ben D. Sawyer and Matthew Canham [record_id:93]. Its abstract positions the talk as a two-year follow-up to a 2023 talk called “Evil Digital Twin.” It claims that earlier warnings about LLMs exploiting human cognitive vulnerabilities have increasingly become real-world concerns. The talk centers on human digital twins, longitudinal interaction data, low-cost social engineering labor, AI-human hybrid attacks, and the future of cognitive cyberwarfare [record_id:93].

The second record is a BSidesLV 2025 talk titled “Human Attack Surfaces in Agentic Web: How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the AI Apocalypse,” authored by Matthew Canham [record_id:2466]. It focuses on AI agents and the “Agentic Web,” described as an internet increasingly created, interacted with, and consumed by machines rather than humans. The talk asks what happens when agents buy goods, search for jobs and homes, and mediate relationships on behalf of users, while adversarial agents exploit those delegated systems [record_id:2466].

Together, the records show a research landscape concerned with near-future AI-mediated deception. The dominant source type is conference talk abstract rather than paper, tool documentation, dataset description, or postmortem. The records are therefore best read as agenda-setting and concept-framing evidence. They indicate what topics Canham is presenting publicly, but they do not provide enough detail to independently evaluate experiments, data, threat prevalence, or proposed defenses.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Cognitive security as an expansion of cybersecurity

The most prominent theme is “cognitive security”: the idea that security must protect not only devices, accounts, networks, and applications, but also human cognition, perception, trust, and decision-making. In the Black Hat abstract, the authors argue that “the attack surface shifts from networks to minds,” and describe defensive posture as needing to protect “not just digital systems, but the systems that underpin civilization and the human beings they serve” [record_id:93]. The BSidesLV abstract uses similar language, forecasting “the near future of cognitive security” as agentic systems create “an unrelenting cascade of attack surfaces” [record_id:2466].

This theme suggests Canham’s work treats AI security as inseparable from human factors. The concern is not only that AI systems can be attacked, but that AI systems can become instruments for attacking people through persuasion, deception, overload, impersonation, or delegated decision-making [record_id:93], [record_id:2466].

### AI lowering the cost of deception and social engineering

Both records argue that AI changes the economics of manipulation. In the Black Hat record, LLMs and human digital twins are described as enabling “low-cost social engineering labor” and “high power AI-human hybrid attacks,” while the future is framed as one where “the cost of deception approaches zero” [record_id:93]. The BSidesLV record similarly describes scammers using agents to exploit the agents of unsuspecting humans, emphasizing that the threat is not distant but “happening right now” [record_id:2466].

The shared concern is that attacks once limited by labor, customization effort, and human operator attention may become cheaper, more persistent, and more personalized. This aligns with the records’ emphasis on longitudinal interaction data, agent delegation, and scaled simulation of human behavior [record_id:93], [record_id:2466].

### Human digital twins and simulated persons

The Black Hat record introduces a specific concept: human digital twins, or HDTs, described as systems “trained on the core patterns of human individuals” and deployed to simulate “everything from human workflows to relationships” [record_id:93]. This is one of the more distinctive contributions in the corpus. The abstract suggests that such systems could be used both as research artifacts and as attack tools, especially where they enable personalized deception or simulation of trusted people [record_id:93].

The same record describes interactive demonstrations involving “a human digital twin of a Supreme Court justice,” “a perfect AI assistant for insider threat,” and “a NIST research-based LLM that speaks in phishing emails” [record_id:93]. These examples imply an interest in using vivid, concrete demonstrations to make abstract AI-manipulation risks tangible. They also show that the talk connects legal, organizational, and phishing contexts to the broader idea of AI-mediated persuasion and impersonation [record_id:93].

### Agentic Web and bot-vs-bot exploitation

The BSidesLV record extends the human attack surface theme into agent-mediated internet use. It describes a coming “Agentic Web” in which machines dominate creation, interaction, and consumption, and where agents act for humans in activities such as shopping, job searching, housing searches, and relationship seeking [record_id:2466]. The security risk is that attackers will not only target humans directly; they will target the agents that represent humans.

This produces a “bot-vs-bot warfare” framing: scammers deploy agents to exploit user agents [record_id:2466]. That framing marks a shift from classic phishing, where adversaries manipulate a human recipient, to agentic exploitation, where adversaries may manipulate automated representatives that make decisions, filter information, or execute transactions. The record does not detail specific attack mechanics, but it signals concern about prompt manipulation, adversarial content, fraudulent agent-to-agent interactions, and delegated authority risks [record_id:2466].

### AI as a social and civilizational risk, not only a technical risk

The rhetoric of both records is broad and societal. The Black Hat abstract says AI-driven psychological manipulation is “rewriting our civilization’s core realities” and forecasts “persistent cognitive cyberwarfare” [record_id:93]. The BSidesLV abstract uses the phrase “AI Apocalypse” in its title and asks about “an internet not built or experienced by, humans” [record_id:2466].

While these phrases are conference-abstract language rather than empirical claims, they are important evidence of how Canham’s presentations frame AI security: as a systemic transformation in the relationship between humans, information, trust, and machines. The records emphasize that the relevant attack surface includes society-scale patterns of communication and reliance on AI-mediated services [record_id:93], [record_id:2466].

### Defensive optimism through AI-native users and new posture

Although the records are alarm-oriented, they are not purely fatalistic. The Black Hat abstract explicitly says “a brighter future” remains attainable and suggests that “AI natives,” meaning people who grew up in AI-saturated contexts, can help build defensive posture extending beyond infrastructure into cognitive security [record_id:93]. The BSidesLV abstract also promises to conclude with “near future scenarios,” implying a forward-looking analysis rather than only threat description [record_id:2466].

The defensive content is less detailed than the threat content. Still, both records point toward the need for new mental models, awareness, and security architectures suited to AI-mediated online life [record_id:93], [record_id:2466].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records are abstracts and do not provide step-by-step technical methods, but they do identify several approaches and demonstration formats.

One approach is the construction or use of human digital twins. In the Black Hat talk, HDTs are described as models trained on “core patterns of human individuals” and used to simulate workflows and relationships [record_id:93]. The talk also references research in collaboration with the US Military Academy at West Point that “pits humans and human digital twins against one another in competitions of manipulation and deception” [record_id:93]. This suggests an experimental or evaluation method in which humans and modeled human-like systems are compared in adversarial persuasion settings. However, the abstract does not provide methodology, sample sizes, experimental design, measurement criteria, or results [record_id:93].

Another discussed approach is the use of longitudinal interaction data to support social engineering. The Black Hat abstract states that such data is “facilitating low-cost social engineering labor and high power AI-human hybrid attacks” [record_id:93]. This points toward an attack model in which accumulated personal interaction histories, behavioral traces, and communication patterns improve targeting and persuasion. Again, the record does not specify data sources, collection practices, or defensive controls [record_id:93].

The Black Hat talk also appears to use live or illustrative demonstrations as a method for communicating risk. The abstract says audience members will interact with a human digital twin of a Supreme Court justice, encounter an AI assistant framed around insider threat, and see a “NIST research-based LLM that speaks in phishing emails” [record_id:93]. These examples suggest Canham and Sawyer use scenario-based demonstration to show how LLMs can be adapted to social engineering, insider risk, phishing, and institutional impersonation contexts [record_id:93].

The BSidesLV record discusses agentic systems as both tools and targets. It says the talk will “delve into the mechanics of AI agents and the economic pressures driving their rapid adoption,” examine “real-world examples of how agents are already being exploited,” and close with near-future scenarios [record_id:2466]. The implied analytic method is threat modeling for agent-mediated online life: identify where humans delegate tasks, how agents interact with web services or other agents, what incentives drive adoption, and where adversaries can exploit those interactions [record_id:2466].

Across both records, the main methodological pattern is scenario-driven security analysis. The talks appear designed to connect present capabilities to plausible near-future attack surfaces, using demonstrations, examples, and conceptual models rather than narrowly scoped technical benchmarks [record_id:93], [record_id:2466].

It also provides concrete examples of demonstrations, including a Supreme Court justice digital twin, an insider-threat AI assistant, and a phishing-oriented LLM [record_id:93].

The BSidesLV 2025 record, “Human Attack Surfaces in Agentic Web: How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the AI Apocalypse,” is important because it shifts the focus from AI systems manipulating people directly to AI agents mediating human activity online [record_id:2466]. It frames the internet’s future as increasingly machine-created and machine-consumed, and asks what security risks arise when agents conduct shopping, job searches, housing searches, and relationship discovery for users [record_id:2466]. Its most representative concept is “bot-vs-bot warfare,” in which scammers’ agents exploit the agents of ordinary users [record_id:2466].

Together, the records suggest a coherent public research trajectory for Matthew Canham in 2025: from AI-powered psychological manipulation and digital twins to agentic-web attack surfaces. The evidence is strongest for identifying this agenda and its recurring language: cognitive security, human attack surfaces, deception economics, AI-mediated social engineering, human digital twins, and agentic exploitation [record_id:93], [record_id:2466].
