# Topic: Author: Janet Vertesi

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Janet Vertesi describe the same DEF CON 33 talk, co-authored or co-presented with Andy Hull, about “ReclaimTech” / “Reclaim Tech” as a community movement aimed at helping people move away from dominant Big Tech platforms. Both records contain identical abstract text, framing the project as a collective effort by “conscientious objectors to the Big Tech ecosystems” who seek to recover “data,” “autonomy,” and “sovereignty” from “multi-billion dollar companies and VC backed unicorns” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086].

Across the records, Vertesi’s contribution is represented less as a narrow technical exploit or privacy tool and more as a sociotechnical intervention: building a movement, cultivating peer-to-peer support, and making technology sovereignty accessible “to the masses” through gradual, confidence-building community practices [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. The central claim is that opting out of extractive technology ecosystems should not be understood merely as “digital minimalism,” but as “opting in” to stronger relationships, more ethical systems, and a better technological future [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086].

The evidence base is very small and highly redundant: both records appear to be versions of the same talk abstract, with different source video identifiers and slightly different title capitalization. As a result, the records provide a strong indication of the talk’s stated framing and themes, but limited detail about actual implementation, specific tools, technical architecture, empirical evidence, audience reception, or how the ReclaimTech movement operates in practice.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Reclaiming technology from dominant platforms

The strongest recurring theme is the desire to move “away from the major platforms” and to “#reclaimtech” from large technology companies [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. The records portray dominant platforms as holding excessive power over user data, autonomy, and sovereignty. The phrase “from the clutches of multi-billion dollar companies and VC backed unicorns” gives the abstract a critical stance toward both established Big Tech firms and venture-backed startups [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086].

The records do not define exactly which platforms are targeted, nor do they name specific companies. Instead, they describe a broad ecosystemic problem: dependence on platform infrastructures that are characterized as extractive. This makes the talk relevant to privacy and data leakage, but also to digital rights, anti-monopoly technology culture, platform studies, and community technology movements.

### Technology sovereignty as a community practice

A second major theme is “tech sovereignty,” framed not simply as individual consumer choice but as something that can be brought “to the masses” through support, connection, and collective action [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. The records emphasize that the movement is “building community around peer-to-peer support and connection” as participants “exit from these extractive ecosystems” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086].

This language suggests that Vertesi and Hull’s approach treats technical migration as socially embedded. Leaving major platforms is not presented as a one-time act of deletion or a purely private optimization project. Instead, the abstract frames it as a journey requiring community, mutual aid, and incremental learning [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086].

### Opting out as opting in

Both records explicitly reject a narrow “digital minimalism” framing. The talk description states that “opting out of toxic systems” is “not about digital minimalism” but about “opting in to stronger connections, more ethical systems, and a better future” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. This is one of the clearest conceptual contributions in the records.

The distinction matters because it reframes privacy-preserving or anti-platform behavior as constructive rather than abstentionist. The goal is not simply to use less technology, but to use different technology, build different relationships, and participate in more ethical systems. This creates a positive vision of technological change, rather than a purely defensive or withdrawal-oriented posture.

### Meeting people where they are

The records emphasize an accessibility-oriented approach: “meeting people where they are,” “joining them on their journey,” and “building confidence step by step” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. This suggests that the movement is aware of the difficulty many users face when attempting to leave dominant platforms. Rather than demanding immediate total migration, the approach appears gradual and supportive.

This theme is especially important for downstream researchers interested in adoption barriers for privacy tools or alternative technologies. Many privacy and security interventions fail because they assume users are already technically skilled, ideologically committed, or willing to accept major convenience tradeoffs. The ReclaimTech framing in these records instead suggests a pedagogy of incremental empowerment [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086].

### Ethical systems and better futures

The records repeatedly connect technical choices to ethics and future-making. The abstract speaks of “more ethical systems” and “a better future” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. This indicates that the talk is not merely about replacing one app with another, but about aligning technology use with broader values: autonomy, sovereignty, community, and resistance to extraction.

However, the records do not specify what criteria define an ethical system, what technologies qualify, or how tradeoffs between usability, privacy, security, cost, accessibility, and sustainability are evaluated. This remains an important gap.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records do not name specific software tools, protocols, platforms, or technical architectures. There is no mention of particular self-hosted services, encrypted communications tools, federated platforms, open-source operating systems, peer-to-peer protocols, hardware practices, or data migration workflows. Instead, the described methods are social, educational, and movement-building approaches.

The most concrete approach described is community-based peer support. The speakers describe themselves as part of “a collection of conscientious objectors to the Big Tech ecosystems” who are “building community around peer-to-peer support and connection” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. This implies a model where participants help each other identify alternatives, reduce dependency on major platforms, and sustain motivation through social connection.

Another method is staged, user-centered transition. The talk abstract says the approach involves “meeting people where they are,” “joining them on their journey,” and “building confidence step by step” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. This suggests a gradual migration or coaching model rather than an all-or-nothing demand. For researchers, this points to a potentially important design principle: privacy and sovereignty transitions may need scaffolding, community accountability, and emotional support as much as technical documentation.

The records also describe a rhetorical or organizing method: reframing the act of leaving extractive platforms. Rather than defining platform exit as deprivation, inconvenience, or “digital minimalism,” the movement frames it as “opting in to stronger connections, more ethical systems, and a better future” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. This positive framing may be central to recruitment, retention, and cultural legitimacy.

Finally, the records use the language of “movement” rather than “product,” “tool,” or “service” [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086]. That suggests the initiative is intended to scale through community norms, shared identity, and mutual aid rather than solely through software deployment. The available evidence does not show how formal or informal this movement is, what governance structures it has, or whether it provides specific curricula, workshops, documentation, or technical support channels.
