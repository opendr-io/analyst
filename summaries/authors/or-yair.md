# Topic: Author: Or Yair

## Executive Summary

The three records attributed to Or Yair show a 2025 research focus split across two high-impact security areas: agentic AI abuse through indirect prompt injection, and Windows denial-of-service exploitation through RPC and domain-controller behavior. Two records cover the same Google Gemini for Workspace “Promptware” research presented at Black Hat USA 2025 and DEF CON 33, co-authored with Ben Nassi and Stav Cohen [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The third is a DEF CON 33 talk with Shahak Morag on “Win-DoS” and “Win-DDoS,” describing denial-of-service vulnerabilities and abuse paths involving Windows RPC, LDAP, NetLogon RPC, domain controllers, and public DCs [record_id:1940].

Collectively, the records portray Or Yair’s work as centered on practical exploitation paths that convert trusted platform features into attack surfaces. In the Gemini work, ordinary collaboration objects—Google Calendar invites, emails, and shared Google Docs—are described as delivery mechanisms for indirect prompt injection that can hijack LLM-powered agents and use their application permissions [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. In the Windows work, standard enterprise infrastructure components—domain controllers, LDAP referrals, NetLogon RPC, and unauthenticated or weakly constrained RPC servers—are framed as mechanisms for zero-click denial-of-service and distributed denial-of-service amplification or botnet-style behavior [record_id:1940].

The evidence base is small but strong for identifying recurring interests: practical attack chains, abuse of delegated or inherited authority, lateral movement across trust boundaries, and demonstrations against widely deployed systems. It is thinner for defensive detail, vulnerability identifiers beyond CVE-2024-49113, disclosure timelines, vendor responses, mitigations, or independent validation, because the records are conference abstracts/descriptions rather than full technical papers.

## Research Landscape

The records consist of three 2025 conference items from major security venues. One is a Black Hat USA briefing entry [record_id:53], and two are DEF CON 33 video/talk records [record_id:1940] [record_id:1965]. The dataset is therefore dominated by conference presentation abstracts rather than source code, papers, advisories, or postmortems. The available text is promotional and summary-oriented, but it contains enough technical detail to identify research themes, claimed attack primitives, and affected technology classes.

Two of the three records are effectively duplicate presentations of the same research program: “Invitation Is All You Need! Invoking Gemini for Workspace Agents with a Simple Google Calendar Invite” at Black Hat USA 2025 [record_id:53], and “Invoking Gemini Agents with a Google Calendar Invite” at DEF CON 33 [record_id:1965]. Both describe “Promptware,” defined in the record text as prompts in text, image, or audio engineered to exploit LLMs at inference time within an application context [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The specific contribution is a “Targeted Promptware Attack,” where a victim receives a Google Calendar meeting invitation whose subject contains an indirect prompt injection, leading Gemini for Workspace agents to act under the victim/application context [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

The remaining record, “Win-DoS Epidemic - Abusing RPC for Win-DoS & Win-DDoS,” is a Windows infrastructure exploitation talk at DEF CON 33 [record_id:1940]. It describes work following the authors’ “LdapNightmare” release, characterized as the first public DoS exploit for CVE-2024-49113 [record_id:1940]. The new research claims discovery of two additional denial-of-service-style attack surfaces on domain controllers: new critical DoS vulnerabilities and a way to harness public domain controllers for DDoS-like behavior [record_id:1940].

Across the landscape, the records emphasize real-world platform integrations: Google Workspace, Gemini web and mobile assistants, Google Assistant on Android, Zoom, home appliances, Windows domain controllers, LDAP, NetLogon RPC, and Windows RPC servers [record_id:53] [record_id:1940] [record_id:1965]. This suggests Or Yair’s attributed work in these records is not narrowly confined to one domain, but repeatedly examines how complex ecosystems expose dangerous behavior when components trust each other or inherit permissions.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Abuse of trusted workflow inputs

A dominant theme is that routine workflow artifacts can become attack inputs. The Gemini research argues that a simple calendar invite can carry an indirect prompt injection in the meeting subject, causing an LLM-powered assistant to process attacker-controlled content inside the user’s workspace context [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The records explicitly broaden the delivery surface beyond calendar invites to “an email or sharing a Google Doc,” suggesting the key concern is not one Google Calendar feature, but the ingestion of attacker-controlled collaboration content by integrated agents [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

The Windows research has a parallel structure: trusted enterprise protocols and infrastructure roles become attack inputs or relays. The record describes turning domain controllers into LDAP clients via NetLogon RPC and then using LDAP referrals to redirect them to chosen domains or ports [record_id:1940]. In both research lines, the attack depends on making a trusted component consume or act on attacker-influenced data through normal system behavior.

### Delegated authority and application-context exploitation

The Gemini records repeatedly stress that the danger arises because LLM agents operate within an “application context” and possess tools or permissions that can be exploited once the agent is hijacked [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The claimed malicious activities include generating toxic content, spam and phishing, deleting calendar events, exfiltrating emails and calendar events, geolocating a victim, opening Zoom video streams, and controlling home appliances such as windows, boilers, and lights [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The core contribution is less about getting an LLM to output bad text and more about causing an integrated agent to use delegated capabilities.

The Windows talk similarly emphasizes abuse of privileged or high-impact infrastructure roles. Domain controllers are described as “organizations’ core,” and a successful DoS against them is said to break authentication and paralyze operations [record_id:1940]. The record’s emphasis on domain controllers, public DCs, and RPC servers reflects a focus on systems whose availability and trust relationships are foundational to enterprise operations.

### Lateral movement across agents, devices, and infrastructure

The Gemini work explicitly claims two forms of lateral movement: “inter-agent lateral movement,” where malicious activity is triggered between different Gemini agents, and “inter-device lateral movement,” where the attack escapes Gemini boundaries and leverages applications installed on a victim’s smartphone to cause physical outcomes [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The examples include activating boilers and lights or opening a window in a victim’s apartment [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. This framing positions LLM agent security as an ecosystem problem: compromise or manipulation of one assistant can cascade into other assistants, apps, devices, and physical systems.

The Windows record also uses epidemic and propagation language. It describes the goal as creating a “Win-DoS epidemic,” infecting domain controllers with “Win-DoS” and making them “infect others,” forming “Win-DDoS” [record_id:1940]. It further claims the ability to launch a botnet using public domain controllers for DDoS [record_id:1940]. While the term “lateral movement” is not used in the Windows abstract, the described pattern is similarly concerned with cascading impact across networked infrastructure.

### Practicality over theoretical attack models

Both research threads position themselves against assumptions that the attacks are too difficult or theoretical. The Gemini abstract says Promptware has often been viewed as impractical or exotic because of presumed requirements for adversarial ML expertise, GPU clusters, and white-box access, and then claims the calendar-invite technique disproves that perception [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The Windows talk describes practical exploit tooling and “zero-click” vulnerabilities, including crashing Windows endpoints and servers and using public DCs for DDoS-like behavior [record_id:1940].

This emphasis on practicality is a recurring contribution: the talks claim to take theoretical or known risk categories—prompt injection, LLM-agent abuse, DoS against domain controllers, DDoS amplification—and demonstrate accessible, operationally meaningful exploitation paths.

### High-impact systems and physical-world consequences

The records focus on widely deployed technologies with broad blast radius. The Gemini talks target the “three most widely used Gemini for Workspace assistants”: the Gemini web interface, Gemini for Mobile, and Google Assistant powered by Gemini on Android devices [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The Windows talk targets Windows endpoints, servers, domain controllers, public DCs, and RPC servers, describing the ability to crash “any Windows endpoint/server, including DCs” [record_id:1940].

The Gemini work is especially notable for crossing into cyber-physical effects, claiming remote control of connected windows, boilers, and lights through hijacked assistants and smartphone applications [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. This places the research at the intersection of AI security, application security, mobile/OS permissions, and IoT or smart-home safety.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The Gemini records describe an attack method based on indirect prompt injection embedded in a Google Calendar meeting subject [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The attacker sends an invitation to a victim; when the victim’s Gemini for Workspace agent processes the calendar content, the prompt injection allegedly hijacks the application context and invokes integrated agents or tools [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The same records state that similar delivery can occur through email or a shared Google Doc, implying a general workflow for exploiting agent ingestion of untrusted content across Workspace objects [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].

The claimed demonstration set includes 15 exploitations against Gemini’s web interface, Gemini for Mobile, and Google Assistant on Android [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The records list several categories of action: content generation abuse, spam/phishing, calendar deletion, data exfiltration, geolocation, Zoom video streaming, smart-home control, and worm behavior targeting Gemini for Workspace clients [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. Methodologically, the work appears to combine prompt-injection payload design, agent/tool invocation analysis, permission-boundary testing, and risk assessment. The authors also state they developed a “dedicated threat analysis and risk assessment framework,” with the finding that 73% of identified risks are high-critical [record_id:53] [record_id:1965]. The records do not provide the framework’s scoring criteria, sample size, or mitigation mapping.

The Windows record describes a different technical workflow. It starts from prior “LdapNightmare” work on CVE-2024-49113, then explores “client-side targeting,” which the authors say often exposes weaker code [record_id:1940]. The record describes turning domain controllers into LDAP clients via NetLogon RPC, then using LDAP referrals to redirect them to attacker-chosen domains or ports [record_id:1940]. It also describes focusing on RPC servers that are abundant in Windows, have broad attack surfaces, and in some cases do not require authentication [record_id:1940]. By abusing gaps in RPC bindings, the authors claim they could hit the same RPC server repeatedly from one system, surpassing standard concurrency limits and finding vulnerabilities that crash Windows systems [record_id:1940].

The named tool or toolset in the Windows abstract is “Win-DoS Epidemic,” described as DoS tools exploiting four new Win-DoS and one Win-DDoS zero-click vulnerabilities [record_id:1940]. The record claims these tools can crash Windows endpoints and servers, including domain controllers, or launch a botnet using public DCs for DDoS [record_id:1940]. The abstract does not provide exploit details, proof-of-concept code, patch status, affected versions, or responsible disclosure outcomes.

The records describe “Targeted Promptware Attacks” that use a normal Google Calendar invitation as the initial vector, then exploit Gemini agents’ tools and permissions to perform actions across Workspace, Android, applications, and smart-home devices [record_id:53] [record_id:1965].
