# Topic: Author: Dwayne McDaniel

## Executive Summary

The four records attributed to Dwayne McDaniel depict a coherent security research and practitioner agenda centered on identity, credentials, developer risk, and practical defensive controls. Across BSidesLV 2025 and BSidesLV 2026 talks, McDaniel’s work focuses on how modern systems depend on machine identities, automation, developer environments, and secrets, and how attackers exploit misplaced trust in these assets.

Two 2025 records emphasize non-human identity security: one frames long-lived credentials as a flawed shared pattern for both humans and machines and asks whether application and workload identity should be rethought [record_id:2468]; the other expands the concern into governance, OWASP guidance, shadow IT, AI-powered automation, and developer education around non-human identities such as service accounts, bots, and automation [record_id:2572]. The 2026 records broaden the same core concern into software supply chain and detection engineering: one argues that the developer laptop is now a critical perimeter because it can expose cloud credentials, CI tokens, SSH keys, npm credentials, and package publishing rights [record_id:2754]; the other proposes cyber deception, especially honeytokens, as a lightweight way to detect intruders who search for and exploit hardcoded credentials [record_id:2755].

Collectively, these records show recurring themes: credential sprawl, machine-to-machine trust, non-human identities outnumbering human users, attackers targeting developer workflows, and the need for practical controls that go beyond secret storage alone. The evidence base is compact—only four talk abstracts—but internally consistent. It supports a strong characterization of McDaniel’s attributed talks as security education aimed at developers, identity practitioners, application security teams, and defenders responsible for modern software and cloud environments.

## Research Landscape

The records are all conference talk listings from BSidesLV, specifically tagged with PasswordsCon and held in the Tuscany track. The set includes two BSidesLV 2025 talks and two BSidesLV 2026 talks, all authored by Dwayne McDaniel. These are short abstracts rather than full transcripts, slide decks, or papers, so they provide strong evidence for talk topics and intended framing, but limited evidence about detailed technical implementation, empirical results, or specific recommendations.

The 2025 material is concentrated on non-human identity, secrets, governance, and access delegation. “I’m A Machine, And You Should Trust Me: The Future Of Non-Human Identity” presents the broad conceptual problem: security still relies heavily on “long-lived credentials,” and both humans and machines are trusted through this “same flawed pattern” [record_id:2468]. “What to Tell Your Developers About NHI Secrets Security and Governance” gives a more operational and governance-oriented version of the same concern, naming service accounts, bots, and automation as examples of non-human identities and describing their growth as outpacing traditional security controls [record_id:2572].

The 2026 material extends this identity-and-credentials focus into adjacent domains. “The Laptop Is the Perimeter” treats developer endpoints as a central software supply chain control point because developer machines can contain or access high-value credentials and publishing authorities [record_id:2754]. “Who Goes There?” treats attacker behavior around hardcoded credentials as a detection opportunity and introduces honeytokens as a lightweight deception mechanism [record_id:2755].

The overall research area represented by these records is therefore not simply “passwords” in the narrow human-authentication sense. Instead, the talks appear to use the PasswordsCon setting to discuss the broader credential ecosystem: tokens, workload identities, machine identities, hardcoded secrets, cloud and CI credentials, package registry credentials, SSH keys, and the organizational governance problems that emerge when automation and developers become central to security boundaries.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Non-human identity as a central security problem

The clearest theme is the rise of non-human identities and the inadequacy of legacy credential practices for them. McDaniel’s 2025 talk “I’m A Machine, And You Should Trust Me” frames the issue as one of architectural trust: organizations rely on long-lived credentials for both people and machines, and the abstract asks what would happen if application and workload identity were rethought [record_id:2468]. The language is conceptual and forward-looking, suggesting a talk about the future of application identity rather than only today’s secrets-management hygiene.

The companion 2025 talk “What to Tell Your Developers About NHI Secrets Security and Governance” gives this theme more urgency and specificity. It states that non-human identities such as “service accounts, bots, and automation” outnumber humans “by at least 45 to 1” and are a “top target for attackers” [record_id:2572]. It further argues that their growth has “outpaced traditional security controls,” and that “simply securing secrets is not enough” because attackers exploit “blind trust in tokens and credentials” [record_id:2572]. This makes non-human identity not just a technical authentication issue but a security governance issue.

Together, these two records establish non-human identity as the strongest and most explicit through-line in the collection. The talks appear to distinguish between secret possession and trustworthy identity: the problem is not only that credentials leak, but that systems often trust tokens, service accounts, workloads, and automation too broadly or for too long [record_id:2468] [record_id:2572].

### Long-lived credentials, tokens, and secrets as recurring weak points

All four records involve credentials or secrets in some form. The 2025 future-of-NHI abstract directly names “long-lived credentials” as the flawed pattern underlying much of security [record_id:2468]. The NHI governance abstract says attackers exploit “tokens and credentials every day” and that securing secrets alone is insufficient [record_id:2572]. The software supply chain talk lists cloud credentials, CI tokens, SSH keys, npm credentials, GitHub tokens, and package publishing rights as assets exposed through developer laptops or stolen by malicious packages [record_id:2754]. The cyber deception talk focuses on attackers expanding their foothold by finding and exploiting hardcoded credentials [record_id:2755].

This repetition suggests that McDaniel’s attributed work treats credentials as both an access mechanism and an attacker objective. Credentials are not merely an authentication artifact; they are the connective tissue among cloud infrastructure, CI/CD systems, package ecosystems, developer workstations, service accounts, automation, and detection strategies.

A notable trend across the records is movement from prevention and governance toward detection and response. The 2025 records emphasize rethinking identity and governing NHI risks [record_id:2468] [record_id:2572]. The 2026 cyber deception record assumes attackers may enter an environment and proposes setting “an alarm that an attacker would trigger” while limiting damage [record_id:2755]. This does not represent a contradiction; rather, the records collectively imply a defense-in-depth view: organizations should improve identity architecture and governance, but also assume intruders will search for credentials and build detection mechanisms around that behavior.

### Developer-centered security

Another major theme is the developer as both a key audience and a high-value target. The 2025 NHI governance talk is explicitly framed around “What to Tell Your Developers,” and says developers need to understand risks, use best practices, and advocate for holistic non-human identity security across teams [record_id:2572]. The 2026 software supply chain talk makes the developer workstation central: attackers target developers because they “sit closest to the systems that build, ship, and operate software” [record_id:2754]. It argues that a single developer laptop can expose many forms of sensitive access, including cloud credentials, CI tokens, SSH keys, and package publishing rights [record_id:2754].

The title “The Laptop Is the Perimeter” captures a shift from traditional network perimeter thinking to endpoint- and workflow-centric security [record_id:2754]. In this view, the developer machine is not just another endpoint; it is a privileged access hub for the software supply chain. The talk links developer compromise to recent package-based attacks and warns that agentic AI ecosystems and skill marketplaces may introduce new ways for malicious automation to steal credentials or execute code [record_id:2754].

This developer-centered view aligns with the NHI records because developers often create, use, and troubleshoot service accounts, CI tokens, bots, automation credentials, and package publishing workflows. The records collectively portray developers as essential participants in identity governance and as targets whose local environments may define the practical security boundary.

### Supply chain attacks and poisoned trusted packages

The 2026 software supply chain record provides the most concrete attack examples in the dataset. It states that supply chain attacks have evolved recently and specifically names the Nx “s1ngularity” attack as the start of a pattern involving “poisoned trusted packages” that steal GitHub tokens, npm credentials, SSH keys, and other secrets from developer systems [record_id:2754]. It also names the Shai-Hulud campaign as pushing the model further with a “self-replicating npm worm” [record_id:2754].

This talk connects software supply chain risk to credential theft from developer endpoints. Rather than describing supply chain attacks solely as malicious dependency insertion or build tampering, it emphasizes packages as vehicles for stealing developer-held credentials and tokens [record_id:2754]. The abstract also extends the supply chain frame to “agentic AI ecosystems and skill marketplaces,” where malicious skills and prompt-based payloads could turn automation into credential theft and code execution [record_id:2754]. This is the only record in the set that explicitly mentions AI-related security risk, and it does so as an extension of supply chain and credential theft patterns.

### Governance and organization-wide responsibility

The NHI governance record argues that non-human identity security is “an urgent, organization-wide challenge that goes far beyond IT” [record_id:2572]. It points to “Shadow IT and AI-powered automation” as accelerants and says “strong identity governance and access management (IAM)” are essential [record_id:2572]. This suggests that McDaniel’s framing is not limited to tooling; it includes organizational awareness, prioritization, ownership, and cross-team advocacy.

The same record references the “OWASP Top 10 Non-Human Identity Risks in 2025” as providing guidance on major threats and remediation priorities [record_id:2572]. The abstract presents OWASP as part of a broader industry agreement that NHI security is urgent [record_id:2572]. Because the record is an abstract rather than the OWASP document itself, it should be treated as evidence that the talk discusses or relies on OWASP guidance, not as independent evidence of the contents or validity of that guidance.

### Deception and attacker-behavior-driven detection

The cyber deception talk adds a detection engineering dimension. It argues that intrusion detection is most useful when defenders discover attackers “while they are still in the system,” rather than after the fact [record_id:2755]. It describes a desired control as an alarm an attacker triggers while damage is limited [record_id:2755]. The abstract then uses a common attacker behavior—searching for hardcoded credentials to expand access—as the basis for deception [record_id:2755].

The talk introduces honeytokens as a “subclass of honeypots” with low overhead, simple deployment, broad industry use, and the ability to lure attackers into triggering alerts while they attempt to gain further access [record_id:2755]. This is a practical defensive complement to the credential and identity risks discussed in the other records. If long-lived credentials, tokens, and secrets are persistent attacker targets, honeytokens can be used to identify credential-hunting behavior.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records are abstracts, so they do not enumerate detailed playbooks, code, architectures, or vendor tools. Still, they indicate several methods and approaches.

One approach is to rethink application and workload identity rather than continuing to rely on long-lived credentials. The future-of-NHI talk asks what would happen if organizations rethought application and workload identity, after stating that much of security depends on trusting humans and machines through long-lived credentials [record_id:2468]. This implies movement toward more dynamic, contextual, or governed identity models, though the abstract does not specify exact mechanisms.

A second approach is governance-driven non-human identity security. The NHI governance talk says that securing secrets is not sufficient and that organizations need strong IAM, awareness, best practices, and governance across teams [record_id:2572]. It identifies OWASP’s 2025 Top 10 Non-Human Identity Risks as a prioritization aid [record_id:2572]. The record also recommends that developers understand risks, use best practices, and advocate for a holistic approach to NHI security [record_id:2572]. The exact best practices are not listed in the abstract, but the method is clearly organizational and risk-prioritized rather than purely technical.

A third approach is treating the developer workstation as a key software supply chain control point. The 2026 supply chain talk argues that developer laptops can expose cloud credentials, CI tokens, SSH keys, and package publishing rights, making them attractive targets [record_id:2754]. The abstract says the talk will walk through recent attacks and practical defenses developers can adopt immediately [record_id:2754]. It does not list those defenses, but the approach is endpoint-aware, developer-focused, and tied to supply chain protection.

A fourth approach is cyber deception using honeytokens. The deception talk explains that defenders can exploit attackers’ tendency to search for hardcoded credentials by planting or using deceptive artifacts that trigger alerts [record_id:2755]. Honeytokens are described as lightweight compared with fuller honeypot systems: they require “almost none of the overhead,” are “simple to deploy,” and are used across many industries [record_id:2755]. The approach is behavior-driven: rather than only blocking credential theft, it detects credential-seeking activity during an intrusion.
