# Topic: Author: Rob King

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Rob King both concern the same 2025 research topic: discovering Operational Technology (OT) devices that sit behind protocol gateways in converged OT/IT environments. The strongest and most detailed evidence comes from the DEF CON 33 abstract for “There and Back Again: Detecting OT Devices Across Protocol Gateways,” which frames the problem as a consequence of OT/IT convergence: industrial and real-world control systems that were historically isolated increasingly use TCP/IP and commodity networking, making them easier to manage but also more reachable by attackers [record_id:2104]. The BSidesSF 2025 record appears to describe a closely related or likely earlier/alternate version of the same talk, titled “There and Back Again: Discovering OT Devices Across Protocol Gateways,” emphasizing security implications, OT protocol deep dives, and discovery of devices “even behind legacy protocol gateways” [record_id:2303].

Across the records, King’s contribution is focused and technical: he highlights a visibility gap in OT security created by gateways that translate between TCP/IP-facing networks and proprietary or legacy OT protocols. The talks argue that defenders need ways to identify assets beyond those gateways in order to understand and protect OT environments. The DEF CON record gives concrete examples—CIP message forwarding and DNP3 address discovery—as practical techniques for asset discovery across gateways [record_id:2104]. The BSidesSF record reinforces the same central thesis at a higher level: IT/OT convergence changes the security model, and defenders must understand OT protocols and gateway behavior to discover hidden or legacy-connected devices [record_id:2303].

## Research Landscape

The record set is small and cohesive: it consists of two 2025 conference-talk records attributed to Rob King, both about OT device discovery across protocol gateways. One comes from DEF CON 33 and provides a relatively detailed abstract, including historical framing, a problem statement, and two named technical examples [record_id:2104]. The other comes from BSidesSF 2025 and is much shorter, giving a concise program-style description of a talk in Theater 9 at AMC Metreon, scheduled as “THERE AND BACK AGAIN: DISCOVERING OT DEVICES ACROSS PROTOCOL GATEWAYS” [record_id:2303].

The research area represented here is OT and industrial-network visibility rather than exploit development or vulnerability disclosure. Both records treat OT systems as devices that control real-world operations: the DEF CON abstract lists factories, assembly lines, and medical equipment as examples [record_id:2104], while the BSidesSF abstract mentions factories and generators [record_id:2303]. The dominant concern is that OT devices once thought of as isolated are increasingly attached to commodity networks. King’s framing is that this “OT/IT convergence” brings operational advantages but creates security exposure when the security posture of OT devices has not kept pace [record_id:2104].

The records also point to a specific research niche within OT security: asset discovery and network detection where direct visibility is blocked or distorted by protocol gateways. Rather than focusing on scanning ordinary IP-connected devices, King’s talks focus on devices “on the other side” of gateways [record_id:2104] or “behind legacy protocol gateways” [record_id:2303]. This suggests a practical defender problem: network security tools may see the gateway but not the devices, controllers, or addresses beyond it. The available evidence indicates King’s work is intended to encourage further research into discovering and protecting such devices, not merely to demonstrate isolated protocol tricks [record_id:2104].

## Major Themes And Trends

A central theme is the security consequence of OT/IT convergence. The DEF CON abstract states that OT historically used custom protocols and communications media and was “isolated (more or less) from the wider world,” but that over the past 15 to 20 years these environments increasingly adopted commodity protocols and media, including the standard TCP/IP protocol suite [record_id:2104]. This shift is presented as beneficial for cost and manageability, but risky because it makes OT devices more accessible to attackers while their security posture has often lagged [record_id:2104]. The BSidesSF record condenses the same theme into a talk about “the security implications of the convergence of IT and OT” [record_id:2303].

A second recurring theme is that gateways complicate visibility. The DEF CON abstract explains that, during convergence, many OT devices are connected through protocol gateways that speak TCP/IP on one side and then translate communications to proprietary OT protocols, or provide a NAT-style private network inside an OT device rack [record_id:2104]. This architecture can hide actual OT devices from conventional network discovery. King’s talk therefore targets techniques for detecting devices across those gateways, not just detecting the gateway itself [record_id:2104]. The BSidesSF description similarly emphasizes “device discovery - even behind legacy protocol gateways” [record_id:2303].

A third theme is protocol-specific, practical research. The DEF CON record names two concrete examples: CIP, or Common Industrial Protocol, message forwarding; and DNP3, or Distributed Network Protocol version 3, address discovery [record_id:2104]. These examples indicate that King’s approach is not purely conceptual. It depends on understanding how specific industrial protocols and gateway functions behave, then using that behavior to infer or enumerate devices beyond the visible TCP/IP boundary. The BSidesSF record’s phrase “deep dives into OT protocols and device discovery” aligns with this protocol-centered approach [record_id:2303].

A fourth theme is defensive asset management. The talks are not framed as offensive exploitation talks in the abstracts provided. Instead, the DEF CON description says the techniques are examples intended to illustrate OT device discovery and encourage further research into how such devices may be discovered on networks and “ultimately, protected” [record_id:2104]. This framing makes asset discovery a prerequisite for protection: defenders cannot secure, monitor, or risk-assess devices they cannot identify. The BSidesSF description is shorter, but its emphasis on security implications and discovery behind gateways supports the same defensive visibility concern [record_id:2303].

There is no apparent disagreement between the records. They appear to describe the same research line, possibly the same talk adapted for different venues. The title wording shifts slightly from “Discovering” at BSidesSF to “Detecting” at DEF CON, but the underlying problem and content are consistent: finding OT devices across protocol gateways in converged industrial networks [record_id:2104] [record_id:2303].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records discuss approaches rather than named software tools. The clearest method is cross-gateway OT asset discovery: identifying devices that are not directly visible on the TCP/IP-facing side of an OT network because a protocol gateway mediates communications [record_id:2104]. King’s DEF CON abstract describes gateways that translate from TCP/IP into proprietary OT protocols or create a NAT-like private network inside an OT device rack [record_id:2104]. The methodological implication is that defenders need to interact with, observe, or reason about gateway-mediated protocol behavior to infer what lies beyond the gateway.

The first named technical example is CIP message forwarding. CIP, or Common Industrial Protocol, is presented in the DEF CON abstract as a way to demonstrate discovery of assets across gateways [record_id:2104]. The abstract does not provide procedural details, packet formats, or command sequences, but it indicates that message forwarding can be used to reach or enumerate devices that are not directly exposed on the visible network [record_id:2104]. For downstream research agents, this is a strong lead: examine how CIP routing or forwarding capabilities expose downstream device identity, paths, modules, or rack-local assets.

The second named example is DNP3 address discovery. The DEF CON abstract identifies DNP3, “Distributed Network Protocol, version 3,” as another practical example of discovering assets across gateways [record_id:2104]. Again, the record does not provide operational details, but it makes clear that DNP3 addressing can support discovery of devices beyond a gateway boundary [record_id:2104]. This suggests a research direction around enumerating DNP3 outstations, addressing schemes, or gateway mappings in environments where the gateway is the only directly reachable IP endpoint.

The BSidesSF record describes the approach more generally as “deep dives into OT protocols and device discovery” and explicitly includes discovery “behind legacy protocol gateways” [record_id:2303]. This supports the interpretation that King’s method is protocol-aware discovery rather than generic IP scanning. The important architectural concept is that conventional network discovery may stop at the gateway, while OT-aware discovery must account for translated, forwarded, or privately addressed protocol domains behind it [record_id:2104] [record_id:2303].

There is also limited evidence about validation. They do not quantify detection coverage, false positives, safety risks, or device compatibility. The phrase “two practical examples” in the DEF CON record suggests applied work, but the record does not provide enough detail to evaluate repeatability or generality [record_id:2104].

Several open research questions follow from the records:

- Which gateway types and vendors support or expose behaviors that make cross-gateway discovery possible?
- How do CIP message forwarding and DNP3 address discovery differ in assumptions, safety impact, and visibility outcomes?
- Can these approaches be incorporated into passive network detection and response workflows, or do they require active probing?
- What safeguards are necessary when discovering OT assets to avoid disrupting industrial processes?
- How common are NAT-style private OT rack networks, and how well can they be mapped from the TCP/IP side?
- Are these techniques suitable for continuous monitoring, one-time asset inventory, incident response, or pre-assessment scoping?

The records frame asset discovery as a defensive prerequisite for protection, but they do not address governance, operational approval, change-control constraints, or the risk that active discovery traffic could affect sensitive OT equipment [record_id:2104] [record_id:2303]. These are important gaps for anyone turning the talks’ ideas into field practice.
