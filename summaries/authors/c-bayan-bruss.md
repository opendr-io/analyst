# Topic: Author: C. Bayan Bruss

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to C. Bayan Bruss are CAMLIS presentations from 2018 and 2019 focused on machine learning for malware analysis at scale. Both records address the same high-level operational problem: modern malware arrives in volumes and variations that make manual reverse engineering or purely static signature-based approaches insufficient. Bruss’s work, as represented here, emphasizes learned representations—first at the code-instruction level through neural embeddings of opcodes and operands, and then at the ecosystem level through graph embeddings over heterogeneous malware-related networks.

The 2018 talk, “Worm2Vec: Embedding Malicious Code for Efficient Clustering & Classification,” proposes an unsupervised neural embedding approach for disassembled malware binaries, using opcode and operand usage patterns to create representations that support efficient clustering and classification of unseen binaries [record_id:233]. The 2019 talk, “Applying Deep Graph Representation Learning to the Malware Graph,” extends the representation-learning theme from code structure to graph topology, treating malware as nodes in a heterogeneous graph connected to infrastructure such as IP addresses, URLs, and domains, and applying graph embedding techniques such as DeepWalk and GraphSage [record_id:217].

Collectively, the records present Bruss as contributing to malware analysis research through scalable machine-learning methods that transform difficult, sparse, heterogeneous security artifacts into lower-dimensional learned representations. The evidence base is small but coherent: both records are talk abstracts from CAMLIS, both are tagged with Capital One, and both belong primarily to malware analysis and reverse engineering. The main limitation is that the records are abstracts only; they describe motivations, proposed methods, and claimed or preliminary results, but do not provide datasets, evaluation metrics, experimental results, implementation details, or reproducibility artifacts.

## Research Landscape

The record set contains two conference presentation abstracts from CAMLIS, one from 2018 and one from 2019. Both are attributed to C. Bayan Bruss and both concern malware analysis and reverse engineering, specifically the use of machine learning to classify, cluster, compare, or otherwise understand malware at scale [record_id:233; record_id:217].

The 2018 record focuses on malicious code itself. It frames the malware-analysis problem around the daily influx of new malware specimens, many of which are variants or recombinations of existing functionality. The abstract argues that analysts need to determine whether a new sample resembles known malware and what known functionality it may contain, but that scale and latency constraints make full reverse engineering impractical [record_id:233]. The proposed approach is “Worm2Vec,” a neural embedding model for opcodes and operands in disassembled binaries [record_id:233].

The 2019 record shifts the unit of analysis from individual binaries to malware within a broader relational environment. It describes malware as nodes in a heterogeneous graph, with edges to IP addresses, URLs, domains, and similar contextual entities. In this framing, topology provides information beyond static and dynamic attributes of individual malware samples [record_id:217]. The challenge becomes applying machine learning to sparse, high-dimensional graph structures, which motivates the use of graph embedding techniques such as DeepWalk and GraphSage [record_id:217].

The overall research area represented by these records is not traditional manual reverse engineering, nor is it purely malware-family signature creation. Instead, it sits at the intersection of malware analysis, representation learning, unsupervised or semi-supervised machine learning, and scalable security analytics. Both talks treat malware detection and classification as problems of learning useful vector representations from raw or relational security data. The records suggest an applied research orientation: the methods are positioned as practical responses to real-world scale, heterogeneity, scarcity of labels, and adversarial variability.

## Major Themes And Trends

A dominant theme across both records is the need to handle malware at operational scale. The 2018 abstract states that “a large number of new specimens of malware are detected worldwide” every day and that scale and latency leave “little room for full reverse engineering of each sample” [record_id:233]. The 2019 abstract similarly describes malware as widespread, increasing in ubiquity and diversity, and notes that “the sheer scale of the problem makes in-depth investigation impossible” [record_id:217]. In both cases, machine learning is presented not as an optional enhancement but as a necessary response to the volume and variety of malware.

A second recurring theme is the inadequacy of shallow or brittle features. In 2018, the record criticizes static methods such as signature matching, saying they have limited efficacy and can be bypassed by small source-code changes [record_id:233]. In 2019, the record says static and dynamic attributes of a specific malware sample “only tell you so much,” implying that local attributes alone are insufficient for detection, classification, and understanding [record_id:217]. The two talks therefore push toward richer contextual representations: contextual usage patterns within code in 2018, and network/topological context in 2019.

A third theme is robustness to variation, obfuscation, and sparse labels. The 2018 Worm2Vec abstract emphasizes the need for a solution that is fast, scalable, and robust to small changes and obfuscation tactics [record_id:233]. It also highlights unsupervised learning as a way to avoid problems caused by heterogeneous or scarce labeled data [record_id:233]. The 2019 graph-learning abstract does not discuss labels as explicitly, but it similarly addresses sparse, high-dimensional structures and seeks embeddings that encode topology, node and edge attributes, and neighborhood statistics [record_id:217]. Together, the records indicate interest in learned representations that generalize beyond exact matches or hand-engineered signatures.

A fourth theme is the movement from local code semantics to ecosystem-level context. The 2018 presentation focuses on disassembled binaries, with each function treated as a context in which lines of opcodes and operands appear near one another [record_id:233]. The 2019 presentation instead treats malware samples as graph nodes connected to other observables such as IP addresses, URLs, and domains [record_id:217]. This shift suggests a broadening of analytical scope: from learning representations of malicious code internals to learning representations of malware within an operational threat-intelligence graph.

There is no visible disagreement between the records. Rather, they appear complementary. The 2018 talk develops representations from program-internal structure, while the 2019 talk develops representations from external relational structure. Both approaches aim to support downstream tasks such as clustering, classification, and similarity search [record_id:233; record_id:217].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The 2018 record describes a neural embedding model for malware binaries, named “Worm2Vec” [record_id:233]. The method learns representations of opcodes and operands in disassembled binaries based on usage patterns across a large corpus of binaries [record_id:233]. The abstract states that each line of a function is treated as a combination of opcodes and operands, with each drawn from an embedding space [record_id:233]. Lines that appear near each other within a function are assumed to be contextually relevant, and the model learns to distinguish contextually relevant embedded lines from those that are not [record_id:233]. Once trained, these learned embeddings can generate statistical representations of unseen binaries, supporting clustering and classification [record_id:233].

This method is explicitly positioned as unsupervised. The abstract argues that unsupervised training avoids problems associated with highly heterogeneous or scarce labeled data [record_id:233]. It also claims that the approach allows clustering of malware based on functionality in a way that is not affected by small code changes, and that initial results suggest the embeddings can help train classifiers when labels are available [record_id:233]. The record does not provide model architecture specifics beyond the embedding framing, nor does it include evaluation metrics, but it clearly describes a workflow: disassemble binaries, learn opcode/operand embeddings from contextual co-occurrence, represent unseen binaries statistically, then cluster or classify.

The 2019 record discusses graph representation learning applied to a malware graph [record_id:217]. The proposed data model treats malware as nodes in a heterogeneous graph, with edges to related entities such as IP addresses, URLs, and domains [record_id:217]. The abstract argues that topological information can add context beyond individual node attributes and can be useful for visualization and investigation [record_id:217]. However, graph structures are described as sparse and high-dimensional, creating challenges for machine-learning tasks such as node classification, similarity search, and clustering [record_id:217].

To address this, the 2019 talk applies graph embedding techniques, including DeepWalk and GraphSage, to malware data [record_id:217]. The record describes graph embeddings as machine-learning methods that learn lower-dimensional vector representations of graphs while encoding topology, node and edge attributes, and neighborhood statistics [record_id:217]. The presentation’s stated goal is to investigate model outputs, assess whether they learn meaningful representations in this domain, and share lessons for incorporating graph ML techniques into large malware networks [record_id:217].

Across both talks, the central methodological pattern is representation learning. In one case, the raw material is disassembled code; in the other, it is a heterogeneous graph of malware and related infrastructure. In both cases, the desired output is a lower-dimensional representation useful for clustering, classification, similarity search, or analyst triage [record_id:233; record_id:217]. This gives the two-record corpus a strong conceptual throughline: machine learning as a way to compress complex malware evidence into vector spaces that preserve operationally meaningful similarity.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The largest limitation is the small size and type of the evidence base. There are only two records, and both are conference talk abstracts rather than full papers, transcripts, slide decks, code repositories, or experimental reports. As a result, they provide useful summaries of motivation and approach but little detail about implementation, evaluation, or results [record_id:233; record_id:217].

Several technical questions remain open for the 2018 Worm2Vec work. The abstract does not specify the exact neural architecture, training objective, corpus composition, disassembly tooling, malware families, normalization strategy for operands, handling of packed or obfuscated binaries, or evaluation benchmarks [record_id:233]. It claims robustness to small code changes and usefulness for clustering by functionality, but does not provide evidence sufficient to determine how robust the method is in practice or how it compares to other binary similarity methods [record_id:233]. It also leaves unclear how the statistical representation of a full unseen binary is constructed from learned line, opcode, or operand embeddings [record_id:233].

The 2019 graph-learning talk leaves similar open questions. The abstract names DeepWalk and GraphSage and says they are applied to a malware graph, but it does not describe the graph schema in detail, the edge types used, the scale of the graph, how labels were obtained, how temporal leakage was avoided, or how heterogeneous node and edge types were encoded [record_id:217]. It also does not state whether the learned embeddings improved node classification, similarity search, clustering, analyst workflows, or visualization relative to baselines [record_id:217].

Another gap concerns deployment and operational integration. Both records are motivated by scale and latency constraints, implying operational use cases, but neither explains how the methods would be integrated into a production malware-analysis pipeline, how analysts would interpret embedding outputs, how false positives or false clusters would be handled, or how models would be updated as malware evolves [record_id:233; record_id:217].
