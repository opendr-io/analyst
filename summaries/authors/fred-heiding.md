# Topic: Author: Fred Heiding

## Executive Summary

The seven records attributed to Fred Heiding present a coherent research agenda centered on AI-enabled cyber risk, especially the automation and scaling of social engineering, the security of AI infrastructure itself, and the governance/policy implications of both. Across the records, Heiding’s work repeatedly examines how large language models, AI agents, and voice models reduce attacker cost, automate previously labor-intensive steps, and create new incentives for cybercrime. This is clearest in the phishing and vishing records: automated OSINT, vulnerability profiling, spear-phishing email generation, phishing-domain setup, spoofed websites, credential harvesting, and AI voice scams are treated as components of an increasingly scalable attack lifecycle [record_id:2192] [record_id:2394] [record_id:2736] [record_id:2800].

A second major strand focuses on AI infrastructure as a strategic target. These records frame frontier AI labs, model weights, supply chains, chips, networks, and human insiders as emerging high-value targets for espionage, nation-state operations, and cybercriminal abuse [record_id:2531] [record_id:2636]. The work draws analogies from past attacks on semiconductors, biotechnology, critical infrastructure, and U.S. companies to anticipate likely attack patterns against AI systems and organizations [record_id:2531] [record_id:2636].

A third strand is meta-research and field-building. Heiding’s work includes efforts to map the AI security landscape, identify fragmented research communities, catalog benchmarks and mitigations, and define AI systems as attack vectors, attack targets, and attack amplifiers [record_id:2803]. This complements the applied social-engineering benchmark work in ScamBench, which aims to measure real-world risk across email, voice, and multi-step fraud scenarios while informing pre-deployment model assessments and policy interventions [record_id:2800].

Collectively, the records portray Heiding as contributing to AI security through empirical risk measurement, attacker-workflow modeling, social-engineering experimentation, AI infrastructure threat analysis, and policy-oriented security frameworks. The strongest evidence comes from records describing concrete experiments, datasets, surveys, and benchmark designs; the thinner areas are records that preview future or planned findings rather than reporting completed results.

## Research Landscape

The records are mostly conference talk abstracts from security venues, with BSidesLV dominating the dataset. Five records are BSidesLV talks or presentations from 2025 and 2026 [record_id:2394] [record_id:2531] [record_id:2736] [record_id:2800] [record_id:2803]. One record is a Prompt||GTFO presentation or video summary from 2026 [record_id:2192], and one is a Black Hat USA 2026 briefing abstract [record_id:2636]. The years represented are 2025 and 2026, suggesting a recent and rapidly developing body of work focused on frontier AI security concerns.

The research area is not limited to one narrow technical subfield. Instead, the records span several adjacent domains:

- AI-enabled phishing and social engineering, including automated spear phishing, phishing infrastructure generation, vishing, and fraud benchmarking [record_id:2192] [record_id:2394] [record_id:2736] [record_id:2800].
- AI infrastructure security, including espionage, insider threats, supply-chain attacks, model-weight theft, and critical infrastructure analogies [record_id:2531] [record_id:2636].
- AI security governance and policy, including regulatory countermeasures, national cybersecurity strategy lessons, model release policy, public-private collaboration, workforce development, KYC requirements for domain registrars, and pre-deployment safety assessments [record_id:2394] [record_id:2531] [record_id:2736] [record_id:2800].
- Research mapping and taxonomy-building for the AI security field, including a proposed 12-category threat/defense taxonomy, a catalog of 200+ benchmarks and evaluation frameworks, and a framework of 30 mitigation strategies [record_id:2803].

The overall landscape is therefore best understood as applied AI security research with a strong empirical and policy orientation. Heiding’s records often ask how AI changes attacker economics and defender requirements. They tend to emphasize measurement: task completion rates, costs, time requirements, human intervention, click-through rates, survey susceptibility, voice detection performance, benchmark design, and historical incident datasets [record_id:2192] [record_id:2394] [record_id:2736] [record_id:2800] [record_id:2636].

The dataset also shows recurring collaboration patterns. Several records are coauthored with Simon Lermen and focus on AI-enabled social engineering or phishing infrastructure [record_id:2192] [record_id:2394] [record_id:2736]. Other records involve coauthors from policy or AI infrastructure security contexts, such as Andrew Kao on national cybersecurity strategies and AI infrastructure [record_id:2531], and Chris Inglis on a broad Black Hat briefing about AI security’s “good, bad, and ugly” [record_id:2636]. Two 2026 BSidesLV records list Fred Heiding as the sole author and appear to consolidate broader research programs: ScamBench for measuring social-engineering risk [record_id:2800], and a comprehensive mapping of the AI security landscape [record_id:2803].

## Major Themes And Trends

### AI lowers the cost and increases the scale of social engineering

The most consistent theme across the records is the claim that AI reduces the cost, labor, and skill required for social engineering. In the Prompt||GTFO record, Heiding and Simon Lermen demonstrate an “AI-powered phishing agent” that automates the full attack chain: OSINT gathering, target vulnerability profiling, personalized spear-phishing email generation using psychological persuasion techniques, and phishing-domain purchase/configuration [record_id:2192]. The key trend identified there is economic: the work argues that AI eliminates much of the cost difference between mass phishing and spear phishing, making highly personalized attacks possible at near-zero marginal cost [record_id:2192].

The BSidesLV 2025 phishing infrastructure record extends this theme from message generation to backend operations. Instead of focusing only on persuasive emails, it studies whether LLMs and agents can autonomously register domains, configure DNS, deploy spoofed sites, and harvest credentials [record_id:2394]. This broadens the social-engineering threat model from content creation to end-to-end attack infrastructure. The record frames the risk as not merely better phishing text, but a scalable, low-cost phishing lifecycle.

The ScamBench record makes the trend explicit: “AI systems are rapidly lowering the cost and increasing the scale of phishing and social engineering attacks” [record_id:2800]. It also formalizes this concern into a benchmark spanning email, voice, and multi-step scenarios such as pig butchering [record_id:2800]. The vishing record adds a similar economic argument for voice scams: human-operated vishing is described as unprofitable at U.S. wages, while AI-powered vishing is already profitable for several models [record_id:2736]. Across these records, the central claim is that AI changes the feasibility and incentives of attacks more than it necessarily introduces entirely new persuasion mechanisms.

### Personalization, OSINT, and vulnerability profiling recur as core attack enablers

Several records emphasize personalized attacks. The Prompt||GTFO record describes gathering OSINT, building vulnerability profiles, and using psychological persuasion techniques for spear phishing [record_id:2192]. ScamBench describes modeling personalized, semi-personalized, and generic attacks, where personalization can use affiliation and collaborators, while semi-personalization can use information such as a user’s zip code [record_id:2800]. ScamBench also describes OSINT collection, email personalization, and construction of individual vulnerability profiles [record_id:2800].

This repeated attention to personalization suggests that Heiding’s work treats AI social-engineering risk as a combination of model capability, available personal data, attacker workflow, and user-specific susceptibility. The defensive implications also follow from this. In the Prompt||GTFO record, the defensive angle includes training people against their specific persuasion vulnerabilities [record_id:2192]. In ScamBench, the defensive angle includes data minimization guidance that weighs the value of personal information to the user against its utility to an attacker, based on vulnerability profile analysis [record_id:2800].

### AI voice models are framed as a scalability problem, not necessarily a “superhuman persuasion” breakthrough

The vishing record is one of the most empirically detailed records in the set. It describes a large-scale survey experiment with N=4100 and qualitative interviews with N=12, assessing U.S. adults’ susceptibility to AI-powered voice phishing [record_id:2736]. Participants encountered audio recordings or transcripts of scam scenarios generated by models including Llama Full Duplex, Sesame, Gemini, OAI AVM, Play.AI, and ElevenLabs [record_id:2736]. The record reports success rates up to 36% for certain scam categories, identifies caller persuasiveness as the strongest predictor of compliance, and notes that Sesame achieved ratings comparable to or slightly above human voices [record_id:2736].

Importantly, the record concludes that the primary present-day risk is “improved scalability,” not novel or “superhuman” persuasion techniques [record_id:2736]. This aligns with the broader theme that AI changes attacker economics. The models make vishing cheaper and more automatable, creating incentives for more attacks, even if they do not radically outperform humans in persuasion quality [record_id:2736].

### AI infrastructure is emerging as both a strategic asset and a strategic target

A separate but connected theme is the security of AI infrastructure itself. The BSidesLV 2025 talk with Andrew Kao frames AI labs as “the next high-value targets for espionage and cyberattacks,” drawing analogies to decades of state actor compromises in sectors such as semiconductors and biotechnology [record_id:2531]. The record describes using findings from over 200 prior cyber and espionage incidents across industries to assess risks to the AI model supply chain [record_id:2531]. It also compares national cybersecurity strategies from countries including the United States, Australia, Singapore, and the United Kingdom to derive policy lessons for AI infrastructure protection [record_id:2531].

The Black Hat USA 2026 briefing with Chris Inglis develops this theme into a three-part framing: “the good,” “the bad,” and “the ugly” of AI security [record_id:2636]. “The good” is selective model access, where AI companies give select defense companies pre-release access to models so they can identify and patch vulnerabilities [record_id:2636]. “The bad” is nation-state targeting of AI infrastructure through cyber espionage, insider threats, and supply-chain attacks to steal model weights, trade secrets, and sensitive data [record_id:2636]. “The ugly” is cybercriminal use of American AI models to infiltrate, manipulate, and defraud American infrastructure and citizens [record_id:2636]. This record also introduces a practical framework for securing AI infrastructure across “chips, wires, and humans” [record_id:2636].

Together, these records position AI infrastructure as a new critical sector. They suggest that defenders should learn from historical attacks on other strategic industries but adapt those lessons to the specific architecture and incentives of AI labs, model supply chains, and frontier model deployment [record_id:2531] [record_id:2636].

### Governance and policy are treated as necessary complements to technical defenses

The records repeatedly argue or imply that technical defenses alone are insufficient. The phishing infrastructure record calls for “regulatory, technical, and policy countermeasures” in response to the ease and low cost of scaling phishing infrastructure with AI [record_id:2394]. The AI infrastructure strategy record highlights workforce development, public-private collaboration, and clear cyber governance as recurring practices from national cybersecurity strategies that may inform AI system protection [record_id:2531]. The vishing record raises concerns for consumer protection and model release policies while emphasizing a lack of technical and regulatory protection mechanisms [record_id:2736].

ScamBench includes policy interventions such as strengthened KYC requirements for domain registrars, alongside user-level data minimization advice and collaboration with Frontier AI labs for pre-deployment security assessments [record_id:2800]. The landscape-mapping record proposes mitigation strategies spanning technical, organizational, and regulatory layers, reinforcing the view that AI security must be addressed across institutional levels [record_id:2803].

### The field is fragmented, and defenses may be misaligned with current attacks

The most explicit field-level critique appears in the AI security landscape mapping record. It states that AI systems are being used to conduct cyberattacks and are becoming prime targets, but that the field studying this phenomenon is fragmented across AI safety, human-computer interaction, cybersecurity, misinformation research, psychology, law, and governance [record_id:2803]. The record proposes to map literature and research clusters across five academic databases, identify prominent and neglected communities, and analyze definitions of AI/cybersecurity intersections across sectors [record_id:2803].

This record hypothesizes fragmented research communities, weak cross-disciplinary collaboration, and defense research that is poorly aligned with the latest attack capabilities [record_id:2803]. That claim resonates with the applied social-engineering and infrastructure records: Heiding’s empirical work appears designed to close gaps between abstract AI security concerns and measurable attacker workflows, such as automated phishing infrastructure, vishing economics, and pre-deployment risk assessment [record_id:2394] [record_id:2736] [record_id:2800].
