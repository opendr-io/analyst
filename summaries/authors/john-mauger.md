# Topic: Author: John Mauger

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to John Mauger come from DEF CON 33 in 2025 and center on maritime security as a domain where physical safety, national security, operational technology, and cybersecurity increasingly overlap. Together, they portray the sea as a strategic and technical frontier: ports, ships, undersea infrastructure, drug-smuggling routes, naval operations, and extreme maritime technologies are all presented as areas where cyber, surveillance, regulation, and safety failures can have serious consequences [record_id:1946] [record_id:2092].

Mauger’s most distinctive contribution in this small corpus is the connection between maritime operational risk and cybersecurity governance. In one record, he appears as part of a broader national-security discussion about maritime threat dynamics in the digital age [record_id:1946]. In the other, he uses the 2023 Titan submersible disaster as a case study for failures of oversight, certification, testing, and risk management, then extends those lessons to maritime cybersecurity [record_id:2092]. The recurring concern is that innovation in maritime environments can outpace regulation and assurance, creating catastrophic risk when complex systems operate remotely, harshly, or in legal and technical gray zones.

The evidence base is narrow but coherent. Both records are DEF CON talk descriptions rather than transcripts or full papers, so they provide strong evidence about the stated topics and framing of Mauger’s talks, but limited evidence about the detailed arguments, technical depth, or specific recommendations delivered during the sessions.

## Research Landscape

The records consist of two DEF CON 33 entries from 2025. Both are talk or presentation descriptions rather than long-form source texts. They are attributed either solely to John Mauger or jointly to John Mauger with Michael Sulmeyer and Adam Segal. Both records are secondarily connected to maritime operational technology and cybersecurity, but they approach the topic from different angles.

The first record, “Threat Dynamics on the Seas,” is a multi-speaker session featuring John Mauger, Michael Sulmeyer, and Adam Segal. Its description frames the maritime domain as a central arena for “power projection and commerce” by nations, companies, and militaries, and says that “surveillance and cybersecurity tradecraft” are reshaping maritime threat dynamics [record_id:1946]. The talk appears broad, strategic, and geopolitical, covering examples from “port cranes to drug smuggling, and Navy ships to undersea cables” [record_id:1946]. Its primary classification is OT and IoT security, with network security and NDR as a secondary topic, but the raw text itself emphasizes maritime conflict, surveillance, cybersecurity, and national-security implications.

The second record, “The Ultimate Hack: Applying Lessons Learned from the loss of TITAN,” is attributed solely to John Mauger. It focuses on the Titan submersible disaster and uses it as an analogy and warning for maritime cybersecurity. The raw text describes the Titan loss as exposing “dangerous gaps in safety oversight, design practices, and regulation in extreme maritime environments” [record_id:2092]. Mauger’s perspective is presented as firsthand: the description states, “As leader of the international search-and-rescue response, I witnessed firsthand the human consequences of operating innovative technologies in legal gray zones without sufficient safeguards” [record_id:2092]. This record’s research area is governance, risk, regulation, independent testing, and proactive incident response as applied to maritime cyber-physical systems.

Across the two records, the landscape is not traditional enterprise cybersecurity. Instead, it is cyber-physical maritime security: ships, ports, undersea cables, submersibles, remote operations, and other maritime systems where digital compromise or inadequate technical assurance can interact with environmental hazards and geopolitical conflict. The records suggest that Mauger’s DEF CON 33 contributions were aimed at expanding cybersecurity thinking beyond networks alone and into the safety, policy, and strategic consequences of maritime digital dependence.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Maritime environments as a strategic cybersecurity frontier

Both records treat the maritime domain as newly central to digital-age security. “Threat Dynamics on the Seas” explicitly states that “the seas are the key frontier for power projection and commerce by nations, companies, and militaries” [record_id:1946]. This establishes the maritime domain not merely as a transportation or logistics environment, but as a strategic arena where states, companies, militaries, and criminal actors may compete.

The same record also suggests that cyber and surveillance capabilities are changing how maritime power and threat activity work. Its description says “surveillance and cybersecurity tradecraft are rapidly reshaping sea-side threat dynamics” [record_id:1946]. The examples are deliberately broad: port cranes, drug smuggling, Navy ships, and undersea cables [record_id:1946]. These examples imply a threat landscape that includes commercial infrastructure, illicit logistics, military systems, and critical communications infrastructure.

The Titan-focused record extends the maritime frontier framing into the realm of extreme environments and emerging technologies. It describes “extreme maritime environments,” “harsh environmental conditions,” and “remote operations” as factors that compound the impacts of maritime cyber incidents [record_id:2092]. This adds an operational-safety dimension to the broader strategic framing in the first record.

### Cybersecurity as inseparable from physical safety and operational resilience

A major recurring theme is that maritime cybersecurity is not only about confidentiality, access control, or network monitoring. The records connect cyber risk to physical harm, mission failure, infrastructure disruption, and death.

The clearest example is the Titan talk. The description says the 2023 Titan submersible loss exposed gaps in “safety oversight, design practices, and regulation” [record_id:2092]. It then draws a direct parallel to maritime cybersecurity, arguing that vessels increasingly rely on “digitally interconnected yet inadequately secured systems,” which can create risks leading to “catastrophic failures” [record_id:2092]. This is a cyber-physical risk argument: cybersecurity weaknesses in maritime systems could have consequences analogous to engineering or certification failures in extreme environments.

The first record also hints at this cyber-physical convergence by listing assets and activities where digital security interacts with physical maritime systems: port cranes, Navy ships, and undersea cables [record_id:1946]. Port cranes are operational technology; Navy ships are military platforms; undersea cables are physical infrastructure carrying digital communications. The record’s phrase “the fight is everywhere” suggests a dispersed threat environment where cyber, surveillance, logistics, and kinetic or physical systems intersect [record_id:1946].

### Governance, regulation, and oversight as cybersecurity necessities

The Titan record is strongly governance-oriented. It argues that Titan’s creators “leveraged regulatory loopholes to push design boundaries,” “dismissed expert warnings,” and bypassed “standard safety certifications” [record_id:2092]. The description presents these as not only causes or contributing factors in a maritime tragedy, but as a pattern that “mirrors critical vulnerabilities now facing maritime cybersecurity” [record_id:2092].

This is one of the most specific themes in the corpus. Mauger’s argument, as represented in the record, is that innovation without independent validation and clear regulation can produce unacceptable risk. The proposed cybersecurity lesson is not limited to better technical controls; it includes “clear international regulation, rigorous independent testing, and proactive incident response planning” [record_id:2092]. That combination places maritime cybersecurity squarely within governance, risk, compliance, safety engineering, and crisis response.

The broader “Threat Dynamics on the Seas” record does not mention regulation directly, but its framing around nations, companies, militaries, commerce, surveillance, and conflict implies a multi-stakeholder problem where governance and authority are complicated [record_id:1946]. The Titan talk supplies the explicit governance argument that the first record leaves implicit.

### Legal gray zones and unchecked innovation

The Titan record repeatedly emphasizes legal ambiguity and insufficient safeguards. It says the Titan tragedy involved “operating innovative technologies in legal gray zones without sufficient safeguards” [record_id:2092]. It also describes “unchecked innovation, inadequate oversight, and hubris” as a pattern that maps onto maritime cybersecurity vulnerabilities [record_id:2092].

This theme is important because it frames cyber risk as an institutional and cultural problem as much as a technical one. The warning is that maritime actors may deploy digital or autonomous systems, remote-control systems, sensor networks, or interconnected operational technologies before there are mature standards, certifications, or enforcement mechanisms. In Mauger’s framing, the danger is not just that systems can be hacked; it is that they may be built, deployed, and trusted under conditions where their security and safety have not been independently validated [record_id:2092].

### Broadening maritime threat models

The “Threat Dynamics on the Seas” record broadens the maritime threat model beyond conventional naval warfare or port operations. It includes “drug smuggling” alongside “Navy ships” and “undersea cables” [record_id:1946]. That combination suggests attention to multiple actor types and missions: criminal networks, military forces, commercial operators, infrastructure owners, and states.

The record also foregrounds “surveillance” as part of the changing tradecraft [record_id:1946]. This is notable because maritime cybersecurity is often discussed in terms of shipboard OT, AIS spoofing, GPS interference, port systems, or logistics software. Here, surveillance appears alongside cybersecurity, implying that information collection, tracking, situational awareness, and maritime intelligence are part of the same evolving threat environment.

The Titan record broadens the model in a different way by treating safety failures and cyber failures as structurally similar when systems operate in extreme environments without adequate review. Its warning that vessels rely on “digitally interconnected yet inadequately secured systems” points toward risks in modern maritime automation, remote operation, and integrated vessel systems [record_id:2092].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records are talk descriptions and do not provide detailed technical methods, tools, or implementation workflows. However, they do identify several approaches and conceptual methods relevant to maritime cybersecurity research.

One approach is strategic threat analysis across maritime assets and actor types. “Threat Dynamics on the Seas” appears to use a wide lens, covering threats from “port cranes to drug smuggling, and Navy ships to undersea cables” [record_id:1946]. This implies a method of mapping maritime cyber and surveillance threats across commercial, criminal, military, and infrastructure domains rather than treating each system in isolation.

Another approach is analogy-based risk analysis using the Titan submersible disaster as a case study. “The Ultimate Hack” explicitly connects “lessons learned from the loss of TITAN” to “urgent maritime cybersecurity needs” [record_id:2092]. The method is to examine a catastrophic maritime safety failure—especially failures in design assurance, oversight, certification, and response—and apply those lessons to cyber-physical maritime systems. The record frames Titan as a warning about what can happen when innovative technologies operate in extreme environments without adequate safeguards [record_id:2092].

The Titan record also identifies several concrete governance and assurance approaches:

- clear international regulation for maritime cybersecurity and safety-sensitive technologies [record_id:2092];
- rigorous independent testing, analogous to standard safety certification and validation [record_id:2092];
- proactive incident response planning for maritime cyber incidents, especially where remote operations and harsh conditions make failures more severe [record_id:2092].

These are not “tools” in the software sense, but they are central methods in the governance, risk, and compliance approach described by the record. They indicate that Mauger’s contribution emphasizes institutional controls and preparedness as much as technical countermeasures.

The “Threat Dynamics” record additionally suggests surveillance and cybersecurity tradecraft as fields of practice reshaping maritime threats [record_id:1946]. The description does not define specific techniques, but downstream researchers may want to investigate whether the full talk covers maritime domain awareness, port OT monitoring, network detection and response in maritime environments, undersea cable monitoring, AIS/GPS manipulation, or cyber-enabled interdiction. Those specifics are not present in the raw record and should not be assumed without reviewing the talk.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The most strategically broad record is “Threat Dynamics on the Seas,” a DEF CON 33 session by John Mauger, Michael Sulmeyer, and Adam Segal [record_id:1946]. Its importance lies in its framing: the seas are described as a “key frontier” for power projection and commerce, while surveillance and cybersecurity tradecraft are said to be reshaping maritime threats [record_id:1946]. The range of examples—port cranes, drug smuggling, Navy ships, and undersea cables—makes this record representative of an expansive maritime threat landscape [record_id:1946]. It is especially relevant for researchers interested in national security, critical infrastructure, port OT, maritime logistics, and cyber-enabled conflict.

The most distinctive John Mauger solo record is “The Ultimate Hack: Applying Lessons Learned from the loss of TITAN” [record_id:2092]. This talk is notable because it links a high-profile maritime tragedy to cybersecurity governance. The description presents Mauger as having firsthand search-and-rescue experience, stating that he led the international response and “witnessed firsthand the human consequences” of operating innovative technologies without sufficient safeguards [record_id:2092]. It then argues that the same pattern—regulatory loopholes, dismissed warnings, bypassed certifications, unchecked innovation, inadequate oversight, and hubris—mirrors vulnerabilities in maritime cybersecurity [record_id:2092].

This second record is also the clearest source for Mauger’s prescriptive recommendations. It calls for “clear international regulation, rigorous independent testing, and proactive incident response planning” to prevent similar disasters at sea [record_id:2092]. For downstream agents, this record is the strongest evidence for Mauger’s governance-centered view of maritime cybersecurity risk.

Taken together, the records show two complementary roles: Mauger as a participant in a broad national-security conversation about maritime threat dynamics [record_id:1946], and Mauger as a solo speaker drawing operational and governance lessons from the Titan disaster for maritime cybersecurity [record_id:2092].
