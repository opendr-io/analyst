# Topic: Author: David Krisiloff

## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to David Krisiloff are CAMLIS talks focused on machine learning for malware analysis, with particular emphasis on how security-domain knowledge can improve data quality and model performance. The 2018 talk examines malware labeling over time using VirusTotal reports, treating antivirus detections as noisy, evolving, crowdsourced signals and proposing machine-learning-based strategies for deciding when to rescan files and how to improve training labels more quickly [record_id:240]. The 2024 talk shifts from label production to model architecture, arguing that static malware classifiers should not be forced into a binary choice between expert-crafted features and raw-byte deep learning; instead, lightweight structural and semantic hints from Windows PE files can be injected into deep models to improve performance and robustness [record_id:141].

Collectively, the records show a coherent research trajectory around practical, production-relevant malware machine learning. Krisiloff’s attributed work is not presented as purely theoretical ML research; both records are grounded in operational constraints: noisy antivirus labels, VirusTotal rescan limits, evolving scanner behavior, large-scale malware datasets, commercial classifier baselines, and deployment-scale endpoint protection [record_id:240] [record_id:141]. The strongest common theme is that malware ML systems benefit from carefully incorporating domain structure—whether in the form of temporally aware label aggregation or PE-aware model design—rather than relying naively on raw data or static assumptions.

The evidence base is small but focused: two CAMLIS records, one from 2018 and one from 2024. They cover different stages of the malware ML pipeline: dataset labeling and model construction. The main limitation is that the records are abstracts rather than full papers or transcripts, so they provide strong signals about research questions, claims, datasets, and contributions, but limited detail about implementation, quantitative metrics, ablation results, or reproducibility.

## Research Landscape

The included records are both CAMLIS entries attributed to David Krisiloff. They are conference-style talk or paper abstracts rather than blog posts, code releases, tutorials, or full technical papers. Both belong primarily to the malware analysis and reverse engineering area, with secondary relevance to machine learning model security, and one also intersects endpoint security and EDR [record_id:141] [record_id:240].

The 2018 record, “Measure Twice, Quarantine Once: A Tale of Malware Labeling over Time,” is situated in the context of antivirus data aggregation and the use of VirusTotal as a label source for machine-learning-based antivirus systems [record_id:240]. It frames VirusTotal as a crowdsourcing-like system, but one with unusual properties: antivirus engines improve over time, detections are public, and scanner behavior can be influenced by the shared visibility of other scanners’ detections [record_id:240]. The talk focuses on how these temporal and feedback-loop properties complicate malware labeling, especially for training datasets.

The 2024 record, “Structure and Semantics-Aware Malware Classification with Vision Transformers,” is situated in the context of static malware classification and deep learning architectures for binaries [record_id:141]. It identifies a historical tension between expert-crafted malware features and deep learning systems operating directly on raw bytes. The talk argues that byte-based approaches have generally struggled to match traditional expert-feature-based models, and proposes adding explicit structural and semantic hints to deep learning architectures [record_id:141].

Together, the research landscape is best understood as applied malware ML under real-world constraints. The records are not about general-purpose cybersecurity policy, offensive exploitation, incident response workflows, or malware family reverse engineering in the narrow manual-analysis sense. Instead, they focus on the data and modeling foundations needed to make automated malware detection effective at scale. One record addresses how to generate better labels from changing antivirus consensus signals [record_id:240]; the other addresses how to build stronger static classifiers by combining representation learning with file-format knowledge [record_id:141].

## Major Themes And Trends

A major theme across the records is skepticism toward naive data-driven malware ML. In the 2018 talk, the naive assumption would be that VirusTotal reports can be converted into stable labels by simply counting detections or waiting for enough vendors to agree. Krisiloff’s abstract instead emphasizes that AV scanner outputs change over time, that engines improve as new signatures are incorporated, and that public detections create feedback loops among scanners [record_id:240]. This means labels are not fixed ground truth; they are temporally dynamic artifacts of scanner update cycles and ecosystem behavior.

In the 2024 talk, the analogous naive assumption is that sufficiently powerful deep neural architectures can learn effective malware representations directly from raw binary bytes. The abstract states that byte-based approaches have struggled to reach the performance of traditional machine-learning models using expert features, despite extensive exploration of architectures and training regimes [record_id:141]. The proposed solution is not to abandon deep learning, but to supply it with structural and semantic cues drawn from expert knowledge.

A second theme is the value of domain knowledge as a middle ground. The 2024 record explicitly describes “a rich, unexplored continuum of expert knowledge” between fully hand-crafted features and pure raw-byte representation learning [record_id:141]. The approach investigates whether providing “explicit structural and semantic hints” at varying specificity improves deep-learning-based classifiers [record_id:141]. This theme is also implicit in the 2018 labeling work: rather than treating VirusTotal as a simple crowdsourced voting pool, the research models antivirus detection dynamics over time and uses knowledge of rescanning constraints to improve dataset construction [record_id:240].

A third recurring concern is temporal change. The 2018 record is centrally about time: AV scanners improve, old files can be rescanned, reports evolve, and labels may change as engines update [record_id:240]. It asks when files should be rescanned, how rescans should be prioritized, and how to predict which files are likely to change labels over time [record_id:240]. The 2024 record is less directly temporal, but it includes considerations that matter in changing adversarial environments, including training set size and robustness to adversarial evasion [record_id:141]. Across both records, the malware ML problem is depicted as dynamic rather than static.

A fourth theme is operational scale. The 2018 talk discusses VirusTotal’s aggregation of results from more than 70 antivirus scanners and constraints such as daily rescan limits [record_id:240]. The 2024 talk reports evaluation on three Windows PE malware datasets, including EMBER at 800K samples and “a full production-grade malware dataset containing more than 100M unique samples” [record_id:141]. The 2024 abstract further claims performance equivalent to “a commercial malware classifier deployed to millions of endpoints” [record_id:141]. These details indicate that the work is concerned with systems that must operate under production-like data volumes and deployment constraints.

A fifth theme is improving the reliability of machine-learning-based antivirus. The 2018 record focuses on producing more accurate labels faster than passive waiting for AV vendors on VirusTotal to reach consensus [record_id:240]. Better labels directly affect downstream ML classifier quality. The 2024 record focuses on improving classifier performance by integrating PE structure and semantic information, with claims that lightweight structural information can allow deep-learning-based models to match traditional malware classifiers for the first time [record_id:141]. The two talks therefore address complementary points in the same pipeline: label quality and model quality.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The 2018 record discusses methods for malware label production from historical VirusTotal reports. VirusTotal is described as aggregating results from more than 70 antivirus scanners and as allowing old files to be rescanned with newer AV engines, subject to daily rescan limits [record_id:240]. The proposed approach examines “temporal dynamics of virus detections” and considers “a variety of models for producing labels from the reports” [record_id:240]. The abstract states that changes in AV detections over time are generally predictable using machine learning models, enabling prioritization of files most likely to change labels over time [record_id:240]. It also claims to present optimal strategies for rescanning files to build improved datasets [record_id:240].

The practical workflow in the 2018 record can be summarized as: collect historical VirusTotal reports; model how scanner detections change over time; estimate which files’ labels are likely to change; prioritize rescans under VirusTotal’s daily limits; and produce more accurate malware labels sooner than passive consensus-based approaches [record_id:240]. The approach treats the labeling system itself as a prediction and resource-allocation problem, not merely an aggregation problem.

The 2024 record discusses static Windows PE malware classification using deep learning and Vision Transformers. It contrasts two existing approaches: expert-crafted static features and deep architectures that operate directly on raw bytes [record_id:141]. The proposed approach injects explicit structural and semantic hints into the architecture. The abstract specifically mentions lightweight structural information such as PE file sections and semantic information such as parsing errors [record_id:141]. It evaluates these design choices across three Windows PE malware datasets, including EMBER with 800K samples and a production-grade dataset with more than 100M unique samples [record_id:141].

The 2024 approach is significant because it positions file-format-aware design as an architectural technique. Instead of relying only on manually engineered features or raw-byte end-to-end learning, it uses different degrees of structural and semantic specificity to guide representation learning [record_id:141]. The abstract claims that adding lightweight PE section information directly into the architecture allows deep-learning-based models to match traditional malware classifier performance and reach equivalence with a commercial classifier deployed to millions of endpoints [record_id:141].

Both records use machine learning not only as a classifier but as a way to model the malware detection ecosystem. In 2018, ML predicts label evolution and informs rescan strategy [record_id:240]. In 2024, ML performs classification but is shaped by expert knowledge about executable structure and semantic parsing behavior [record_id:141]. In both cases, the method is hybrid: algorithmic learning is combined with domain-specific structure, constraints, or operational knowledge.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The 2018 CAMLIS record, “Measure Twice, Quarantine Once: A Tale of Malware Labeling over Time,” is notable because it addresses a foundational problem for malware machine learning: how to assign reliable labels when using antivirus aggregation services such as VirusTotal [record_id:240]. The abstract argues that VirusTotal differs from ordinary crowdsourcing because AV scanners improve over time, public detections create feedback loops, and users can rescan old files with newer engines under rate constraints [record_id:240]. These properties undermine simplistic assumptions about static labels or one-time consensus.

The record’s most important contribution is the framing of malware labeling as a temporal prediction and optimization problem. It asks how labels should be assigned from noisy VirusTotal reports, when files should be rescanned, and how rescans should be prioritized [record_id:240]. It then claims that AV detection changes are generally predictable using machine learning models, and that this enables strategies that produce more accurate labels faster than waiting for vendor consensus [record_id:240]. For downstream researchers, this record is especially relevant to dataset construction, label-noise handling, antivirus telemetry interpretation, and temporal validation of malware classifiers.

The 2024 CAMLIS record, “Structure and Semantics-Aware Malware Classification with Vision Transformers,” is notable because it addresses a later-stage modeling problem: how deep learning architectures for static malware classification can be improved by incorporating expert knowledge [record_id:141]. The abstract states that raw-byte deep learning approaches have generally underperformed traditional models using expert-crafted features, despite extensive exploration [record_id:141]. It then proposes a middle path: explicit structural and semantic hints, such as PE sections and parsing errors, incorporated into model architecture [record_id:141].

The 2024 record is also notable for its scale and claimed performance. It reports evaluation on three Windows PE malware datasets, from EMBER at 800K samples to a production-grade dataset with more than 100M unique samples [record_id:141]. It claims that incorporating lightweight structural information allows deep-learning-based models to match traditional malware classifiers “for the first time” and achieve performance equivalent to a commercial malware classifier deployed to millions of endpoints [record_id:141]. For downstream researchers, this record is especially relevant to static PE classification, Vision Transformers for malware, hybrid expert/deep learning representations, adversarial evasion, and production-scale evaluation.

Taken together, the two records provide evidence that Krisiloff’s attributed work emphasizes practical improvements to malware ML systems rather than isolated algorithmic novelty. The 2018 talk improves the inputs to ML systems by producing better labels from evolving AV signals [record_id:240]. The 2024 talk improves the model itself by incorporating executable structure and semantic hints into deep learning architectures [record_id:141]. The records are separated by six years, but they share a consistent concern with the gap between academic ML assumptions and operational malware detection realities.

## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The evidence base is limited to two records, both conference abstracts. As a result, the records provide high-level claims and research framing but not enough detail to fully assess reproducibility, experimental design, or statistical strength. It also does not state how ground truth was established, which is a crucial issue for malware labeling research.

Downstream research should investigate whether rescan prioritization strategies risk amplifying existing ecosystem biases.
