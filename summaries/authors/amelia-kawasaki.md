## Executive Summary

The two records attributed to Amelia Kawasaki describe a focused research trajectory in large language model security, especially white-box analysis and intervention in transformer-based LLMs. Across the 2024 and 2025 CAMLIS records, Kawasaki’s work centers on how internal model structure can both enable defensive detection of attacks and introduce new classes of vulnerability. Taken together, the records portray Kawasaki’s contributions as situated at the intersection of AI security, prompt injection and jailbreaking, and machine learning model security. The recurring emphasis is white-box access: both records assume or involve visibility into the internal workings of an LLM rather than treating the model purely as a black-box API. The evidence base is thin but coherent: only two records are available, both from CAMLIS, both presented as papers or talks, and both concerned with model-internal mechanisms for either detecting or enabling adversarial behavior.

## Research Landscape

The available corpus consists of two CAMLIS records, one from 2024 and one from 2025, each attributed to Amelia Kawasaki. Both are short descriptions of technical work on large language model security rather than full papers, transcripts, slides, or detailed experimental reports. The records therefore provide clear topic signals but limited methodological detail.

The 2024 record, “Defending Large Language Models Against Attacks With Residual Stream Activation Analysis,” is framed as a defensive contribution. It begins from the premise that widespread LLM adoption, exemplified by ChatGPT, creates an imperative to defend against adversarial threats that manipulate model outputs through malicious inputs [record_id:148]. The described approach requires white-box access and uses residual activation analysis between transformer layers to identify distinctive activation patterns associated with attack prompts [record_id:148].

The 2025 record, “ShadowLogic: Hidden Backdoors in Any Whitebox LLM,” is framed around a newly described vulnerability class. It states that ShadowLogic injects hidden backdoors into white-box LLMs by modifying their computational graphs, with the backdoors activated by a secret trigger phrase that allows the model to generate uncensored responses [record_id:114]. This record moves from detecting attacks to showing that internal model access can be used to introduce covert behavior.

The research landscape represented by these two records is therefore narrow but meaningful: both works treat LLM security as a model-internals problem. Rather than focusing only on input filtering, prompt engineering, policy wrappers, or post-hoc moderation, the records emphasize transformer internals, residual streams, computational graphs, activation signatures, and white-box manipulation [record_id:148] [record_id:114].

## Major Themes And Trends

A central theme across the records is the security significance of white-box access to LLMs. In the 2024 defensive work, white-box access enables inspection of residual activations between transformer layers, which are used to classify attack prompts [record_id:148]. In the 2025 ShadowLogic work, white-box access enables direct modification of computational graphs to implant hidden backdoors [record_id:114]. This creates a dual-use pattern: internal access can be a foundation for robust defense, but it can also expand the attacker’s capabilities.

A second theme is the movement from surface-level prompt attacks toward internal model mechanisms. The 2024 record discusses adversarial inputs that manipulate an LLM’s output and undermine model integrity and user trust, but the proposed response is not merely better prompt screening. Instead, it analyzes “distinctive activation patterns in the residual streams” for attack prompt classification [record_id:148]. The 2025 record similarly frames the issue at a structural level, describing graph-level vulnerabilities created by modifications to a model’s computational graph [record_id:114]. Both records suggest that meaningful LLM security research must account for behavior inside the model, not only the text that enters or exits it.

A third recurring concern is adversarial control over model outputs. In the 2024 record, malicious inputs are said to manipulate an LLM’s output and threaten integrity and trust [record_id:148]. In the 2025 record, a hidden trigger phrase activates a backdoor that allows the model to generate uncensored responses [record_id:114]. The mechanism differs—external adversarial prompts in one case, hidden graph-level backdoors in the other—but both records focus on unauthorized or unsafe shifts in model behavior.

The two records also show a possible progression in Kawasaki’s research agenda. The 2024 work emphasizes detection and mitigation of adversarial inputs through activation analysis and safety fine-tuning evaluation [record_id:148]. The 2025 work highlights a new vulnerability class involving hidden backdoors in white-box LLMs [record_id:114]. This progression could reflect a broader trend in AI security research: as defenses mature around prompt-level attacks, attention shifts toward deeper model-internal threat models, including tampering, backdooring, and compromised model supply chains. The records themselves do not explicitly state this trend, but their chronological ordering and technical focus support that interpretation.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The most detailed method appears in the 2024 record. Kawasaki’s 2024 work proposes using residual stream activation analysis as a defensive strategy against LLM attacks [record_id:148]. The raw description specifies white-box access to an LLM and analysis of activations “between transformer layers” [record_id:148]. The method is described as identifying distinctive activation patterns in residual streams and using those patterns for attack prompt classification [record_id:148]. This suggests a workflow in which prompts are run through a model, intermediate activations are collected, and those activation signatures are classified as benign or adversarial.

The 2024 record also mentions dataset construction and evaluation. It says the authors curate multiple datasets to demonstrate high classification accuracy across multiple attack scenarios, including a newly created attack dataset [record_id:148]. This implies an empirical approach, though the record does not provide dataset names, sizes, attack categories, baselines, quantitative metrics, or model architectures. The same record further states that the work integrates safety fine-tuning techniques for LLMs to measure their effect on attack detection capability [record_id:148]. This is notable because it treats safety fine-tuning not only as a mitigation in itself, but as a variable that may influence the detectability of adversarial prompts via internal activations.

The 2025 record introduces a different approach: computational graph modification for hidden backdoor insertion [record_id:114]. ShadowLogic is described as “a method for injecting hidden backdoors into white-box LLMs by modifying their computational graphs” [record_id:114]. The backdoors are activated by a secret trigger phrase, after which the model generates uncensored responses [record_id:114]. The record is brief, so it does not specify how the graph is modified, what kinds of models were tested, whether the backdoor survives fine-tuning or quantization, or how detectable the modification is. Still, the key method is clear: the vulnerability operates at the graph level rather than by ordinary prompt injection alone.

Together, the methods described in the records imply a technical vocabulary and research approach involving transformer residual streams, intermediate activations, attack prompt classification, safety fine-tuning, white-box model access, computational graph manipulation, trigger phrases, and hidden backdoors [record_id:148] [record_id:114]. These approaches are especially relevant to researchers studying mechanistic interpretability for security, model supply-chain risk, adversarial prompt detection, and tamper-resistant LLM deployment.
