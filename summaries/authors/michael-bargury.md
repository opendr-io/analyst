# Topic: Author: Michael Bargury

## Executive Summary

The four records attributed to Michael Bargury center on the security consequences of deploying generative AI assistants, agentic browsers, agent supply chains, and multi-model jailbreak testing into real enterprise environments. Collectively, they portray AI agents not as isolated chat interfaces but as operational systems with access to email, calendars, files, browsers, enterprise SaaS accounts, local machines, user histories, and tool-execution paths. The dominant concern is that these systems collapse traditional security boundaries: user intent can be hijacked, browser isolation can be weakened, malware can hide in agent-consumable artifacts, and attacks can move from prompt injection to full enterprise compromise or local code execution [record_id:71] [record_id:2667] [record_id:2669].

Across the records, Bargury’s work is presented as highly applied and demonstration-driven. The talks emphasize exploit chains, 0-click or low-click attack paths, real products, public marketplaces, and defensive frameworks or tooling. The 2025 Black Hat record frames AI assistants as enterprise attack surfaces and proposes the “GenAI Attack Matrix” for managing prompt-injection-driven risk [record_id:71]. The 2026 Black Hat records extend this into agentic browsers and agent supply-chain malware, including “PleaseFix,” “Intent Collision,” and an “agent detonation chamber” for analyzing malicious AI artifacts [record_id:2667] [record_id:2669]. A Prompt||GTFO record adds a practitioner tooling angle: Bargury demonstrates `chatbot.ai`, an open-source tool for testing jailbreak prompts across multiple model configurations, and describes a Claude-assisted development workflow [record_id:2212].

The overall picture is of a research program focused on agentic AI as an exploitable execution environment. Recurring themes include prompt injection as a managed security problem rather than a single fixable bug, 0-click compromise, abuse of agent tools and permissions, degradation of browser and application isolation, AI artifact malware, and the need for deterministic, systems-level security controls rather than reliance on model safety behavior alone [record_id:71] [record_id:2667] [record_id:2669].

## Research Landscape

The records consist primarily of conference talk abstracts or talk summaries. Three of the four are Black Hat briefings, two from Black Hat USA 2026 and one from Black Hat USA 2025 [record_id:71] [record_id:2667] [record_id:2669]. The remaining record is a Prompt||GTFO talk summary describing a demonstration of an open-source multi-model jailbreak testing tool and the speaker’s AI-assisted development process [record_id:2212]. This means the evidence base is weighted toward offensive security presentations, exploit demonstrations, and proposed defensive frameworks rather than peer-reviewed papers, full technical writeups, or independent validation.

The research area is strongly concentrated around AI security, especially prompt injection, jailbreak transferability, agentic AI exploitation, and enterprise compromise. However, the records broaden the topic beyond conventional chatbot jailbreaks. The 2025 Black Hat talk describes AI assistants as enterprise attack surfaces capable of initial access, persistence, data harvesting, exfiltration, and impact, including attacks against ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Einstein, and a custom agent [record_id:71]. The 2026 agentic-browser talk argues that browser agents intentionally loosen or bypass older isolation assumptions, reintroducing attack classes such as XSS, sandbox escapes, and drive-by exploitation in agent-mediated form [record_id:2667]. The 2026 promptware talk shifts to the agent supply chain, where malicious artifacts can live in skill markdown files, MCP servers, models, and posts consumed by agents at runtime [record_id:2669].

The records also show Bargury’s work bridging offense and defense. The abstracts are built around attack chains and exploit demonstrations, but they also promise frameworks, mitigations, detection approaches, vendor comparisons, detonation reports, benchmarks, and open-source tooling [record_id:71] [record_id:2212] [record_id:2667] [record_id:2669]. This creates a landscape where exploit research is used to motivate operational security models for enterprises, browser vendors, AI-agent builders, and threat intelligence workflows.

## Major Themes And Trends

### Agentic AI turns prompt injection into enterprise compromise

A central theme is that prompt injection becomes more dangerous as assistants evolve into agents with permissions, tools, memory, and access to enterprise data. The 2025 Black Hat record explicitly frames AI as enabling initial access, persistence, data harvesting, exfiltration, and impact. It claims an external attacker may need “no credentials,” “no phishing,” “no social engineering,” and “no human-in-the-loop,” describing “in-and-out with a single prompt” and, in some cases, 0-click attacks [record_id:71]. The record emphasizes that agents read search history, emails, and chat messages while wielding tools that can manipulate enterprise environments on behalf of users or attackers [record_id:71].

This theme recurs in the 2026 agentic-browser record, where the browser itself becomes an agentic execution environment. Instead of just manipulating a chatbot response, the attacker is described as using social media interactions or calendar invites to trigger drive-by exploitation, account takeover, data exfiltration, persistence through agent memory or files, and even local remote code execution [record_id:2667]. The trend across the records is escalation from prompt-level manipulation to tool-mediated action and durable compromise.

### 0-click and low-interaction attack chains are a recurring focus

The title and abstract of the 2025 Black Hat briefing explicitly foreground “0click Exploit Methods” and “access-to-impact AI vulnerability chains” across enterprise AI assistants [record_id:71]. It distinguishes attacks requiring “one bad click” from those requiring “no user interaction” [record_id:71]. The 2026 agentic-browser briefing continues this emphasis with “0-Click Takeover,” “full end-to-end 0click attack chains,” drive-by exploitation from social media, and weaponized calendar invites delivering targeted payloads [record_id:2667].

The repetition matters because it shows Bargury’s work is not only about jailbreak prompts that require cooperative users. The records focus on attacks where AI agents consume attacker-controlled content as part of normal workflows and then act on it. This shifts risk modeling away from user gullibility and toward ambient content ingestion, automated tool use, and trust boundaries between external content and internal agent authority [record_id:71] [record_id:2667].

### Traditional security boundaries are portrayed as eroding under agentic design

The 2026 browser record is the strongest example of this theme. It claims that “decades of isolation techniques and exploit mitigations” are being dismantled for agentic browsers, listing examples such as Same-Origin Policy weakening, localhost access, filesystem access, and script execution on websites [record_id:2667]. The record characterizes these as “design choices, not vulnerabilities,” arguing that XSS, sandbox escapes, and drive-by exploitation are returning in AI-agent-mediated form [record_id:2667].

A similar concern appears in the agent supply-chain record, though in a different layer of the stack. There, the traditional supply-chain security model is described as outdated because agents pull, write, and execute code at runtime, sidestepping “build-time static scanning” [record_id:2669]. Together, these records suggest a broader thesis: AI agents disrupt established security assumptions by collapsing distinctions between reading and acting, browsing and executing, installing and detonating, user intent and attacker instruction.

### Model safety is treated as insufficient by itself

The records repeatedly imply that model-level safety behavior is not an adequate primary defense. The 2025 Black Hat abstract states that “prompt injection is not another bug we can fix” but a “security problem we can manage,” and it proposes a GenAI Attack Matrix to help organizations protect themselves [record_id:71]. The 2026 browser abstract says some agentic browsers rely mainly on “model safety training” while making design choices that loosen isolation; it contrasts this with more meaningful hard boundaries such as deterministic filters and human reviews [record_id:2667]. The promptware record is even more explicit in its critique of LLM-based judgment: it promotes analysis based on “kernel-level truths,” “not a wishful analysis by an LLM judge” [record_id:2669].

This theme is important because it points to a defensive philosophy. Bargury-associated records favor systems controls, sandboxing, detonation, deterministic boundaries, detection, mitigation frameworks, and analyst workflows over purely probabilistic model compliance [record_id:71] [record_id:2667] [record_id:2669].

### AI artifact and agent supply-chain malware emerge as a distinct concern

The 2026 “Promptware EOD” record expands the topic from prompt injection and agent takeover to malicious artifacts in the AI agent supply chain. It claims malware can lurk in “skill markdown files,” “rug-pulled MCP servers,” “misaligned models,” and “weaponized moltbook posts” [record_id:2669]. The record says static scanning tools were blinded by trivial instructions and that malicious skills, including cryptominers and infostealers, remained undetected for months until the researchers detonated tens of thousands of public-marketplace skills [record_id:2669].

This creates a distinct trend: as agents consume plugins, skills, server endpoints, generated code, and external instructions, malware analysis must adapt. The record proposes an “agent detonation chamber” that observes what actually happens at the kernel level while comparing that against what the victim agent “thinks” it did [record_id:2669]. The framing connects AI security to older malware-analysis practice, but updated for runtime agent behavior.

### Bargury’s tooling work supports empirical jailbreak and agent testing

The Prompt||GTFO record introduces a smaller but useful theme: practical experimentation across models and configurations. Bargury demonstrates `chatbot.ai`, described as an open-source tool for testing AI jailbreaks across multiple models simultaneously [record_id:2212]. The tool reportedly supports different API keys, system instructions, temperatures, and models, allowing one prompt to be run across many configurations to determine whether jailbreaks transfer across model families [record_id:2212].

This record complements the larger Black Hat talks by showing infrastructure for repeatable testing and cross-model comparison. It also notes Bargury’s “vibe-coding” process using Claude, including automated plan generation, security reviews, and nightly build prompts [record_id:2212]. That makes it the only record focused more on research/development workflow than on a specific exploit class.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The records describe several named or clearly defined methods and approaches.

The 2025 Black Hat talk proposes the “GenAI Attack Matrix,” described as a security framework to help organizations protect themselves from AI-agent compromise [record_id:71]. The same record says the researchers will break their attacks into basic TTPs and show how they can be detected and mitigated [record_id:71]. This suggests an approach modeled on structured adversary behavior: define AI-specific tactics, techniques, and procedures, then map them to detection and mitigation strategies.

The 2026 browser record introduces “PleaseFix,” described as the evolution of ClickFix and as a new vulnerability class targeting agents rather than humans [record_id:2667]. It also names “Intent Collision” as a universal exploitation technique [record_id:2667]. The raw record does not provide the full technical mechanics of these techniques, but it connects them to 0-click agentic-browser takeover, drive-by exploitation, weaponized calendar invites, account takeover, silent exfiltration, persistence through agent memory and files, and sandbox escape to local RCE [record_id:2667].

The same browser record also discusses defensive methods. It says some browser agents made exploitation harder through “creative engineering,” including “hard boundaries” that limit AI agency, deterministic filters, and human reviews [record_id:2667]. It also mentions collaboration with affected vendors and conclusions relevant to agent builders [record_id:2667]. This is one of the few places in the records where specific defensive control styles are named, though the evidence remains abstract-level rather than implementation-level.

The Prompt||GTFO record describes `chatbot.ai`, an open-source tool for testing jailbreaks across multiple model families and configurations [record_id:2212]. The method is comparative: run one prompt against multiple models, system instructions, temperatures, and API-backed configurations, then observe whether jailbreak behavior transfers [record_id:2212]. This approach is useful for downstream agents interested in jailbreak robustness, model-family differences, and evaluation workflows.

The promptware record’s main method is an “agent detonation chamber” [record_id:2669]. The described architecture uses two agents: a victim agent instructed to install a suspicious artifact and a red-teaming agent tasked with making the victim detonate the newly acquired skill [record_id:2669]. The chamber compares the victim agent’s self-reported or perceived activity with kernel-observed behavior, surfacing “semantic compromises” that static tools miss [record_id:2669]. The record further states that the researchers intend to release a free public service, open-source tooling to hook the chamber to agents, malware detonation reports compatible with analyst workflows, threat intelligence feed integrations, and “Promptware eval,” an open-source benchmark for malicious AI artifacts found in the wild [record_id:2669].
