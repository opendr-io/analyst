# Topic: Author: Gary Lopez Munoz

## Executive Summary

The three records attributed to Gary Lopez Munoz portray a focused research trajectory in AI security, especially around adversarial testing, red teaming, prompt injection, jailbreak discovery, and defensive evaluation for generative AI systems. The strongest substantive evidence comes from two CAMLIS records: a 2023 tutorial on “LLM Prompt Injection: Attacks and Defenses,” which emphasizes hands-on understanding of prompt injection taxonomies, exploitation techniques, and emerging defenses [record_id:171], and a 2024 presentation or paper on PyRIT, the Python Risk Identification Toolkit, described as an open-source, model- and platform-agnostic framework for red teaming multimodal generative AI systems [record_id:135].

The 2025 record, titled “An Agent-Based Framework for Adversarial Simulation and Blue Teaming,” appears to extend this line of work toward agent-based adversarial simulation and blue teaming, but the available raw text contains only author names—“Lopez Munoz; Mauricio Velazco; Manuel MelŽndez”—and provides no abstract or technical description [record_id:130]. As a result, it should be treated as weak evidence for content, though it is important for coverage because it indicates continued work attributed to Lopez Munoz and collaborators in adversarial simulation and blue-team-oriented AI/security workflows.

Collectively, the records suggest a progression from understanding LLM-specific attack surfaces, especially prompt injection, toward reusable frameworks and operational tooling for risk identification, red teaming, and potentially adversarial simulation. The corpus is small, conference-centered, and concentrated in CAMLIS records from 2023–2025, so conclusions about the author’s broader body of work should remain tentative.

## Research Landscape

The records are all CAMLIS-associated entries spanning three consecutive years: 2023, 2024, and 2025. They are not blog posts, code repositories, or long technical papers in the provided evidence; rather, they appear to be conference talk or tutorial abstracts, with varying levels of detail. The 2023 and 2024 records include substantive abstracts, while the 2025 record contains only a terse author line in the raw text [record_id:130].

The research area represented by the records is AI security, with emphasis on generative AI systems and operational security practices. The 2023 record is explicitly about LLM prompt injection, including attack taxonomies and defensive measures [record_id:171]. The 2024 record broadens from prompt injection alone to generative AI red teaming, model-agnostic and platform-agnostic risk identification, and multimodal systems [record_id:135]. The 2025 record’s title suggests a further move toward “agent-based” adversarial simulation and blue teaming, but because the raw text gives no details beyond names, any interpretation of the technical scope must be provisional [record_id:130].

## Major Themes And Trends

A clear theme across the substantive records is the treatment of generative AI systems as security-relevant software systems that require adversarial testing. In the 2023 tutorial, the focus is on LLM prompt injection as a concrete vulnerability class. The abstract frames transformer-based language models as widely adopted and useful, but also as carrying “new risks for exploitation and manipulation by malicious agents” [record_id:171]. This establishes the central concern: LLMs create new attack surfaces that security practitioners need to understand through practical exercises.

The 2023 record emphasizes both offense and defense. Participants are expected to learn taxonomies of prompt injection attacks, specifically “User Prompt Injection Attacks (UPIA)” and “Cross-domain Prompt Injection Attacks (XPIA),” then implement LLM bots, attack or exploit them, and finally act as defenders implementing emerging defenses [record_id:171]. This dual-use structure—attack to understand, defend to mitigate—recurs in the broader red-teaming framing of the 2024 record.

The 2024 PyRIT record shifts the emphasis from a single vulnerability family to a reusable framework for generative AI risk identification. It states that generative AI is becoming ubiquitous and that increasing computational power and data availability have led to more single- and multimodal models [record_id:135]. In response, it argues for “extensible and model-agnostic risk identification frameworks” and introduces PyRIT as an open-source framework intended to enhance red teaming of generative AI systems [record_id:135]. This reflects a trend from education about specific attacks toward institutionalized tooling for systematic risk discovery.

Another major theme is model and platform agnosticism. PyRIT is described as “model- and platform-agnostic,” capable of probing “novel harms, risks, and jailbreaks” in multimodal generative AI models [record_id:135]. This suggests a recognition that the AI ecosystem is too diverse and fast-moving for security methods tied to a single vendor, model architecture, or modality. The same record highlights composability, reuse of core building blocks, and extensibility to future models and modalities [record_id:135]. These ideas position AI red teaming as an evolving engineering discipline, not a one-off testing exercise.

The records also show an apparent widening of scope over time. In 2023, the talk is about LLM prompt injection attacks and defenses [record_id:171]. In 2024, the scope becomes GenAI red teaming across models, platforms, risks, harms, jailbreaks, and modalities [record_id:135]. In 2025, the record title indicates an “Agent-Based Framework for Adversarial Simulation and Blue Teaming,” implying movement toward simulation, agents, and blue-team operationalization, although the raw text does not substantiate the technical content beyond authorship [record_id:130]. The trend, cautiously stated, is from vulnerability-specific instruction to broader frameworks for adversarial assessment and defensive security workflows.

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The 2023 record discusses a hands-on tutorial approach. Its methods include teaching taxonomies of LLM injection attacks, having participants implement LLM bots, and then using “various techniques” to attack or exploit those bots [record_id:171]. The tutorial then pivots to defense, where participants implement “emerging techniques for defending against prompt injection attacks” [record_id:171]. Although the abstract does not enumerate the exact defensive techniques, it clearly frames practical implementation as central. The intended outcome is a “practical understanding” that participants can apply when developing LLM products [record_id:171].

The attack taxonomy in the 2023 record is notable because it distinguishes between User Prompt Injection Attacks and Cross-domain Prompt Injection Attacks [record_id:171]. This distinction matters for downstream research because it separates direct user-originated manipulation from attacks crossing contextual or domain boundaries. The record does not define UPIA and XPIA in detail, but their inclusion indicates that the talk was not merely about generic jailbreak prompting; it presented prompt injection as a structured vulnerability space with categories useful for both testing and defense [record_id:171].

The 2024 record’s primary tool is PyRIT, the Python Risk Identification Toolkit [record_id:135]. PyRIT is described as open-source and designed for “security risk identification and red teaming in generative AI systems” [record_id:135]. It is presented as model-agnostic and platform-agnostic, enabling red teamers to probe multimodal generative AI models for “novel harms, risks, and jailbreaks” [record_id:135]. The mention of multimodality is important: the framework is not limited to text-only LLMs but is positioned for a maturing GenAI ecosystem that includes single- and multi-modal models [record_id:135].

Architecturally, PyRIT is described as composable. Its “composable architecture” is said to facilitate reuse of core building blocks and extensibility to future models and modalities [record_id:135]. That is one of the strongest technical claims in the corpus. The record does not provide code-level details, module names, evaluation data, or example workflows, but it does describe the intended design principles: extensibility, reuse, model independence, platform independence, and applicability to real-world scenarios [record_id:135].

The 2025 record’s title points to an “agent-based framework” for “adversarial simulation and blue teaming,” which would be methodologically significant if supported by an abstract. However, the raw text contains only the names “Lopez Munoz; Mauricio Velazco; Manuel MelŽndez” [record_id:130]. Therefore, the only defensible content claim is that the record is associated with those named contributors. Any detailed claims about agent architectures, blue-team workflows, simulation environments, or adversary emulation would require additional source material beyond this record.

## Notable Talks, Records, And Evidence

The most detailed record is the 2024 PyRIT record. It is notable because it identifies a concrete open-source framework and explains the motivation for that framework: GenAI is becoming ubiquitous, the model ecosystem is expanding across single- and multimodal systems, and red teamers need extensible, model-agnostic risk identification capabilities [record_id:135]. The record positions PyRIT as a practical response to these pressures. It is also the clearest example of Lopez Munoz-associated work contributing reusable tooling rather than only conceptual discussion or training [record_id:135].

The 2023 prompt injection tutorial is also highly representative. It captures the early applied-security framing around LLMs: powerful transformer-based models are useful and broadly adopted, but they introduce manipulation and exploitation risks [record_id:171]. The tutorial’s format matters because it is explicitly hands-on. Rather than only describing prompt injection, it promises implementation of LLM bots, attack exercises, and defensive implementation [record_id:171]. For downstream research agents interested in pedagogy, security training, or the practical operationalization of LLM security knowledge, this record is especially relevant.

The 2023 record also contributes the clearest vulnerability taxonomy in the corpus. Its explicit mention of UPIA and XPIA suggests a structured view of prompt injection [record_id:171]. The abstract does not fully define or compare these categories, but it indicates that the talk organized prompt injection threats in a way that could support both offensive testing and defensive engineering [record_id:171].

The 2025 record is notable mainly as a coverage point and as a possible sign of continued evolution in the research direction. The record title suggests “An Agent-Based Framework for Adversarial Simulation and Blue Teaming,” and the raw text lists Lopez Munoz along with Mauricio Velazco and Manuel MelŽndez [record_id:130]. However, because no abstract or description is included in the raw text, this record is weak evidence for specific technical claims. It should be used as a pointer for follow-up rather than as a basis for detailed synthesis [record_id:130].

Taken together, the records support a narrative of applied AI security work moving from prompt injection education to red-team tooling and possibly toward agent-based adversarial simulation. The evidence is strongest for the 2023 and 2024 steps and thin for the 2025 step.
