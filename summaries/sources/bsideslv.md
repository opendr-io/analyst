# Topic: Source: bsideslv

## Executive Summary

The BSidesLV source is a broad 2025 conference corpus covering technical security research, hands-on training, community programming, policy discussion, career development, and social/logistical events. The dominant pattern is breadth: records range from deep exploit and vulnerability research in identity, browsers, Active Directory, cloud, mobile, PHP, Postgres, F5, endpoint tools, and containers, to strategic sessions on critical infrastructure resilience, cyber policy, workforce development, public safety, and community care.

## Major Themes And Trends

### AI as both attack surface and security substrate

The most visible trend is the normalization of AI across offensive, defensive, governance, and software-development contexts. Multiple records treat LLMs and agents as practical systems that now need conventional security testing, governance, and monitoring. AI governance is addressed directly through lifecycle oversight, NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act alignment, and tabletop incident response [record_id:2387]. Strategic planning for AI and cybersecurity is framed through “mental models” for anticipating threats and opportunities [record_id:2488], while AI infrastructure is treated as critical infrastructure vulnerable to espionage, supply-chain attacks, and national-security competition [record_id:2531]. Energy resilience is presented as a prerequisite for AI dominance, shifting AI security discussion from model behavior to power-sector dependencies and transformer shortages [record_id:2504].

Offensively, records describe autonomous or semi-autonomous AI-enabled misuse. “Agentic AI Malware” proposes an autonomous PowerShell agent that dynamically generates and executes code in memory and adapts behavior, while also qualifying that current malware implementations still have limited practical advantages over traditional methods [record_id:2391]. AI agents for phishing infrastructure broaden the threat model beyond phishing-email generation to domain registration, DNS setup, landing-page deployment, and credential harvesting [record_id:2394]. “Human Attack Surfaces in Agentic Web” argues that AI agents will interact with other agents on behalf of humans, creating new fraud and cognitive-security risks [record_id:2466]. Another record describes weaponizing LLM fine-tuning as a covert C2 and exfiltration channel, including the challenge that traditional security tools may struggle to detect such channels [record_id:2573].

Defensively, AI and ML are used for detection, triage, and workflow support. RAG and MCP are positioned as ways to inject policy, threat intel, and log data into LLM workflows for SOCs, auditors, and compliance teams [record_id:2515]. RAGnarok uses a local quantized LLM, RAG, and a multi-agent system to generate Sigma rules from threat intelligence while keeping environmental data local [record_id:2516]. A cognitive detection framework proposes SLM/LLM agents that link signals, reconstruct timelines, prioritize alerts, and guide response [record_id:2530]. A network intrusion-detection proposal combines standardized feature extraction with dynamic ensemble learning and reports 97.92% detection accuracy [record_id:2390]. Other ML-focused records examine poisoned training-data visualization [record_id:2503], predicting lifespans of Internet services with lessons from failed ML attempts [record_id:2505], and interpretable ML for alert/log correlation without black-box LLMs [record_id:2525].

A related subtheme is skepticism toward AI hype. Desktop applications with local LLMs are described as creating new attack surfaces while preserving old bug classes like memory corruption and unsafe file parsing [record_id:2428]. “Vibe coding” is framed as an enterprise risk that can generate superficially functional but insecure code, distort hiring pipelines, and overinflate executive expectations [record_id:2568]. The “Torment Nexus” record questions the reliability of ML-based malware detection by showing that minor malware modifications can reduce detectability without changing behavior [record_id:2420]. AI censorship-evasion and content-moderation bypass are presented through an adversarial lens [record_id:2449]. The overall trend is not “AI solves security,” but “AI becomes another complex socio-technical system requiring threat modeling, governance, red teaming, and operational discipline.”

### Identity, credentials, passwords, and non-human identities

The records repeatedly return to the fragility of identity systems. OAuth/OIDC implementation errors—missing state parameters, unvalidated discovery documents, token-validation failures, and JWT replay—are presented as ways to bypass authentication and impersonate users [record_id:2383]. Active Directory remains a major target: one workshop covers NTLM relay, Kerberoasting, Machine Account Quota abuse, unconstrained delegation, LDAP signing, Windows Event Forwarding, and Azure-hosted labs [record_id:2388], while another record introduces “BadSuccessor,” an attack abusing Windows Server 2025 delegated Managed Service Accounts to obtain Kerberos tickets for high-privilege principals or NTLM hashes [record_id:2547].

Passwords receive sustained attention through PasswordsCon. One record claims experience cracking 936 million passwords with 92% found, discussing hardware, tools, wordlists, custom rules, CPU/GPU tradeoffs, and defenses [record_id:2417]. Another argues that forced password rotation reduces entropy and encourages predictable mutations, proposing time-to-crack scoring, event-driven rotations, and credential-risk thresholds instead [record_id:2500]. Hidden identities outside corporate SSO are framed as unmanaged SaaS-era accounts that evade organizational password policy [record_id:2418]. Malicious browser extensions and browser-cookie theft extend identity risk to the endpoint and browser layer [record_id:2438] [record_id:2543]. Session protection is also explored historically and prospectively through Device Bound Session Credentials [record_id:2570].

Non-human identity is a growing cluster. One record asks whether application and workload identity can move away from long-lived credentials [record_id:2468]. Another states that non-human identities such as service accounts, bots, and automation outnumber humans by at least 45 to 1 and cites the OWASP Top 10 Non-Human Identity Risks in 2025 [record_id:2572]. Cloud-provider-managed non-human identities are threat-modeled across major clouds [record_id:2552], and machine identity misconfiguration is tied to attack-path mapping in multi-cloud environments using tools such as SecBridge, Cartography, and AWSPX [record_id:2484]. Secret-free authentication across AWS and Azure Kubernetes clusters is proposed via AWS IRSA and Azure Workload ID with Terraform source code [record_id:2396]. Secret scanning appears from a different angle: distributed scanning of VS Code extensions and public sources using Kubernetes and cloud credits [record_id:2408].

The evidence suggests a shift from user-password-centric identity toward a broader identity fabric: humans, browsers, SaaS accounts, service accounts, workloads, cloud-managed identities, tokens, and certificates all require governance.

### Cloud, containers, Kubernetes, and infrastructure misconfiguration

Cloud security records emphasize misconfiguration, visibility, and preventive controls. Shadow cloud deployments and forgotten proof-of-concept environments are framed as unmonitored weak links; Luminaut is presented as an open-source tool for identifying internet-exposed services in AWS or Google Cloud and correlating internal cloud resources with audit timelines and external scanner context [record_id:2414]. Kubernetes is addressed through prevention-first policy enforcement with Kyverno and KubeArmor [record_id:2429], cluster “shenanigans” that emerged from interview-question research [record_id:2446], and seccomp profiles for containers using Kubescape and an Apache Druid vulnerability case study [record_id:2456]. Multi-cloud offensive/defensive training appears as a four-part workshop series across AWS, Azure, and GCP using CWL RedCloud OS for adversary simulation and blue-team response [record_id:2489] [record_id:2490] [record_id:2491] [record_id:2492].

GCP receives focused attention through Identity-Aware Proxy misconfiguration and a claimed new IAP vulnerability enabling data exfiltration behind hardened network perimeters [record_id:2495]. Google-owned container images in Artifact Registry are said to have been scanned for vulnerabilities and secrets [record_id:2467]. Managed database risk appears in CVE-2024-10979, described as a Postgres RCE affecting cloud-hosted databases [record_id:2528]. These records collectively show a pattern: cloud perimeters and managed services can create a false sense of safety if identity bindings, service exposure, runtime controls, and supply-chain artifacts are not continuously examined.

### Application security: from bug hunting to bug-class elimination

Application security records range from individual exploit classes to systemic defenses. Business-logic vulnerabilities appear in invitation systems used by social platforms such as Facebook and Snapchat [record_id:2406], SaaS data platforms with high-risk architectural and logic pitfalls [record_id:2473], and autonomous discovery of logic-based API vulnerabilities using reinforcement-learning agents and the Marvin framework for vulnerable REST/GraphQL APIs [record_id:2395]. PHP object injection is revisited through a technique that bypasses `__wakeup()` using arbitrary object instantiation outside `unserialize()` [record_id:2567]. iOS reverse engineering is presented through non-jailbroken YouTube app modification using app packaging, method swizzling, dynamic analysis, and Apple Silicon Macs [record_id:2537].

A major countertrend is moving beyond one-bug-at-a-time patching. A workshop and a talk from Javan Rasokat argue for eliminating entire bug classes using browser security features: CSP v3, Trusted Types, and Sec-Fetch-Metadata [record_id:2433] [record_id:2580]. Google’s frontend-scale XSS record describes strict CSP, Trusted Types, compile-time protections, and platform-level defaults as a path toward a “post-XSS world” [record_id:2532]. Product-security strategy also appears in a talk distinguishing application security, platform security, and product security while rethinking “shift left” [record_id:2384]. Security theater is criticized when policies and frameworks lack product or business context [record_id:2533]. The trend is toward scaled, embedded, context-aware application security.

### Detection engineering, SOC overload, CTI fragmentation, and adversary emulation

Detection and threat intelligence records show both technical and organizational concerns. SIGMA appears twice as a shared detection format used to translate logic across SIEMs and EDRs and to mature hunting workflows [record_id:2448] [record_id:2529]. A structured-analytics talk brings traditional intelligence methods—Analysis of Competing Hypotheses, Key Assumptions Check, Red Team Analysis—into CTI workflows alongside MITRE ATT&CK, the Diamond Model, and the Intelligence Cycle [record_id:2561]. A threat-landscaping workshop teaches business functions to identify threat actors, tools, and assets and continuously update threat landscapes [record_id:2424]. Threat and adversary emulation training covers CTI gathering, ATT&CK mapping, emulation plans, and defensive tooling such as AV, proxies, EDR, and SIEM [record_id:2562].

The corpus also presents CTI as fragmented and overwhelming. One record claims analysis of over 50 commercial CTI providers across more than two years, with 1.2 trillion IOCs in 2024 and a 2 trillion projection for 2025, and argues that fragmentation stacks the deck against defenders [record_id:2439]. Open-source and commercial-tool tension appears in the MISP-focused talk on augmenting enterprise workflows with open-source threat investigation tools [record_id:2559]. Botnet takedown work emphasizes inter-provider collaboration: Black Lotus Labs’ takedown of “ngioweb,” the NSOCKS proxy network, required cooperation among ISPs and ASNs, followed by coercive responses from botnet operators [record_id:2550]. A Turla/Secret Blizzard case study describes actor-on-actor infrastructure compromise and mapping activity through rogue C2 visibility [record_id:2527].

SOC automation and correlation are prominent. Attack Flow Detector uses clustering, temporal knowledge graphs, and Markovian sequencing to reveal multi-stage attack flows [record_id:2525]. RAGnarok and cognitive-agent SOC frameworks use LLMs/SLMs to generate rules and reason over alerts [record_id:2516] [record_id:2530]. SMTP/IMAP C2 via Outlook is framed as a bypass of web-isolated environments and includes detection-rule and mitigation discussion [record_id:2536]. Tree-sitter is proposed for parsing, detecting, deobfuscating, and blocking obfuscated PowerShell, Python, and JavaScript, including an AMSI provider DLL demonstration [record_id:2430].

### Critical infrastructure, public safety, and societal resilience

The I Am The Cavalry and related records form one of the most coherent clusters in the source. Critical infrastructure is not treated abstractly; records discuss water, power, healthcare, emergency medical services, food, emergency communications, household preparedness, and cyber incident command. Cascading failure is a recurring concept: compromised water utilities, power infrastructure, telecommunications, and healthcare can produce direct public-health consequences [record_id:2413]. Water-sector risk is emphasized through current threats, Volt Typhoon, Iran-linked Cyber Avengers, municipal and rural water-system breaches, regulatory fragility, and deteriorating public-private trust [record_id:2427]. Cyber-Informed Engineering workshops teach how to design out cyber risk before systems enter operation, using water-sector case studies [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437].

Healthcare and emergency care records stress ransomware, phishing, supply-chain disruption, legacy medical devices, unsegmented networks, third-party dependencies, zero trust, incident response planning, and workforce training [record_id:2434]. End-of-life equipment is described as creating “forever-day” vulnerabilities across healthcare, manufacturing, energy, and transportation [record_id:2435]. Food-sector cyber risk is framed as a national-security and economic-stability issue involving logistics, processing, refrigeration, and supply-chain coordination [record_id:2451]. Energy resilience is tied to AI competitiveness and national security [record_id:2504]. Nuclear reactor control-system targeting is presented as a Skytalks case story [record_id:2544]. Power-grid physical compromise appears in a red-team war story reaching an EMP-secure server room [record_id:2546].

Preparedness and command structures are another subtheme. Cyber Incident Command System adapts NIMS-style incident command as a “people orchestration layer” [record_id:2423]. Critical infrastructure mapping at city level exposes disconnects between federal sector definitions and local preparedness [record_id:2487]. WarGames 2027 uses a choose-your-own-adventure format for destructive attacks on lifeline functions [record_id:2534]. Household resilience advocates one-month supplies of food, water, and medicine [record_id:2494]. A concluding session focuses on short-term, incremental actions to reduce community and household risk [record_id:2564]. Ransomware is described as a canary for societal disruption and a driver for both defensive investment and possible countermeasures [record_id:2517].

### Vulnerability management, CVE governance, and risk prioritization

Several records challenge vulnerability-management orthodoxy. EPSS, CVSSv4, SSVC, and VEX are proposed as a unified approach for triaging vulnerabilities in regulated sectors [record_id:2523]. Another talk argues teams often focus on interesting CVSS scores, headlines, or zero-days rather than the largest risks to the organization [record_id:2524]. CVE transparency and governance are major concerns: one talk explores concealment and incomplete disclosure in CVE processes [record_id:2549], while a panel asks what CVE should become amid funding challenges, EU Vulnerability Database developments, congressional audit attention, and risks of ecosystem fragmentation [record_id:2571]. Financial systemic risk is framed as broader than CVEs: operational vulnerabilities in IT and information-security processes can amplify CVE risk and potentially spark a financial crisis [record_id:2569].

This theme intersects with supply chain and open source. Malicious packages are treated as a broad class of risk from typosquatting to compromise of popular packages, with SCA tools described as useful but insufficient against malicious packages [record_id:2485]. LLM-assisted open-source supply-chain analysis claims to have found over 900 undisclosed vulnerabilities, thousands of malicious packages, and a live Lazarus Group operation involving the official Ripple SDK [record_id:2474]. Open-source defensive stacks using Trivy, GitLeaks, Checkov, ZAP, and OpenGrep are presented as practical alternatives or complements to expensive vendor offerings [record_id:2440].

### Hardware, physical, RF, mobile, OT, and biosecurity edges

The records include a strong maker/hardware/physical-security strand. HID attacks using O.MG Devices and DuckyScript are taught as a physical red-team technique [record_id:2453]. Physical intrusion case studies compare Japan and the United States and emphasize cultural differences, social engineering, and staff cooperation [record_id:2472]. Hotel physical/digital vulnerabilities are described through a Skytalks story about persistent access and sheltering homeless people [record_id:2496]. DIY hacking devices and Wi-Fi assessment rigs show a hands-on culture around hardware and RF [record_id:2540] [record_id:2578]. Laser/light hardware attacks are framed as becoming affordable through maker approaches, including laser fault injection, hardware-malware detection, supply-chain chip-replacement detection, and laser logic-state imaging [record_id:2479]. GPU destruction for key-protection and supply-chain defense is proposed as a physical guarantee stronger than zeroization [record_id:2563].

IoT and cyber-physical examples include NFC bioimplant ransomware [record_id:2482], BLE reverse engineering of a smart blender [record_id:2553], ATM penetration testing [record_id:2545], and power-grid/server-room intrusion [record_id:2546]. ## Gaps, Limits, And Open Questions

The primary limitation is that the records are abstracts. They frequently promise live demos, open-source tools, case studies, or metrics, but the raw text rarely includes full methodology, code, datasets, slides, reproducibility instructions, or peer review. Claims such as high ML accuracy [record_id:2390], massive password-cracking success [record_id:2417], CTI forecast value of aged-out IOCs [record_id:2439], LLM-assisted discovery of hundreds of vulnerabilities [record_id:2474], and dramatic reductions in phishing clicks [record_id:2582] need follow-up evidence before downstream researchers treat them as established findings.

Some records deliberately signal sensitive or unrecorded contexts, especially Skytalks. “Ask the Fed” says it is a chance to ask federal-government members questions “you don’t want recorded” [record_id:2393]. Several token sessions describe provocative or potentially sensitive topics at high level without enough detail for validation, including foreign corporate espionage removal [record_id:2386], biotech circumvention [record_id:2389], drone-strike-linked ransomware recovery [record_id:2442], nuclear reactor targeting [record_id:2544], and hotel exploitation [record_id:2496]. These may be valuable leads, but the evidence is thin from the abstract alone.

Duplication and scheduling variants also limit interpretation. Some records are repeated sessions or related variants: Career Campaigns has Monday AM/PM versions [record_id:2411] [record_id:2412], Engineering Cyber Resilience for the Water Sector appears twice [record_id:2436] [record_id:2437], the multi-cloud workshop spans four sessions with identical descriptions [record_id:2489] [record_id:2490] [record_id:2491] [record_id:2492], HIBR appears as both Ground Truth and Skytalks variants [record_id:2470] [record_id:2471], Sigma appears in workshop/talk form with similar text [record_id:2448] [record_id:2529], and browser bug-class elimination appears as both a workshop and a talk [record_id:2433] [record_id:2580]. These should not be overcounted as independent research findings.

The corpus is heavily skewed toward 2025 conference programming. It does not include prior-year comparison unless individual abstracts mention historical context. Downstream agents asking about long-term BSidesLV trends would need additional years. Similarly, while many records discuss policy, governance, or regulation, they rarely include policy text or detailed legal analysis beyond session descriptions [record_id:2392] [record_id:2421] [record_id:2579].

Open questions for further research include: Which promised tools are actually public and maintained? Which vulnerability claims resulted in CVEs, patches, advisories, or public writeups? Which AI-security talks include reproducible benchmarks rather than demonstrations? How did BSidesLV handle dual-use content in talks on phishing automation, malware, C2, exploitation, physical intrusion, and biotech? How do the critical infrastructure recommendations align with existing federal, state, and local programs? And how much of the career/workforce content reflects broader industry conditions versus the BSidesLV community’s specific needs?
