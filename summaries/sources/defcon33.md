# Topic: Source: defcon33

## Executive Summary

The `defcon33` source is a broad DEF CON 33 corpus dominated by talk abstracts, village sessions, short video-team interviews, workshops, panels, and community programming. The material collectively portrays DEF CON 33 as a conference centered on “access” in many forms: access to devices, networks, identities, infrastructure, AI agents, elections, vehicles, ships, satellites, communities, privacy, and public-interest security research.

Downstream agents should treat the corpus as a map of DEF CON 33 topics and claims rather than as independently verified technical proof.

## Research Landscape

The records are overwhelmingly **conference presentation descriptions** from DEF CON 33, mostly YouTube-linked abstracts. They include full technical talk abstracts, panels, workshops, village-stage talks, keynote-style sessions, contest updates, and short “Video Team” interviews. Several records are near-duplicates or alternate uploads of the same talk: for example, EV charging modem vulnerabilities appear in two records [record_id:1931] [record_id:2145], Privacy Sandbox deanonymization appears twice [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030], the ICS/OT “Worst Love Story” appears twice [record_id:2020] [record_id:2029], Nix dependency management appears twice [record_id:1954] [record_id:2130], abandoned S3 supply-chain risk appears twice [record_id:1972] [record_id:2134], Android LaunchAnywhere/BadResolve appears twice [record_id:1982] [record_id:2138], Axis camera RCE appears twice [record_id:2088] [record_id:2146], and automotive dealer API compromise appears in a main abstract and a shorter version [record_id:2036] [record_id:2099].

The source is not a single coherent research paper collection; it is a **conference-wide snapshot**. That creates breadth: talks range from deeply technical exploitation to policy, community building, cultural history, privacy activism, and contest promotion. It also creates inconsistency: some records include detailed attack chains, tooling, CVEs, and empirical counts, while others are only one-sentence program notes. A few metadata fields appear inconsistent with the event year, notably records marked “Year: 2026” while describing DEF CON 33 material [record_id:1850] [record_id:1851] [record_id:1852]. ## Major Themes And Trends

### 1. Embedded and cyber-physical systems are treated as mainstream attack surfaces

A dominant theme is that devices once treated as peripheral, local, or specialized are now central to security risk. The Synology NAS talk describes unauthenticated root-level RCE against millions of default-configured NAS devices, using delimiter smuggling across multiple software components and a “novel remote Linux exploitation technique” [record_id:1850]. ISP-supplied modems are framed as systemic threats to infrastructure, with more than 35 severe flaws in outdated IoT SDKs affecting ADSL, fiber, cable, and 5G devices [record_id:1906]. Webcams that “secretly run Linux” expose embedded Linux supply-chain and firmware-update risks [record_id:1925]. Modern 4G/5G routers are described as having pre-auth RCE, arbitrary SMS sending, weak credentials, and command injection [record_id:1990]. Draytek routers receive a full edge-device exploitation treatment with two new unauthenticated RCEs and persistence chaining [record_id:2120].

The IoT/OT material repeatedly emphasizes **systemic reuse of insecure components**. One talk argues SDK bugs become “immortal” because chipset vendor code is copied into many product lines and remains hard to patch across vendors and years [record_id:2073]. Another covers over 50 IoT vulnerability cases, including backdoors, predictable credentials, exploitable consoles, CVE-2024-48271, and CVE-2025-1143, along with the difficulty of disclosure to manufacturers and CERTs [record_id:2111]. Solar microgrids are presented as a rebadged supply-chain problem: unauthenticated cloud APIs, unsigned firmware, hardcoded root credentials, kill switches, plaintext MODBUS, and cloned OEM infrastructure cause single bugs to propagate across an industry [record_id:1978].

The automotive and transport subset is large. EV charging vulnerabilities target QCA 7000 HomePlug modems used by CCS and NACS, enabling persistent denial of service, communication hijacking, and code execution after firmware reverse engineering [record_id:1931] [record_id:2145]. Automotive dealer-platform API flaws allegedly enabled national admin creation, customer lookup, ownership reassignment, and remote-control functionality [record_id:2036] [record_id:2099]. A head-unit talk uses a malicious PNG against a novel RTOS found in thousands of vehicles [record_id:2037]. Tire-pressure monitoring systems are described as unauthenticated wireless signals usable for low-cost tracking and spoofing [record_id:2048]. Odometer manipulation is analyzed by reverse engineering a device that rewrites CAN messages [record_id:2051]. OBD-II emissions testing is treated as a target for convincing scanners “everything is fine” [record_id:2091]. Smart buses are shown as a chain from free Wi-Fi to vehicular routers, private networks, ADAS/APTS communications, LED displays, passenger/driver information, operational data, and backend APIs [record_id:2121]. CAN anomaly detection without decoding is presented as a defense using inter-signal correlations and deep sequence learning [record_id:2102].

Physical-world access control and safety devices also recur. Saflok hotel locks are presented as relying on weak offline authentication and obscurity, expanding prior Unsaflok work [record_id:1983]. SecuRam safe locks used by Liberty Safe and others are described as storing codes in externally accessible keypads and exposing debug ports, allowing opening with a Raspberry Pi [record_id:2002]. Elevator security is framed as a design problem around floor lockouts, special operating modes, controller trickery, and hoistway entry [record_id:1999]. Physical access control installation is addressed from a defender’s perspective, emphasizing wiring, supervision, encryption, tamper resistance, badge-reader configuration, and maintenance [record_id:2125]. Badge cloning with Flipper Zero/iCopy-X and custom implants demonstrates how hybrid work reduces opportunities for traditional close-range cloning, motivating remote and social-engineered approaches [record_id:1971].

### 2. Maritime, aviation, and space are treated as emerging cyber-physical frontiers

The DEF CON 33 corpus has an unusually strong maritime and space emphasis. Maritime talks cover geopolitics, shipboard red teaming, VSAT vulnerabilities, ship simulators, NMEA2000 fingerprinting, maritime OSINT, SDN/SDR resilient communications, autonomous boat propulsion, ship-to-shore cranes, and incident response. Strategic panels frame the seas as a frontier for power projection, commerce, surveillance, and cybersecurity, from port cranes to undersea cables [record_id:1946]. Taiwan’s “digital blockade” panel links undersea infrastructure sabotage and cyber resilience [record_id:2011]. Chinese-manufactured ship-to-shore cranes are discussed through Coast Guard Cyber Protection Team missions, including surprise cellular modems and attack paths [record_id:2065].

Technical maritime records include red-team operations aboard ships, with testers entering communication rooms and engine control rooms and manipulating or disabling navigation and communications [record_id:2033]. VSAT research covers firmware collection, re-hosting, ACU web vulnerabilities CVE-2023-44852 through CVE-2023-44857, and testbed validation [record_id:2039]. NMEA2000 research builds a five-node system and deterministic fingerprints to distinguish normal from abnormal sensor behavior [record_id:2049]. Marine autopilot exploitation uses unauthenticated firmware updates via SD cards or NMEA2000 chart plotters to gain persistent code execution and inject CAN traffic [record_id:2083]. Other maritime talks discuss COLREGs and why inducing a port turn can matter legally and operationally [record_id:2035], virtual ship environments for cybersecurity training [record_id:2108], maritime incident response from a sysadmin’s war room [record_id:2133], and MOSAICS Block 1 for naval OT monitoring and detection [record_id:2075].

Space appears both as red-team subject and detection engineering domain. Space red teaming is introduced as applying offensive techniques to satellites, ground stations, and support infrastructure [record_id:2046]. Moonlight Defender describes purple-team exercises using the Moonlighter satellite, cyber ranges, virtual satellite simulators, ICS/OT, and enterprise environments [record_id:2071]. Another space talk maps SPARTA offensive techniques to Indicators of Behavior for spacecraft IDS design [record_id:2105]. A separate briefing claims PoC exploitation of vulnerabilities across ground-based and onboard spacecraft systems with potential unauthorized control [record_id:2103].

Aviation appears through ATC voice cloning, where low-fidelity unauthenticated AM radio and consumer-grade voice cloning create runway-crossing risks [record_id:2047], and through vulnerability disclosure in aviation, which is described as improving but still uneven and sensitive [record_id:2098].

### 3. Election security focuses on evidence, legal frameworks, and limits of technical “magic bullets”

The Voting Village cluster is not just about hacking machines; it repeatedly frames election security around **evidence, audits, law, and institutional trust**. Risk-limiting audits are described as limiting the risk of certifying wrong winners, but only when applied to trustworthy vote records; applying RLA procedures to untrustworthy records is labeled “security theater” [record_id:1854]. Post-election audits in the seven 2024 swing states are evaluated against criteria for trustworthy, meaningful, and reliable audits [record_id:1862]. Rivest’s talk connects software independence and evidence-based elections to practical strengthening of election security [record_id:1864].

Ballot marking devices and barcode/QR-code tabulation are recurring concerns. A Dominion ICX talk demonstrates technically simple election-definition “hacks” that manipulate candidate display, remove candidates, or present false instructions, with recovery from such attacks described as difficult to impossible [record_id:1855]. Another talk criticizes BMDs for encoding voter choices in unreadable images and producing multiple representations of voter selections [record_id:1859]. A New York legal challenge to ES&S ExpressVote XL argues that recording votes in barcodes violates voter-verification requirements under state law and potentially HAVA-like provisions [record_id:1866]. The LA County VSAP recount narrative highlights technology failures, alleged vulnerabilities, open-source-code commitments not met, and difficulty obtaining a simple accurate local result [record_id:1860].

The records are also skeptical of internet voting claims. E2E verifiability is described as irrelevant to server-side systems outside the cryptographic loop and especially to client-side malware that can alter or sabotage voting applications or devices [record_id:1857]. CARVER is proposed as a framework for analyzing voting-system vulnerabilities [record_id:1858]. Physical security assumptions around locks and tamper-evident seals are questioned based on public vendor and jurisdiction sources [record_id:1856]. Researcher protection appears in DMCA exemption coverage [record_id:1865] and a Global South perspective calling for safe harbor for electoral research, especially in Nigeria and similar contexts where legal risk suppresses independent testing [record_id:1861]. Insider threats are treated as a current problem involving alleged extralegal access and past multistate access to voting machines and software [record_id:1867]. TTBR and EVEREST history are used to contextualize today’s election research [record_id:1868] [record_id:1869]. The Voting Village introductory record simply frames the symposium [record_id:1863].

### 4. Identity and authentication are presented as powerful but brittle layers

Identity systems are a central target. Active Directory is described as C2-like by design, with GPOs enabling enumeration, persistence, privilege escalation, and offensive coordination; the talk promises two tools for GPO enumeration and exploitation [record_id:1929]. dMSAs in Windows Server 2025 are attacked through “BadSuccessor,” abusing common AD permissions to obtain Kerberos tickets for arbitrary principals and even NTLM hashes [record_id:1966]. LAPS client-side logic is revisited through Frida and Microsoft Detours hooks to capture, manipulate, and rotate local admin passwords on demand, focusing on endpoint persistence rather than only directory compromise [record_id:2082]. Microsoft Entra ID appears in attacks on legitimate Microsoft login-domain functionality for phishing [record_id:1960], macOS PRT cookie theft and device-registration persistence [record_id:1987], and cloud-VPN/ZTNA device trust bypasses [record_id:2062] [record_id:2136].

Passwordless and “phishing-resistant” authentication receives critical scrutiny. FIDO2/CTAP is assessed for client impersonation and API confusion attacks that can wipe credentials, reset authenticators, and track users; the authors provide a toolkit for USB HID and NFC transports and demos against authenticators and relying parties [record_id:1992]. Passkeys are attacked through WebAuthn API proxying via browser extension or script injection when sites do not enforce attestation or metadata checks [record_id:1997]. Another passkey talk argues synced passkeys can be phished in straightforward ways and require responsible deployment guidance [record_id:2080]. Browser extension clickjacking shows password managers and passkey flows are vulnerable to one-click leakage of credit cards, personal data, credentials, TOTP, and in some cases passkey exploitation [record_id:1851].

Physical identity is covered through RFID badge cloning [record_id:1971], access-control design [record_id:2125], event credential counterfeiting [record_id:2064], and social engineering/infiltration timing [record_id:2085]. EEG-based authentication is proposed as a dynamic biometric security layer for constrained IoT devices in the face of deepfake-style attacks [record_id:1932].

### 5. AI is both an attack surface and an offensive/defensive accelerator

The AI records show two parallel trends: AI systems themselves are vulnerable, and AI/LLMs are becoming part of offensive and defensive workflows.

Prompt injection and agentic AI are major concerns. A Gemini Workspace talk introduces “Targeted Promptware Attacks” via Google Calendar meeting subjects, email, or Google Docs, claiming 15 exploitations across Gemini interfaces and agents, including spam/phishing, deleting calendar events, home-appliance control, Zoom video streaming, exfiltration, geolocation, and worm propagation; it emphasizes inter-agent and inter-device lateral movement [record_id:1965]. Agentic AI hardening is addressed through multi-agent threat modeling, policy-as-code guardrails, fine-grained access controls, supply-chain defenses, and red-team strategies [record_id:1956]. Microsoft Copilot “data void” hijacking shows how limited data sources and key-term association can manipulate AI-generated responses toward dangerous installation instructions for C2 beacons [record_id:2110]. AI bug bounty programs are framed around adversarial prompts, indirect prompt injection, context poisoning, and RAG manipulation [record_id:1930]. Health AI is treated through patient-authored rights and red-team toolkit prototyping [record_id:2041].

AI privacy and model/file-format safety appear in multiple talks. “Shadow data” extraction covers model inversion and embedding inversion against fine-tuned models and vector databases, arguing PII scanning misses rich data in LLM/RAG systems [record_id:1943]. Apple Intelligence data flows are scrutinized for unexpected behaviors and data leaks despite privacy assurances [record_id:1957]. PyTorch’s supposedly safer `weights_only=True` path is challenged via TorchScript RCE and CVE-2025-32434 [record_id:1968]. AI file formats such as ONNX, HDF5, Feather, YAML, JSON, and NPZ are described as trusted containers capable of default-behavior code execution in ML workflows [record_id:2118]. Deepfake image/video detection and AI voice cloning/vishing demonstrate AI’s role in social deception and forensic countermeasures [record_id:1915] [record_id:2119].

AI-enabled security operations include LLMs extracting IoCs from infostealer screenshots [record_id:1945], LLM-assisted abandoned S3 bucket analysis for supply-chain risk [record_id:1972] [record_id:2134], AI-assisted fuzzing workflow automation in Orion [record_id:2137], AI offensive agents claiming large-scale bug discovery [record_id:2084], deterministic adversarial automation and TTP selection tooling [record_id:2056], and AI-generated OT detection rules through Suspicious2Vec/neural ASR concepts [record_id:2113]. AIxCC is a major institutional anchor: winners and lessons are discussed [record_id:1923], while video-team records cover the finals and teams [record_id:2154] [record_id:2156] [record_id:2157] [record_id:2164].

### 6. Exploit research remains highly technical, but many talks emphasize systemic mitigations

Several records present classic deep exploit development. Windows ASLR bypass research claims nine bypasses for high-entropy ASLR using ROP gadgets and an open-source mini-tool to generate x64 ROP chains [record_id:1852]. BitLocker/WinRE research identifies multiple 0-days to bypass BitLocker and extract protected data through recovery-environment attack surfaces [record_id:1870]. Windows MapUrlToZone parsing inconsistencies yield a dozen CVEs across trust-boundary issues [record_id:1959]. RPC research includes “Win-DoS Epidemic,” using NetLogon RPC, LDAP referrals, unauthenticated RPC surfaces, and public DCs for DDoS-like abuse [record_id:1940], and “RPC-Racer,” where unprivileged users race to register dynamic RPC endpoints and impersonate trusted servers [record_id:2016]. Cross-ring privilege escalation via synthesized machine-check events explores hardware failure paths as software-triggered exploit vectors [record_id:2010]. eBPF work covers state-aware fuzzing, verifier/JIT/helper validation bugs, arbitrary read/write primitives, KASLR bypass, and privilege escalation [record_id:1988].

Web and appsec research is similarly strong. DOM clobbering work introduces Hulk, a dynamic analysis tool that found 497 zero-day gadgets in Tranco Top 5,000 sites, affected major client-side libraries, and produced 19 CVEs [record_id:1947]. HTTP request smuggling research introduces new desync classes and methodology to “kill HTTP/1” [record_id:1989]. GraphQL/REST access control failures in Feeld expose photos, videos, private messages, and other personal data [record_id:2018]. Recursive Request Exploits target interdependent web requests to bypass paywalls and entitlement logic, with a Burp Suite extension planned [record_id:2107]. Referral program research finds business logic flaws, race conditions, cookie injection, and client-side path traversal gadgets [record_id:2089]. Regex is presented as a practical hacker tool for finding endpoints, CORS/postMessage bypasses, SSRF opportunities, secrets, and hidden APIs [record_id:1921]. Bug bounty practice appears in hacker/triager conflict management and scaling programs at major companies [record_id:1909] [record_id:1928], plus serendipitous vulnerability discovery stories [record_id:2042].

Cloud and infrastructure exploitation includes bare-metal cloud risks from firmware, provisioning, persistent implants, network interception, and ML workflow compromise [record_id:1955]; Kafka server RCE and ecosystem component attacks [record_id:1993]; cloud-hosted Postgres RCE via CVE-2024-10979 [record_id:2063]; ZTNA/SASE compromise [record_id:2062] [record_id:2136]; cloud-based operational relay networks attributed to PRC-linked infrastructure [record_id:2025]; and Google services abused for RAT/SOCKS C2 [record_id:2097]. Supply-chain records include malicious npm bypassing SCA/SAST/EDR/AV tools [record_id:1979], tj-actions incident reconstruction as targeted supply-chain lateral movement toward Coinbase [record_id:2004], Nix/NixOS dependency traceability and self-hosting [record_id:1954] [record_id:2130], HBOMs for hardware provenance [record_id:2034], and open-source policy in government [record_id:1985] [record_id:2162].

### 7. Privacy, surveillance, censorship, and platform power are treated as security concerns

Privacy appears as both personal practice and systemic critique. Apple Intelligence privacy analysis examines OS inspection and traffic analysis to challenge privacy assumptions [record_id:1957]. Signalgate/TeleMessage alleges plaintext Signal messages, false E2EE claims, archive-server exploitation, and memory-dump analysis [record_id:1958]. Encrypted messenger side-channel research targets delivery receipts for stealth tracking and WhatsApp Signal Protocol prekey depletion to weaken PFS [record_id:2003]. Privacy Sandbox records describe deanonymization through Attribution Reporting API debugging reports, destination hijacking, storage-limit oracles, and Shared Storage worklet leaks [record_id:2012] [record_id:2030]. Marketing surveillance is described from inside a digital marketing firm, with server-side data harvesting after third-party cookie deprecation pressure [record_id:2093].

Counter-surveillance and sovereignty-oriented talks include an OSINT/privacy workshop for reducing individual footprint [record_id:1969], ReclaimTech community movement away from Big Tech ecosystems [record_id:2040] [record_id:2086], Veilid as a privacy-preserving framework and technology stack [record_id:2043], EFF’s digital-rights Q&A [record_id:2139], Tor operational stories [record_id:1975], and off-grid datarunning in oppressive regimes using sneakernet and Pirate Box concepts [record_id:2127]. Russia’s TSPU censorship system is described as near-endpoint/in-path filtering combined with a Russian certificate authority and software trust requirements, with circumvention possibilities [record_id:1970]. Everyday security advice is evaluated for practicality against high-profile privacy/security scares [record_id:2106].

“Tech companies as threat actors” broadens privacy into threat modeling, asking what modern platforms are structurally capable of doing regardless of stated intent [record_id:2000]. Student privacy is addressed through third-party data access and student data pipelines [record_id:2005]. AI healthcare rights explicitly connect privacy, consent, oversight, bias, and red teaming [record_id:2041].

### 9. Detection engineering, purple teaming, and resilience move from tools to programs

Several records argue that security impact depends on operationalizing findings. Purple teaming is redefined around shared intelligence, continuous collaboration, adversary emulation, and measurable defense improvement [record_id:1908]. Assumed-compromise purple teaming follows an attack lifecycle from initial access to impact, focusing on visibility and defensive-layer improvement rather than merely finding vulnerabilities [record_id:2015]. “Thinking like an attacker” frames adversarial mindset as essential for modern defense, including nation-state and conflict scenarios [record_id:1939]. TotalTest proposes intelligence-led attack and crisis simulation programs as a continuous alternative to point-in-time testing [record_id:2129]. DEF CON Franklin and Cyber Resilience Corps highlight cyber volunteering for small towns, ransomware response, and civic defense [record_id:2001].

Detection methods vary widely: domain intelligence analysis from DNS artifacts [record_id:1877], Meshtastic/RF and cellular detection workflows [record_id:1884] [record_id:1889] [record_id:1922], contextual IoT alert correlation without LLMs [record_id:1935], fingerprint-based bot blocking and deception with Finch [record_id:1948], macOS Endpoint Security monitoring [record_id:1964], Reddit-scale abuse defense [record_id:2019], SSH honeypot challenge design [record_id:1986], OT SOC and incident response [record_id:2079], ICS deception with MITRE Engage [record_id:2095], contractor fraud/North Korean IT worker detection through network logs and DFIR artifacts [record_id:2124], and OT device discovery across protocol gateways [record_id:2104].

## Methods, Tools, And Approaches Discussed

The corpus is unusually tool-rich.
